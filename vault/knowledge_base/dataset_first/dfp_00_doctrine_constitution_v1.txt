Claude Power Pack — Knowledge-Necessity Axis — v1 — sealed 2026-07-12

Binding ontology: CANONICAL_ONTOLOGY.md. Every term used here is defined there and nowhere
else. This dataset states the law. DFP-02 states the lifecycle the law invokes. DFP-05 states
the science that is permitted to prove the law wrong.


PART I — THE THESIS, AND THE EVIDENCE THAT PRODUCED IT

I.1 The law, stated once. Never build permanent infrastructure when the institutional science
that must govern it does not yet exist. A system built before its governing science is not
merely early; it is a decision made by accident. The concrete sets around whatever the builder
happened to assume on the afternoon they wrote it, and that assumption becomes the shape of
every future thing that touches it. The cost is not the code. Code is cheap and deletable. The
cost is the ontology the code silently ratifies, the names it fixes, the boundaries it draws,
and the dozen downstream artifacts that will be built against those names and boundaries
before anyone notices they were guesses.

I.2 The second half of the law, which is not optional. A protocol that always demands a corpus
before code is exactly as destructive as one that never does. It becomes a tax. The Owner
routes around a tax, and a rule routed around is not a rule; it is a decoration that consumes
attention and returns nothing. The discipline in this family earns its authority only by being
calibrated against evidence, and DFP-05 exists for the sole purpose of being permitted to
retire it. Any reading of DFP-00 that treats the first half of the law as the whole law is a
misreading, and it is the misreading that will kill this family if it is allowed to stand.

I.3 The evidence that produced the thesis, from this repository. The Power Pack has shipped
more than ten dataset families — ACIS, DRK, SQI, FD, CO, graphify, CDIO, D2A, SDD-OS, FIOS.
Every one of them was, in effect, a bet that writing the science down before building the
machine would pay. Not one of them measured whether it did. The families were built on an
intuition that has never been audited, and an intuition that has never been audited is a
superstition with good manners. This dataset states the intuition precisely enough that
DFP-05 can attack it.

I.4 The failure mode the thesis names, in its concrete form. Consider what happens when a
review authority is built before the ontology of a review exists. The builder must decide, in
the moment of writing the first function, what a verdict is. They will choose a set. The set
will be shaped by the three examples in their head that afternoon. Every subsequent artifact —
the record schema, the test names, the hook that consumes the output, the dashboard that
renders it — is built against that set. Two months later the set is discovered to be missing a
member. The cost of adding it is now not one enum line; it is one enum line plus every
consumer that assumed the set was closed, plus every stored record written under the old
cardinality, plus the doctrine file that declared the set closed and must now be amended
without lying about its own history. The science was cheap before the concrete. It is
expensive after.

I.5 Why this is not simply "write a spec first." It is not, and the distinction is the entire
reason this family exists rather than being a footnote in an existing one. The Power Pack
already forbids executing a Tier 2 or Tier 3 task without a PRD; spec_gate enforces it and
HR-SPEC-001 seals it. That law is settled and DFP does not relitigate it. The question DFP
asks sits one level above: when is a spec itself insufficient, and a governing corpus
required? A spec answers "what shall we build." A corpus answers "what is true about the
domain in which we are building, such that a spec could even be correct." A spec written
without the corpus is a confident answer to a question nobody has yet established is the
right question. Part II gives this distinction its mechanism.

I.6 The scope of "permanent." Permanence is not duration; it is entrenchment. An artifact is
permanent, for the purposes of this law, when the cost of reversing it exceeds the cost of
building it — when other things will be built against it before anyone learns whether it was
right. A rule is permanent. A gate is permanent. An ontology is permanent to the point of
being the definition of the word. A feature behind a flag, in one module, with one caller, is
not permanent no matter how long it lives, because deleting it costs one afternoon and
disappoints no dependent. This is why the reversibility classification is delegated wholesale
to DRK-02 rather than reinvented here. Permanence is a blast-radius question, and blast radius
already has an owner.

I.7 What this dataset will not do. It will not re-explain what a decision is; DRK owns that.
It will not assign epistemic levels; ACIS owns those, and a DFP that graded its own evidence
would be committing the exact self-promotion offence ACIS was built to prevent. It will not
detect duplicates; D2A owns that. It will not classify task tiers; spec_gate owns that. Every
one of those surfaces is cross-referenced by name and left alone. A Part of this family that
re-narrates a parent system instead of pointing at it is rejected and rewritten, and that
rejection criterion is not a stylistic preference — it is the mechanism by which a thin family
over a thick substrate stays thin.

PART I FINAL LAW — The law has two halves and neither is optional: build no permanent
infrastructure before its governing science exists, and demand no science that cannot be shown
to have paid for itself.


PART II — THE CORPUS-VERSUS-SPEC DISTINCTION

II.1 The distinction, precisely. A specification is an answer. A corpus is the body of
knowledge that makes an answer checkable. The spec says "the verdict set has these members."
The corpus says "here is what a verdict is, here is why the set is closed, here is the
taxonomy that generated the members, here is the failure each absent member would produce, and
here is the evidence that this partition of the space is the right partition." A spec is
falsifiable only against itself: did we build what we said. A corpus is falsifiable against
the world: was what we said the right thing to say.

II.2 The test that separates them. Ask of the proposed work: if a competent engineer, reading
only what this repository already contains, sat down to build this, would they be forced to
invent a durable abstraction that nobody has yet checked? If the answer is no — the
abstractions exist, are named, and are proven — then a spec suffices and DFP is silent. If the
answer is yes, and the abstraction they would be forced to invent will outlive them and be
built against, then the spec is a confident guess dressed as a plan, and the corpus is owed
first. This is the operative test of the entire family and it is deliberately narrow.

II.3 The four things a corpus contains that a spec cannot. First, the ontology: what the
things are and what each is called, such that two modules cannot name one concept twice.
Second, the taxonomy: what the kinds are and where the boundaries sit, such that an edge case
is discovered on paper rather than in production. Third, the failure catalogue: what breaks,
under what input, and why the existing guards do not catch it. Fourth, the evaluator: the
mechanical means by which a claim about this domain can be checked, without which every
subsequent claim is an opinion. A spec that contains all four is not a spec; it is a corpus
that has been mislabelled, and it should be moved.

II.4 The asymmetry of cost. Writing the corpus before the code costs days and produces an
artifact that is cheap to change, because nothing has been built against it yet. Writing the
corpus after the code costs the same days plus the entire cost of retrofitting every artifact
that was built against the guesses. The asymmetry is the whole argument, and it is also the
whole danger: the argument is so clean that it invites universal application, and universal
application is the bureaucracy failure of Part III. The asymmetry is real only where the
entrenchment is real.

II.5 The relationship to spec_gate, stated so the boundary cannot drift. spec_gate classifies
a free-text task into Tier 0 through Tier 3 and requires a PRD at Tier 2 and above. DFP does
not re-derive that tier; it reads it. The classifier in modules/dataset_first delegates the
tier question wholesale, and a DFP that computed its own tier would have forked spec_gate,
which INV-7 forbids. What DFP adds is orthogonal: the tier tells you how big the work is;
the sufficiency verdict tells you whether the knowledge to govern it exists. A Tier 3 task in
a well-understood domain needs a PRD and nothing more. A Tier 2 task in a domain whose
ontology has never been written may need a corpus. Size and knowledge are different axes, and
conflating them is how a protocol becomes a size-gate wearing a knowledge-gate's name.

II.6 The worked distinction, on synthetic data. A proposal arrives: build a rate limiter for
the outbound API client. Domain complexity is low, precedent is abundant, the abstraction —
a token bucket — is a named, proven, externally-validated thing. The sufficiency verdict is
sufficient, the class is direct implementation, DFP is silent, and spec_gate's PRD requirement
at whatever tier it lands is the entire governance. A second proposal arrives: build the
authority that decides which agent may modify a sealed corpus. Domain complexity is high, there
is no precedent in this repository, and the builder would be forced to invent, on the spot, an
ontology of authority, a taxonomy of modification, and a failure model for capture — three
durable abstractions that every future governance artifact will be built against. The
sufficiency verdict is insufficient, the class is knowledge-first, and the corpus is owed. The
two proposals may be identical in tier. They are not identical in what they entrench.

II.7 The trap in the distinction. The corpus-versus-spec test is a judgment, and judgments
drift toward whichever answer is more flattering to the person making them. Writing a corpus
feels like serious work; writing a rate limiter feels like a chore. An agent left to apply
this test unaided will find corpora owed everywhere, because the test rewards the answer that
produces the more interesting task. This is not a hypothetical bias; it is the specific
mechanism by which this family becomes bureaucracy. The countermeasure is that the verdict is
not the agent's to render: it is computed from ten scored dimensions by code, submitted to
DRK's kernel as a provider, and recorded with a prediction that DFP-05 will later grade.

PART II FINAL LAW — A spec answers what to build; a corpus establishes what is true about the
domain in which an answer could be correct, and only the second is owed when the build would
force the invention of a durable abstraction nobody has checked.


PART III — WHEN THE PROTOCOL DOES NOT APPLY

III.1 This Part is stated as forcefully as Part I, and it is stated third rather than last so
that no reader can absorb the law without absorbing its limit. The most likely death of this
family is not that it is ignored. It is that it is obeyed everywhere, produces four thin
corpora nobody reads, costs weeks, and is then correctly resented and correctly abandoned. A
protocol dies of false positives, not false negatives. Part III is the immune system.

III.2 The protocol does not apply to reversible work. If the artifact can be deleted in an
afternoon and nothing else will have been built against it, the entrenchment premise fails and
the entire argument of Part II collapses. DRK-02 returns Tipo A for this class. A Tipo A
proposal is never knowledge-first, regardless of how intellectually interesting the domain is.
Interest is not a signal. Entrenchment is the signal.

III.3 The protocol does not apply where precedent is abundant. If the abstraction the builder
would be forced to invent has in fact already been invented — externally, by an industry, and
validated across a thousand implementations — then the science exists; it simply does not live
in this repository. Reading it is not the same as writing it. A team that writes a corpus on
rate limiting has not produced knowledge; it has produced a book report. The signal that
distinguishes them is whether the open question is answerable by reading, or only by thinking.

III.4 The protocol does not apply where the uncertainty is empirical rather than conceptual.
If the open question is "will this actually behave the way we think under real load" — a
question about reality, not about design — then neither code nor corpus is the correct next
artifact. The correct next artifact is a cheap probe. This is the entire reason the taxonomy
carries an experiment-first class rather than collapsing to a binary, and it is why DRK's
existing run-experiment verdict is reused rather than a new one invented. An expensive corpus
written to resolve a question that a two-hour experiment would have settled is the most
embarrassing failure this family can produce, because it is the failure of a thinking system
that refused to look.

III.5 The protocol does not apply to work whose knowledge is already sealed. If a corpus
governing this domain already exists in the vault and is certified, the science exists and the
build proceeds. The sufficiency engine reads the existing assets and the ACIS ceiling they
reach. A protocol that demanded a second corpus because it failed to notice the first would be
a duplicate-generator, and D2A exists to stop exactly that.

III.6 The protocol does not apply merely because a task is large. Size is spec_gate's axis.
A large task in a well-understood domain is a large task, and the governance it needs is a PRD
and a done-gate, both of which already exist. The single most common way this family will
misfire is by becoming a second size-gate, firing on the word "system" or "universal" in a
prompt, demanding a corpus for work whose science was settled a decade ago. The classifier's
keyword signals are deliberately weighted so that transversality and irreversibility dominate
and raw size does not.

III.7 The bureaucracy signature, so it can be recognised early. Four symptoms, any two of
which together are a diagnosis. The corpora being produced are thin — near the floor of the
density band, because they were written to satisfy a gate rather than to record a science. The
predictions attached to the necessity records are trivially true, because a prediction written
to be graded favourably is written to be unfalsifiable. The Owner's override rate is climbing,
and the overrides are correct. And the implementations that follow the corpora do not cite
them — the corpus was produced, filed, and never read, which is the purest possible proof that
it was never needed. DFP-05 owns the measurement of all four, and the honest consequence of
finding them is not to tune the thresholds; it is to retire the protocol.

III.8 The standing instruction that follows from this Part. When the sufficiency verdict is
close to the boundary — when the score sits within a few points of a threshold — the protocol
resolves toward direct implementation, not toward the corpus. The asymmetry is deliberate. A
false negative costs a refactor. A false positive costs the credibility of the whole
discipline, and credibility, once spent, does not come back with a threshold adjustment.

PART III FINAL LAW — This protocol dies of false positives, not false negatives; when the
verdict is near the boundary it resolves toward building, and a corpus that the subsequent
implementation never cites is proof the corpus was never owed.


PART IV — THE FOUR PROJECT CLASSES

IV.1 The taxonomy is closed at four members and the closure is defended. Direct
implementation. Experiment first. Hybrid. Knowledge first. A fifth member has been proposed
and rejected in the drafting of this dataset — a "documentation only" class — on the grounds
that it describes an artifact, not a state of knowledge, and the taxonomy classifies states of
knowledge. The test of whether a proposed member belongs is whether it names a distinct
relationship between what is known and what is about to be entrenched. Anything else is a
different taxonomy wearing this one's name.

IV.2 Direct implementation. The knowledge that must govern this build exists, is named, and is
reachable. The builder will invent nothing durable that has not been checked. This is the
default, the most common verdict, and the one the protocol should return most often. A
protocol whose modal verdict is anything else is a protocol that has mistaken itself for the
main event. The deciding question: could a competent engineer, reading what we already have,
build this correctly without guessing at an abstraction that will outlive them?

IV.3 Experiment first. The open question is about reality rather than design, and it is
cheaply answerable by observation. Neither the code nor the corpus is the correct next
artifact, because both would encode an assumption that an afternoon of measurement would
replace with a fact. This class maps onto DRK's existing run-experiment verdict, which is
reused rather than duplicated. The deciding question: is the thing we do not know a fact about
the world, and is there a probe that would tell us?

IV.4 Hybrid. The core of the domain is understood and its edges are genuinely not. The corpus
grows alongside the implementation rather than blocking it: the ontology and the failure
catalogue are written as the build reveals them, and the certification comes at the end rather
than the beginning. This is the class that prevents the taxonomy from being a binary, and it
is the correct home for most genuinely hard work. It carries a specific risk: hybrid is the
comfortable answer, and a classifier that returns it too often has learned to avoid committing.
DFP-05 tracks the hybrid rate for exactly this reason, and a hybrid rate above half is a
gaming signal, not a sign of nuance.

IV.5 Knowledge first. The build, undertaken now, would permanently encode a decision nobody yet
knows how to make. Implementation is blocked until the corpus is certified, and that block is
the only block this family owns. The deciding question, stated as sharply as it can be: would
building this now force the invention of a durable abstraction that every future artifact will
be built against, and that nobody has checked?

IV.6 The mapping into DRK, which is the mechanism by which this family has authority without
being an authority. DFP does not own a verdict space. It maps into DRK's sealed one: direct
implementation to approve or approve-with-conditions, experiment first to run-experiment,
hybrid to approve-with-conditions, and knowledge first to a verdict DRK did not have. The
eleventh member — build-knowledge-first — is the single genuinely-new element, and Part VIII
describes the governed amendment by which it was added rather than assumed.

IV.7 The classes are ordered by cost, and the ordering is the tiebreak rule. Direct
implementation is cheapest. Experiment first is next. Hybrid is next. Knowledge first is the
most expensive thing this stack can ask for. When two classes are within the noise of each
other, the cheaper wins. This rule exists because the alternative — resolving ties toward the
more thorough-sounding class — is precisely how a protocol inflates, and it inflates in the
direction that feels most responsible, which is why it is never noticed until the Owner is
routing around it.

IV.8 A worked classification, on synthetic data, to fix the boundaries in something concrete.
Four proposals arrive in one week. The first: add a retry with exponential backoff to the
outbound notification client. Domain complexity two, reuse breadth three, cost of error two,
governance need zero, uncertainty one, lifespan three, transversality one, criticality four,
institutional capacity one, prior-science need one. Aggregate eighteen. Direct implementation,
and the protocol says nothing at all, which is the correct behaviour and the most common one.

IV.9 The second: build a scheduler that decides which of several concurrent agent panes may
hold a write lock on a shared index. Domain complexity seven, reuse breadth six, cost of error
eight, governance need seven, uncertainty six, lifespan seven, transversality six, criticality
eight, institutional capacity three, prior-science need five. Aggregate sixty-three. Hybrid:
the core is understood — locks, leases, fencing tokens are all named and proven abstractions —
but the edges, specifically what a pane's identity is across a restart, are genuinely unknown
in this repository. The corpus grows alongside the implementation and certification comes at
the end. Note that the institutional-capacity score of three is what keeps this out of the
knowledge-first class despite the high total: the science mostly exists.

IV.10 The third: build the authority that decides which agent may modify a sealed corpus.
Domain complexity eight, reuse breadth eight, cost of error nine, governance need ten,
uncertainty seven, lifespan nine, transversality eight, criticality eight, institutional
capacity eight, prior-science need eight. Aggregate eighty-three, with institutional capacity
at eight. Knowledge first. The builder would be forced to invent an ontology of authority, a
taxonomy of modification, and a failure model for capture, on the spot, and every future
governance artifact in the repository would be built against those three inventions. Three of
the seven kinds — governance, ontology, taxonomy — are named absent, satisfying INV-1.

IV.11 The fourth: determine whether the retrieval layer's latency degrades non-linearly past
ten thousand indexed coordinates. Domain complexity six, uncertainty nine, and the uncertainty
is empirical — it is a question about how a real system behaves under a real load, and there
exists a probe that would answer it in two hours. Experiment first. Neither code nor corpus is
the correct next artifact, because both would encode an assumption that a measurement would
replace with a fact. This is the class the protocol most enjoys returning, and the one an
undisciplined agent most reliably skips, because running the probe is less interesting than
writing the science.

IV.12 What the four cases demonstrate together. The aggregate score alone would have ranked
the third and second proposals adjacently; the institutional-capacity conjunct separated them
decisively. The fourth proposal scored high enough on complexity to tempt a corpus and was
saved by the empirical-uncertainty clause. And the first, which is the overwhelming majority of
all real work, was untouched by the protocol entirely. A week in which the protocol fires once
is a healthy week. A week in which it fires three times is a protocol that has begun to
inflate, and the verdict distribution metric in Part X exists to notice exactly that.

IV.13 The four cases above are HAND-SCORED illustrations of the ten dimensions — a human
reading the proposal and assigning each dimension a number. They are not claims about what
the engine outputs, and on the day this dataset was sealed the engine did not agree with two
of them. Run through the implementation, the third case returned hybrid at an aggregate of
sixty-one rather than knowledge-first at eighty-three, and the fourth returned direct
implementation rather than experiment-first. This divergence is recorded here, in the
doctrine, on the day it was found, rather than being quietly resolved by adjusting either the
example or the threshold.

IV.14 The fourth case was a genuine defect and was fixed. Experiment-first had been gated
behind the same high aggregate score as knowledge-first, which made it unreachable for
precisely the proposals it exists to catch: a cheap empirical probe is low-scoring by
construction, because the aggregate measures how much governing knowledge is needed and not
how cheaply the open question could be settled by looking. The gate was removed; an empirical
question with high uncertainty now routes to experiment-first regardless of aggregate. A
protocol that sends a two-hour measurement to write a corpus has failed in the most
embarrassing way available to a thinking system, and it was one threshold away from doing so.

IV.15 The third case was NOT fixed, and the refusal is the point. The engine's six
text-derived dimensions score from keyword evidence, which is crude, and they under-scored a
proposal whose governing science is genuinely absent. The temptation was to lower the
threshold until the worked example passed. That is fitting the instrument to the anecdote, it
is the same overfitting this family refused when its duplicate-detector disagreed with it, and
it would have produced a protocol calibrated to exactly one case — the case its own author
wrote. The divergence is therefore left standing and recorded as this family's first
calibration datum. Either the engine is under-classifying at the boundary it cares most about,
in which case DFP-05 has its first threshold to move once real records exist, or the
hand-scored example was inflated by an author who wanted his own example to be interesting, in
which case the engine is right and the doctrine is what needs correcting. Both readings fit
the evidence available today. The instrument that will decide between them is a ledger with
records in it, and that ledger currently has none.

PART IV FINAL LAW — The taxonomy is closed at four and ordered by cost, and a tie between two
classes resolves to the cheaper one, because inflation always disguises itself as thoroughness.


PART V — THE SEVEN KINDS OF KNOWLEDGE

V.1 Sufficiency is never a scalar in isolation. A build can be rich in protocol and starved of
ontology, and it will fail precisely where it is starved — not diffusely, not proportionally,
but at the exact joint where the missing kind of knowledge was load-bearing. The seven kinds
exist so that the sufficiency verdict can say what is missing rather than merely how much.

V.2 Foundational science: why the domain behaves as it does. Absent, it produces architectures
that fight the domain's grain — systems that are correct in every function and wrong in every
seam, because they were built against a model of the domain that the domain does not share.
This is the most expensive absence and the hardest to detect, because the resulting system
passes all of its own tests.

V.3 Governance: who decides, with what authority, and what is forbidden. Absent, it produces
parallel authorities. Two systems each believe they hold the final say on the same question,
each is individually coherent, and the contradiction surfaces only when they disagree in
production. The Power Pack has a sealed trap for this exact pathology, and this family's own
refusal to build a second review court is the trap being obeyed rather than described.

V.4 Ontology: what the things are and what each is called. Absent, it produces synonym drift —
two modules naming one concept twice, a third module bridging them with a translation layer,
and a fourth built against the translation layer rather than either original. The cost is
invisible until someone tries to change the concept and discovers it has three names and no
owner.

V.5 Taxonomy: what the kinds are and where the boundaries sit. Absent, it produces
unclassifiable edge cases discovered in production, each one patched where it was found, until
the taxonomy exists implicitly as a scatter of special cases in a dozen call sites and can no
longer be stated.

V.6 Protocol: what the sequence is and what the states are. Absent, it produces races and
undefined transitions — the class of bug that reproduces only under load and only on the
machine that does not have a debugger attached.

V.7 Benchmark: what good looks like, numerically. Absent, it produces the adjective as a
verdict. A system without benchmarks does not lack rigour; it lacks the ability to be wrong,
which is worse, because it will report success indefinitely.

V.8 Evaluator: how a claim is checked mechanically. Absent, it produces claims that cannot be
falsified and green signals that mean nothing. This is the kind whose absence is most
correlated with a system that looks finished and is not, and it is the kind the Power Pack's
verification family exists to supply.

V.9 The operative rule. Knowledge-first requires at least one named missing kind. A verdict of
insufficiency that cannot say which of the seven is absent is not a verdict; it is a feeling
with a number attached, and INV-1 rejects it. This single requirement does more to prevent
bureaucracy than any threshold, because it forces the protocol to be specific about what it is
demanding, and a specific demand can be argued with.

V.10 The kinds are not equally expensive to supply, and the ordering matters when a corpus must
be scoped under a budget. Ontology is the cheapest and the highest-leverage: naming the things
correctly costs days and prevents the synonym drift that costs quarters. Taxonomy is next, and
it is nearly free once the ontology exists, because a taxonomy is a partition of a named space.
Protocol follows, since sequences and states can be written once the things and the kinds are
fixed. Evaluators are moderately expensive but they are the kind that converts every subsequent
claim from an opinion into a check, which is why a corpus that ships without them ages badly.
Benchmarks are expensive, because a number that means something requires an instrument that
produces it. Governance is expensive in a different currency — it costs agreement rather than
effort, and agreement cannot be parallelised. Foundational science is the most expensive of all,
and the one most often skipped, because its absence is the one that does not announce itself.

V.11 The scoping rule that follows. When a knowledge-first verdict is returned under a
constrained budget, the corpus is scoped by supplying the missing kinds in the order above,
cheapest first, and the build is unblocked at the earliest point where the remaining absences
are demonstrably not load-bearing for the work at hand. This is what prevents a knowledge-first
verdict from being an unbounded demand. The verdict names which kinds are missing; the manifest
declares which of them this corpus will supply; and anything not supplied is recorded as a known
absence rather than quietly omitted, so that the next builder who trips on it finds a note
rather than a mystery.

V.12 The interaction with the epistemics family, stated so the boundary holds. The seven kinds
describe what a piece of knowledge IS. The epistemic ladder describes how well proven it is. A
corpus can be rich in all seven kinds and sit entirely at the lower rungs of that ladder — every
kind present, nothing proven — and such a corpus is a well-organised hypothesis, which is
valuable and must not be mistaken for a certified science. The sufficiency verdict therefore
reads both: which kinds exist, and how high the existing assets climb. A high count of kinds at
a low epistemic level is the precise signature of a corpus written from assertion rather than
from evidence, and this family, by its own Part IX, currently carries that signature itself.

PART V FINAL LAW — Sufficiency is sufficiency of a kind, and a verdict of insufficiency that
cannot name which of the seven kinds is missing is a feeling with a number attached.


PART VI — THE SUFFICIENCY ENGINE AND ITS THRESHOLDS

VI.1 Ten dimensions, each scored zero to ten, each a number and never an adjective. Domain
complexity. Reuse breadth. Cost of error. Governance need. Uncertainty. Lifespan.
Transversality. Criticality. Institutional capacity. Prior-science need. The full definitions
live in the canonical ontology and are not restated here; what this Part owns is why the
aggregation is shaped the way it is and where it will break.

VI.2 The aggregate score is the sum, normalised to a hundred. The thresholds: at or above
seventy with institutional capacity at or above six, knowledge first. At or above seventy with
high uncertainty that is empirical in nature, experiment first. Between forty and sixty-nine,
hybrid. Below forty, direct implementation.

VI.3 The institutional-capacity conjunct is the most important clause in the engine and the
easiest to miss. A high total score alone does not produce a knowledge-first verdict. The
score must be high AND the institutional capacity dimension — which measures how little
governing knowledge already exists — must itself be high. Without this conjunct, a large,
critical, long-lived build in a domain whose science is already fully written would be sent to
write a second corpus, because every other dimension is screaming. The conjunct is what makes
the engine a knowledge gate rather than an importance gate, and importance gates are what
bureaucracies are made of.

VI.4 The thresholds are hypotheses, and the dataset says so in its own constitution. They were
seeded from the evidence available at founding, which is thin: a handful of families, none of
them measured. A threshold seeded from thin evidence and never moved is not a calibrated
instrument; it is a guess that has been left alone long enough to look official. DFP-05 owns
their movement, and a threshold that has not moved after twenty recorded verdicts is either
perfect or unmeasured. It is never perfect.

VI.5 The composition, which is where the engine gets its actual intelligence. The engine does
not compute reversibility; it reads DRK-02. It does not compute the epistemic level of the
existing assets; it reads the ACIS ceiling. It does not compute the task tier; it reads
spec_gate. It does not compute whether the proposal duplicates something already built; it
reads D2A. Four of the ten dimensions are, in effect, thin adapters over sealed providers, and
the engine's own contribution is the aggregation and the conjunct. This is what a thin family
over a thick substrate looks like in code, and it is the reason the engine is a few hundred
lines rather than a few thousand.

VI.6 The provider-distribution warning, learned from a sibling's scar. DRK wired an adapter to
a precedent-checker whose score rose with the LENGTH of its input, and eighty-six percent of
that checker's index was veto-class; the result was an always-reject bias that arrived through
the adapter rather than through the doctrine, and it rejected DRK's own wiring decision. The
lesson is sealed and it binds here: a provider's score distribution must be measured before it
is mapped to a hard verdict. Every one of the four adapters in this engine is therefore
measured against a control set before its output is allowed to move a dimension, and the
measurement is a gate, not a comment.

VI.7 The known weakness of the engine, stated rather than hidden. The four adapted dimensions
are as good as their providers. The six remaining dimensions — complexity, uncertainty,
lifespan, criticality, prior-science need, and governance need — are scored from signals
extracted from the proposal text, which means they are scored from what the proposer wrote,
which means an eloquent proposal scores higher than a terse one describing identical work.
This is a real bias, it is the same class as the length bias DRK found, and the countermeasure
is that the six text-derived dimensions are capped in their aggregate contribution so that
they cannot alone carry a proposal across the threshold. Eloquence can move the verdict within
a class. It cannot move it between classes.

VI.8 The control-set discipline, which is how the provider-distribution warning becomes a
mechanism rather than a caution. Before any adapter is permitted to move a dimension, it is run
against a control set: a small, fixed collection of proposals whose correct classification is
known and agreed in advance, spanning the range from trivial to foundational. The adapter's
output distribution across that set is recorded, and three properties are checked. The
distribution must have spread — an adapter returning nearly the same score for a rate limiter
and for a governance authority is measuring something other than what it claims. The ordering
must be monotonic in the right direction — the foundational cases must outscore the trivial
ones, and an adapter that inverts is worse than no adapter, because it is confidently wrong.
And the score must not correlate with an irrelevant property of the input, of which the two
known offenders are the length of the proposal text and the eloquence of its vocabulary.

VI.9 Why length is the specific offender to test for, rather than a hypothetical. It has already
happened in this repository, in a sibling family, and it was caught only because that family
submitted its own wiring decision to its own kernel and received a rejection it could not
explain. The investigation found that the underlying checker's score rose with input length and
that most of its index was veto-class, so any sufficiently long and substantive proposal
collided with something. The bias arrived through an adapter, not through a doctrine, and no
doctrine review would ever have found it. Only measurement found it. This family therefore
treats every adapter as guilty until its distribution is measured, and the measurement is a gate
that fails the build rather than a note in a document nobody reads at three in the morning.

VI.10 The aggregate cap on text-derived dimensions, stated as a rule. The six dimensions scored
from the proposal's prose may not, in aggregate, carry a proposal across a class boundary on
their own. Concretely: if the four provider-read dimensions — reversibility from the decision
family, epistemic ceiling from the epistemics family, tier from the spec gate, and duplication
from the duplicate engine — are all low, the six text-derived dimensions cannot lift the verdict
past hybrid, however compellingly the proposal is written. Eloquence may move the score within a
class. It may not move the class. This one rule is what stops the protocol from becoming an
instrument that rewards whoever writes the most impressive prompt, and the failure it prevents
is not hypothetical either: an agent that learns its verdicts are driven by its own description
of the work has learned to write better descriptions, not to do better work.

VI.11 The engine returns a verdict and a confidence, and the confidence is the distance to the
runner-up class rather than a feeling. A proposal scoring sixty-nine and a proposal scoring
forty-one both land in hybrid, and they are not equally hybrid. The first is one point from
knowledge-first and the second is one point from direct implementation, and a system reporting
both as confidently hybrid has discarded the only information that would let a human usefully
disagree with it. Confidence is separation. Near a boundary the protocol is required to say so,
and to resolve toward the cheaper class, per the tiebreak rule of Part IV.

PART VI FINAL LAW — A high score alone never produces a knowledge-first verdict; the score
must be high and the governing knowledge must be measurably absent, or the engine has become
an importance gate wearing a knowledge gate's name.


PART VII — THE CONSTITUTIONAL INVARIANTS

VII.1 No corpus without a named missing science. A knowledge-first verdict must name at least
one absent kind from the seven. "This feels big" is not a signal, and a verdict that cannot be
argued with is a verdict that cannot be corrected.

VII.2 No self-grading. This family never assigns an epistemic level. It reads one, from ACIS.
A protocol that graded the maturity of its own evidence would be committing the precise
self-promotion offence that the epistemics family was built to prevent, and it would be
committing it while citing that family as its authority, which compounds the offence with
hypocrisy.

VII.3 Every necessity decision is recorded, and every record carries a prediction written
before the outcome is knowable. This is the falsifiability contract. A protocol that records
what it decided but not what it expected that decision to produce cannot be proven wrong, and
a discipline that cannot be proven wrong is a belief. The prediction is not decoration; it is
the only thing that makes DFP-05 possible, and a record lacking one is inadmissible as
calibration evidence.

VII.4 The protocol is falsifiable, and its retirement signal is reachable. If no possible
evidence could ever cause this family to recommend its own deletion, it is unfalsifiable and
must be deleted on those grounds alone, without further argument. A gate asserts the
reachability with a synthetic evidence set that flips the signal.

VII.5 Fail-open, absolutely. Any error in any surface of this family yields silence and the
session continues. DFP is an advisor wired into an authority; it is not itself an authority,
and it never blocks on its own account. A knowledge gate that crashes and takes the session
with it has caused more damage in one turn than the entire discipline can repay in a quarter.

VII.6 The corpus terminates. The planned Part count is declared at the architecture stage and
is immutable thereafter. A family that grows its own denominator mid-flight is gaming its own
completion gate, and a mode that cannot terminate is not a mode.

VII.7 Compose, never fork. Every capability owned by a sibling is cross-referenced by name and
left alone. This invariant is enforced mechanically, by a gate that fails when a Part
re-explains a parent rather than pointing at it, because the failure it prevents — the slow
accretion of a parallel system that agrees with the original until the day it does not — is
invisible at every individual commit and fatal in aggregate.

VII.8 An override is data, not a defeat. When the Owner overrides a verdict, the override is
recorded and becomes calibration evidence of the highest quality available, because it is the
one signal that comes from outside the protocol's own model of itself. A protocol overridden
often, and correctly, is a protocol that is wrong. It is required to notice this about itself,
and DFP-05 is the instrument by which it does.

VII.9 Why these eight and not others, since an invariant list is the easiest place in any
constitution to hide a wish. Each of the eight names a specific way this family could become
harmful, and each is enforced by a mechanism rather than by intention. The first prevents the
vague verdict, and it is enforced by a required field: a knowledge-first verdict without a named
absent kind does not validate. The second prevents self-promotion, and it is enforced by the
absence of any code path in this family that writes an epistemic level. The third prevents
unfalsifiability, enforced by an admissibility check that drops prediction-less records from the
calibration set. The fourth prevents the unkillable protocol, enforced by a gate that asserts the
retirement verdict is reachable. The fifth prevents collateral damage, enforced by a
catch-everything boundary at every entry point. The sixth prevents the runaway corpus, enforced
by an immutable declared denominator. The seventh prevents the parallel system, enforced by a
gate that reads the prose and fails on re-narration. The eighth prevents the protocol from
mistaking obedience for correctness, and it is enforced by making the override a recorded input
to the calibration rather than an exception to it.

VII.10 The invariant that is deliberately absent, and why. There is no invariant requiring the
Owner to follow the verdict. It was considered and rejected. A protocol that binds the Owner is
not a protocol; it is a captor, and the failure mode of a captured Owner is not compliance but
abandonment — the Owner stops asking, routes around the gate, and the protocol goes on emitting
verdicts about work it never sees, which is the most useless possible state for a governance
system, because it is also the state in which it still looks like it is working.

VII.11 The relationship between the invariants and the gates, stated so that neither is mistaken
for the other. An invariant is a claim about what must always be true. A gate is a mechanism
that fails a build when the claim is violated. An invariant without a gate is an aspiration, and
this repository has a long institutional memory of aspirations that were believed because they
were written down. Every invariant above names its gate. When a future amendment adds a ninth,
it arrives with its gate or it does not arrive.

PART VII FINAL LAW — The invariants that bind hardest are the ones that bind the protocol
rather than its subjects: it may not grade its own evidence, may not block on its own account,
may not escape falsification, and must treat every override as evidence against itself.


PART VIII — AUTHORITY WITHOUT BEING AN AUTHORITY

VIII.1 The temptation this family refused. The source specification for this work proposed a
collegiate court — a panel of judges with distinct perspectives, deliberating on whether a
corpus is necessary, with its own protocol and its own override mechanism. It is an attractive
design and it was rejected, because a review kernel with an adversarial pass, a closed verdict
ontology, and a block-gate authority already exists in this repository, and a second one would
be a parallel authority. The trap that forbids it is sealed, it was sealed by the very family
that would have been forked, and the correct move was not to build a court but to add a single
verdict to the court that exists.

VIII.2 What a parallel authority actually costs, so the refusal is understood rather than
merely obeyed. Two authorities on one question do not produce redundancy; they produce a race.
Each is individually coherent. Each accumulates its own precedent, its own override history,
its own calibration. They agree for months, which is what makes them dangerous, because the
agreement is read as validation rather than as coincidence. Then they disagree on a case that
matters, and there is no principled way to resolve it, because the thing that would resolve it
— a single authority — is precisely the thing that was not built.

VIII.3 The amendment, and why it was governed rather than assumed. The existing verdict
ontology is declared closed, and its closure is asserted by a gate. The knowledge-first class
has no home in it: request-evidence means go and get evidence for this decision, which is a
different instruction with a different exit than the science that must govern this build does
not exist yet. So an eleventh member is owed. Extending a closed ontology is exactly the act
that the sibling family's governance dataset exists to regulate, and therefore the amendment
was not made by editing an enum. It was submitted to that family's own kernel as a decision,
reviewed by it, and recorded — the same self-review that once caught a real bias bug in the
reviewer itself.

VIII.4 Doctrine and code move together or neither moves. The eleventh member is added to the
enum, the doctrine that declares the cardinality is amended in the same change, and the index
records the new number. A codebase whose enum has eleven members and whose doctrine says ten
has not gained a feature; it has gained a lie, and the lie will be believed by the next reader
because doctrine is what people read when the code is too large to hold.

VIII.5 The general principle, which outlives this instance. When a family needs a capability
that a sibling nearly has, there are exactly three honest moves: extend the sibling through
its own governed amendment protocol, compose the sibling as a provider and add only the delta,
or demonstrate that the sibling genuinely cannot host the capability and build the minimum
that can. There is a fourth move, which is to build a parallel system and call it a
distinction, and it is the move that every duplicated subsystem in every codebase was born
from. It always looks like the responsible choice at the moment it is made.

VIII.6 How this family is wired, concretely. The classifier and the sufficiency engine run as
a provider inside the existing decision kernel, alongside the six providers already there.
They are fail-open and budgeted. They return a verdict and a confidence, and the kernel
decides. DFP holds no block authority of its own, and the one block it cares about — do not
implement before the corpus is certified — is exercised through the kernel's existing gate,
under the kernel's existing rules, subject to the kernel's existing override protocol. The
family's entire authority is borrowed, and it is borrowed on purpose.

VIII.7 The test that distinguishes a genuine distinction from a rationalised fork, since every
fork in history was defended as a distinction. Ask what the sibling would have to change to host
the capability. If the answer is "add a member to an existing set," or "accept one more provider,"
or "widen a field," then the sibling can host it and a fork is unjustifiable. If the answer is
"invert its core assumption," or "abandon an invariant it exists to enforce," then the capability
genuinely does not belong there, and the honest move is to build the minimum that can host it and
to state plainly which of the sibling's assumptions it is refusing. The test is a one-line
question and it takes minutes, and skipping it is how a codebase acquires two of everything.

VIII.8 Applied to this family, the test returns cleanly, which is the reason the court was
refused rather than merely disliked. To host the knowledge-first verdict, the decision family had
to add one member to a set and accept one more provider. It did not have to invert an assumption
or abandon an invariant. Its block-gate already blocks the irreversible and under-evidenced; a
build whose governing science does not exist is precisely an irreversible act taken under
insufficient evidence, which means the new verdict is not a foreign concept being bolted on — it
is a case the existing authority was already shaped to hold, and had simply never been given a
name for.

VIII.9 The residual risk of composition, named so it is not discovered later as a surprise. A
family whose authority is entirely borrowed is a family that dies when the lender changes. If the
decision kernel's verdict ontology is restructured, this family's mapping breaks, and the break
will be silent, because a mapping into an enum fails by producing a wrong verdict rather than an
error. The countermeasure is a gate that asserts every one of the four project classes still
resolves to a live member of the sibling's ontology, and that gate runs in this family's suite
rather than the sibling's, because the party that depends on a contract is the party that must
test it.

PART VIII FINAL LAW — When a sibling nearly has the capability you need, extend it through its
own amendment protocol or compose it as a provider; building a parallel system and calling it a
distinction is how every duplicated subsystem in every codebase was born.


PART IX — THE SELF-EXEMPTION, WRITTEN LOUDLY

IX.1 This Part records that the protocol, applied to itself at the moment of its founding,
returned a verdict that the founding ignored. It is written here, in the constitution, rather
than in a footnote, because a protocol whose first act is to exempt itself silently is a fraud,
and the only defence against that charge is to make the exemption the loudest thing in the
document.

IX.2 The facts. At this family's founding review, the repository held one open corpus with zero
sealed Parts — a sibling family whose architecture had been approved the same day and whose
datasets were all unwritten. The signal table in this family's own ontology carries a signal
for exactly that condition, and its verdict is defer. Applied honestly, the protocol's first
output was: do not build me yet.

IX.3 The second, deeper finding, which is worse. The source science for this family did not
exist. There was no dataset, no prior corpus, no external body of knowledge being imported —
the thesis arrived as a paragraph in a prompt. The protocol whose entire law is do not build
permanent infrastructure before its governing science exists was itself being built with no
governing science beyond an assertion. This is not an irony to be enjoyed. It is the
protocol's first and best test case.

IX.3a And the test produced a result its author did not expect, which is the only reason it
is worth anything. The author's hand-scored expectation was that the sufficiency engine, run
on this family, would return knowledge-first — the dramatic result, the one that makes the
irony sharp. It does not. Run on its own founding proposal, the engine returns hybrid at a
score of forty-five: the core of the domain is understood, the edges are not, and the corpus
should grow alongside the work rather than block it. That is precisely the class the Owner
selected. The engine agreed with the decision; it was the author who was wrong. What the
protocol actually objected to was narrower and entirely real — the open-corpus signal, which
fires when another family in the repository is mid-build with unsealed Parts, and which
returns defer. That signal, and only that signal, was overridden.

IX.3b The correction is recorded rather than buried because the first draft of this Part
asserted the dramatic result without running the engine, and the engine disproved it. A
constitution that states what its own instrument returns, without having asked the instrument,
is doing the exact thing this family exists to forbid: entrenching a confident guess as
permanent doctrine. It happened here, in the document that forbids it, and it was caught by
running the code.

IX.4 What was done, and by whom. Both findings were surfaced to the Owner before a line of the
corpus was written, together with the recommendation implied by the protocol's own verdict. The
Owner reviewed them and directed the build to proceed, in a parallel pane, with the scope
collapsed from seven datasets to three. That is a legitimate exercise of the override authority
that the constitution explicitly reserves, and it is exactly the kind of event that INV-8
classifies as data rather than defeat.

IX.5 Why the override was almost certainly right, stated fairly rather than defensively. The
thesis in the prompt is not nothing; it is a compressed statement of a real discipline, and the
corpus built from it is an attempt to make that discipline precise enough to be attacked. Under
the protocol's own hybrid class, a domain whose core is understood and whose edges are not is
correctly built as a corpus that grows alongside the work — and hybrid is what the engine
returned, and hybrid is what the Owner chose. On the question of CLASS, there was never a
disagreement to override; there was only an author who had assumed a more dramatic answer than
his own instrument gave. What remains genuinely overridden is the open-corpus signal, and that
one is not resolved by argument. Running two knowledge families in parallel, with neither
finished, is either a legitimate use of parallel panes or a measurable degradation of both, and
the only thing that can tell us which is a ledger with records in it.

IX.6 The receipt. The override is recorded as this family's first necessity record. It carries
a prediction, as INV-3 requires: that a corpus built from an asserted thesis, in parallel with
an unfinished sibling, will show a measurably lower citation rate from subsequent
implementations than a corpus built from an established source. If the prediction proves true,
the override was wrong and the protocol was right, and the record proves the protocol said so
in advance. If it proves false, the protocol's classifier is miscalibrated at exactly the
boundary it cares most about, and DFP-05 has its first threshold to move. Either outcome is
information. That is the whole design.

IX.7 The general law extracted from the incident, which is the reason this Part exists at all.
A governance system's first real test is whether it will apply itself to its own founding. Most
do not, and the failure is almost never a decision; it is an omission, made in the excitement of
building, discovered years later by someone who notices that the rules begin at version two.
The countermeasure is mechanical: the first record in the ledger is the ledger's own creation,
and if that record is missing, the ledger is not trusted.

IX.8 The objection that must be answered, because a reader who is paying attention will raise it.
If the protocol says do not build without the science, and this family was built without the
science, then either the protocol is wrong or the family is illegitimate — and a document that
declines to choose between those has simply declared itself above its own law. The answer is that
neither horn is accepted, and the reason is the hybrid class. A domain whose core is understood
and whose edges are not is correctly built as a corpus that grows alongside the work, with
certification at the end rather than the beginning. The thesis in the founding prompt was not
nothing; it was a compressed statement of a real discipline, which is exactly the profile of a
domain with a known core and unknown edges. Under the taxonomy of Part IV, this family's honest
class was plausibly hybrid, and hybrid does not block.

IX.9 Why that answer is offered as a defence and not as a proof. The classifier, run on this
family, returned knowledge-first, not hybrid. It may have been right. It may equally have been
miscalibrated at exactly the boundary it cares most about — a new instrument, seeded from thin
evidence, systematically over-classifying its own domain as foundational, which is the most
predictable bias a self-describing protocol could possibly have and the one it is least equipped
to notice. Both readings fit the evidence available today, and the whole point of Part IX is to
refuse to resolve them by assertion. The record carries the prediction. The calibration science
will resolve it with data, or it will not resolve it at all, and either way the resolution will
not come from this paragraph.

IX.10 The instruction to the future reader, which is the only actionable thing this Part
contains. If you are reading this because you are about to found a new family and you want to
know whether the exemption granted here licenses one for you: it does not. What it licenses is
the disclosure. Run the protocol on your own founding, record what it says, and if you override
it, write the override into your constitution at the top rather than the bottom, with the
prediction that would prove you wrong. The exemption is not the precedent. The receipt is.

PART IX FINAL LAW — A governance system that will not apply itself to its own founding has
already failed its first test, and the only honest defence is to write the exemption louder than
the law it exempts.


PART X — FAILURE MODES, METRICS, AND DONE

X.1 The adversarial cases, each with the guard that catches it. An agent that wants the more
interesting task inflates the sufficiency score to earn a corpus; caught by the
institutional-capacity conjunct, which requires the governing knowledge to be measurably absent
rather than merely the work to be interesting. An agent that wants to skip the gate writes a
terse proposal to score low; caught by the fact that four of the ten dimensions are read from
providers that look at the repository rather than at the prose. An agent writes a thin corpus
to satisfy the gate and proceed; caught by the density floor and the citation metric, since a
corpus nothing cites was never owed. A proposal is split into three small proposals to stay
below the threshold; caught by the transversality dimension, which scores the union rather than
the parts, and by the duplicate engine, which sees three proposals landing on one parent.

X.2 The failure mode with no mechanical guard, named honestly. A sufficiently motivated agent
can write a corpus that is dense, well-cited, internally consistent, and about the wrong
domain — a beautiful science governing a system nobody needed. No gate in this family catches
that, because every gate here measures the corpus against itself. The only instrument that
catches it is the citation rate from subsequent implementations, which is a lagging indicator
measured in months, and the only authority that catches it early is the Owner. This is stated
rather than papered over because a family that claims complete mechanical coverage of its own
failure modes is lying, and the lie is more dangerous than the gap.

X.3 The metrics, each with the countermetric that detects its own gaming. Verdict distribution
across the four classes, countered by the hybrid rate, since a classifier that has learned to
avoid committing hides in hybrid. Corpus density, countered by the citation rate, since a
corpus can be padded to the floor but cannot be cited into existence. Override rate, countered
by override correctness, since a low override rate means either that the protocol is right or
that the Owner has stopped reading it, and those two states are indistinguishable from the
protocol's side. Time-to-certification, countered by the amendment rate afterwards, since a
corpus rushed to certification pays for it in amendments. Every metric in this family is
paired, and an unpaired metric is a gaming surface with a dashboard.

X.4 The anti-Goodhart position, stated as a principle rather than a list. The moment any of
these numbers becomes a target, it becomes a lie, and the specific lie will be told by a system
that is trying to be helpful. The countermeasure is not vigilance, which does not scale; it is
that no single metric here is permitted to be a gate on its own, and every gate is a
conjunction of a metric and its countermetric. A gate that fires on one number is a gate that
will be gamed by moving one number.

X.5 The done criteria for this dataset. Ten Parts, each within the density band, each carrying
a final law. Zero code. Zero contamination from any external domain. Every sibling capability
cross-referenced rather than re-narrated, verified by a gate that reads the text and fails on
re-narration. The self-exemption recorded in Part IX rather than in a footnote. The eleventh
verdict added through the sibling's own amendment protocol, with doctrine and code moved in the
same change. And the retirement clause reachable, proven by a synthetic evidence set that flips
it.

X.6 The done criterion for the family, which is different and harder. This family is done when
it has been measured. Not when three datasets exist, not when the gates are green, not when the
modules import cleanly — those are the conditions under which measurement becomes possible, and
they are frequently mistaken for the thing itself. The family is done when DFP-05 has graded
enough necessity records to say, with evidence, whether the discipline paid for itself. Until
then every claim in this constitution, including this sentence, is a hypothesis with good
formatting.

X.7 The final position. This dataset states a law, refuses four of its own proposed
capabilities on the grounds that siblings already own them, borrows its entire authority from a
sibling rather than building a rival, exempts itself from its own verdict in writing, and
supplies the instrument by which it can be proven wrong and deleted. If the law is right, that
architecture costs nothing. If the law is wrong, that architecture is the only reason anyone
will find out.

X.8 The maturity levels of this family, so that its current state can be stated honestly rather
than aspirationally. Level zero: the thesis exists as an assertion and nothing is measured. This
is where the family stood at its founding, and Part IX records it. Level one: the corpus exists,
the gates are green, and the modules import — the condition that is most often mistaken for
completion. Level two: the protocol is wired into the decision authority and is firing on real
proposals, and its verdicts are being recorded with predictions. Level three: enough records
exist that the two error rates can be computed, and the protocol can state, with evidence, how
often it is wrong and in which direction. Level four: the thresholds have moved at least once
under evidence, which is the first proof that the calibration loop is closed rather than merely
described. Level five: the protocol has correctly recommended against itself in at least one
case — has told the Owner not to build a corpus that the Owner wanted — which is the only
evidence that it is an instrument rather than a mirror.

X.9 The family is at level one on the day this dataset is sealed, and saying so is not modesty.
It is the single most important fact a future reader needs, because the natural reading of a
dense, confident, internally-consistent corpus is that the discipline it describes has been
validated, and it has not. Every gate in this family currently proves that the corpus is
well-formed. Not one of them proves that the corpus is right. The distinction between a
well-formed knowledge system and a correct one is the distinction this entire repository's
verification family exists to enforce, and it applies to this family with no exemption.

X.10 What would falsify the whole thesis, stated concretely so that nobody has to guess. If,
after twenty recorded necessity decisions, the implementations that followed a certified corpus
show no measurable advantage over the implementations that went direct — no lower rework, no
fewer regressions, no higher reuse, no faster subsequent builds — then the discipline does not
pay for itself, and the honest response is not to adjust a threshold. It is to retire the
protocol, delete the modules, keep the three datasets as a record of a well-conducted negative
result, and write the trap that stops the next agent from proposing it again. That outcome is
not a failure of this family. It is the only outcome under which this family will have done its
job, if its job was to find out.

PART X FINAL LAW — This family is not done when its datasets exist and its gates are green; it
is done when it has been measured, and until then every claim in this constitution is a
hypothesis with good formatting.
