CRAIF-01 — TRANSACTIONAL REPAIR RUNTIME & AUTHORITY COMPOSITION
Closed-Loop Repair & Activation Integrity Fabric — the execution axis
Version 1. Founded 2026-07-21. Composes CRAIF-00 in full; binding on CRAIF-02's Activation Verification Contract consumer.


PART I — MISSION AND JURISDICTION

1.1 This dataset is the only system in Claude Power Pack chartered to execute a repair against canonical estate state, and every other capability this corpus touches — detection, diagnosis, authority, immune classification — belongs, per CRAIF-00 Part II and Part XI, to a system this dataset consults rather than replicates. Where CRAIF-00 defines what a Repair Intent, a Repair Candidate, an Authority Decision, and a Repair Certificate are, this dataset defines the transaction that turns the first into the last: the concrete sequence of states a Repair Intent moves through, the concrete evidence each transition requires, and the concrete failure behavior when any step does not go as planned.

1.2 The runtime's jurisdiction begins the moment a Repair Intent exists in CRAIF-00's OPENED state and ends the moment it reaches one of that object's four closed states — CLOSED-REPAIRED, CLOSED-ROLLED-BACK, CLOSED-STALE, or CLOSED-REJECTED. Everything the runtime does inside that span is Execution, per CRAIF-00 Part IX's separation of powers, and everything the runtime consults outside that span — the Finding that produced the Intent, the reversibility classification that authorized a tier, the immune registry that will receive an outcome — belongs to a different role the runtime never performs itself.

1.3 A specific boundary deserves restating here because CRAIF-01's proximity to real mutation makes it the single most tempting place in the entire family for scope to creep: this dataset does not decide whether a Finding is real, does not decide how risky a candidate is in the abstract, and does not decide whether a repaired Failure Pattern deserves a standing guardian. It receives those decisions as inputs — a validated Repair Intent, an Authority Decision, a Verification Contract result — and its own contribution is exclusively the mechanics of moving safely from an authorized intent to a verified, ledgered outcome.

1.4 The runtime's design responds directly to the founding evidence CRAIF-00 Part XII fixes permanently: the third fixture, hook and settings divergence, is the shape of failure this runtime is built to close correctly rather than merely quickly, and the second fixture, reachability debt at scale, is the shape of failure this runtime is built to close without generating a hundred and fifty-six orphan modules in exchange for closing one hundred and fifty-five.

1.5 A worked framing situates the runtime's value relative to the estate's existing capability rather than in isolation. Before this dataset, an estate with `modules/liveness/reachability.py`, SQI's reconciliation, and the Decision Review Kernel already possessed the ability to know precisely which modules were orphaned, precisely how risky registering one would be, and precisely what evidence would license a claim that the registration held. What it lacked was the mechanism that takes those three already-available answers and turns them into a registered module, snapshotted, sandboxed, verified, and either committed or rolled back — the mechanism this dataset now specifies.

1.6 The runtime is explicitly a specification in this dataset, not a running system; CRAIF-00 Part XVI's maturity model places this corpus's content at the SPECIFIED stage, and every claim this dataset makes about how the runtime behaves is marked, per CRAIF-00 Part XV, as PROPOSED unless a specific reproduction is cited. No Part in this dataset asserts that the mechanisms it specifies have executed against a real Repair Intent, because none had, at the time of this dataset's authoring.

1.7 The chapters that follow are ordered to mirror the transaction's own lifecycle rather than to group related concepts topically, because a reader who needs to understand what happens at Commit should be able to trace backward through exactly the Parts the transaction itself passed through to reach that point, in the same order the runtime itself would have executed them.

1.8 A worked comparison against the estate's existing rollback mechanism situates this dataset's own scope precisely, because the two are easy to conflate on a first reading and the conflation would misdirect an implementer toward rebuilding what already exists. `modules/rollback/*` answers a narrower, already-solved question: given a known target class — an rsync directory, a pg_dump database, a docker volume — and a request to restore it, execute the restoration. It does not decide when a restoration is warranted, does not verify that the state prior to restoration was actually broken, and does not connect its own execution to any upstream Finding, diagnosis, or authority chain. CRAIF-01 is not a fourth restoration mechanism alongside these three; it is the transactional envelope that decides when any of the three is warranted, confirms the decision against external authority, executes the corresponding change, and independently proves the result — with the restoration mechanism itself remaining exactly what it already was, called at exactly one point in a much longer chain this dataset specifies and the restoration mechanism does not.

1.9 The same distinction holds against `owner_queue`, which answers a comparably narrow question: given an escalation with a payload, record it, notify the relevant party, and clear it when resolved. It has no opinion about which conditions warrant escalation, no model of a repair's own lifecycle, and no mechanism for confirming that a resolved escalation actually corresponds to a working fix rather than a dismissed one. CRAIF-01's Owner Escalation object, defined operationally across this dataset's later Parts, is a payload `owner_queue` carries; the judgment about when to produce that payload, and what to do with the escalation's eventual resolution, belongs to this dataset and nowhere else.

1.10 A final scoping clarification addresses a question a reader familiar with the estate's existing `cascade_prevention` module might reasonably ask: does a repair transaction's own dangerous-command detection duplicate that module's blocking logic? It does not, and the reason is instructive rather than merely definitional. `cascade_prevention` blocks a proposed action before it is attempted, based on pattern-matching a command's surface form against a registry of known-dangerous shapes — a defensive perimeter around the harness itself. CRAIF-01's own safety machinery operates entirely downstream of that perimeter, on candidates that have already cleared it, and concerns itself with a different question: not whether an action's surface form looks dangerous in isolation, but whether this specific, already-vetted candidate, against this specific target, under this specific authority grant, can be proven to work and proven to be undoable. The two systems compose rather than compete: a repair candidate that would trigger `cascade_prevention`'s own pattern match is refused at that perimeter before it ever reaches CRAIF-01's Intake at all, and CRAIF-01 assumes, per CRAIF-00 Part II's consultation hierarchy, that anything reaching it has already cleared that outer gate.

PART I FINAL LAW

CRAIF-01 owns exactly one thing: the transactional mechanics that turn an already-authorized Repair Intent into a verified, ledgered, reversible outcome. It does not detect, diagnose, authorize, or promote — those remain, without exception, the consulted authorities' own jurisdiction, and this dataset's proximity to real mutation is precisely why that boundary is restated here before a single state is defined.


PART II — THE REPAIR TRANSACTION STATE MACHINE

2.1 A Repair Intent's lifecycle, sketched at the object level in CRAIF-00 Part IV, is given its full operational shape here as an explicit state machine with named transitions, because a transaction whose states are only implied by prose invites exactly the kind of silent shortcut CRAIF-00 Part IX's separation of powers exists to prevent — a state machine with unnamed transitions has unnamed gaps, and unnamed gaps are where an executing role quietly substitutes its own judgment for a role it does not hold.

2.2 The full state sequence, in order, is: OPENED, INTAKE-VALIDATED, EVIDENCE-FROZEN, PRECONDITIONS-CHECKED, CANDIDATE-SELECTED, AUTHORITY-ACQUIRED, SNAPSHOT-TAKEN, SANDBOXED, STATICALLY-VALIDATED, TARGETED-VERIFIED, REGRESSION-VERIFIED, ACTIVATION-VERIFIED (where applicable), CANARY-DEPLOYED, and finally one of CLOSED-REPAIRED, CLOSED-ROLLED-BACK, CLOSED-STALE, or CLOSED-REJECTED. Each named state corresponds to a Part later in this dataset; a transaction is never permitted to skip a state on the strength of an earlier state's evidence looking unusually strong, because Part VIII's authority-tier discipline already accounts for confidence and no additional discretion to skip steps exists anywhere in this machine.

2.3 Transitions are one-directional except for a small, explicitly named set of regression transitions: EVIDENCE-FROZEN may return to OPENED if the freeze reveals the underlying Finding has already lapsed past its staleness threshold, per CRAIF-00 Part III; PRECONDITIONS-CHECKED may return to CANDIDATE-SELECTED if a precondition failure is specific to the chosen candidate rather than to the Intent as a whole, permitting a different falsifiable candidate to be tried without re-opening the entire Intent; and any verification state may transition directly to the CLOSED-ROLLED-BACK terminal state, from any point after SNAPSHOT-TAKEN, the instant a verification check fails, regardless of how many subsequent checks were scheduled.

2.4 A transaction's current state is never inferred from what artifacts happen to exist on disk at a given moment; it is recorded explicitly, as a field on the Repair Intent object itself, because inferring state from artifacts is exactly the presence-versus-effectiveness confusion CRAIF-00's founding evidence identifies — a snapshot file existing on disk does not, by itself, prove the transaction reached SNAPSHOT-TAKEN in an orderly way rather than crashing immediately after writing it and before recording the transition.

2.5 Time bounds are attached to specific states, not to the transaction as a whole, because a single overall timeout cannot distinguish a transaction stuck waiting on an external authority's response from one stuck in an infinite retry loop inside its own sandbox execution. AUTHORITY-ACQUIRED carries the expiry CRAIF-00 Part V's Authority Decision object defines; SANDBOXED and later states carry a bounded execution window past which the transaction is treated as hung and escalated rather than left running indefinitely, because an unbounded sandbox execution is itself an observability failure this dataset's own Part XVI names directly.

2.6 A transaction may be paused, distinct from failed, at any state prior to SNAPSHOT-TAKEN — for instance, when a higher-priority transaction targeting an overlapping reality object needs the concurrency-control mechanism in Part XVII to resolve first — and a paused transaction re-validates EVIDENCE-FROZEN before resuming, never resuming directly into a later state on stale evidence, per the same staleness discipline CRAIF-00 Part III establishes generally.

2.7 The state machine's own liveness is itself subject to CRAIF-00's non-duplication contract: this dataset does not build a parallel process-monitoring system to detect a hung transaction. It emits the transaction's current state and last-transition timestamp as a signal `modules/liveness/liveness_ledger.py`'s existing probe infrastructure can consume, per the file-mtime and co12-signal probe types that infrastructure already implements, rather than instrumenting CRAIF's own independent liveness check.

2.8 A worked trace through the third founding fixture illustrates the full sequence concretely: a Reachability Finding for the unregistered hook opens an Intent at OPENED; Intake confirms the Finding is current and the target ownership is unambiguous, reaching INTAKE-VALIDATED; Evidence Freeze captures the current settings file content and confirms the hook file itself exists but the settings key does not, reaching EVIDENCE-FROZEN; Preconditions confirms a rollback mechanism exists for settings-file edits, reaching PRECONDITIONS-CHECKED; a candidate asserting the specific settings key and its expected value is selected, reaching CANDIDATE-SELECTED; DRK's classification of this change as Tipo A and the resulting L3 grant reach AUTHORITY-ACQUIRED; and the transaction proceeds through snapshot, sandbox, verification, and canary exactly as Parts VII through X specify, closing at CLOSED-REPAIRED only once CRAIF-02's Activation Verification Contract confirms the hook fires on a real invocation, not merely that the settings key now exists.

2.9 The state machine's design rationale rests on a principle worth stating explicitly rather than leaving implicit in the sequence itself: every state boundary corresponds to a point where this dataset's own evidentiary confidence about the transaction genuinely changes, not merely a point where convenient subdivision happens to fall. INTAKE-VALIDATED represents a stronger claim than OPENED — the Finding is confirmed current and the target confirmed unambiguous — and EVIDENCE-FROZEN represents a stronger claim still, because a durable, timestamped "before" capture now exists that INTAKE-VALIDATED alone does not provide. A state machine designed around convenient subdivision rather than genuine confidence boundaries would produce transitions that look meaningful on paper while adding no actual evidentiary value, and this dataset's own discipline, stated once here, is that no future state may be added to this machine unless it corresponds to a specific claim about the transaction that the immediately preceding state did not yet support.

2.10 A second design rationale concerns why CANARY-DEPLOYED sits after the three verification states rather than before or interleaved with them. Canary exposes a candidate to a bounded slice of real conditions specifically to catch what sandbox execution's necessarily incomplete isolation could not; running Canary before Targeted, Regression, and Activation Verification would mean exposing an un-verified candidate to real conditions, which defeats Canary's own purpose as a final confirmation rather than a first attempt, and running it interleaved with verification — exposing to real conditions between individual verification passes — would make it impossible to attribute a canary failure cleanly to a specific verification dimension, since the candidate's state at the moment of exposure would differ depending on which passes had or had not yet run.

2.11 A specific ambiguity this dataset resolves explicitly, because leaving it implicit invited exactly the kind of inconsistent interpretation a future implementer would resolve differently on different days: whether a transaction demoted mid-flight, per CRAIF-00 Part VIII.7, re-enters this state machine at its current state under the new, lower tier's requirements, or restarts the machine from OPENED under the new tier. The answer is the former — a demoted transaction re-validates only the specific preconditions its new, lower tier requires that its prior, higher tier did not, per Part VI.6, and continues forward through the same state sequence rather than discarding the evidentiary progress a demotion does not actually invalidate. A demotion is a statement about authority, not about the transaction's own accumulated evidence, and conflating the two would discard sound evidence merely because the tier it was gathered under changed.

2.12 The state machine's own liveness observability, beyond the general composition with `liveness_ledger.py` this Part already establishes, carries one further obligation: a transaction's current state and the timestamp of its last transition are queryable by any external system — an Owner reviewing an in-flight repair, a future dashboard summarizing active transactions — without that system needing to understand this dataset's full object model, because a state machine whose current position is only decodable by re-reading this entire specification has failed the same observability requirement CRAIF-00 Part XIV.7 names for the emergency-stop mechanism specifically, restated here as a general requirement on the machine's own transparency.

PART II FINAL LAW

Every Repair Intent moves through one fixed, named sequence of states, and no confidence acquired at an earlier state licenses skipping a later one. State is recorded explicitly on the object, never inferred from artifacts on disk, because an artifact's existence proves only that some step wrote it, not that the transaction reached that step in the order this machine requires.


PART III — INTAKE AND EVIDENCE FREEZE

3.1 Intake is the first state transition a Repair Intent undergoes, and its entire responsibility is narrower than it might appear: confirm the contributing Findings are current per CRAIF-00 Part III's staleness discipline, confirm the target identity is precise enough that a Snapshot in Part VII can be scoped to it exactly, and confirm no unresolved sibling Intent already exists against the identical target with an overlapping condition class, per CRAIF-00 Part III.8's deduplication discipline. Intake does not judge whether the Finding's underlying diagnosis is correct — that judgment belongs upstream, to the producer and to the Decision Review Kernel where consulted — it judges only whether the Intent, as normalized, is ready to proceed.

3.2 Evidence Freeze is the transition immediately following, and it is deliberately a separate state from Intake rather than folded into it, because the two perform different work at different points in time: Intake validates the Finding's currency at the moment the Intent opens, while Evidence Freeze captures a durable, timestamped snapshot of the target's current condition specifically to serve as the "before" half of the before-and-after pair CRAIF-00 Part XV.3 requires for any strong-evidence repair claim. A transaction that queues between these two states for any length of time re-validates currency at Evidence Freeze rather than trusting Intake's earlier check to still hold.

3.3 The freeze itself is read-only by construction: it observes the target reality object's current state through whichever mechanism the target class already exposes — a file read, a settings-key lookup, a module-import trace — and it never mutates anything, because a "freeze" that also touched the target would contaminate the very before-state it exists to preserve, corrupting the before-and-after comparison Verification will later perform.

3.4 A specific failure this state is built to prevent, distinct from staleness, is silent target drift during the queue between Intake and Evidence Freeze: a target reality object that changes for reasons entirely unrelated to this transaction — a concurrent human edit, an unrelated repair, a scheduled process — between the moment Intake validated the Finding and the moment Evidence Freeze actually runs. The freeze's captured state, not Intake's earlier read, is what the Verification Contract compares against later, precisely so this drift, if it occurred, is visible in the comparison rather than silently absorbed.

3.5 Evidence Freeze also captures, alongside the target's state, the identity and version of every consulted-authority artifact the transaction will depend on later — the specific Decision Review Kernel classification version, the specific liveness scan timestamp — because Part V's Preconditions check and Part VI's Authority Acquisition both require confirming these have not been superseded between freeze and use, and confirming supersession requires knowing precisely what was frozen in the first place.

3.6 A malformed Evidence Freeze — one that captures a target identity broader or narrower than the Repair Intent's own declared target, per CRAIF-00 Part IV.1 — is rejected at this state rather than allowed to proceed with a Snapshot scoped incorrectly later, because a Snapshot taken against the wrong scope produces a Rollback Contract in Part VII that cannot actually restore what the transaction ultimately changes, silently narrower or wider than the repair itself.

3.7 The frozen evidence is retained, unmodified, as part of the eventual Repair Outcome regardless of how the transaction ultimately closes, because CRAIF-00 Part VI.4 requires the Outcome to record the Verification Contract's result in full, and a full record of a before-and-after comparison requires the actual "before" capture, not a description of it reconstructed after the fact from memory of what the freeze presumably showed.

3.8 A worked contrast between Intake rejecting an Intent and Evidence Freeze rejecting one clarifies the boundary between the two states precisely: Intake rejects a Finding that has already gone stale before the Intent ever opened, or a target whose ownership is contested from the start; Evidence Freeze rejects an Intent whose target has drifted during the queue after Intake already validated it, or whose scope is malformed relative to the Intent's own declaration. The two rejections look similar from outside — both return the Intent to CLOSED-STALE or an equivalent early exit — but they catch different failure windows, and collapsing the two states into one would blind the transaction to whichever window the collapse removed.

3.9 A worked case distinguishes a legitimate re-validation at Evidence Freeze from an unnecessary one that would waste the very re-check budget CRAIF-00 Part XIII's cost accounting exists to protect. If the queue between Intake and Evidence Freeze was measured in seconds — the ordinary case for a transaction that proceeds without contention — re-validation still runs, because this dataset's discipline does not exempt short queues from the check; the cost of a redundant re-validation against an unchanged target is negligible, while the cost of skipping it the one time a fast-moving concurrent edit actually occurred is a corrupted before-state feeding every downstream verification. The rule is uniform rather than threshold-based specifically because a threshold invites exactly the judgment call this dataset's own discipline elsewhere refuses to leave to the runtime's discretion.

3.10 The freeze's captured evidence is stored using content-addressable identity — a hash of the captured state alongside the state itself — rather than merely a timestamp, because a timestamp alone cannot answer whether two Evidence Freezes captured against the same nominal target actually observed the identical content, a distinction that matters directly for the cross-transaction idempotency case Part XVIII.5 addresses: two independently-opened Intents converging on the same target can only be recognized as having observed the same before-state, rather than two different before-states that happen to share a timestamp, if the captured evidence itself carries a content identity that can be compared directly.

3.11 A final boundary condition addresses what happens when the target reality object does not exist at all at the moment Evidence Freeze runs — not merely absent a specific key or value, but entirely absent as an artifact, the case for a Repair Candidate whose target-state assertion is that a file or module should be created rather than modified. Evidence Freeze still runs and still produces a durable record, capturing the explicit fact of non-existence as the before-state, because a before-and-after comparison requires a "before" even when the before is the absence of the artifact itself, and treating non-existence as an exemption from the freeze requirement would leave exactly the creation-class repairs — arguably the highest-risk class, since there is no existing artifact to accidentally preserve alongside a bad change — without the before-and-after discipline every other repair class receives.

PART III FINAL LAW

Intake validates that a Finding is current and a target is unambiguous at the moment an Intent opens; Evidence Freeze independently captures the target's actual condition, read-only, as the durable "before" half of the eventual proof — and re-validates currency rather than trusting Intake's earlier check, because the two states exist specifically to catch drift in two different windows of time, and folding them into one erases exactly the window each was built to watch.


PART IV — PRECONDITION AND SCOPE PROOF

4.1 Preconditions, introduced as an object in CRAIF-00 Part V, becomes an operational gate in this Part: no transaction proceeds past this state without every precondition specific to its target Authority tier — named generically in CRAIF-00 Part V.2 and made concrete per tier in this dataset's Part VI — independently confirmed against the frozen evidence from Part III, not against Intake's earlier, less durable read.

4.2 The gate is structured as a strict conjunction rather than a scored average, mirroring CRAIF-00 Part VIII.5's ladder discipline exactly: ownership unambiguous, a tested rollback mechanism available for this specific target class, the Decision Review Kernel's reversibility classification current and not superseded since Evidence Freeze, and — for any tier above L1 — observability sufficient to detect a partial or failed application actively confirmed rather than assumed present because it exists for other target classes.

4.3 Scope Proof runs alongside Preconditions and produces the operational Scope and Blast-Radius Record CRAIF-00 Part V.3 defines: the specific reality objects the candidate touches directly, the objects it touches transitively through a dependency this transaction is aware of, and an explicit, unhedged acknowledgment of what the estimate cannot rule out, consulted from the Decision Review Kernel's classifier rather than computed by this runtime's own heuristic, per CRAIF-00 Part II's consultation hierarchy restated at the operational layer here.

4.4 A template-matched candidate's inherited blast-radius estimate, per CRAIF-00 Part IV.6, is re-estimated at this state rather than trusted from the prior confirmed instance, because Preconditions is precisely the checkpoint where the current target's specific divergence from its predecessor — a changed dependency version, a drifted file content — would surface if it exists, and re-estimating anywhere later would mean discovering the divergence only after Authority has already been acquired on the strength of the wrong number.

4.5 A failure at this state produces one of two outcomes depending on which element of the conjunction failed: a failure specific to the currently-selected candidate — an unrollbackable side effect this specific candidate's approach would introduce — regresses the transaction to CANDIDATE-SELECTED so a different falsifiable candidate, per CRAIF-00 Part IV.5's preference ordering, may be tried against the same Intent; a failure inherent to the target itself — genuinely contested ownership, no rollback mechanism for this target class under any candidate — drops the transaction to CRAIF-00 Part VIII's L0 floor and produces an Owner Escalation, because no candidate substitution can repair a target-level rather than candidate-level precondition failure.

4.6 The Imperial-Boundary-Creep prohibition CRAIF-00 Part V.4 states in the ontology is enforced operationally here: Scope Proof rejects, at this state rather than later, any Scope and Blast-Radius Record naming an entity outside the estate's own boundary as a direct scope target, redirecting the record to describe only the estate's exposure to that external entity, per the same discipline IAS-D2's own corpus already applies to its containment mechanism.

4.7 A worked illustration distinguishes a genuine Preconditions pass from a superficially similar failure: a candidate targeting the third fixture's settings key passes Preconditions when the estate's `modules/rollback/*` adapter, per CRAIF-00 Part VI.3, can prove a tested restoration path specifically for settings-file edits — not merely because version control exists for the repository generally, which would satisfy a much weaker and insufficiently specific reading of "rollback available" than this Part requires.

4.8 The gate's output — a signed-off Preconditions record and a Scope and Blast-Radius Record, both versioned and referenced by identity from the Intent per CRAIF-00 Part V.5 — persists past the transaction's closure specifically so a later audit asking under what grounds a repair proceeded finds these objects intact, rather than a description of a check that ran once and left no trace beyond the transaction's own now-closed working state.

4.9 A worked case against a genuinely contested-ownership scenario illustrates the L0-floor path this Part's conjunction can produce even for a candidate that looks, on its surface, low-risk: a Drift Finding from DAIF-21 naming a reality object also monitored by CPP-IAS's D2 dataset for pathogen exposure creates exactly the jurisdiction ambiguity CRAIF-00 Part V.7 names, and Scope Proof, discovering this overlap during its own blast-radius consultation, does not attempt to adjudicate which system's jurisdiction should prevail — adjudication of cross-system jurisdiction belongs to IIC's own separation-of-powers machinery, per CRAIF-00 Part XI, not to this runtime — and the transaction drops to L0 and produces an Owner Escalation naming the specific jurisdictional overlap rather than the underlying Finding alone, because the escalation an Owner needs to resolve here is about jurisdiction, not about the repair itself.

4.10 A second worked case addresses a Preconditions pass that appears, incorrectly, to satisfy the rollback element: a candidate whose target class has a rollback mechanism proven for a broader category — settings-file edits in general — but whose specific target is a settings file the estate's own rollback adapter explicitly excludes from its coverage, per a configuration this Part requires Preconditions to check at the specific-target level rather than the category level. A Preconditions check that confirms "settings-file rollback exists" without confirming "settings-file rollback exists for this specific file" has satisfied a weaker claim than the conjunction actually requires, and this distinction — category-level versus target-level confirmation — is precisely the granularity CRAIF-00 Part V.2's precondition-per-tier discipline is written to enforce, restated here at the operational layer where the temptation to accept category-level confirmation as sufficient is strongest, because it is cheaper to check.

4.11 The relationship between Scope Proof's blast-radius estimate and this dataset's own Canary decision in Part X deserves explicit connection here, because the two are produced at different states but share a common input: a Scope and Blast-Radius Record whose acknowledgment of what it cannot rule out is unusually broad — many transitive dependencies the runtime is aware of but cannot fully characterize — is itself evidence feeding directly into Part X's determination of whether Canary is required for this specific transaction, because a narrow, well-characterized blast radius is precisely the condition under which Part X.2 permits skipping Canary, and a broad, honestly-acknowledged uncertainty is precisely the condition under which Canary's bounded real-condition exposure earns its cost.

PART IV FINAL LAW

Preconditions and Scope Proof are re-checked against the frozen evidence, not the Intake-time evidence, and re-estimated rather than inherited even for a template-matched candidate, because this state is precisely the checkpoint where a current target's divergence from its predecessor must surface before Authority is acquired on the strength of a number that may already be wrong.


PART V — CANDIDATE GENERATION AND SELECTION IN OPERATION

5.1 CRAIF-00 Part IV specifies what a Repair Candidate is and what makes one falsifiable; this Part specifies how the runtime, operationally, produces and chooses among candidates for a specific Repair Intent that has already cleared Intake and Evidence Freeze, and it is deliberately positioned before Preconditions is finally re-confirmed in Part VI's authority step, because a candidate must exist and be selected before there is anything concrete for Preconditions to evaluate against — the ordering in CRAIF-00 Part IV.5's preference list is not merely a ranking rule but a generation sequence this runtime follows literally, template match first, fresh proposal only when no template applies.

5.2 Template matching begins by querying the estate's own Guardian Promotion registry and this dataset's own Failed Repair Record history for the frozen target's Failure Pattern classification, and a match is accepted as a starting candidate only when the matched instance's own Repair Certificate remains unrevoked at the moment of matching — a matched instance whose certificate was later subject to Certification Revocation is disqualified from template use entirely, because reusing a template whose own prior success has since been withdrawn would propagate exactly the false-healing failure CRAIF-00 Part XIV.2 names, laundered through a second occurrence rather than caught at the first.

5.3 When no template exists, or every candidate template is disqualified, fresh candidate generation constructs a target-state assertion directly from the frozen evidence and the Finding's own subtype-specific fields, and the generation step is required, by CRAIF-00 Part IV.4's refusal condition, to name the specific verification instrument — an SQI reconciliation check, a liveness reachability re-scan, an Activation Verification Contract — before the candidate is considered complete, never after, because naming the instrument after generation invites exactly the after-the-fact rationalization CRAIF-00 Part XV forbids.

5.4 Selection among multiple falsifiable candidates for a single Intent is never performed by a scored weighting the runtime computes on its own initiative; it follows the fixed preference order CRAIF-00 Part IV.5 states — verified template over fresh proposal, narrower blast-radius estimate over wider among fresh proposals — and any apparent tie remaining after that order is applied is broken by consulting the Decision Review Kernel for a tie-breaking judgment rather than by the runtime's own discretion, because a tie broken on any basis outside the stated order is, per CRAIF-00 Part IV.5, a tie broken wrongly regardless of how reasonable the runtime's own basis felt in the moment.

5.5 A candidate rejected at this state for unfalsifiability, per CRAIF-00 Part IV.4, produces a specific record distinct from a Failed Repair Record: an Unfalsifiable Candidate Rejection, retained because a pattern of rejections at this specific state, accumulating across many Intents against a similar Finding subtype, is itself diagnostic evidence that the Finding's producer is under-diagnosing before it ever reaches CRAIF, and that evidence belongs in a Learning Candidate routed back to the producer's own maintainers rather than silently discarded once the immediate rejection is logged.

5.6 A worked case distinguishes correct template reuse from an error the runtime must specifically guard against: a Failure Pattern's third instance may resemble its first two closely enough to match on Finding subtype and target class while differing in one respect a naive matcher would miss — the third instance's target lives in a directory the estate's rollback adapter does not yet cover, a difference invisible to a matcher that only compares Finding subtype and Failure Pattern identity. Selection at this state requires confirming the matched template's own Rollback Contract, not merely its Verification Contract, still applies to the current target's specific class before accepting the match, precisely because Part VII's snapshot and rollback machinery, not Part IX's verification machinery, is what a template mismatch of this kind would actually break.

5.7 Candidate selection is timestamped and retained as part of the eventual Repair Outcome regardless of how the transaction closes, because a later audit or a Guardian Promotion Candidate review asking why this specific candidate was chosen over an available alternative must be answerable from the record, not reconstructed from memory of a selection that happened once and left no trace of the alternatives it passed over.

5.8 A worked case distinguishes an Unfalsifiable Candidate Rejection at generation from a candidate that is falsifiable but simply weak evidence for its own success. A candidate asserting "the module becomes reachable, checked by the next liveness scan" is falsifiable and proceeds to selection even if its confidence, drawn from a producer's own uncertain diagnosis, is low — falsifiability and confidence are independent properties, and this Part's discipline rejects only the former's absence, never the latter's weakness, because a low-confidence but genuinely falsifiable candidate that fails verification produces exactly the valuable Failed Repair Record CRAIF-00 Part VI.6 values, while a candidate rejected for unfalsifiability never even reaches the point where its confidence could be tested against reality.

5.9 The relationship between this Part's template-matching mechanism and CRAIF-01 Part XIV's Guardian Promotion pipeline deserves explicit connection, because the two share a registry but serve opposite directions of the same information flow: Part XIV's pipeline is how a repeatedly-successful candidate approach becomes a template in the first place, accumulating evidence across instances until cross-project validation warrants promotion; this Part's template matching is how an already-promoted template gets consumed on a fresh Intent. A template that has not yet cleared Part XIV's promotion gate is not available for matching here regardless of how many times its underlying approach has succeeded locally, because a template consumed before cross-project validation would let a single project's idiosyncratic success propagate as though it were general, precisely the local-to-global promotion error CRAIF-00 Part XIV.6 names.

5.10 A closing clarification addresses candidate generation's own resource cost, because an implementer under time pressure might be tempted to generate every falsifiable candidate a Finding could plausibly support and let selection sort through the full set, rather than generating candidates in the preference order itself and stopping at the first template match found. This dataset requires the latter: generation attempts a template match first and generates a fresh candidate only on a confirmed absence of any usable template, because generating multiple fresh candidates speculatively, before confirming no template exists, wastes exactly the computation CRAIF-00 Part XIII's cost accounting exists to discourage, for a search whose answer — template exists or does not — is checkable directly rather than requiring an exhaustive parallel generation to discover.

PART V FINAL LAW

Candidate selection follows one fixed order — verified template, then fresh proposal by narrowest blast radius — with ties broken by external consultation, never by the runtime's own discretion. A template match is disqualified the moment its own certificate is later revoked, and a candidate that cannot name its verification instrument at generation time is refused before selection is ever reached.


PART VI — AUTHORITY ACQUISITION: THE LADDER IN OPERATIONAL USE

6.1 CRAIF-00 Part VIII specifies the five tiers in the abstract; this Part specifies the concrete acquisition procedure a transaction follows to move from a selected candidate and a confirmed Preconditions pass to a recorded Authority Decision, and the procedure is the same regardless of which tier the transaction ultimately receives, because the acquisition act itself — consult, do not compute — is invariant across tiers even though the eligibility conditions each tier demands are not.

6.2 Acquisition begins by evaluating the L0 floor conditions from CRAIF-00 Part VIII.2 first, not last, because a transaction that satisfies every higher-tier precondition but also independently triggers an L0 floor condition — contested ownership, a destructive blast-radius component, a Rollback Contract that cannot be provably tested — is capped at L0 regardless of how strong its other qualifications look, exactly as CRAIF-00 Part VIII.1 states: tier is a function of what can go wrong, and an L0 floor condition is a statement about what can go wrong that no accumulation of other favorable evidence overrides.

6.3 Where no L0 floor condition triggers, acquisition proceeds by checking the L3 conjunction from CRAIF-00 Part VIII.5 in full before considering L2 as a fallback, because attempting L3 first and falling back only on an actual conjunction failure is the ordering that produces the richest evidence trail — a transaction that never attempted L3 tells a later reviewer nothing about which specific L3 precondition it would have failed, while a transaction that attempted and fell back records exactly which element of the conjunction was missing, evidence Part XIX's metrics and CRAIF-00 Part XIII's escape-rate accounting both depend on.

6.4 The Decision Review Kernel is consulted at this state for the transaction's Tipo A/B/C classification specifically, and the consultation is scoped narrowly to the current candidate and target, never to the Failure Pattern class in general, because a class-level classification obtained once and reused across many transactions would violate the re-confirmation discipline CRAIF-00 Part V.9 establishes for exactly this reason: a classifier's own judgment about a target class can and does revise as evidence accumulates, and a cached class-level answer from an earlier transaction is not current evidence for this one.

6.5 The resulting Authority Decision, once recorded, names its own expiry independent of the transaction's overall timeout, per CRAIF-00 Part V.6's boundary between grant validity and repair durability, and a transaction whose Authority Decision expires before it reaches Snapshot in Part VII does not proceed on the expired grant; it returns to this state and re-acquires, because an expired grant carried forward silently is precisely the malformation CRAIF-00 Part V's validation rule exists to reject.

6.6 Demotion, per CRAIF-00 Part VIII.7, is available at this state and at every subsequent state without requiring the transaction to unwind back to Candidate Selection first — a transaction acquiring L3 and subsequently, during Sandbox Execution in Part VIII, encountering evidence that contradicts one of L3's conjunctive elements demotes immediately to L2 in place, re-running whatever Part VIII steps L2's narrower scope still requires, rather than restarting the entire transaction from Candidate Selection.

6.7 A worked contrast illustrates why the L3 conjunction's evaluation order — ownership, then rollback, then reversibility classification, then determinism, then observability — matters operationally rather than being a stylistic sequencing choice: a transaction failing on the rollback element alone is demoted to L2 with a specific, actionable gap recorded — no tested restoration path for this target class — while a transaction that short-circuited evaluation at the first satisfied element and never checked the remaining four would demote with the same L2 outcome but without the diagnostic specificity Part XIX's metrics and Part V's own future candidate-template disqualification decisions depend on.

6.8 No transaction may name its own prior output as the consulted evidence for its Authority Decision, restated here at the operational layer exactly as CRAIF-00 Part V.6 states it constitutionally, and this runtime's implementation enforces the restriction by requiring every Authority Decision's evidence field to resolve to an identity outside CRAIF-01's own object namespace — a Decision Review Kernel classification identity, an owner_queue record identity, a standing policy identity — with any resolution back into CRAIF-01's own namespace treated as a malformed object rejected before Snapshot, never merely logged as a warning and allowed to proceed.

6.9 A worked case against the estate's own documented always-reject bias — the decision-authority adapter whose score rose with input length rather than the property it claimed to measure, discovered when the kernel reviewed its own wiring decision — grounds this Part's re-confirmation discipline in a concrete precedent rather than a hypothetical risk. Had CRAIF-01's Authority Acquisition cached that adapter's classification once and reused it across many subsequent transactions, every one of those transactions would have inherited the same always-reject corruption silently, discovered only when volume eventually made the pattern conspicuous rather than when the first affected transaction occurred. This Part's requirement that consultation be scoped to the current candidate and target specifically, rather than cached at the pattern level, is what would have surfaced the corruption on the very first transaction it affected rather than the hundredth.

6.10 The distinction between Preconditions' rollback-mechanism check in Part IV and this Part's own reversibility-classification consultation deserves explicit separation, because the two are easy to conflate as a single "is this safe" question when they answer genuinely different ones. Preconditions asks whether a tested restoration mechanism exists for this target class — an engineering fact about the estate's own tooling. Authority Acquisition's DRK consultation asks whether the candidate's own change, independent of whether it could be mechanically undone, is the kind of change a reasonable decision authority would classify as low-consequence — a judgment about the change's nature, not about the tooling available to reverse it. A change can have excellent rollback tooling and still receive a Tipo C classification if its blast radius, once applied, touches something DRK's own classifier weighs as high-consequence regardless of technical reversibility, and this Part requires both questions answered independently rather than inferring one from the other's favorable answer.

6.11 A final operational note addresses what happens when Authority Acquisition succeeds at a tier below what the transaction's Preconditions would have supported — a deliberately conservative grant, for instance, issued during an estate-wide period of elevated caution the Decision Review Kernel's own policy reflects. This Part requires the runtime to accept the granted tier without protest or retry-at-a-higher-tier, because the grant, once issued by the consulted authority, is authoritative regardless of whether this runtime's own Preconditions evaluation would have supported something more permissive — the entire point of consulting rather than computing is that the runtime does not get the final word on its own authority, even when it believes, with good evidence, that a higher tier was technically available.

PART VI FINAL LAW

Acquisition consults; it never computes. The L0 floor is checked before any higher tier is attempted, L3's conjunction is evaluated in full rather than short-circuited, and demotion at any later state returns the transaction to the narrower tier's own requirements in place, never forcing a full restart from Candidate Selection merely because a later state revealed what an earlier one should have caught.


PART VII — SNAPSHOT AND ISOLATION

7.1 Snapshot is the first state at which this runtime touches the target reality object with intent to prepare for mutation rather than merely to observe it, and the distinction from Evidence Freeze's earlier, purely observational read matters precisely because Snapshot's product — a restorable copy of the target's current state — is what the Rollback Contract in this Part will later restore from, while Evidence Freeze's product is what the Verification Contract will later compare against; conflating the two into one artifact would mean a restoration accidentally reverting to a state already contaminated by whatever drift occurred between the two captures, rather than to the state Evidence Freeze actually certified as current.

7.2 The Snapshot mechanism composes `modules/rollback/*` as an adapter for every target class that mechanism already covers — a settings file, a tracked repository file, a database record where the estate's `restore_pg_dump` runner applies — and this Part explicitly forbids CRAIF-01 from implementing a competing snapshot mechanism for any target class the existing adapter already handles, per CRAIF-00 Part XI.8's non-duplication contract restated here at the point where the temptation to "just write a quick copy" is most immediate and least likely to be checked.

7.3 For a target class the existing rollback adapter does not yet cover, Snapshot does not proceed by inventing an ad hoc mechanism on the spot; it halts the transaction at this state, produces an Owner Escalation naming the specific coverage gap, and routes the gap as a proposed extension to the rollback adapter's own maintainers, because a snapshot mechanism improvised under transaction pressure, untested against the estate's own rollback discipline, is exactly the kind of unaccountable parallel infrastructure CRAIF-00 Part XI.9's closing test exists to prevent.

7.4 Isolation, the second half of this Part's responsibility, establishes the sandbox boundary within which Part VIII's execution will run: a copy or a scoped view of the target sufficiently separated from canonical state that no action taken inside it can mutate anything outside the Scope and Blast-Radius Record's own declared boundary, and the isolation boundary is verified, not merely asserted, before Sandboxed is reached — a candidate whose isolation cannot be independently confirmed drops to L0's observe-only floor rather than proceeding on an unverified assumption of containment.

7.5 The Rollback Contract, formalized as an object at this state per CRAIF-00 Part VI.3, names the specific tested mechanism, the specific adapter it composes, and an estimated restoration time and cost, and it is produced before Sandboxed is reached, never after, because a transaction that entered sandbox execution without first confirming exactly how it would be undone has inverted the ordering CRAIF-00 Part V.3's Preconditions discipline requires — provable reversibility before action, not action followed by a hope that reversibility can be improvised if needed.

7.6 A specific edge case in Isolation deserves explicit treatment: a target whose full effect includes an external side effect the estate's sandboxing cannot fully contain — a hook registration that also fires a notification to an external system the moment it is registered, for instance — is not isolable in the full sense this Part requires, and a transaction targeting such an object is capped at L2 regardless of how favorable its other Preconditions look, because Sandbox Execution's entire value depends on the sandbox actually being able to contain what it runs, and a partially-containable target undermines that value in exactly the dimension the ladder's L2-versus-L3 distinction exists to price.

7.7 Snapshot and Isolation together produce the last artifacts recorded before any canonical mutation becomes possible, and both are retained, versioned, and referenced by identity from the Repair Intent exactly as CRAIF-00 Part V.5 requires for authority objects, because a rollback executed later must be traceable to the exact snapshot it restores from, not to a general assertion that "a snapshot was taken" without the specific artifact identity a real restoration requires.

7.8 A worked case against database-class targets, distinct from the settings-file examples this dataset has favored so far, exercises Snapshot and Isolation against a genuinely different mechanism to confirm the Part's discipline generalizes rather than being settings-file-specific in practice despite its general phrasing. A candidate targeting a database record composes `restore_pg_dump` as its adapter; Snapshot triggers a scoped dump of the affected table rather than the full database, per the Scope and Blast-Radius Record's own declared boundary, and Isolation for this target class means executing the candidate's change against a restored copy of that scoped dump in a separate, disposable database instance rather than a file-level sandbox — a materially different isolation mechanism from the settings-file case, and this Part's requirement that isolation be independently verified before Sandboxed is reached applies identically regardless of which underlying mechanism a given target class requires.

7.9 A specific interaction between Snapshot and this dataset's concurrency-control discipline in Part XVIII deserves note here rather than being left solely to that later Part: the Snapshot itself, once taken, becomes the shared reference point against which Part XVIII's cross-transaction idempotency check operates — two Intents converging on an identical target after Part III.8's deduplication failed to catch the overlap in time can be recognized as targeting the same before-state specifically because both Snapshots, taken independently, hash to the same content-addressable identity Part III.10 establishes for Evidence Freeze and this Part extends to Snapshot proper.

7.10 The cost of Snapshot and Isolation, measured against CRAIF-00 Part XIII's accounting, is not treated as fixed overhead applied uniformly regardless of target size; a Snapshot scoped tightly to the Scope and Blast-Radius Record's own narrow boundary, per this Part's discipline, costs less than one that over-captures out of an abundance of caution, and this Part explicitly rejects over-capture as a false economy — a Snapshot broader than the declared scope does not provide extra safety margin; it provides a larger restoration surface that Part XI's Rollback must then correctly narrow back down to only the actually-mutated portion, adding complexity to the one state this corpus can least afford ambiguity in.

PART VII FINAL LAW

Snapshot produces the restoration point Rollback will use; Evidence Freeze produced the comparison point Verification will use; the two are never the same artifact. No target class without existing rollback-adapter coverage receives an improvised snapshot mechanism under transaction pressure — it halts and escalates instead, because untested reversibility invented in the moment is not reversibility this corpus recognizes.


PART VIII — SANDBOX EXECUTION AND STATIC VALIDATION

8.1 Sandbox Execution is the first state at which the selected candidate's target-state assertion is actually attempted, and it is attempted exclusively within the isolation boundary Part VII established — no candidate, at any authority tier including L3, executes directly against canonical state without first executing, successfully, inside a sandbox scoped to the same Rollback Contract that would govern a canonical attempt, because L3's permission to mutate canonical state without human re-confirmation per transaction is a statement about who confirms, not a license to skip the sandboxed rehearsal that de-risks the mutation before it is attempted for real.

8.2 Static Validation runs immediately after sandboxed execution completes and before any verification check consumes its result, and its narrow responsibility is confirming the executed change is well-formed by the target class's own native rules — a settings file that parses, a module that imports without a syntax error, a configuration that satisfies its own schema — because a change that fails static validation has not produced a coherent artifact for Part IX's verification to even meaningfully check, and proceeding to verification against a malformed artifact would produce a verdict about noise rather than about the repair.

8.3 A sandboxed execution that fails — the candidate's target-state assertion is not achieved even within the sandbox — produces a Failed Repair Record immediately, per CRAIF-00 Part VI.6, and this record carries exactly the same institutional weight as one produced against canonical state, because a candidate refuted cheaply in sandbox before any real mutation occurred is the L2 value CRAIF-00 Part VIII.4 names directly: a candidate that fails at this state has been refuted at the lowest possible cost, and the refutation itself is valuable evidence regardless of how the transaction eventually closes.

8.4 A sandboxed execution that succeeds and passes static validation does not, by itself, license a transition to Canary or Commit; it licenses only a transition to Targeted Verification in Part IX, because sandbox success proves the candidate is capable of achieving its target-state assertion under isolated conditions, and isolated-condition success is deliberately a weaker claim than the full verification chain this dataset requires before any certificate is issued — the gap between "worked in sandbox" and "verified against the real target" is precisely the gap CRAIF-00 Part XIV.2 names as false healing's proximate cause when it is collapsed.

8.5 Sandbox Execution's own resource cost is bounded and monitored, per this dataset's own emergency-stop discipline in Part XVI, because an execution that runs unboundedly inside its sandbox — a candidate whose target-state assertion the sandbox never definitively confirms or refutes — is itself a failure this Part must surface rather than allow to silently consume resources indefinitely; a bounded timeout at this state transitions the transaction to a Failed Repair Record with an explicit INDETERMINATE outcome rather than either a false pass or an infinite wait.

8.6 A worked case distinguishes a genuine static-validation failure from a false one: a candidate that produces a syntactically valid settings file with the expected key present, but whose value type does not match the schema the settings loader itself enforces, fails Static Validation correctly — a genuine malformation. A candidate that produces a settings file the estate's own linter flags for an unrelated, pre-existing stylistic convention violation, one the file already carried before this transaction touched it, does not fail Static Validation on this runtime's own account, because Static Validation checks what this transaction's own change introduced, not the pre-existing condition of the file the transaction happened to touch — conflating the two would make every repair against an already-imperfect file impossible to certify, for reasons unrelated to the repair itself.

8.7 The sandboxed result, whether it passed or failed Static Validation, is retained in full as part of the eventual Repair Outcome, because a Failed Repair Record's diagnostic value, per CRAIF-00 Part VI.6, depends on the specific static-validation failure being named precisely — which schema rule, which parse error — rather than summarized as an undifferentiated "sandbox failed," the same summary-over-report corruption CRAIF-00 Part VI.1 already forbids for the Verification Contract's own result.

8.8 A worked case explores what happens when Sandbox Execution succeeds but takes materially longer than the Failure Pattern's prior instances, without exceeding the bounded timeout Part 8.5 sets — a signal this Part treats as diagnostically interesting even though it does not, by itself, block progression to Verification. An execution duration trending upward across successive instances of the same Failure Pattern, while each individual instance still completes within bounds, is recorded as part of the transaction's own metrics contribution to Part XIX, because a gradually lengthening execution time can be an early signal of a target class whose underlying complexity is drifting — a settings file accumulating more keys over time, say — well before any single instance actually times out, and surfacing the trend early is cheaper than waiting for the eventual timeout that trend is heading toward.

8.9 A second worked case addresses Static Validation's boundary against a schema that is itself ambiguous or under-specified for the specific value a candidate proposes — a settings key whose accepted value type the schema declares loosely enough that more than one candidate value would technically validate, only one of which actually matches the candidate's own target-state assertion. Static Validation checking schema-conformance alone would pass such a candidate incorrectly, on the strength of a valid-but-wrong value, which is precisely why Static Validation is never treated as sufficient on its own — it is a necessary gate before Targeted Verification in Part IX, never a substitute for it, and a candidate that passes Static Validation with a technically-valid but assertion-mismatched value is caught, correctly, at Targeted Verification's own comparison against the frozen before-state and the candidate's specific declared target value, not at this earlier, coarser gate.

8.10 The relationship between a sandboxed execution's own resource consumption and this dataset's L2-tier discipline deserves final clarification: a transaction operating at L2 may run Sandbox Execution and Static Validation repeatedly, exploring multiple candidate variants within the sandbox before any candidate is selected for the canonical attempt Parts IX through XI would govern, because L2's own definition in CRAIF-00 Part VIII.4 explicitly values sandboxed evidence even absent canonical authorization — provided each such exploratory run is itself recorded, per this Part's own retention requirement, rather than discarded as scratch work, because an unrecorded sandbox exploration that happened to fail is exactly the kind of institutional waste CRAIF-00 Part VI.6 names for a Failed Repair Record produced and never consulted.

PART VIII FINAL LAW

No candidate executes against canonical state, at any tier, without first executing successfully inside the sandbox Part VII isolated. Sandbox success licenses only a transition to Targeted Verification, never directly to Canary or Commit, because the gap between isolated-condition success and verified-against-the-real-target success is exactly the gap this corpus's founding evidence found still open in a sealed sibling system.


PART IX — TARGETED, REGRESSION, AND ACTIVATION VERIFICATION

9.1 Verification at this state operationalizes the Verification Contract CRAIF-00 Part VI drafted before execution ever began, and it is performed in three distinct passes, each with a different scope and a different failure consequence, rather than as a single undifferentiated check, because collapsing the three into one pass would obscure exactly which class of guarantee a passing result actually licenses — a distinction CRAIF-00 Part XV's admissibility discipline depends on being able to name precisely.

9.2 Targeted Verification checks the specific target-state assertion the selected candidate declared at generation, against the frozen before-state from Part III, using the specific instrument the candidate named at generation per CRAIF-00 Part IV.3 — never a different, more convenient instrument substituted at verification time, because substituting instruments after generation is the same after-the-fact rationalization CRAIF-00 Part IV.4 forbids at generation and Part XV.5 forbids at evidence-admission, restated here at the point of actual measurement where the temptation to substitute a more forgiving check is strongest.

9.3 Regression Verification checks a scope wider than the target itself — the estate's existing test-reach and reconciliation machinery, consumed from SQI per CRAIF-00 Part XI.4 rather than reimplemented, confirms that the repair did not silently degrade coverage or behavior elsewhere in the estate, and a Regression Verification failure, even when Targeted Verification passed cleanly, halts the transaction and routes to rollback exactly as a Targeted failure would, because a repair that fixes its declared target while breaking something adjacent is not a repair this corpus's own Repair Certificate may be issued for.

9.4 Activation Verification is required, not optional, for any Finding whose contributing evidence touches an activation-chain segment, and it consumes CRAIF-02 Part XII's Activation Verification Contract as an external check this dataset does not itself define the mechanism for, per CRAIF-00 Part VI.2's explicit statement that presence-only evidence for an activation-class repair is never sufficient — a transaction whose target is an activation-chain object and which attempts to close without an Activation Verification pass is malformed by construction and blocked from reaching Canary regardless of how cleanly its Targeted and Regression passes went.

9.5 The three passes are ordered — Targeted first, Regression second, Activation third where applicable — and a failure at any pass halts the sequence immediately rather than continuing to run the remaining passes for completeness, because a transaction already known to have failed one verification dimension gains nothing from also learning the result of a second dimension before rollback begins, and the additional resource cost of running checks whose result cannot change the transaction's outcome is exactly the kind of avoidable cost CRAIF-00 Part XIII's cost accounting is built to discourage.

9.6 A worked case against the third founding fixture again grounds the three-pass structure concretely: Targeted Verification confirms the settings key now holds its expected value; Regression Verification confirms no other estate capability depended on the key's prior absent state in a way this change now breaks; Activation Verification confirms the hook itself fires and produces its designated side effect under a real or faithfully simulated invocation of its registered event — and only the third pass, not the first, is capable of catching the specific failure mode this corpus's founding evidence identified as open in a sealed sibling system: a settings entry present and correct while the capability it was meant to enable still does not, in fact, activate.

9.7 Every verification pass's full result — not a summary — is retained as part of the eventual Repair Outcome, per CRAIF-00 Part VI.1's corruption-of-summarization warning restated at the operational layer, because the specific instrument invoked, the specific evidence it returned, and the specific pass/fail boundary it applied are each independently required by a later Guardian Promotion Candidate review or a later Certification Revocation investigation, neither of which can be conducted against a compressed "verification passed" boolean.

9.8 A worked case addresses a Regression Verification result that is itself ambiguous — SQI's reconciliation returns a result classified at its own MISLEADING or ENVIRONMENT-DEPENDENT verdict tier rather than a clean pass or fail, per SQI-02's own verdict ontology. This Part treats an ambiguous Regression result identically to a failure for the purposes of this transaction's own progression: the transaction does not proceed to Canary on an ambiguous regression signal, because proceeding on ambiguity would mean this dataset's own Verification Contract accepted evidence weaker than CRAIF-00 Part XV requires, and the transaction instead halts, produces a Failed Repair Record noting the specific ambiguous verdict, and escalates the ambiguity itself to whichever authority — typically SQI's own maintainers — is positioned to resolve why the environment or the test surface produced an unclear signal rather than a clean one.

9.9 A second worked case grounds the ordering discipline in 9.5 against a scenario where skipping the ordering would have hidden real information: a candidate that fails Regression Verification specifically because it introduced a genuinely new dependency the estate's test surface was not exercising before, versus a candidate that fails Regression for a reason entirely unrelated to its own change — a flaky, pre-existing test failure the estate's own testing corpus already knows about. This Part requires Regression Verification to check the failure's relationship to the transaction's own declared Scope and Blast-Radius Record before treating any regression failure as attributable to this repair; a regression failure outside the declared scope, confirmed independently as a pre-existing flake rather than a consequence of this candidate, does not block the transaction — it is recorded as an unrelated observation and the transaction proceeds, because attributing an unrelated pre-existing flake to this repair would produce a false rollback exactly as costly, in wasted transaction cost, as a false certificate is costly in false confidence.

9.10 A closing distinction addresses what Activation Verification consumes versus what it produces, because the boundary between this Part and CRAIF-02's own Part XII is easy to blur without an explicit statement here: this Part's responsibility is invoking CRAIF-02's Activation Verification Contract at the correct point in the sequence and correctly halting or proceeding based on its result; the contract's own internal mechanism — how a hook's firing is actually confirmed under a real or simulated invocation — belongs entirely to CRAIF-02 and is neither reimplemented nor second-guessed by this dataset, per CRAIF-00 Part XI's non-duplication discipline applied to CRAIF-01's own sibling dataset rather than only to systems outside the CRAIF family.

PART IX FINAL LAW

Three passes, not one: Targeted against the declared assertion, Regression against the estate's wider surface, Activation against the chain itself where relevant — ordered, halting on first failure, each retained in full. A transaction closing without a required Activation pass has not verified a repair; it has verified a file changed, which this corpus's founding evidence already proved is not the same claim.


PART X — CANARY AND CONTROLLED ROLLOUT

10.1 Canary is the state at which a candidate that has passed every required verification is exposed to a bounded slice of real conditions before the transaction commits to the full canonical mutation, and it exists specifically for the class of repair whose full effect cannot be completely characterized by sandbox execution and static verification alone — a repair whose target participates in concurrent access, timing-sensitive behavior, or an external dependency the sandbox's isolation could not fully model.

10.2 Not every transaction requires a Canary state; a candidate whose target class is fully deterministic and whose sandbox execution already exercised the identical real conditions the canonical target will face — the third fixture's settings-file case, for instance, where sandbox and canonical execution are behaviorally identical — may proceed directly from Regression or Activation Verification to Commit, and requiring an unnecessary Canary pass for every transaction regardless of target class would impose cost without adding evidence, exactly the kind of cost CRAIF-00 Part XIII's accounting discourages.

10.3 Where Canary is required, its scope is bounded explicitly by the Scope and Blast-Radius Record from Part IV — a single instance, a single non-critical invocation path, a time-boxed observation window — and the bound is set before Canary begins, never expanded mid-observation on the strength of early results looking favorable, because expanding scope mid-canary on favorable-looking early evidence is a miniature version of the same premature-confidence failure CRAIF-00 Part VIII.5's conjunction-not-average rule forbids at the tier-acquisition level.

10.4 A Canary failure — the bounded real-condition exposure reveals a problem sandbox execution did not — produces a Failed Repair Record and routes immediately to rollback from whatever partial canonical exposure the canary itself introduced, and the record explicitly names the gap between sandbox and canary results as its own diagnostic finding, because a Failure Pattern whose canary consistently catches problems its sandbox execution consistently misses is evidence that the sandbox's isolation, for this specific target class, is not adequately modeling real conditions — a Learning Candidate CRAIF-01's own sandbox specification in Part VII should eventually consume.

10.5 A Canary success does not, by itself, retroactively upgrade a transaction's authority tier; a transaction operating at L2 that runs a successful Canary within its sandbox-equivalent bound remains at L2, because Canary success is additional verification evidence, not a substitute for the Preconditions and reversibility classification Part VI's acquisition procedure requires independently — conflating strong verification evidence with sufficient authority would let a transaction's own favorable results argue it into a tier its Preconditions never actually cleared.

10.6 The Canary window's duration and scope are recorded, versioned, and retained as part of the eventual Repair Outcome, because a later question about why a specific repair's canary ran for the duration it did, or why its scope was bounded the way it was, must be answerable from the record rather than reconstructed from an implementer's memory of a judgment call made once during a single transaction.

10.7 A worked case distinguishes a target genuinely requiring Canary from one a naive reading of 10.1 might over-apply the requirement to: a candidate touching a database record participates in concurrent access by the database's own nature, which per 10.1's criteria would suggest Canary is required — but if the specific record this candidate touches is provably accessed by exactly one, single-threaded consumer process, confirmed through the Scope and Blast-Radius Record's own dependency trace, the concurrent-access property that ordinarily triggers Canary does not actually apply to this specific instance, and Canary may be skipped per 10.2's narrower exception, provided the single-consumer confirmation is itself independently verified rather than assumed from the target class's general reputation for concurrency.

10.8 A second worked case addresses Canary's own bounded-window discipline under a specific failure mode: a Canary that appears to succeed throughout its observation window but whose window happened to fall entirely within a period of unusually low real traffic, such that the bounded exposure never actually exercised the condition the Canary was meant to test. This dataset requires Canary's own Scope and Blast-Radius Record to specify not only a time bound but a minimum-exercise condition — a minimum number of real invocations the target must actually receive within the window for the Canary to count as having run at all — because a Canary window that elapses without genuine exercise has produced the appearance of a passed check while actually providing no evidence, precisely the presence-versus-effectiveness confusion this corpus's founding evidence identifies at every other layer and which Canary's own bounded-window design must not silently reintroduce here.

10.9 The relationship between a failed Canary and this dataset's Concurrent Repair Control discipline in Part XVIII deserves brief connection: a Canary failure that reveals a concurrency-related defect specifically — two processes racing on the same target in a way sandbox isolation could not model — feeds directly into whether this target class should be reclassified, for future transactions, as requiring the stricter Concurrent Collision lock Part XVI.1 establishes even for targets not previously flagged as needing it, because a Canary is, among other things, the mechanism most likely to surface exactly the concurrency risk a purely sandboxed evaluation is structurally blind to.

PART X FINAL LAW

Canary is required only where sandbox execution cannot fully characterize real-condition behavior, its scope is bounded before observation begins and never expanded on favorable early evidence, and a successful Canary strengthens verification evidence without ever substituting for the authority-tier conjunction Preconditions independently requires.


PART XI — COMMIT OR ROLLBACK DECISION

11.1 Commit-or-Rollback is the single decision point at which every prior state's evidence converges into one of two outcomes, and the decision itself is mechanical rather than discretionary: a transaction commits if and only if every required verification pass from Part IX succeeded, Canary from Part X either was not required or succeeded within its bound, and no demotion or contradicting evidence arrived between Authority Acquisition and this state; any other combination routes to rollback, with no judgment call available to the runtime at this specific junction beyond confirming which of the two exhaustive conditions actually held.

11.2 A Commit executes the canonical mutation using the same mechanism the sandbox execution in Part VIII already rehearsed successfully, never a different mechanism substituted at commit time for convenience or speed, because a commit mechanism that diverges from its own rehearsed sandbox equivalent has, by that divergence, invalidated every piece of evidence the sandbox pass produced — the evidence licensed a claim about what the rehearsed mechanism does, not about what a different, unrehearsed mechanism might do under the same name.

11.3 A Rollback at this state restores the target to the state Snapshot captured in Part VII, using the Rollback Contract's own named mechanism, and the restoration's own success is independently verified before the transaction is permitted to close as CLOSED-ROLLED-BACK — a rollback attempt whose own success cannot be confirmed does not close cleanly; it produces an Owner Escalation naming a failed rollback specifically, because CRAIF-00 Part XVI's own reflexive discipline requires this corpus to admit, rather than paper over, the case its own Rollback Contract was unable to fully honor.

11.4 The decision's mechanical nature is precisely what makes it auditable independent of any single implementer's judgment: a later reviewer checking why a specific transaction committed needs only confirm the same three conditions — required verifications passed, Canary bound honored where applicable, no intervening contradiction — rather than reconstruct a discretionary judgment call that might have weighed the same evidence differently on a different day, which is the same auditability property CRAIF-00 Part V.5 states for versioned authority objects, restated here for the decision that consumes them.

11.5 A specific failure mode this state guards against directly is a transaction that commits on the strength of verification results that were current when Authority was acquired but have since been superseded by new evidence arriving during Snapshot, Sandbox, or Canary — a Decision Review Kernel reclassification, a Finding staleness threshold lapsing mid-transaction — and Commit-or-Rollback re-checks currency of every consulted authority's evidence one final time immediately before executing, not merely relying on checks performed at earlier states, because this is the last possible checkpoint before canonical mutation and the last opportunity to catch drift that accumulated across the transaction's own duration.

11.6 The decision and its full evidentiary basis are recorded as an immutable Repair Outcome the instant the decision is made, before the Commit or Rollback action itself executes, so that even a runtime crash occurring during the action itself leaves a durable record of what was decided and why, rather than losing the decision's own grounds to the same failure that may have interrupted its execution.

11.7 A worked case addresses the specific scenario this Part's re-check in 11.5 is built to catch: a transaction whose Authority was acquired under a DRK Tipo A classification, which held throughout Snapshot and Sandbox Execution, but which DRK itself revised to Tipo B during the Canary window — new evidence about the target class having arrived from an unrelated transaction elsewhere in the estate during the intervening time. This Part's final re-check discovers the revision before Commit executes, and the transaction, now holding a grant whose grounds have changed since acquisition, does not commit on the original Tipo A grant; it treats the revision as a demotion trigger per Part XVII.4 and proceeds under the tier the new Tipo B classification actually supports, which may mean routing to rollback if the demoted tier's own Preconditions are not independently satisfied.

11.8 A second worked case distinguishes a genuine Commit from an action that merely appears complete: a Commit action for a multi-key settings change that writes all declared keys successfully but whose final confirmation read — the step that independently verifies the write actually persisted, distinct from the write call itself reporting success — has not yet run. This Part requires the confirmation read to complete and match the intended state before the transaction is recorded as committed, because a write call reporting success is exactly the kind of self-report CRAIF-00 Part IX.6 already refuses to accept as sufficient verification, restated here for the commit action's own completion rather than only for the later Verification Contract's result.

11.9 The relationship between this state's mechanical decision and the emergency-stop discipline in Part XVII deserves explicit sequencing: an Emergency Stop signal arriving at the exact moment Commit-or-Rollback is evaluating its two conditions takes precedence over the mechanical decision itself — the transaction halts at the nearest safe boundary per Part XVII.2 rather than completing an in-progress Commit merely because the evidentiary conditions for committing were, at that instant, satisfied, because Emergency Stop's absolute reach, per Part XVII.1, does not carve out an exception for a transaction that happens to be mid-decision when the stop arrives.

PART XI FINAL LAW

Commit-or-Rollback is mechanical, not discretionary: it re-checks currency of every consulted authority one final time, commits only through the exact mechanism already rehearsed in sandbox, and records its full decision as an immutable Outcome before executing — so a crash mid-action loses no evidence of what was decided, even if it disrupts what was being done.


PART XII — POST-REPAIR LIVENESS OBSERVATION

12.1 A transaction that reaches CLOSED-REPAIRED has satisfied every verification this dataset requires at the moment verification ran, but CRAIF-00 Part XIV.2's false-healing failure mode names a further risk this Part exists to address: a repair verified correct at commit time can still fail to hold shortly afterward, under conditions the transaction's own bounded Canary window did not observe, and a repair whose durability is never checked past the moment of its own certification is trusting a single point-in-time measurement to stand permanently for a claim about ongoing behavior.

12.2 Post-Repair Liveness Observation composes `modules/liveness/liveness_ledger.py`'s own existing post-ship monitoring, per CRAIF-00 Part XI.3's non-duplication contract, rather than building a parallel monitoring mechanism specific to CRAIF's own repairs — the repaired target is registered as a row in that ledger's existing probe registry, if it is not already present, so that the ledger's own daily audit cycle extends coverage to the repair without CRAIF-01 maintaining a second, redundant observation loop.

12.3 A repair whose post-repair observation later returns a non-LIVE verdict — WIRED-BUT-SILENT, DRIFTED, or ORPHANED per the ledger's own vocabulary — triggers a Certification Revocation against the Repair Certificate this transaction issued, per CRAIF-00 Part VII.6, and the revocation is not treated as a failure of this Part's observation; it is treated as exactly the evidence CRAIF-00 Part XIII's False Healing Rate countermetric exists to surface, because a certificate correctly issued at commit time and correctly revoked days later when conditions changed is the system working as specified, not the system failing.

12.4 The observation window's duration is bounded and explicit rather than indefinite, matching the ledger's own existing freshness cadence — its default measured in tens of hours — because an unbounded observation obligation would mean no repair is ever fully closed in the institutional-learning sense Part XIV requires, while a bounded window that the repair must survive before its Learning Candidate is finalized gives the closing act a definite, checkable condition rather than a perpetual watching brief.

12.5 A worked distinction separates this Part's responsibility from Part IX's Regression Verification: Regression checks, at commit time, whether the repair broke something else in the estate that already existed; Post-Repair Liveness Observation checks, over the following observation window, whether the repair itself continues to hold under conditions its own bounded Canary and verification passes did not have time to observe — the third founding fixture's hook, verified to fire once under a faithful simulation at commit time, is exactly the kind of repair this Part's extended observation is built to confirm continues firing under real, recurring invocation rather than only the single verified instance the transaction itself produced.

12.6 The observation's result, whether it confirms LIVE or surfaces a non-LIVE verdict requiring revocation, feeds directly into the Learning Candidate this Repair Intent ultimately produces, per Part XIV, because a Learning Candidate finalized before the observation window closes would be finalized on incomplete evidence about whether the repair actually held — precisely the premature-certification pattern CRAIF-00 Part XV.4 forbids for the Verification Contract, restated here for the institutional-learning step that consumes its result.

12.7 A worked case addresses a repair whose Post-Repair Observation window spans a period during which the target's surrounding environment changes for reasons entirely unrelated to the repair itself — a dependency upgrade elsewhere in the estate that happens to touch the same module the repair registered a hook against. This Part requires the observation to continue treating the repair's own certificate as provisional throughout the window regardless of the cause of any environmental change, because SQI's own corpus already establishes that a certificate can become false without any new defect being introduced — the environment around it changed — and this Part's discipline does not attempt to distinguish "the repair broke" from "something unrelated changed and now the repair's assumptions no longer hold" at the observation layer; both produce the identical non-LIVE verdict and the identical Certification Revocation, with the causal distinction reserved for whatever Learning Candidate or diagnostic review follows the revocation.

12.8 A second worked case grounds the observation window's bounded duration against a specific risk of setting it too short: a repair whose underlying Failure Pattern only manifests under a weekly-cadence event — a scheduled job that runs once every seven days — would pass a 36-hour default observation window without ever exercising the specific condition its own Verification Contract was meant to confirm holds durably. This Part requires the observation window's duration to be set per Failure Pattern class where a class's own natural cadence is known to exceed the estate's default freshness window, rather than applying the default uniformly regardless of the target's own event frequency, because an observation window shorter than the phenomenon it observes produces exactly the same false-confidence gap a presence check produces relative to a live demonstration.

12.9 The interaction between this Part's observation and Part XIV's Learning Handoff is bidirectional rather than one-directional: while 12.6 already establishes that observation results feed into the Learning Candidate, a Learning Candidate's own accumulated pattern-level evidence — this Failure Pattern's prior instances showing a specific window duration was insufficient to catch a specific decay mode — feeds back into this Part's own window-duration determination for future instances of the same pattern, closing a loop in which the observation mechanism itself improves from what its own outputs, aggregated over time, reveal about its adequacy.

PART XII FINAL LAW

Verification at commit time is a point-in-time claim; durability is a claim about time this corpus does not certify without observing it. Post-Repair Liveness Observation composes the estate's existing ledger rather than building a parallel one, and a non-LIVE verdict during the bounded observation window revokes the certificate — correctly, as the system working as specified, not as a failure of the observation itself.


PART XIII — LEDGER UPDATES AND SIDE-EFFECT RECORDING

13.1 Every transaction, regardless of which terminal state it reaches, produces exactly one Incident Closure per CRAIF-00 Part VII.5, and this Part specifies the concrete mechanics by which that closure reaches the estate's shared ledgers without requiring any of those ledgers to understand CRAIF's own internal object graph — the closure is adapted, at this state, into each consuming ledger's own native shape, because CRAIF-00 Part VII.5 already establishes that consumers should not need to parse CRAIF's ontology directly, and this Part is where that adaptation actually happens rather than merely being asserted as a design principle.

13.2 The liveness registry receives a reachability-restoration confirmation, keyed to the specific module or hook identity the transaction targeted, formatted exactly as `modules/liveness/liveness_ledger.py`'s own registry rows expect, so the ledger's next scheduled audit cycle picks up the change through its own existing mechanism rather than through a side channel CRAIF invented; the Decision Review Kernel's accountability ledger receives the transaction's Authority Decision outcome — whether the predicted reversibility held, whether the repair committed or rolled back — formatted as a prediction-versus-outcome record its own accountability module already consumes; the Institutional Immune System, where the originating Finding was an Immune-Threat Finding, receives a confirmation update distinguishing whether the underlying confirmation was a structural presence check or a live adversarial demonstration, per CRAIF-00 Part III.3's carried flag, closing exactly the gap IAS-D2's own Part XIV names as open.

13.3 The Side-Effect Ledger, distinct from the Repair Outcome itself, accumulates every observed effect the transaction produced outside its own declared target scope during Sandbox, Canary, and Commit — an unexpected file touched, a downstream process that restarted, a cache that invalidated — and its purpose is specifically to catch effects the Scope and Blast-Radius Record from Part IV did not anticipate, because an unanticipated side effect discovered only through this ledger's own accumulation, rather than through the original estimate, is itself evidence the blast-radius estimation for this target class needs revision, feeding directly into the Learning Candidate Part XIV produces.

13.4 A specific discipline governs what the Side-Effect Ledger records versus what it interprets: it records observed effects as raw facts — this file's modification time changed, this process restarted — without attempting to classify, at recording time, whether each effect was harmful, neutral, or beneficial, because that classification belongs to whichever downstream consumer — the Decision Review Kernel reviewing the transaction's outcome, a future Guardian Promotion Candidate review — is positioned to judge it in context, and pre-classifying at recording time risks discarding a raw observation a later reviewer, with more context than this transaction had, might have interpreted differently.

13.5 Ledger updates are themselves subject to the same fail-open discipline the estate's own probes already apply: a ledger update that cannot complete — the target registry is unreachable, the accountability ledger's write path is temporarily unavailable — does not silently drop the update or block the transaction's own closure indefinitely; it retries per a bounded policy and, failing that, produces its own Owner Escalation naming the specific ledger it could not reach, because a transaction that closes successfully while its downstream ledger updates silently fail has produced exactly the kind of orphaned learning CRAIF-00 Part VII.10 names as institutional waste.

13.6 A worked case against the second founding fixture illustrates the ledger fan-out concretely: a repaired orphan module produces a liveness-registry update confirming the specific `via` surface that now proves reachability, a Side-Effect Ledger entry if the repair required touching a second file to create that surface, and — because this Finding subtype had no immune or decision-authority dimension — no update to the immune registry or the accountability ledger, illustrating that not every closure fans out to every ledger; each closure updates exactly the ledgers relevant to its own contributing Findings' subtypes, never all of them by default.

PART XIII FINAL LAW

Every closure fans out to exactly the ledgers its contributing Findings' subtypes make relevant, adapted into each consumer's own native shape rather than requiring the consumer to parse CRAIF's ontology. A ledger update that cannot complete escalates rather than silently drops, because a transaction that closes cleanly while its downstream learning silently fails to land has produced exactly the orphaned learning this corpus exists to prevent.


PART XIV — LEARNING HANDOFF AND GUARDIAN PROMOTION

14.1 The Learning Candidate CRAIF-00 Part VII specifies as an object is produced, at this state, from the complete transaction record — every prior Part's output, from Intake through Post-Repair Liveness Observation — and its thinness or thickness, per CRAIF-00 Part VII.7's floor, is determined mechanically by the transaction's own novelty: a first-of-its-kind candidate against a previously unseen Failure Pattern produces a thick Learning Candidate naming the full diagnostic chain; a routine templated repair produces the thin floor — Failure Pattern identity, recurrence increment, first-attempt-success flag — and nothing this runtime does may reduce a Learning Candidate below that floor regardless of transaction volume pressure.

14.2 Guardian Promotion Candidate generation is evaluated, not automatically triggered, once a Failure Pattern's accumulated recurrence count — tracked across many Learning Candidates over time, never inferred from a single transaction — crosses the threshold the estate's own promotion pipeline sets, and crossing the threshold produces a proposal, never a self-installed guardian, routed to whichever existing authority owns the target registry, per CRAIF-00 Part VII.3's explicit prohibition restated here at the point where the temptation to "just install the fix everywhere" is most operationally immediate.

14.3 The proposal's evidence package names every prior instance's Repair Certificate, cross-project where the pattern has been observed in more than one repository per CRAIF-02's own cross-repo activation science, and explicitly declines to propose promotion when every prior instance originated from a single project — the local-recurrence-then-cross-project-validation sequence CRAIF-00 Part XIV.6 requires is enforced here as a hard gate on the proposal's own eligibility, not merely as guidance an implementer is trusted to remember under time pressure.

14.4 A specific interaction with Certification Revocation deserves explicit treatment: a Failure Pattern whose recurrence count crossed the promotion threshold, but one of whose contributing instances was subsequently revoked per Part XII's post-repair observation, has its promotion proposal held pending re-evaluation with the revoked instance excluded from the count, because a promotion proposal resting even partly on an instance later proven not to have held is resting on exactly the false-healing evidence CRAIF-00 Part XIV.2 names, and excluding the revoked instance may drop the pattern back below threshold entirely.

14.5 A Failed Repair Record's own learning handoff, distinct from a successful closure's, produces a Learning Candidate whose promotion recommendation, where warranted, targets not a new guardian but an update to the Finding producer's own diagnostic logic — a Failure Pattern whose candidates repeatedly fail Targeted Verification for the same underlying reason is evidence that the producer's own Finding classification is insufficiently specific, and this Part's obligation is to name that gap explicitly rather than let repeated candidate failures accumulate as isolated, unconnected incidents.

14.6 The handoff closes each Repair Intent's full lifecycle by confirming, before the Intent is marked CLOSED-REPAIRED in its permanent record, that its Learning Candidate has actually been produced and its target consumer named — an Incident Closure without a corresponding Learning Candidate is itself a malformed closure per CRAIF-00 Part I's founding law, because a capability is not institutionally complete merely by having been fixed once; it is complete only when the fix has left the estate measurably harder to fool by the same defect twice.

PART XIV FINAL LAW

A Learning Candidate's thinness floor is mechanical, never discretionary; a Guardian Promotion proposal is gated hard on cross-project validation, never proposed from a single repository's recurrence alone; and a promotion proposal resting on a since-revoked instance is held for re-evaluation, because false healing discovered after promotion was already proposed is exactly the failure this corpus's own countermetrics exist to catch before it compounds.


PART XV — FAILURE MODES I: RETRY, TIMEOUT, PARTIAL FAILURE, FAILED ROLLBACK, STALE INTENT

15.1 Retry is available only at specific, named states — Sandbox Execution and Static Validation, where a transient environmental condition rather than the candidate itself may explain a first failure — and it is bounded to a small, fixed count rather than attempted indefinitely, because an unbounded retry at any state is indistinguishable, from outside, from the thrashing failure mode CRAIF-00 Part XIV.4 names, and a retry policy without an explicit ceiling is a thrashing policy that has not yet been triggered often enough to reveal itself as one.

15.2 A retry that exhausts its bounded count does not silently escalate to a stronger action; it produces a Failed Repair Record naming the specific number of attempts and the specific result of each, and the transaction closes CLOSED-REJECTED rather than lingering in an ambiguous state, because a transaction that has exhausted its retries and still has no clear path forward gains nothing from remaining open, and an open transaction with no forward path is itself an observability gap this dataset's own Part XVI names.

15.3 Timeout is bounded per state rather than globally, per Part II.5's design, and a timeout at any state produces the same disposition: the transaction transitions toward rollback if canonical mutation has already begun, or toward CLOSED-STALE if it has not, because a timed-out transaction's own evidence about why it is taking longer than expected is itself unreliable — the timeout exists precisely because the transaction cannot be trusted to self-report its own hung condition accurately.

15.4 Partial Failure — a canonical mutation that completed some but not all of a candidate's declared target-state changes before failing — is the specific case Snapshot and Rollback in Part VII exist to make recoverable, and this Part requires that a Partial Failure's rollback restore to the exact pre-transaction Snapshot state, never to an intermediate "best effort" state the transaction happened to reach, because a partial-completion state left standing under the appearance of a completed rollback is precisely the false-healing risk CRAIF-00 Part XIV.2 names, relocated to the rollback mechanism itself rather than to verification.

15.5 Failed Rollback — the case in which the Rollback Contract's own restoration mechanism does not succeed — is this dataset's single most severe failure mode, because it leaves the target in neither its pre-transaction nor its intended post-transaction state, and it is handled by immediate, unconditional Owner Escalation naming the specific target, the specific attempted restoration, and the specific evidence of the restoration's own failure, with no further automated action attempted, because an automated system that responds to a failed rollback with another automated action has demonstrated, in that single choice, that it has not understood why the first mechanism failed.

15.6 Stale Intent — a Repair Intent whose contributing Findings lapse past their staleness threshold while the transaction is paused, queued, or awaiting an external authority's response — transitions to CLOSED-STALE per CRAIF-00 Part IV.2 regardless of how much progress the transaction had made toward Commit, because progress made against evidence that has since gone stale is not evidence the eventual repair still addresses a current condition, and this Part's discipline requires discarding sunk progress rather than committing on stale grounds merely because discarding feels wasteful.

15.7 A worked case distinguishes Partial Failure from a successful Commit that merely took longer than the transaction's typical duration: a candidate that writes three related settings keys and successfully writes all three, verified in full, has committed successfully regardless of elapsed time; a candidate that writes the first two keys and then encounters an error before writing the third has partially failed, and the distinction is not about duration but about whether every declared target-state change actually completed — a distinction this Part requires be checked explicitly rather than inferred from the absence of an error message, because CRAIF-00 Part XV.5 already names absence of an error as weak evidence at best.

PART XV FINAL LAW

Retry is bounded and available only at states where transient conditions plausibly explain failure; a Failed Rollback triggers immediate, unconditional escalation with no further automated action, because a system that responds to a failed restoration with another automated attempt has not understood the failure; and Stale Intent discards sunk transaction progress rather than committing on evidence that has already gone stale.


PART XVI — FAILURE MODES II: CONCURRENT COLLISION, THRASHING, DEPENDENCY-UNAVAILABLE, EVIDENCE CORRUPTION

16.1 Concurrent Collision occurs when two Repair Intents targeting overlapping reality objects reach mutation-capable states — Sandbox or later — at the same time, and this dataset's discipline is strict precedence rather than arbitration: the Intent that reached Snapshot first holds an exclusive lock on its declared target scope until it closes, and any later-arriving Intent whose scope overlaps is held at Precondition Proof, per Part IV, until the lock releases, because arbitrating between two simultaneously-executing mutations against overlapping scope is a race condition no verification discipline can retroactively untangle once both have already touched canonical state.

16.2 A held Intent's own Findings are re-validated for staleness, per CRAIF-00 Part III, at the moment the lock releases and the held Intent resumes, never resumed directly on evidence frozen before the wait began, because the target it was waiting to touch may have changed as a direct result of the Intent that held the lock first, and resuming on pre-wait evidence in that specific case would mean acting on a before-state the intervening transaction has already invalidated.

16.3 Thrashing, introduced conceptually in CRAIF-00 Part XIV.4, is detected operationally at Intake per Part III.9's obligation to check recent Incident Closure history against the same target: an Intent opening within a short window of a prior closure against the identical target does not proceed to a fresh transaction silently; it escalates to Owner attention, naming the prior closure and the new Finding, because a target that requires repair twice in rapid succession has revealed that the first repair addressed a symptom rather than the underlying condition, and CRAIF-01's own runtime is not equipped to diagnose that deeper condition — diagnosis belongs upstream, per CRAIF-00 Part IX, and the escalation exists to route the deeper question to where it can actually be answered.

16.4 Dependency-Unavailable — a required consulted authority (the Decision Review Kernel, the estate's rollback adapter, SQI's reconciliation) is unreachable at the moment a transaction needs it — never defaults to proceeding without the consultation; per CRAIF-00 Part II.4, absence of an answer is treated as a stop, not as permission, and a transaction blocked on an unavailable dependency pauses at its current state, re-attempting the consultation on a bounded schedule, and escalates if the dependency remains unavailable past a threshold this Part sets deliberately short for any state at or past Authority Acquisition, because a long pause holding an exclusive lock on a target scope, per 16.1, blocks every other Intent that might need the same target.

16.5 Evidence Corruption — a frozen before-state, a Snapshot, or a verification result that is discovered to be internally inconsistent or unreadable at the moment it is needed — is treated as equivalent to Dependency-Unavailable in its disposition: the transaction does not proceed on corrupted evidence under any authority tier, and it does not attempt to silently regenerate the evidence and continue as though nothing had happened, because a transaction that quietly re-derives evidence it cannot verify was ever sound has broken the entire before-and-after admissibility chain CRAIF-00 Part XV requires, and the correct response is to reopen from Intake with a fresh Evidence Freeze, not to patch over a corrupted one.

16.6 A worked case distinguishes genuine Concurrent Collision from a false one: two Findings against the same module, one a Reachability Finding and one a Quality Finding, deduplicated at Intake per CRAIF-00 Part III.8 into two separate Intents sharing a target identity but distinct condition classes, are not automatically a Concurrent Collision merely because they share a target — the lock in 16.1 applies to overlapping mutation scope specifically, and two Intents whose candidates would touch genuinely disjoint aspects of the same broader target (a settings key versus a module's docstring, say) may, if their Scope and Blast-Radius Records confirm the disjointness explicitly, proceed without one holding the other, provided the confirmation is independently verified rather than assumed from the Findings' differing subtypes alone.

PART XVI FINAL LAW

Concurrent Collision is resolved by strict precedence on overlapping scope, never by arbitration; Thrashing is detected at Intake by checking recent closure history against the same target and escalates rather than repeating silently; and both Dependency-Unavailable and Evidence Corruption stop the transaction rather than proceeding on an absent answer or a re-derived evidence chain, because absence and corruption are both, per this corpus's own consultation discipline, treated as a stop, never as permission to continue on faith.


PART XVII — EMERGENCY STOP AND DEMOTION

17.1 Emergency Stop is a mechanism reachable by any Owner action, at any moment, that halts every in-flight transaction immediately regardless of the state each currently occupies, and its reach is stated here as an absolute rather than a best-effort target precisely because CRAIF-00 Part XIV.7 names the specific risk of a future authority policy whose emergency stop does not actually cover every transaction it authorized — this Part's own obligation, restated at the operational layer, is that no transaction may execute under any authority tier without an active, verified emergency-stop path already covering it before execution begins.

17.2 A transaction halted mid-state by Emergency Stop does not attempt to complete its current state's action before stopping; it halts at the nearest safe boundary — immediately if no canonical mutation has begun, or by transitioning directly into Rollback using the already-prepared Rollback Contract if mutation is in progress — because completing an in-progress action before honoring a stop request treats the stop as advisory rather than as the absolute halt CRAIF-00 Part XVI.3 requires it to be.

17.3 Every halted transaction produces a record distinct from a normal closure, timestamped and marked EMERGENCY-HALTED rather than any of the four ordinary terminal states, and this distinct marking matters because CRAIF-00 Part XIII's metrics must be able to separate a transaction stopped by deliberate Owner action from one that failed or succeeded on its own evidentiary merits — conflating the two would corrupt the False Healing Rate and the recurrence-elimination metrics with data points that say nothing about whether a repair actually worked.

17.4 Demotion, distinct from Emergency Stop, is available continuously rather than only at Authority Acquisition, per CRAIF-00 Part VIII.7's asymmetry: any state from Snapshot onward may demote a transaction to a lower tier the instant contradicting evidence arrives, and a demotion at a mutation-capable state — L3 demoted to L2 after canonical mutation has already begun — does not retroactively undo the mutation already made; it converts the remainder of the transaction's authority to the lower tier's requirements going forward, while the mutation already committed is handled through the ordinary Commit-or-Rollback and Post-Repair Observation machinery in Parts XI and XII exactly as if it had been authorized at the demoted tier from the start.

17.5 Both Emergency Stop and Demotion are themselves subject to CRAIF-00 Part X's third law: neither mechanism may be invoked by the runtime against itself as a means of avoiding an inconvenient verification result — a transaction whose Targeted Verification is failing does not "emergency stop itself" to avoid producing a Failed Repair Record, because Emergency Stop is reachable only by Owner action or by the specific, named contradicting-evidence conditions Demotion's own trigger enumerates, never by the executing role's own discretion to preempt an unfavorable outcome.

PART XVII FINAL LAW

Emergency Stop's reach must be verified complete before any transaction executes under any tier, and a halted transaction stops at the nearest safe boundary rather than completing its current action first. Neither Stop nor Demotion may be invoked by the runtime against itself to preempt an unfavorable verification result — both are triggered by Owner action or by named external evidence, never by the executing role avoiding its own inconvenient outcome.


PART XVIII — CONCURRENT REPAIR CONTROL AND IDEMPOTENCY

18.1 Beyond the pairwise Concurrent Collision case Part XVI addresses, this Part specifies the runtime's overall concurrency discipline: multiple transactions against non-overlapping targets execute independently and in parallel without coordination overhead, because CRAIF-00 Part XIII's cost accounting treats unnecessary serialization as its own form of waste, and a runtime that serializes every transaction regardless of target overlap has traded a theoretical safety margin for a real, measurable throughput cost against risk that does not exist for genuinely disjoint targets.

18.2 Idempotency is required of every state's own action, meaning a transaction that crashes after completing a state's work but before recording the transition to the next state must be safely resumable by re-attempting the same state's action without duplicating its effect — a Snapshot taken twice against the same target because the first recording crashed must not produce two divergent snapshot artifacts; it must recognize the prior snapshot's existence and either reuse it or explicitly supersede it, never silently accumulate parallel, inconsistent snapshot copies.

18.3 The mechanism that makes idempotency checkable rather than merely asserted is the same explicit state-recording discipline Part II.4 establishes: because state is recorded on the object itself rather than inferred from artifacts, a resumed transaction can determine precisely which state it last completed and resume from exactly that point, rather than guessing from an ambiguous mix of completed and incomplete artifacts what its own prior progress actually was.

18.4 A specific idempotency hazard is named because it was nearly overlooked during this dataset's own design: a Commit action that partially completes — the third fixture's example, writing two of three related settings keys before crashing — and is then resumed must not naively re-attempt writing all three keys from scratch, which could produce an inconsistent intermediate artifact if the resumption's own write ordering differs from the original attempt's; the correct resumption re-reads the target's actual current state first, determines which of the three keys still require writing, and proceeds only against the genuine remaining delta, treating the crash-interrupted attempt's partial progress as authoritative evidence of what remains rather than restarting blindly.

18.5 Cross-transaction idempotency — two independently-opened Intents that happen to converge on an identical candidate against an identical target, perhaps because two different Findings from two different producers triggered near-simultaneously before Part III.8's deduplication caught the overlap — is resolved by treating the second transaction to reach Snapshot as having discovered an already-current target state, short-circuiting directly to Targeted Verification against the first transaction's already-applied change rather than re-attempting an already-satisfied mutation, because re-applying an idempotent change a second time should be harmless by the target class's own nature, but re-verifying rather than assuming harmlessness is what confirms the assumption rather than merely trusting it.

PART XVIII FINAL LAW

Non-overlapping transactions run in parallel without coordination overhead, because unnecessary serialization is its own cost this corpus's own accounting refuses to spend without reason. Every state's action is idempotent by requirement, and a crash-interrupted resumption re-reads actual current state as authoritative evidence of what remains, rather than restarting blindly from an assumption about what should have completed.


PART XIX — INTEGRATION CONTRACTS

19.1 This Part formalizes, as explicit bidirectional contracts, the consultation relationships CRAIF-00 Part II establishes in principle and this dataset has invoked by name throughout — each contract names what CRAIF-01 sends, what it expects in return, and what happens on either side's failure to honor the contract, because a consultation relationship stated only as prose scattered across many Parts is harder to audit for completeness than one collected here as a single reference surface.

19.2 With `modules/liveness/reachability.py` and `liveness_ledger.py`: CRAIF-01 sends target-registration requests at Post-Repair Observation and consumes REACHABLE/ORPHAN/UNKNOWN and LIVE/SILENT/DRIFTED/ORPHANED verdicts as inputs to Certification Revocation; on the liveness family's own failure to respond, CRAIF-01 treats the absent verdict as UNKNOWN, never as an implicit LIVE, per the liveness family's own fail-open-to-UNKNOWN discipline this corpus inherits rather than weakens.

19.3 With SQI: CRAIF-01 consumes reconciliation results as Regression Verification's primary instrument and sends no data back except a Repair Outcome reference SQI's own reconciliation may optionally cross-check against; on SQI's unavailability, Regression Verification cannot complete and the transaction pauses per Part XVI.4's Dependency-Unavailable discipline rather than proceeding without a regression check.

19.4 With the Decision Review Kernel: CRAIF-01 sends a candidate-and-target pair for classification at Authority Acquisition and an eventual prediction-versus-outcome record to DRK's own accountability ledger at Ledger Updates; DRK's unavailability blocks Authority Acquisition entirely, per Part XVI.4, because no tier above L0 is reachable without a current classification.

19.5 With the Institutional Immune System: CRAIF-01 consumes Immune-Threat Findings carrying the confirmation-method flag from CRAIF-00 Part III.3 and sends immune-memory updates at Ledger Updates distinguishing repairs that closed a live-adversarial-confirmed threat from those that closed only a presence-confirmed one, directly feeding the closure IAS-D2's own Part XIV names as its unresolved question.

19.6 With DAIF-21 and DAIF-00: CRAIF-01 consumes Drift Findings as one Finding subtype and, per CRAIF-00 Part XI.9's closing test, is itself subject to DAIF-00's ownership-reconciliation discipline for any proposed extension to its own scope — this contract runs in both directions, CRAIF consuming DAIF-21's output as data and submitting to DAIF-00's doctrine as governance.

19.7 With `modules/rollback/*` and `owner_queue`: CRAIF-01 composes the former as its exclusive snapshot-and-restoration adapter, per Part VII.2, and the latter as its exclusive escalation channel, per every Owner Escalation this dataset produces, sending neither system a request outside their own existing interface contracts and building no competing mechanism for either responsibility, per CRAIF-00 Part XI.8.

PART XIX FINAL LAW

Every consulted system's contract names what CRAIF-01 sends, what it expects back, and what happens when the consulted system is unavailable — and unavailability is never silently treated as a favorable default answer.
