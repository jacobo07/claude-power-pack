CRAIF-00 — CONSTITUTION & CANONICAL ONTOLOGY
Closed-Loop Repair & Activation Integrity Fabric — the repair-execution axis of Claude Power Pack
Version 1. Founded 2026-07-21. Binding on every dataset in the CRAIF family.


PART I — MISSION: THE GAP THIS CONSTITUTION EXISTS TO CLOSE

1.1 This system is not another detector, and it is not another immune system, and it is not another decision authority. Claude Power Pack already has all three, and each of them is honest about what it does not do. The Sovereign Quality Intelligence family measures whether executable reality is verified and explicitly states, in its own governing index, that it measures and does not remediate. The Decision Review Kernel judges whether a proposed decision is correct, reversible, and proportional, and explicitly states that it recommends and forks nothing — it is, in its own words, files on disk and a function that reads them, no new brain, no parallel accountant. The Institutional Immune System classifies pathogens, quarantines exposure, and tracks decay, and in the Part of its own corpus devoted to auditing its own failure modes, it names as an open and explicitly unresolved question whether its confirmation step should require a live adversarial test that the deployed gate actually intercepts the pathogen it was built for, or whether a structural presence check is sufficient. That question sitting open, inside a sealed and otherwise rigorous corpus, is not a gap in that corpus's competence. It is a gap in the estate's architecture. Nothing in Claude Power Pack, before this dataset, owned the act of closing it.

1.2 The gap has a precise shape and it is not the shape of any of the three systems above. Detection answers whether something is wrong. Diagnosis and decision-review answer whether a proposed response is sound. Neither answers whether a response was actually carried out, correctly, safely, reversibly, and with independent proof that the thing it claimed to fix now works. A system can detect a hook that exists but is not registered in settings, and a decision authority can approve registering it, and neither of those steps produces a registered hook, nor proves that once registered the hook actually fires on the event it was built for. The distance between an approved repair and a verified, activated, learned-from repair is where every one of the founding incidents of this corpus actually lived, and it is a distance no existing system in the estate is chartered to cross.

1.3 The founding evidence for this gap is not hypothetical and it is not imported from outside the estate. It is the estate's own recent history, read at content tier rather than assumed from a title. Five incidents motivate this corpus, and none of them was resolved by an existing detector plus an existing decision authority acting alone; each required a human to notice that detection and approval, even both present, had not produced a working, verified capability. The first is the Activation Bootstrap Defect: the vocabulary a skill needs in order to be selected lived inside the skill itself, so selecting the skill required having already selected it, a circular dependency that no reachability scanner and no decision kernel is positioned to see, because both operate downstream of a selection that never happened. The second is Capability Reachability Debt at scale: a hundred and fifty-five modules present, syntactically correct, individually passing whatever tests they had, and reachable from no live surface the harness would ever invoke — a fact the estate's own liveness scanner now proves by construction, but proving the fact is not the same act as fixing any one of the hundred and fifty-five, and nothing before this corpus closed that second act. The third is Hook and Settings Divergence: a hook built, correct in isolation, and never registered, so that its existence created the appearance of a capability where none operated. The fourth is Cross-Repository Activation Failure: capability that worked inside the estate's own repository and nowhere else, discovered only because an Owner went looking across thirty projects and counted. The fifth is Owner Intervention Escape itself: the fact that discovering all four of the above required the Owner to ask for an audit, rather than the estate surfacing them unprompted.

1.4 Read together, the five incidents are not five bugs. They are one law wearing five costumes, and the law is this: an estate can accumulate arbitrarily sophisticated detectors, arbitrarily rigorous decision authorities, and an arbitrarily well-taxonomized immune system, and still leave every discovered defect exactly where discovery left it, because discovery and correction are different acts requiring different authorities, different evidence, and a different closing condition, and nothing forces the second act to follow the first. A detector that fires forever without a corresponding closing mechanism is not a safety net. It is a permanent, growing list of known problems that nobody is chartered to finish.

1.5 This corpus's jurisdiction is therefore narrow and specific, on purpose, because the single most common failure mode observed while designing it was the temptation to let it re-absorb the very systems it depends on. CRAIF governs the transactional execution of a repair once a Finding, a diagnosis, and an authorization already exist, produced by systems this corpus does not own and will not re-implement; and it governs the end-to-end simulation of whether a capability actually activates, a science distinct from whether a module is reachable in the abstract (which the liveness family owns) or whether a test protects a code path (which SQI owns). Everything upstream of a Repair Intent — the detection, the diagnosis, the authorization decision, the immune classification — is composed by reference. Everything this corpus adds is what happens after all of that exists and nothing has yet been executed, verified, and learned from.

1.6 The ambition, stated plainly and bounded deliberately, is not an estate with zero defects. That is unreachable and any corpus that promises it is selling theatre. The ambition is an estate in which a discovered defect that is repairable under an explicit, bounded authority is actually repaired, the repair is proven to have restored the specific capability that was broken — not merely that a file changed — and the repair leaves behind a guardian that makes the same defect harder to reintroduce. An estate that reaches that state has not eliminated failure. It has eliminated the specific failure mode in which detection accumulates and correction does not follow, which is the failure mode every one of the five founding incidents shares and the one no existing system in the estate was chartered to close.

1.7 The scope boundary must be stated with the same sharpness the mission was, because a repair authority that quietly expands into diagnosis, into decision review, or into the immune system's own classification work becomes exactly the kind of duplicated, unaccountable authority the estate's own Duplicate-to-Advantage law forbids. This corpus does not decide whether a finding is real — that is the finding's producer's job. It does not decide whether a fix is architecturally sound in the abstract — that is the Decision Review Kernel's job, consulted through the composition contract in Part VI. It does not classify a pathogen or manage quarantine — that is the Institutional Immune System's job. Its product is narrower and, because it is narrower, it is achievable: an accountable, evidenced, reversible act of execution, bounded by an authority tier it did not grant itself, ending in independent verification it did not perform on its own claim.

PART I FINAL LAW

Detection without a closing act is a permanent list, not a safety net. The distance between an approved repair and a verified, activated, learned-from repair is the one gap in Claude Power Pack no existing system was chartered to close, and it is the entirety of what this corpus governs — nothing upstream of a Repair Intent, and nothing beyond proving the intent was honestly closed.


PART II — DUPLICATE-TO-ADVANTAGE SUPREMACY DOCTRINE

2.1 Before this corpus specifies a single object or a single state machine, it must specify the order of authority it obeys, because a repair-execution system that does not explicitly subordinate itself to the estate's existing detectors and decision authorities will, by the ordinary pressure of being useful, begin re-deciding questions those authorities already own. The pressure is not hypothetical. A repair runtime that receives a Finding and can see the underlying evidence will be tempted, the first time it disagrees with the Finding's classification, to reclassify it rather than reject the intent and return it to the producer. A runtime that must choose an authority tier will be tempted, under time pressure, to reason about reversibility itself rather than consult the Decision Review Kernel's existing reversibility classifier. Both temptations produce the same institutional harm: a second, informal, unaccountable copy of an authority that already exists formally and accountably elsewhere.

2.2 The doctrine that forecloses this is composed, not invented. Claude Power Pack's Duplicate-to-Advantage family already established the governing law: no duplication ends in rejection outright, and no duplication is quietly tolerated either; every overlap resolves to reinforcement, extension, composition, shared infrastructure, or, only for the genuinely irreducible remainder, a new sovereign capability. CRAIF is itself the product of that law applied to its own founding proposal, and it must apply the same law recursively, every time it touches a producer, a diagnosis, or an authority it did not itself invent.

2.3 Operationally, the doctrine resolves into a fixed hierarchy of consultation. When CRAIF's runtime needs to know whether something is broken, it consults the Finding's producer and never re-derives the classification itself. When it needs to know how risky a proposed change is, it consults the Decision Review Kernel's reversibility and blast-radius classification and never computes its own. When it needs to know whether a pathogen pattern is already known, it consults the Institutional Immune System's registry and never grows a parallel one. When it needs to know whether a capability is reachable at all, prior to any repair, it consults the liveness family's scan and never re-implements reachability detection. Only for the narrow question none of those four systems answers — was the approved, authorized change actually executed, does the specific capability now work, and was the fact of its working proven independently — does CRAIF's own judgment govern, and even there, the independent verification is performed by composing SQI's reconciliation machinery and CRAIF-02's activation contract rather than by CRAIF inventing a fifth verification method.

2.4 The hierarchy has a strict failure behavior when a consulted authority cannot answer. If the Finding's producer cannot confirm the finding still holds at the moment of execution, the transaction halts and returns to intake rather than proceeding on stale evidence, a rule stated fully in Part IV and enforced structurally in CRAIF-01 Part III. If the Decision Review Kernel cannot classify reversibility with sufficient confidence, the repair is refused at the authority-acquisition step rather than defaulted to a permissive tier, because a system that resolves an unclear authority question in its own favor has, in that single default, appointed itself the authority it was built to consult. Absence of an answer from an existing authority is never treated as an answer of "proceed." It is treated as a stop.

2.5 The doctrine's second obligation is outward rather than inward: CRAIF must strengthen the authorities it consults, not merely consult them. Every repair transaction CRAIF completes produces evidence that its consulted authorities did not have before — a confirmed reachability restoration for the liveness ledger, a confirmed reversibility outcome for the Decision Review Kernel's own accountability ledger, a confirmed immune-memory update for the Institutional Immune System, a confirmed reality-synchronization event for DAIF-21 when the repair follows an upstream drift. None of these are CRAIF re-deciding anything. All of them are CRAIF handing its consulted authorities the one thing none of them can produce unassisted: proof, generated at the moment of execution, that their prior classification held up under an actual attempt to act on it.

2.6 This is the specific form Duplicate-to-Advantage takes for an execution-layer system, and it differs from the form it takes for a knowledge-compilation system like DAIF or a verification system like SQI. DAIF's law governs whether a proposed new capability should exist at all. SQI's law governs whether a claim about verified reality is supported. CRAIF's law governs something narrower still: whether an act of change, once approved elsewhere, is executed in a way that never quietly annexes the authority that approved it. A repair runtime is, definitionally, the part of the estate closest to actually touching production state, and it is precisely that proximity to real mutation that makes annexation both the most tempting failure mode and the most consequential one, which is why this Part exists before any object in this corpus is named.

2.7 The doctrine closes with an explicit self-application. This corpus itself was authorized to exist only after the estate's own Duplicate-to-Advantage machinery — the D2A ownership reconciliation performed at STOP #1 of this corpus's founding session — determined that four-fifths of its originally proposed scope duplicated sealed capability and refused to build it. CRAIF is not exempt from the law it enforces on its own runtime; it is the law's own most recent verified instance, and any future expansion of CRAIF's scope must clear the same reconciliation before a single new Part is authored.

2.8 A worked illustration fixes the hierarchy's operational meaning against a concrete case rather than leaving it abstract, using the third founding fixture — a hook built correctly and never registered — as the walkthrough. A Reachability Finding arrives from the liveness family naming the hook as ORPHAN. CRAIF's runtime does not, at this point, ask whether the hook is important, whether registering it is architecturally sound, or how risky the change might be; each of those questions belongs to a different consulted authority and the runtime's own opinion on any of them is inadmissible as grounds for anything that follows. It normalizes the Finding into a Repair Intent per Part III, generates a candidate whose target-state assertion is that the hook fires on its designated event, and at Authority Acquisition it consults the Decision Review Kernel, not its own instinct that a settings-file edit is trivially reversible, for a formal reversibility classification. Only once that external classification returns Tipo A or Tipo B does the ladder in Part VIII become available above L1, and only once a Rollback Contract proves a tested restoration path for a settings-file edit specifically — not for settings files in general, and not asserted from the fact that version control exists — does L3 become reachable at all. Every step in this walkthrough consulted an external authority or an external, tested mechanism; at no point did the runtime's own confidence substitute for either.

2.9 The walkthrough's value is what it makes visible about the alternative it forecloses. A repair runtime built without this doctrine, facing the identical Finding, would have every incentive to shortcut the exact same chain: the fix looks small, the risk looks low, and the temptation to reason "a settings-file edit is obviously safe" and proceed without consulting DRK's actual reversibility classifier is strongest precisely when the shortcut is least likely to be caught, because a small, successful, unauthorized shortcut produces no visible harm the first ten times it is taken. The doctrine's cost is paid on every transaction, including the nine hundred and ninety-nine that would have gone fine without it; its value is realized on the one transaction where the runtime's own confidence was wrong in a way no local reasoning could have caught, and by the time that transaction arrives, a habit of skipping consultation is already established practice rather than a single lapse.

PART II FINAL LAW

A repair runtime sits closer to real mutation than any detector or decision authority in the estate, which makes annexing their authority the most tempting failure mode available to it. Consult, never re-derive; on any unanswerable question from a consulted authority, stop rather than default to permissive; and strengthen every authority consulted with evidence it could not have produced without the repair actually being attempted.


PART III — ONTOLOGY I: THE FINDING TAXONOMY AND ITS NORMALIZATION INTO REPAIR INTENT

3.1 Every object this corpus defines exists to solve one recurring problem: the estate's detectors do not speak one language. The liveness family emits reachability rows keyed by module unit and evidence string. SQI emits reconciliation verdicts keyed by test file and coverage delta. The Institutional Immune System emits pathogen records keyed by fingerprint and lineage. Left alone, a repair runtime that wanted to act on all three would need three separate intake pipelines, three separate confidence models, and three separate paths to the same authority ladder — and the moment a fourth detector is added, a fourth pipeline. The Finding is the object that ends this proliferation: a single canonical shape every producer emits into, regardless of what internal vocabulary produced it.

3.2 A Finding carries a fixed set of fields regardless of its subtype: a producer identity naming the system that emitted it, a target identity naming the specific reality object affected (a module path, a hook name, a settings key, an activation chain segment), an evidence payload in whatever native form the producer holds it, a confidence value on the producer's own scale, a timestamp, and a subtype tag drawn from a closed set. The subtype tag is the only field CRAIF interprets structurally; every other field is opaque payload CRAIF carries forward without modification, because reinterpreting a producer's native evidence is exactly the annexation Part II forbids.

3.3 Four subtypes are named at this corpus's founding, one per major producer family observed at content tier this session. The Reachability Finding is emitted by the liveness family and asserts that a named module, hook, or capability is ORPHAN, DRIFTED, or ORPHANED-live per that family's own verdict vocabulary, carrying the `via` evidence string liveness already produces. The Quality Finding is emitted by SQI and asserts a reconciliation gap — a test file outside the canonical invocation, a coverage regression, a certification whose environment has since changed — carrying SQI's own admissibility tier for the evidence. The Drift Finding is emitted by DAIF-21 or an equivalent reality-synchronization producer and asserts that a local assumption no longer matches an observed upstream or on-disk reality. The Immune-Threat Finding is emitted by the Institutional Immune System and asserts a confirmed pathogen exposure that has passed IAS-D2's own confirmation step — with an explicit carried flag noting whether that confirmation was a structural presence check or a live adversarial test, because Part XIV of that corpus itself marks the distinction as consequential and unresolved, and CRAIF's own evidence-admissibility rule in Part XV treats the two differently rather than silently averaging them.

3.4 The set is closed but not frozen. A fifth subtype may be added only when a fifth producer family is verified, at content tier, to emit findings no existing subtype's fields can honestly carry — the same discipline Part II applies to authority consultation applies here to ontology extension: absence of an existing fit is not license to force a new Finding into an old subtype's shape, nor is convenience license to invent a subtype for a producer that does not yet exist. Two subtypes collapsing into one, when experience shows their fields never actually diverge, is an equally valid outcome and is preferred when it is honestly available, per the same minimum-fix discipline this corpus's own founding session applied when it declined to build a fourth sovereign dataset for a metric layer that did not independently justify one.

3.5 A Finding is not a Repair Intent, and the distance between the two is deliberate and load-bearing. A Finding is a claim from a single producer, at a single moment, about a single target. A Repair Intent is what results after that Finding survives intake validation — restated in Part IV — and is judged, by the runtime, to describe a plausibly repairable state rather than a permanent condition, an intentional design choice, or a finding whose target no longer exists. The normalization step that converts a Finding into a Repair Intent is the runtime's first and narrowest act of judgment, and it is bounded severely: the runtime may reject a Finding as not-currently-repairable, or accept it and open a Repair Intent, but it may never edit the Finding's evidence, reclassify its subtype, or override its producer's confidence value. Rejection carries a reason code and returns to the producer; it is never silent.

3.6 The normalization step also enforces staleness discipline structurally rather than by convention. Every Finding carries the timestamp of its own production, and no Repair Intent may be opened from a Finding whose age exceeds a threshold defined per-subtype by the producer's own refresh cadence — the liveness ledger's daily cadence sets one threshold, SQI's per-invocation cadence sets another. A Finding presented past its threshold is neither accepted nor silently repaired against; it triggers a re-scan request back to the producer, because acting on stale evidence to close an incident is the single fastest way to manufacture a Repair Certificate for a problem that either no longer exists or has changed shape since it was last observed.

3.7 One subtlety deserves explicit statement because it was nearly missed during this corpus's own design: a single underlying defect frequently produces Findings from more than one producer simultaneously. A hook that is present but unregistered may surface as a Reachability Finding from liveness and, if its absence from settings also breaks a test that asserted its registration, as a Quality Finding from SQI in the same audit cycle. CRAIF does not treat these as two Repair Intents to be executed independently, which would create exactly the concurrent-repair collision named as a hard failure mode in CRAIF-01 Part XVI. Intake performs identity-based deduplication across producers, keyed on target identity, before opening any Repair Intent, and a Repair Intent so formed carries every contributing Finding rather than an arbitrarily chosen first one — a design decision that matters for evidence completeness at Verification time, when the runtime must prove every contributing symptom, not merely the first one noticed, has been resolved.

3.8 A specific edge case in deduplication deserves its own treatment because it was nearly generalized incorrectly during this corpus's own design: two producers may name the same target identity while asserting genuinely different, non-overlapping conditions rather than the same underlying defect observed twice. A Reachability Finding asserting that a module is ORPHAN and a Quality Finding asserting that the same module's associated test is flaky are not automatically the same Repair Intent merely because their target identities collide at the module level; intake's deduplication key is the target identity conjoined with the asserted condition class, not the target identity alone, precisely so that two producers reporting two different problems about one object are not merged into a single Repair Intent whose candidate can satisfy one contributing Finding while leaving the other's condition completely unaddressed, which would itself manufacture the false-healing failure Part XIV names for the runtime's certification step.

3.9 The distinction matters operationally because a Repair Certificate issued against a merged-but-mismatched Intent would carry the outward appearance of having closed both Findings while its Verification Contract, keyed to only one condition class, never actually checked the other. Intake's obligation, stated plainly, is to merge only what is genuinely one condition observed by more than one producer, and to open separate, explicitly cross-referenced Repair Intents — sharing a target identity but distinct condition classes — for everything else, with the cross-reference itself recorded so that a human or an audit later reviewing one Intent can see that a sibling Intent exists against the same target rather than discovering the fact by coincidence.

PART III FINAL LAW

A Finding is a producer's claim, untouched; a Repair Intent is the runtime's narrow judgment that the claim is current, plausibly repairable, and — when more than one producer independently observed the same target — completely gathered. The runtime may reject or accept; it may never edit, reclassify, or silently act past a staleness threshold, because the fastest way to manufacture a false Repair Certificate is to repair against evidence that has already gone stale.


PART IV — ONTOLOGY II: REPAIR INTENT AND REPAIR CANDIDATE

4.1 A Repair Intent is the object CRAIF-01's runtime actually operates on, and it is deliberately heavier than the Finding or Findings it was normalized from, because everything downstream — authority acquisition, snapshot, sandbox execution, verification — must be able to reconstruct why the transaction exists without re-consulting the original producers mid-flight. A Repair Intent carries an identity distinct from any contributing Finding's identity, the full set of contributing Findings unmodified, a target reality object description precise enough that a snapshot can be taken of exactly that object and nothing wider, a plausibility judgment recorded at open time, and a state field drawn from a small closed vocabulary: OPENED, AUTHORITY-PENDING, AUTHORITY-DENIED, CANDIDATE-PENDING, EXECUTING, VERIFYING, CLOSED-REPAIRED, CLOSED-ROLLED-BACK, CLOSED-STALE, CLOSED-REJECTED.

4.2 The state vocabulary is exhaustive by design, and CLOSED-STALE deserves particular attention because it is the state most tempting to skip. A Repair Intent that sat in AUTHORITY-PENDING long enough that its contributing Findings' staleness thresholds lapsed before authority was ever acquired must transition to CLOSED-STALE and re-enter intake as a fresh Finding-normalization cycle rather than being carried forward on its original evidence. The alternative — quietly refreshing the staleness clock without re-consulting the producer — is the same false-currency failure Part III's staleness rule exists to prevent, relocated one step downstream where it is easier to miss because the Intent already exists and deleting it feels like discarding work rather than protecting evidence integrity.

4.3 A Repair Candidate is a distinct object from the Repair Intent that names it, and the distinction matters because more than one candidate may be proposed for a single intent, and the selection between them is itself an auditable act rather than an implicit one. A candidate carries a description of the specific change it proposes — expressed as a target-state assertion (what should be true of the reality object after the change, not a procedural script of how to get there, because CRAIF-00 through CRAIF-02 contain zero executable code and a candidate's description obeys the same discipline), a generation source noting whether the candidate came from a deterministic rule the producer itself proposed, a template matched from a prior successful repair of the same Failure Pattern, or a first-of-its-kind proposal requiring the widest evidence burden, and an estimated blast radius drafted by the candidate's generator but not yet authoritative until Part V's Scope and Blast-Radius Record is produced by consulting the Decision Review Kernel.

4.4 Candidate generation is bounded by an explicit refusal condition that exists because of a documented failure mode named later in Part XIV: a candidate whose target-state assertion cannot be falsified by any verification method available to the estate must not be generated at all, because an unfalsifiable candidate cannot produce a Repair Certificate that means anything — it can only produce a certificate that asserts success by definition. A candidate proposing to "improve activation reliability" without naming which specific activation-chain segment must now reach a specific, checkable state is refused at generation time, before it ever reaches selection, and the refusal is recorded rather than silently dropped, because a repair space with no falsifiable candidate is itself diagnostic information — it usually means the underlying Finding was under-diagnosed and belongs back with its producer, not with a repair runtime asked to guess at a fix.

4.5 Selection among multiple falsifiable candidates for the same intent follows a fixed preference order rather than an open-ended cost-benefit calculation performed fresh each time, because an open-ended calculation is exactly the kind of judgment call Part II reserves for the Decision Review Kernel rather than for CRAIF's own candidate selector. The preference order is: a template matched from a Guardian Promotion Candidate already confirmed effective against the identical Failure Pattern is preferred over a fresh proposal: it carries verified prior evidence a fresh proposal cannot. Among fresh proposals, the one with the narrower estimated blast radius is preferred, all else equal, because Part IX's separation of powers means the executing role does not get to prefer boldness over caution on its own authority. A tie broken on any other basis — speed of generation, elegance, generality — is a tie broken wrongly, and CRAIF-01 Part V states the operational selector that enforces this ordering as code-free procedure.

4.6 One design tension was deliberately left unresolved rather than papered over, because CRAIF-00 Part XIV requires every unresolved tension to be named rather than hidden behind an implied default. A candidate generated as a template match inherits its blast-radius estimate from the prior confirmed instance, but the current target is never byte-identical to the prior one — file contents drift, settings diverge, a dependency version changes between the two occurrences. Whether a template match's inherited blast-radius estimate should be trusted as-is or must always be independently re-estimated for the current target is not settled by this corpus; CRAIF-01 Part V requires re-estimation as the conservative default, and any future evidence that re-estimation adds cost without catching a real divergence would be the trigger to revisit the requirement, never to quietly relax it first and gather the evidence after.

4.7 A worked instance grounds the refusal condition against the second founding fixture, capability reachability debt at scale. A candidate generated against an ORPHAN Reachability Finding whose target-state assertion reads simply "the module becomes reachable" is refused at generation, not because the sentiment is wrong but because it names no method by which reachability would be checked — `modules/liveness/reachability.py`'s own scan requires a specific live surface to reference the module, transitively or directly, and a candidate that does not name which surface it proposes to create that reference through cannot be falsified by anything except a hope that the next scan happens to come back REACHABLE. The corrected candidate instead asserts a specific claim: that the module will be imported from a named seed surface — a hook, a command definition, a tool invocation another live surface already calls — and that `modules/liveness/reachability.py`'s next scan will report REACHABLE with a `via` value naming that specific surface. The corrected assertion is falsifiable in one command; the uncorrected one is falsifiable in none.

4.8 The distinction generalizes beyond this one fixture: a target-state assertion is falsifiable only when it names both the property to be checked and the specific instrument that will check it, because a property alone, absent an instrument, invites exactly the kind of after-the-fact rationalization Part XV's evidence discipline forbids — a runtime that already knows what verification method will be applied cannot quietly redefine success once execution is underway, while a runtime that deferred naming the instrument retains exactly that freedom, and the freedom to redefine success after acting is indistinguishable, from the outside, from the freedom to fail quietly.

PART IV FINAL LAW

A Repair Candidate states what should be true after the change, never how to get there, and a candidate whose target state cannot be falsified by an available verification method must be refused at generation, because an unfalsifiable candidate can only produce a certificate that succeeds by definition. Selection among falsifiable candidates prefers verified templates over fresh proposals and narrower blast radius over wider, in that order, and a tie broken on any other basis is a tie broken wrongly.


PART V — ONTOLOGY III: AUTHORITY OBJECTS

5.1 The Repair Authority Decision is the single object through which CRAIF-01's runtime is permitted to know what it may do, and its existence as a distinct, recorded object rather than an inline check is the mechanism that makes Part IX's separation of powers auditable after the fact rather than merely true in the moment. A Repair Authority Decision names the Repair Intent and selected Candidate it governs, the authority tier from CRAIF-00 Part VIII's ladder that the decision grants, the specific consulted evidence that justified that tier — a Decision Review Kernel reversibility classification, an owner_queue escalation record, or, at the highest tiers, an explicit standing Owner policy — an expiry, because no authority grant is permanent, and a revocation condition under which the grant lapses before its expiry if new evidence contradicts the grounds it was issued on.

5.2 Preconditions are a distinct object from the authority grant itself because a tier being available in principle does not mean every precondition for exercising it has been met for this specific transaction. A Preconditions record enumerates, per authority tier, the specific facts that must independently hold: for a reversible-auto tier, that a working rollback path has actually been proven for this class of target, not merely asserted as a general capability of the estate's rollback adapter; for any tier above observe-only, that the target's ownership is unambiguous rather than contested between two systems; for every tier, that the Repair Intent's contributing Findings have not lapsed past their staleness threshold between authority acquisition and the moment execution actually begins, because time passes between the two and a check performed only once, at grant time, misses drift that occurs during the gap.

5.3 The Scope and Blast-Radius Record formalizes, for a specific candidate against a specific target, the estimate that Part IV's candidate description drafted provisionally. It is produced by consulting the Decision Review Kernel's reversibility and blast-radius classifier rather than computed independently, per Part II's consultation hierarchy, and it names the specific reality objects the change touches directly, the objects it touches transitively through a dependency the runtime is aware of, and — critically — an explicit acknowledgment of what the estimate cannot rule out, because a blast-radius estimate that claims completeness is a stronger claim than any estimation method in the estate can currently support, and CRAIF-00 Part XV's evidence-admissibility rule refuses to treat an unqualified blast-radius claim as strong evidence regardless of how the classifier that produced it is normally regarded.

5.4 A specific failure this record exists to prevent is IAS-D2's own named Imperial Boundary Creep, relocated to the repair-execution layer: a Scope and Blast-Radius Record that names an entity outside the estate's boundary — a third-party dependency, an external repository, the model provider itself — as within scope for direct action. CRAIF's own record inherits IAS-D2's prohibition explicitly: a repair whose blast radius analysis surfaces an external carrier may only scope the estate's own exposure to that carrier as the repair target, never the carrier itself, over which CRAIF holds no authority and for which no scope record can create jurisdiction that does not otherwise exist.

5.5 These three objects — Authority Decision, Preconditions, Scope and Blast-Radius Record — are versioned independently and referenced by identity from the Repair Intent that requires them, rather than embedded inline, for a specific and previously observed reason: an inline authority check that lives only inside the transaction's own working state disappears when the transaction closes, and a later audit asking "under what authority was this repair performed, and did the Owner ever see the grounds" has nothing left to consult. A versioned, identity-referenced object survives the transaction that consumed it, and it is this survival — not the check performed in the moment — that makes CRAIF's authority model auditable rather than merely enforced.

5.6 One boundary condition is stated explicitly because it is the single most consequential failure this Part exists to prevent structurally rather than by discipline alone: no Repair Authority Decision may name, as its consulted evidence, a decision produced by CRAIF's own runtime. The consulted evidence must originate from an external authority — the Decision Review Kernel, an owner_queue record, an explicit standing Owner policy predating the transaction — and an Authority Decision whose evidence field resolves back to CRAIF's own prior output is malformed by construction and must be rejected by the object's own validation, not merely discouraged by convention, because a runtime is not permitted to author the evidence that authorizes itself, under any tier, under any circumstance, ever.

5.7 A boundary case in ownership ambiguity is worth naming explicitly because Preconditions' unambiguous-ownership requirement is easy to satisfy too loosely if left unstated: a target reality object may have a clear technical owner — the module belongs unambiguously to one package, the hook to one plugin — while the Finding concerning it originates from a producer whose own jurisdiction over that object is contested, as when a Drift Finding from DAIF-21 concerns a reality object that CPP-IAS's D2 dataset also monitors for pathogen exposure. Ownership-of-target and ownership-of-finding-jurisdiction are independent facts, and Preconditions requires both to be unambiguous before any tier above L1 is available, because a repair proceeding on a Finding whose producing authority itself contests jurisdiction over the observation is exposed to acting on a diagnosis two systems disagree about, even when the object being changed is uncontested.

5.8 The Authority Decision's expiry deserves a worked distinction from the Repair Certificate's revocation conditions in Part VI, because the two mechanisms are easy to conflate and govern different moments. An Authority Decision's expiry bounds how long a grant remains valid for initiating or continuing a transaction — a decision granted at ten in the morning under evidence that goes stale by evening should not authorize a transaction that stalls in a queue overnight and resumes the next day against evidence nobody re-checked. A Repair Certificate's revocation, by contrast, concerns a transaction that already completed and closed; it un-certifies a claim about a finished repair when the world underneath that claim later changes. An expired Authority Decision blocks a transaction from proceeding at all; a revoked Certificate retroactively withdraws confidence in one that already finished. Confusing the two — for instance, treating a certificate's later revocation as evidence that the original authority grant was invalid at the time it was issued — would corrupt the accountability ledger DRK consumes from CRAIF-01's outcomes, because the grant's validity at issue-time and the repair's durability over time are independent claims that must be tracked independently.

5.9 A last clarification concerns what "consulting" the Decision Review Kernel means operationally, because the word could be misread as a one-time API call whose result is cached indefinitely. Consultation for a specific Scope and Blast-Radius Record is scoped to that record's own validity window, tied to the same staleness discipline Part III establishes for Findings — a reversibility classification obtained days before a transaction finally reaches Authority Acquisition, because it queued behind higher-priority work, is not assumed still current; it is re-confirmed, and a changed classification supersedes the cached one immediately, because DRK's own classifier can and does revise a reversibility judgment as new evidence about a target class accumulates, and an Authority Decision built on a superseded classification is built on evidence Part II's consultation hierarchy no longer recognizes as current.

PART V FINAL LAW

An authority grant, its preconditions, and its blast-radius estimate are versioned objects that outlive the transaction they governed, because a check that exists only in working memory leaves no trace for a later audit to consult. No Authority Decision may cite CRAIF's own prior output as its grounds; the evidence must originate outside the runtime it authorizes, always, without exception.


PART VI — ONTOLOGY IV: VERIFICATION AND OUTCOME OBJECTS

6.1 The Verification Contract is drafted before execution, not after, and this ordering is the single detail that separates genuine verification from the structural-presence-check failure IAS-D2's own Part XIV names as its unresolved question. A contract drafted after a candidate has already been applied can be, consciously or not, shaped to match whatever the applied change actually did. A contract drafted before execution, against the target-state assertion the candidate proposed in Part IV, states in advance what would have to be independently true for the repair to count as successful, and the same contract is then applied unchanged regardless of what execution produces, exactly the discipline SQI's own reconciliation machinery already enforces for test-reach claims and which CRAIF composes rather than reinvents.

6.2 A Verification Contract names, for the general case, the specific reality object and target state the candidate asserted, the verification method the estate already owns that can check that state — an SQI reconciliation run, a liveness reachability re-scan, a settings-consistency check — and, where the underlying Finding concerned an activation-chain segment rather than a static reachability or test-coverage fact, an Activation Verification Contract, defined fully in CRAIF-02 Part XII, is required in addition to any static check, because a hook that is now present and registered has satisfied a static Verification Contract without yet proving it fires on the runtime event it exists to handle, and CRAIF's founding evidence — IAS-D2's own admitted gap between presence and demonstrated interception — is precisely the case in which a static contract alone would repeat the failure this corpus exists to close.

6.3 The Rollback Contract is symmetric to the Verification Contract and equally mandatory: no Repair Candidate may proceed past Snapshot in CRAIF-01 Part VII without a Rollback Contract naming the specific, tested mechanism by which the snapshot state can be restored, the adapter it composes — `modules/rollback/*` for a target class that mechanism already covers, or an explicit gap flagged and escalated to owner_queue when no covering adapter exists yet — and the expected time and resource cost of executing that restoration. A candidate whose target class has no provable rollback mechanism is refused at Preconditions per Part V, and this refusal has no exception carved for high-confidence candidates, because confidence in a fix and provability of its reversal are independent properties and the entire authority ladder in Part VIII is keyed to the second, not the first.

6.4 A Repair Outcome is the object produced when a transaction reaches its Commit-or-Rollback decision, and it is written exactly once, immutably, regardless of which branch the decision takes: it records the Verification Contract's result in full — not a summary, per the same discipline SQI's own constitution names as the specific corruption that defeats an evidence article while appearing to comply with it — the Rollback Contract's result if rollback was exercised, the total elapsed time, and every side effect observed during sandbox and canary execution that the transaction's Side-Effect Ledger, detailed in CRAIF-01 Part XIII, accumulated along the way.

6.5 A Repair Certificate is issued only from a Repair Outcome whose Verification Contract resolved to a positive result under every check it specified, including the Activation Verification Contract where one was required, and a certificate carries an explicit expiry and an explicit set of conditions under which it is automatically subject to revocation — a dependency the repair touched later changes, the target's environment is later reclassified, or a subsequent, unrelated Finding on an overlapping target casts the original verification's completeness into doubt. A certificate with no revocation path is a claim of permanence no verification method in the estate can actually support, and CRAIF-00 Part XV's admissibility rule treats an unrevocable certificate the same way SQI treats an unqualified test-pass: as a claim stronger than its evidence, refused at the object-validation level rather than merely discouraged.

6.6 A Failed Repair Record is produced whenever a transaction reaches Commit-or-Rollback with a negative Verification Contract result, and it is not a lesser or optional cousin of the Repair Certificate — it is evidence of equal institutional value, because a candidate that failed under a specific verification method, for a specific target class, is exactly the negative fixture Part XII and CRAIF-02 Part XIII need to prevent the same unfalsifiable or under-scoped candidate from being regenerated and re-attempted against a superficially similar future Finding. A Failed Repair Record that is written and never consulted by future candidate generation is the same institutional waste CEPS's own Learning Closure gap names for captured-but-unconsumed incidents, and CRAIF-01 Part XIV's learning handoff exists specifically to prevent that waste from recurring inside a system built to prevent exactly this class of waste elsewhere.

6.7 A worked contrast between a satisfied and an unsatisfied Verification Contract, both against the identical candidate, illustrates why the contract's independence from execution's self-report is load-bearing rather than procedural decoration. Suppose a candidate proposes registering the third fixture's unregistered hook. Execution reports, honestly, that the settings file now contains the expected entry — this report is true and is not in dispute. A Verification Contract satisfied by that report alone would issue a Repair Certificate on the strength of a fact the Finding's producer could have observed without CRAIF ever running a transaction, because a settings-file entry existing is exactly the presence check IAS-D2's own Part XIV names as insufficient. The contract this corpus requires instead specifies, in advance of execution, that the hook must fire and produce its designated side effect under a real or faithfully simulated invocation of the event it is registered against — a check CRAIF-02's Activation Verification Contract performs, consuming CRAIF-01's execution as an input but never as its evidence.

6.8 The two paths diverge concretely at exactly one place: the moment the settings entry exists but the hook still fails to fire, because the hook's own internal logic has an unrelated defect the registration alone does not fix. A self-report-satisfied contract would certify this transaction as successful, because execution's report — "the entry now exists" — remains true. The independent contract this Part requires would report a failed Verification Contract despite a technically-accurate execution report, correctly producing a Failed Repair Record rather than a false certificate, and it is precisely this divergence, invisible until the specific failure occurs, that justifies requiring independent re-observation on every transaction rather than only on the ones that seem, in advance, most likely to need it.

6.9 A last object-boundary clarification concerns the relationship between a Repair Outcome and a Repair Certificate, because the two are sometimes conflated as a single output when they serve distinct audiences. The Outcome is the complete, unabridged record of what happened — every side effect, every intermediate result, the full Verification Contract output — and it is retained regardless of the transaction's success, because a failed transaction's Outcome is exactly the input Part VI.6's Failed Repair Record and Part XIV's failure-mode catalogue both depend on. The Certificate is a narrower, downstream artifact derived from a successful Outcome specifically for consumers who need a compact, expiring claim of verified repair rather than the full evidentiary record — a Guardian Promotion Candidate's consumer, for instance, needs to know that a certificate exists and remains unrevoked, not to re-parse the full Outcome every time it checks. Keeping the two objects distinct, rather than collapsing the Certificate into a summary field on the Outcome, is what allows the Certificate's own revocation in Part VII.6 to operate without mutating the Outcome's own immutable historical record.

PART VI FINAL LAW

A Verification Contract is drafted before execution and applied unchanged after, or it is not verification — it is the structural-presence-check failure this corpus exists to end, relocated rather than closed. A Repair Certificate with no revocation path claims a permanence no evidence can support and must be refused at validation; a Failed Repair Record is not a lesser cousin of a Certificate but the same class of institutional asset, and one that is written but never consulted is waste of the exact kind this corpus was built to eliminate.


PART VII — ONTOLOGY V: LEARNING OBJECTS

7.1 A Learning Candidate is the object that carries a completed transaction's institutional residue forward, and its existence is what separates a repair that merely closes one incident from a repair that leaves the estate measurably harder to fool by the same defect twice. Not every closed Repair Intent produces a Learning Candidate of equal weight — a routine, previously-templated repair against a well-understood Failure Pattern produces a thin one, mostly a recurrence-count increment — but every closed intent, repaired or failed, produces one, because CRAIF-00 Part I's mission is specifically the class of failure in which a defect was found, fixed once, and never made the estate any harder to re-break the same way.

7.2 A Learning Candidate names the Failure Pattern it instantiates, drawn from the estate's own existing taxonomy where one already fits — CEPS's error categories, the Institutional Immune System's pathogen classes — rather than a fresh taxonomy CRAIF invents for itself, per the same consultation discipline Part II established for authority and diagnosis; a recurrence count if the pattern has been seen before; the Repair Certificate or Failed Repair Record it derives from; and a promotion recommendation, which is advisory only, because whether a local observation becomes global institutional memory is a governed decision this corpus does not make unilaterally.

7.3 The Guardian Promotion Candidate is the specific Learning Candidate subtype that recommends converting a repeatedly-observed, repeatedly-repaired Failure Pattern into a standing detector or a standing prevention rule, and its promotion path is deliberately the same local-to-global pipeline the estate's own immune system and hard-rules compiler already use: local observation, then local recurrence within a single project, then cross-project validation before any promotion to a global guardian is even proposed, and even then the promotion itself is proposed to the relevant existing authority — the hard-rules compiler for a Hard Rule, the immune registry for an antibody — never self-installed by CRAIF's own runtime, because a repair runtime that can unilaterally install its own permanent detectors has, in that single act, acquired exactly the self-expanding authority Part II and CRAIF-00's third constitutional law exist to forbid.

7.4 An Owner Escalation is produced whenever a transaction reaches a state no lower authority can resolve: an L0 observe-only Finding whose repairability judgment is itself contested, a Preconditions failure with no available remediation path, a Rollback Contract gap for a target class the estate's rollback adapter does not yet cover, or a Failed Repair Record whose failure mode does not map to any existing Failure Pattern and therefore cannot even be classified with confidence. An escalation composes owner_queue's existing mechanism rather than building a parallel queue, and it carries every object accumulated so far in the transaction, so the Owner reviewing it is not asked to reconstruct context CRAIF already holds.

7.5 An Incident Closure is the object that formally ends a Repair Intent's lifecycle regardless of which terminal state it reached, and it is the object CRAIF-01's ledger updates in Part XIII actually write to the estate's shared ledgers — the liveness registry, the immune-memory registry, the Decision Review Kernel's accountability ledger — because those ledgers should not need to parse CRAIF's own internal object graph to learn that a repair concluded; they consume one closure record per intent, in their own native shape, adapted at the integration boundary named in Part XVIII of CRAIF-01, never by requiring the consuming system to understand CRAIF's ontology directly.

7.6 A Certification Revocation is issued against a previously-issued Repair Certificate when one of its own recorded revocation conditions triggers, and the object exists precisely because SQI's own corpus already demonstrates, for test certification, that a certificate issued honestly at one moment can become false without any new defect being introduced — the environment around it changed. CRAIF's certificates inherit the identical exposure, and a revocation is not a failure of the original verification; it is evidence that verification is a claim bounded by the conditions under which it was made, restated here for repair certificates exactly as SQI states it for test certificates, because the same law does not become less true for having been proven once already in a neighboring system.

7.7 The thinness gradient named in 7.1 deserves a concrete floor rather than being left to intuition, because "thin" and "thick" are otherwise judgment calls a runtime under time pressure will always resolve toward thinner. The floor: even the thinnest Learning Candidate, for a routine templated repair against a well-understood Failure Pattern, must still name the Failure Pattern it instantiates, increment that pattern's recurrence count, and record whether this specific instance's Verification Contract passed on its first attempt or required a fallback candidate — because a recurrence count that does not distinguish first-attempt successes from repairs that needed a second candidate silently discards exactly the signal Part XIII's metrics need to detect a Failure Pattern whose templated fix is degrading in reliability over time, one first-attempt-failure at a time, long before any single failure looks alarming on its own.

7.8 A specific institutional failure this Part exists to prevent, distinct from Part VI's Failed-Repair-Record waste, is Learning Candidate accumulation without consumption — a Learning Candidate faithfully produced at every Incident Closure but never actually read by the registry it targets, the exact producer-without-consumer pattern the estate's own PM-03 findings bus already demonstrates in a different subsystem. A Learning Candidate is not institutional memory merely by existing on disk; it becomes institutional memory only when its target registry's own liveness is confirmed — a liveness check this corpus explicitly does not perform itself, per Part XI's non-duplication contract, but instead consumes from the estate's existing liveness probes, closing the loop rather than assuming, from a Learning Candidate's mere production, that learning has occurred.

7.9 A worked distinction between a Learning Candidate and a Guardian Promotion Candidate clarifies why the two are not merely different names for the same object at different maturity levels. A Learning Candidate is produced at every Incident Closure, thin or thick, and its scope is local to the single transaction that produced it — it says "this instance of this Failure Pattern was observed and this is what happened." A Guardian Promotion Candidate is produced only when a Failure Pattern's recurrence count, tracked across many Learning Candidates over time, crosses a threshold the estate's own promotion pipeline sets, and its scope is the pattern class, not any single instance — it says "this class of defect recurs often enough, across enough distinct occurrences, that a standing detector or prevention rule is warranted." Conflating the two would mean either promoting from a single observation, which Part 14.6's local-to-global promotion error names as a failure mode directly, or failing to ever promote because no single Learning Candidate was ever framed as pattern-level evidence, both of which the explicit two-object distinction forecloses.

7.10 The learning objects collectively close a loop this corpus's own founding evidence shows was open in at least one sibling system: CEPS's own capture-without-consumption gap, in which incidents are logged faithfully but never read by anything downstream. CRAIF's Incident Closure, routed to the estate's shared ledgers per 7.5, and its Learning Candidate, targeted at a specific registry per 7.2, are each required to name their consumer at production time — not merely their producer — precisely because an object with no named consumer is, per the estate's own PM-03 bus precedent, indistinguishable from an object that was never produced at all, from the perspective of anyone downstream who might have benefited from it.

PART VII FINAL LAW

Institutional learning is not a side effect of closing an incident; it is a required output of it, thin or thick depending on novelty, but never absent. Promotion of a local pattern to a global guardian follows the estate's existing local-recurrence-then-cross-project-validation pipeline and is proposed to the authority that owns it, never self-installed — a repair runtime that can install its own permanent detectors has acquired the one authority no tier of this corpus's ladder may ever grant it.


PART VIII — THE REPAIR AUTHORITY LADDER L0–L4

8.1 The ladder is the mechanism through which CRAIF-01's runtime is permitted to act at all, and its five tiers are ordered by exactly one variable: how much of the transaction's risk the runtime is trusted to carry without a human or an external authority re-confirming each step. The ladder does not grade candidates by how likely they are to succeed — a low-risk, high-confidence candidate targeting an ambiguously-owned reality object sits no higher than L0, because tier is a function of what can go wrong and how cheaply it can be undone, never a function of how right the runtime believes itself to be.

8.2 L0, Observe-Only, is the mandatory floor for any Repair Intent whose target ownership is ambiguous between two systems, whose contributing Finding touches security or secrets, whose candidate would alter constitutional doctrine or governance text, whose blast radius includes a destructive or irreversible action, or whose Rollback Contract cannot be provably tested in advance. At L0 the runtime may produce every object through the Scope and Blast-Radius Record, but it may not proceed to Snapshot; the transaction terminates in an Owner Escalation carrying full diagnostic context, and the floor applies regardless of how thin or how confident the candidate looks, because confidence is precisely the variable Part 8.1 excludes from the tier calculation.

8.3 L1, Auto-Propose, permits the runtime to generate and record a full Repair Candidate, Scope and Blast-Radius Record, and draft Verification and Rollback Contracts, and to present the complete package to the Owner or to an external decision authority for approval — but it may not mutate any canonical state, sandboxed or otherwise. L1 is the default tier for any Repair Intent whose Preconditions have not yet been proven satisfied, and it is also the tier every higher-tier transaction passes through conceptually even when it does not pause there, because the objects L1 requires are the same objects Parts IV through VI define regardless of which tier ultimately executes them.

8.4 L2, Auto-Repair in Sandbox, permits execution against an isolated copy of the target — the snapshot defined in CRAIF-01 Part VII — with the full Verification Contract run against the sandboxed result, but explicitly forbids any write to canonical state. L2 exists because a Verification Contract's result against a sandboxed execution is itself valuable evidence even when canonical mutation is not yet authorized: a candidate that fails verification in sandbox has been refuted cheaply, before any real state was touched, and a Failed Repair Record produced at L2 carries exactly the same institutional weight per Part VI as one produced at a higher tier.

8.5 L3, Auto-Repair Reversible, is the first tier permitted to mutate canonical state without a human confirming the specific transaction, and it is available only when every one of a fixed conjunction holds: ownership is unambiguous, the Rollback Contract names a mechanism proven for this specific target class rather than asserted generically, the Decision Review Kernel's reversibility classification for this candidate is Tipo A or Tipo B rather than Tipo C, the Verification Contract — including any required Activation Verification Contract — is fully deterministic rather than dependent on non-reproducible external state, and observability sufficient to detect a failed or partial application exists and is active. Any one of these failing to hold drops the transaction to L2 regardless of how many of the others are satisfied, because the conjunction is a floor, not a weighted average, and a ladder that let four strong preconditions compensate for one absent one would have quietly redefined L3's guarantee into something weaker than what this Part states.

8.6 L4, Autonomous Production Repair, is not enabled by this corpus at its founding, and this is stated as an explicit constitutional fact rather than an oversight to be filled in later: L4 requires a standing, explicit Owner policy naming the specific Failure Pattern classes it covers, an evidence threshold measured in confirmed L3 repairs of the identical pattern across multiple occurrences, a bounded authorization with its own expiry independent of any single transaction's expiry, continuous independent monitoring separate from the runtime's own self-report, automatic demotion back to L3 the moment monitoring detects an anomaly, and an emergency stop reachable by any Owner action that halts every L4-tier transaction in flight immediately. No Failure Pattern is pre-approved for L4 by this dataset; the tier exists in the ladder's structure so that a future, evidence-backed policy has somewhere defined to attach, not because any repair class has yet earned it.

8.7 Demotion is unconditional and asymmetric with promotion by design: a transaction or a Failure Pattern class may be demoted from any tier to any lower tier the instant a single piece of contradicting evidence arrives — a rollback that failed once, a verification that a canary run later contradicted, a false-healing instance discovered downstream — and demotion requires no external approval, because refusing trust is always safe to do unilaterally while extending it never is. Promotion, symmetrically, requires the specific evidence threshold named at each tier and is never automatic, never inferred from the mere passage of time, and never granted by the runtime to itself, which is the ladder's structural enforcement of CRAIF-00's third constitutional law stated fully in Part X.

8.8 A worked comparison between two superficially similar Repair Intents illustrates why tier is genuinely a function of the conjunction in 8.5 rather than of any single dominant factor. The first concerns the third founding fixture's unregistered hook, targeting a settings key with a proven rollback mechanism, unambiguous ownership, a Tipo A reversibility classification, a fully deterministic Verification Contract, and active observability — every element of the L3 conjunction holds, and the transaction is eligible for L3. The second concerns a different unregistered hook whose Rollback Contract can restore the settings file but cannot independently verify that restoring the file also un-does a side effect the hook's registration triggered on first load — the rollback mechanism restores the declared state but leaves an uncertain question about whether a side effect it can't observe has already occurred and is not itself reversed by the settings restoration alone. This second transaction fails the L3 conjunction on exactly one element — the Rollback Contract's proof is incomplete for this specific target's full effect, not merely for its settings entry — and it drops to L2 despite being, in every other visible respect, identical to the first. A ladder that let the four satisfied elements outvote the one incomplete element would have certified a rollback that cannot actually undo everything the repair changed, which is precisely the failure Part 8.5's conjunction-not-average framing exists to prevent.

8.9 The ladder's tiers are also not selected once per Failure Pattern and then applied uniformly to every future instance of that pattern; Preconditions in Part V are evaluated per transaction, and a Failure Pattern that has earned L3 eligibility for nine prior instances does not thereby earn it automatically for a tenth instance whose specific target fails even one of the five conjunctive elements — the ninth instance's success is evidence about the pattern's general amenability to L3, consulted when estimating a template candidate's prior per Part IV, but it is never a substitute for re-checking the conjunction against the tenth instance's own specific Preconditions, because the entire purpose of a per-transaction floor is to catch the one instance where a normally well-behaved pattern's target differs from its predecessors in exactly the respect that matters.

PART VIII FINAL LAW

Tier is a function of what can go wrong and how cheaply it can be undone, never of how confident the runtime is that a candidate will succeed. L3's five preconditions are a floor, not an average — one absent precondition drops the tier regardless of how strong the other four are. Demotion is unilateral, immediate, and requires no approval; promotion requires evidence, is never inferred from time passing, and is never self-granted, at any tier, under any circumstance.


PART IX — SEPARATION OF POWERS

9.1 The ladder in Part VIII governs how much a transaction may do without external re-confirmation, but it does not by itself prevent a single role from performing every step of a transaction and thereby grading its own work. Separation of powers is the orthogonal constraint: six roles — Detection, Diagnosis, Authority, Execution, Verification, Learning — are named as distinct, and a single actor occupying more than one of them for the same transaction is a structural defect regardless of what tier that transaction sits at, because a tier answers "how much may be done" while separation answers "who is allowed to check whose work," and the two questions do not collapse into each other no matter how trustworthy any single role has proven itself historically.

9.2 Detection is never CRAIF's own role; it belongs, per Part II, to the Finding's producer — liveness, SQI, the Institutional Immune System, DAIF-21 — and CRAIF's runtime consumes Findings without the authority to originate one on its own initiative, because a repair runtime that can both invent the problem and propose its own fix has collapsed the one check that keeps a Finding honest: that it originated from a system whose entire charter is detection and nothing else.

9.3 Diagnosis, meaning the judgment of why a Finding's underlying condition exists and what class of remediation it calls for, is likewise not performed by CRAIF's runtime in the deep sense; CRAIF's candidate generation in Part IV performs a narrow, mechanical normalization — does a falsifiable target-state assertion exist for this Finding — but the causal question of root cause, contributing conditions, and counterevidence belongs to the estate's diagnostic authorities, principally the Decision Review Kernel's evidence-and-confidence machinery, consulted rather than replicated, exactly as Part II requires.

9.4 Authority is exercised exclusively through the objects in Part V, produced by consulting an external decision authority, and CRAIF's runtime holds no discretion to grant itself a tier its consulted evidence does not support — this is restated here, in the separation-of-powers frame rather than the ontology frame, because the same rule looked at from two angles catches two different failure modes: Part V's framing catches a malformed Authority Decision object; this framing catches an actor who holds both the Authority role and the Execution role and is tempted, under pressure, to skip consulting the external authority and simply proceed, a temptation no object-validation rule alone fully forecloses without an accompanying institutional norm that Execution and Authority are never the same actor's discretion to blend.

9.5 Execution is CRAIF-01's runtime proper — snapshot, sandbox, canary, commit-or-rollback — and it is the role most exposed to real mutation, which is exactly why Parts 9.2 through 9.4 exist to keep three upstream roles independent of it. Execution may request re-diagnosis when a candidate proves inapplicable mid-transaction, and it may request re-authorization when Preconditions drift, but it may never silently substitute its own judgment for either role's answer and continue, because a silent substitution at the execution layer is invisible to every audit that checks the upstream objects and assumes, correctly in the normal case, that execution followed what those objects actually said.

9.6 Verification is structurally required to be independent of Execution even when both are, in the current build, implemented within the same CRAIF-01 dataset — independence here means the Verification Contract was drafted before execution per Part VI and is applied by a check that does not consult the executing step's own self-report of what it did, but instead re-observes the target reality object directly, the same discipline SQI's own root law states for test output: a summary substituting for the runner's actual report is exactly the corruption Part VI's admissibility rule refuses to accept, restated here as an institutional-role requirement rather than an evidence-tier requirement.

9.7 Learning is downstream of every other role and consumes their combined output — the Repair Outcome, the Repair Certificate or Failed Repair Record — but it does not report back into Execution to unlock a higher tier for the next transaction on its own initiative; a Learning Candidate's promotion recommendation is advisory, per Part VII, and the actual promotion decision is made by whichever external authority owns the target registry, which closes the loop that would otherwise let a sufficiently persuasive pattern of successes talk the ladder into raising its own ceiling from the inside.

9.8 The single sentence that operationalizes this entire Part for an implementer building against it later is this: no actor may occupy two of these six roles for the same transaction, and any design that appears to require it — for engineering convenience, for latency, for reduced coordination overhead — is a design that has mistaken an implementation shortcut for a constitutional exception, and the shortcut is refused, not negotiated, regardless of how costly maintaining six independent roles turns out to be in practice.

9.9 A concrete failure this Part was written against, observed once already in a sibling system during this corpus's own founding session, is worth restating in the separation-of-powers frame specifically because it demonstrates the failure occurring across systems rather than only in the abstract. A decision-authority adapter was found, during this session's own verification work, to reject a wiring decision on the grounds of a precedent collision that turned out to be an artifact of the classifier's own scoring — its score rose with input length rather than with the property it claimed to measure, and the review that caught this was the kernel reviewing its own wiring decision as its first live input, discovering the always-reject bias only because the review and the reviewed decision were, in that instance, forced into the same act. Had Authority and Execution been the same actor in that system, the always-reject bias might have simply blocked the wiring silently, forever, with no independent role positioned to notice that the block itself was malformed. CRAIF's own separation exists precisely so a comparable defect in any of its six roles is caught by whichever adjacent role's independence was preserved, rather than requiring the defect to surface by the same fortunate coincidence that caught it in the sibling system.

9.10 The Part closes by naming the single implementation temptation most likely to erode this separation gradually rather than all at once: a shared internal representation — one object graph, one process, one memory space — across roles that are nominally separate on paper but implemented as functions called sequentially within a single, undifferentiated execution context. Separation of powers is not satisfied by six named responsibilities documented in one codebase and invoked in order; it requires that at least one role's output cannot be silently substituted by another role reaching directly into shared state that only convention, not an enforced boundary, prevents it from touching. Where an enforced boundary is not yet technically implemented, this corpus requires the boundary be honored as an inviolable design discipline until it is, rather than treated as an aspiration to be tightened only once convenience runs out.

PART IX FINAL LAW

Six roles — Detection, Diagnosis, Authority, Execution, Verification, Learning — are distinct by constitutional requirement, not by convenience, and a single actor occupying two of them for the same transaction has collapsed the one check that role separation exists to provide, regardless of how trustworthy that actor has proven itself on every prior transaction.


PART X — THE THREE CONSTITUTIONAL LAWS

10.1 Two of this corpus's three constitutional laws originate directly in the source proposal that motivated CRAIF's founding, and they are stated here not as inherited slogans but as derivations, because a law repeated without its derivation is a law nobody can correctly apply to a case its authors did not anticipate. The first, Institutional Existence, holds that no capability of Claude Power Pack is considered to exist by being documented, installed, or implemented — it exists institutionally only when it is reachable, activates under the correct conditions, produces an observable result, can be independently verified, degrades detectably, and has an explicit route to repair, rollback, and learning. The derivation is direct from Part I: every one of the five founding incidents involved a capability that satisfied some prefix of this list — present, even individually tested — while failing a later item, and the law exists precisely to prevent any future capability from being credited with existence on the strength of a prefix alone.

10.2 The second law, Escape Attribution, holds that every failure requiring Owner intervention must be evaluated as a potential failure of the detection, governance, repair, or learning system, not solely as a failure of the component where it surfaced. Its derivation is the fifth founding incident specifically: Owner Intervention Escape was not a defect in any single hook or module; it was evidence that no system in the estate was chartered to notice, at the time, that noticing itself had not happened. The law's operational content is a standing question CRAIF-01's own learning handoff must ask at every Incident Closure: which detector, which scheduler, which authority, which learning-closure step should have caught this before the Owner did, and what does its absence, in this specific instance, imply about that system's own coverage.

10.3 The third law, No Self-Granted Authority, is native to CRAIF rather than inherited, because it addresses a risk the source proposal named descriptively — do not let a repair runtime concede itself power — without deriving the specific mechanisms that make the risk concrete for a transactional execution system. Stated in full: no repair runtime may expand its own authority tier, certify its own repair, or modify the approval criteria that govern it, in the same transaction that applies a repair, or in any transaction at all without the specific external process each of those three acts independently requires under Parts V, VI, and VIII.

10.4 The third law's three clauses are independent and each closes a distinct, previously observed failure shape rather than restating the same idea three times. Expanding one's own authority tier is the ladder-promotion failure Part VIII's demotion-versus-promotion asymmetry exists to prevent — promotion requires external evidence review, period, and no transaction's own internal reasoning about its own success counts as that review. Certifying one's own repair is the Verification-role-collapse failure Part IX names directly — a Repair Certificate issued by the same actor that executed the candidate, without an independent re-observation of the target, is not a certificate; it is a self-report wearing a certificate's shape. Modifying one's own approval criteria is the deepest and least obviously tempting failure, because it does not require a single dramatic act — it can occur gradually, as a runtime that observes its own Verification Contracts failing at a certain rate begins to quietly narrow what those contracts check, each narrowing individually defensible as an efficiency improvement, until the criteria that once would have caught a given failure no longer do. The clause forbids this explicitly: any change to what a Verification Contract, a Preconditions record, or an authority-tier eligibility rule requires is itself a governed decision, routed through the same external consultation Part II establishes for every other judgment CRAIF is tempted to make alone.

10.5 The three laws are not independently sufficient and are not meant to be checked in isolation; a transaction can satisfy Institutional Existence and Escape Attribution in full and still violate No Self-Granted Authority through a subtle criteria-narrowing drift that neither of the first two laws is positioned to detect, because neither asks about the stability of the checking mechanism itself over time. Part XIV's self-failure-mode catalogue and CRAIF-01's own metrics in Part XIX exist specifically to give this drift a detectable signature — a Verification Contract's specificity trending downward across a Failure Pattern's successive instances is the operational tripwire the third law's third clause requires, and its absence from a repair engine's design would leave the clause constitutionally stated but practically unenforceable.

10.6 A common misreading of Institutional Existence deserves preemptive correction: the law does not require every capability to be exercised continuously in order to be considered existing — a capability legitimately dormant, awaiting a condition that has not yet arisen, is not thereby non-existent, provided its reachability, its activation criteria, and its verification route are all provably intact and demonstrable on demand. The law's target is narrower and sharper than "always running": it is the specific configuration in which a capability's existence is asserted from documentation or installation alone, with reachability, activation, and verifiability never actually checked and therefore unknown rather than dormant. `PLANNED`, `SCHEDULED`, and `LIBRARY` classifications in the estate's own liveness registry are the honest expression of legitimate dormancy; an undeclared, unreachable module with none of those classifications is the failure this law names.

10.7 A comparable misreading threatens Escape Attribution: the law does not hold that every Owner intervention is evidence of a systemic failure requiring an elaborate root-cause investigation disproportionate to the incident's scale. A trivial, one-off configuration typo an Owner happens to notice in passing is not, by itself, proof that the estate's entire detection apparatus has failed — proportionality matters, and the law's operational bite is reserved for incidents where the underlying condition was, in principle, within an existing detector's charter and evidence threshold to have caught, yet did not, repeatedly or at scale, which is precisely the shape all five founding fixtures share and precisely the shape a single passing typo does not.

10.8 The interaction between the three laws and the ladder in Part VIII is worth stating explicitly because it is not obvious from either specification read alone: a Repair Intent whose own Escape Attribution analysis reveals that the responsible upstream detector itself has a coverage gap does not, by that revelation, earn a higher authority tier for closing the immediate symptom faster. The coverage gap becomes its own Learning Candidate and, where warranted, its own Owner Escalation targeting the detector's own maintainers; it does not compress the tier discipline Part VIII applies to the symptom currently in front of the runtime, because urgency born of a newly-discovered systemic gap is exactly the kind of pressure Part 2.7's Duplicate-to-Advantage doctrine and Part VIII's own tier-is-not-confidence rule both already refuse to let override the ladder's ordinary discipline.

PART X FINAL LAW

Institutional Existence and Escape Attribution derive directly from the five founding incidents; No Self-Granted Authority is native to CRAIF and closes three independent failure shapes — tier expansion, self-certification, and criteria drift — none of which the other two laws are positioned to catch. A transaction can satisfy the first two in full and still violate the third through a gradual narrowing no single check catches in the moment, which is why the third law requires a standing, measured tripwire, not a one-time verification.


PART XI — NON-DUPLICATION CONTRACT

11.1 This corpus exists because a Duplicate-to-Advantage audit determined, at content tier rather than from titles alone, that roughly four-fifths of its originally proposed scope was already owned by sealed capability, and the discipline that produced that determination does not expire once CRAIF is founded — it becomes CRAIF's own standing obligation toward whatever is proposed to extend it next. This Part states the boundary explicitly and permanently, not as a historical record of what was decided once, but as a live constraint every future Part, every future dataset, and every future runtime capability proposed under the CRAIF name must clear before it is built.

11.2 CRAIF never owns general capability or module reachability. That capability belongs to `modules/liveness/reachability.py`, which computes REACHABLE, ORPHAN, and UNKNOWN status by transitive closure from live polyglot surfaces, with coverage guaranteed by construction rather than by anyone remembering to declare a module. CRAIF consumes Reachability Findings from this system; it does not compute reachability itself, and any future proposal to add reachability-computation logic inside a CRAIF dataset is, by definition, a violation of this Part regardless of how convenient co-locating the logic might seem.

11.3 CRAIF never owns post-ship drift or silence detection. That capability belongs to `modules/liveness/liveness_ledger.py`, which produces LIVE, WIRED-BUT-SILENT, DRIFTED, and ORPHANED verdicts against a registry of wired components and auto-clears owner_queue rows when a component returns to LIVE. CRAIF's Incident Closure objects, defined in Part VII, feed evidence into this ledger's probes; they do not replace the ledger's own verdict computation.

11.4 CRAIF never owns repository reality or test-reach science. Those belong to SQI, whose own governing index states plainly that it measures and does not remediate — the exact half of the problem CRAIF exists to close, and the exact reason SQI's disclaimer is not a gap in SQI but the precise seam CRAIF was built to occupy. CRAIF's Verification Contracts consume SQI's reconciliation output as their evidence source for reachability- and quality-class repairs; they do not reimplement test-reach computation.

11.5 CRAIF never owns blast radius, reversibility classification, or decision authority in general. Those belong to the Decision Review Kernel, whose own family tree states that it recommends and forks nothing — files on disk and a function that reads them, no new brain, no parallel accountant. CRAIF's Scope and Blast-Radius Records and Repair Authority Decisions consult DRK's classifier as their evidentiary basis; a CRAIF dataset that independently derives a reversibility taxonomy of its own has duplicated DRK's core science under a different name, which this Part forbids regardless of how the duplication is labeled.

11.6 CRAIF never owns the immune or pathogen taxonomy, quarantine, or containment. Those belong to the Institutional Immune System, CPP-IAS's D2 dataset, which already maintains a rigorous six-class pathogen taxonomy, a containment ladder, and a decay model, and whose own Part XIV names the specific gap — confirmation as presence-check rather than live test — that CRAIF's Verification Contracts exist to close for the repair-execution case specifically. CRAIF's Immune-Threat Finding subtype carries D2's confirmation-method flag precisely so CRAIF never has to re-derive what D2 already classifies.

11.7 CRAIF never owns reality-synchronization or semantic drift detection in general, nor the Duplicate-to-Advantage doctrine itself. Those belong to DAIF-21 and DAIF-00 respectively, and CRAIF's own Part II is itself an instance of DAIF-00's root law applied to CRAIF's own design — composed, not restated as an independent invention, because restating an already-sealed doctrine under a new name is the specific failure this Part exists to prevent CRAIF from ever committing against itself.

11.8 CRAIF never owns deploy-target rollback mechanics or escalation queueing as general capabilities. `modules/rollback/*` already implements restoration for rsync directories, pg_dump databases, and docker volumes; CRAIF's Rollback Contracts compose this mechanism as an adapter for the target classes it already covers and flag a gap, rather than build a competing restoration mechanism, for target classes it does not yet cover. `owner_queue` already implements escalation and auto-clear; CRAIF's Owner Escalation objects route through it rather than around it.

11.9 The contract closes with the test any future CRAIF extension must pass before a single Part is authored in its name: name the specific mechanism-tier owner of the capability being proposed, read that owner's actual implementation rather than its title, and if an owner is found, either the proposal is refused, or it is restructured as a D2A reinforcement package that strengthens the existing owner, exactly as the eight packages accompanying this corpus's own founding do for the systems named across this Part.

11.10 A specific temptation this Part names in advance, because it is the single most likely path by which CRAIF would violate its own non-duplication contract without anyone deciding to violate it outright, is incremental science creep through convenience functions. A CRAIF-01 implementer, needing to check a candidate's reversibility quickly during development, writes a small local heuristic rather than calling DRK's classifier, intending it as a temporary stand-in; the heuristic works well enough in early testing that removing it before shipping never becomes urgent, and eighteen months later CRAIF-01 is quietly running two reversibility classifiers, one of them undocumented, unversioned, and never audited against DRK's own evolution. This Part's discipline forecloses the temptation at its root: any CRAIF code path that would need a reversibility judgment, a reachability judgment, a pathogen judgment, or a drift judgment must call the owning system's actual interface from the first implementation, including in development and testing, precisely because a convenience shortcut is never actually temporary once it works.

11.11 The same discipline extends to metrics, because a metric is a science-owning artifact exactly as much as a classifier is. CRAIF's own Owner Intervention Escape Rate, defined in Part XIII, composes CO-12's telemetry infrastructure rather than instrumenting its own event stream, and a future implementer who finds CO-12's telemetry inconvenient to integrate against and considers logging CRAIF's own parallel event stream instead is proposing exactly the kind of duplication this Part's closing test is built to catch: name the owner — CO-12 already owns cross-system telemetry — read its actual interface, and either integrate against it or route the friction as a proposed improvement to CO-12 itself, never as a private parallel stream that duplicates its jurisdiction under a different file name.

11.12 A closing note situates this Part's discipline within the estate's own naming for it: the Duplicate-to-Advantage audit that founded CRAIF classified each candidate capability against a verdict vocabulary — NEW, EXTEND, MERGE, CONNECT, GENERALIZE, SPECIALIZE, REFERENCE, DO-NOT-BUILD among others — and every owner named across 11.2 through 11.8 was assigned REFERENCE or DO-NOT-BUILD specifically, never EXTEND, because CRAIF does not even add a thin composition layer atop these systems' own science; it consumes their output as-is. Where this corpus does compose a thin layer — the Authority Ladder's use of DRK's classifier as an input to a CRAIF-native tier decision, for instance — the relationship is properly CONNECT rather than REFERENCE, a distinction worth preserving because a future auditor checking this corpus's own non-duplication claims should be able to verify, verdict by verdict, that no REFERENCE relationship has quietly become an EXTEND without the reconciliation this Part's closing test requires.

PART XI FINAL LAW

Every capability CRAIF might be tempted to duplicate has a named, mechanism-tier owner, verified by reading real code and real indices rather than titles: liveness for reachability and drift, SQI for reality and test-reach, DRK for decisions and blast radius, CPP-IAS for the immune taxonomy, DAIF for reality-sync and the duplication doctrine itself, rollback and owner_queue for restoration and escalation. Any future proposal to add one of these sciences inside a CRAIF dataset fails this Part by construction, regardless of convenience.


PART XII — FOUNDING NEGATIVE FIXTURES

12.1 A corpus that governs repair without a permanent, checkable record of the specific failures that motivated it risks losing, over successive revisions, the concrete grounding that separates a real requirement from a plausible-sounding one. This Part fixes the five incidents that founded CRAIF as permanent negative fixtures — not narrative history, but structured cases each future Part, each future test, and each future Verification Contract template can be checked against directly.

12.2 Fixture One, the Activation Bootstrap Defect: a skill's activation vocabulary lived entirely inside the skill's own body, so that selecting the skill required having already loaded it — a circular dependency invisible to any reachability scanner, because the scanner's own definition of reachability presupposes a selection event that, in this case, never had an external trigger to fire on. The fixture's checkable signature: a capability whose activation criteria are not represented in any surface external to the capability itself. CRAIF-02's Part III and Part IV build directly on this fixture; any future activation-integrity claim must be checked against it by name.

12.3 Fixture Two, Capability Reachability Debt at scale: one hundred and fifty-five modules present, individually correct, unreachable from any live surface, discovered only by a purpose-built scanner and not by any process the estate ran routinely before that scanner existed. The fixture's checkable signature: existence and individual correctness are non-predictive of reachability, at any scale, and a coverage claim computed from a hand-maintained registry rather than full-repository discovery will systematically undercount this class of debt. CRAIF-01's candidate-generation discipline in Part IV, particularly the refusal to generate an unfalsifiable candidate, is checked against this fixture by verifying that a template-matched candidate for an orphan module produces a Verification Contract keyed to `modules/liveness/reachability.py`'s own REACHABLE verdict, not to a weaker proxy.

12.4 Fixture Three, Hook and Settings Divergence: a hook built correctly in isolation, never registered in settings, creating the appearance of a capability where none actually operated. The fixture's checkable signature: a bidirectional consistency failure between two artifacts that are individually well-formed but jointly inconsistent, undetectable by inspecting either artifact alone. CRAIF-01's Verification Contract discipline in Part VI, requiring re-observation of the target rather than the executing step's self-report, is checked against this fixture by confirming that a repair claiming to register a hook produces evidence the hook actually fired on a subsequent real event, not merely that the settings file now contains the expected entry.

12.5 Fixture Four, Cross-Repository Activation Failure: a capability functioning inside its home repository and nowhere else, surfaced only because an Owner manually checked thirty projects and counted. The fixture's checkable signature: activation behavior verified in one environment carries no evidence about activation behavior in a structurally different one, and a Verification Contract scoped to a single repository cannot license a claim of general, federated capability. CRAIF-02's Part VIII, on cross-repo activation, and its Part XI cross-repo activation-rate metric are checked against this fixture directly.

12.6 Fixture Five, Owner Intervention Escape: the fact that all four prior fixtures were discovered because the Owner asked for an audit, not because any system in the estate surfaced them unprompted. The fixture's checkable signature is the least mechanical of the five and the most important: an incident whose root-cause analysis stops at the component that failed, without asking which detection, governance, repair, or learning system should have caught the failure before the Owner did, has not applied Escape Attribution — CRAIF-00 Part X's second law — regardless of how thoroughly the component-level failure itself was analyzed.

12.7 These five fixtures are never treated as historical color once this dataset is sealed. Every Failure Pattern taxonomy this corpus or its sibling datasets reference must be checked for whether it already subsumes one of these five, every metric proposed must be checked for whether it would have made at least one of the five detectable earlier than an Owner's manual audit did, and every adversarial review pass conducted against CRAIF's own design, per Part XIV and the family's Phase 3 review, must re-run at least one of the five as a live scenario rather than merely citing it.

12.8 A common error in applying these fixtures is treating them as satisfied once a superficially similar scenario is named in a Part's prose, rather than actually replayed as a concrete case with real or faithfully simulated inputs. A worked example that says "consider a hook that is present but unregistered" without ever specifying which hook, what its registration entry should look like, what evidence would prove it fires, and what a Verification Contract checking that evidence would concretely assert, has cited Fixture Three's title without exercising its signature. CRAIF-01 Part XX and CRAIF-02 Part XVI, the two datasets' respective worked-example Parts, are required to name concrete, specific instances of at least one fixture each — not paraphrase the fixture's description — precisely so that this corpus's own adversarial review in Phase 3 can check the replay's specificity rather than accept a restated title as evidence the fixture was honored.

12.9 The fixtures also serve a purpose beyond grounding this corpus's own content: they are the seed corpus CRAIF-02's synthetic and real activation-testing science, specified in its own Part V, draws its first negative cases from. A simulation harness built without a concrete, checkable starting corpus risks specifying an elaborate testing architecture that has never actually been exercised against a real failure, the same architecture-without-exercise gap SQI's own founding evidence identified in a neighboring domain. These five fixtures exist, in part, so that CRAIF-02's harness has at least five real, previously-observed cases to replay before it is ever asked to generalize to a case nobody has seen yet.

12.10 A question left deliberately open is whether five fixtures are sufficient or merely the founding minimum, and this corpus states plainly that five is the latter. The source proposal that motivated CRAIF named exactly these five because they were the incidents an Owner had already surfaced by the time CRAIF was founded, not because any analysis established that five is the correct or complete count of activation- and reachability-class failure modes the estate will ever encounter. CRAIF-01's own operation, once it exists, is expected to surface additional fixtures, and this Part's own closing obligation is that any future negative fixture proposed for permanent status must meet the same bar the five founding ones do: a checkable signature independent of its narrative, not merely a description of an incident that felt significant when it occurred.

12.11 A closing caution against fixture proliferation without discipline is warranted precisely because Part XIV's local-to-global promotion error names an analogous risk for guardians, and negative fixtures are vulnerable to the identical failure in a different guise: an incident recorded as a permanent fixture on the strength of a single occurrence, before any recurrence or cross-project pattern establishes that it represents a genuine class rather than an idiosyncratic one-off. The five founding fixtures earn their permanent status by having already been observed, independently, across the estate's own thirty-project federation audit — Fixture Four's own discovery method — and any candidate sixth fixture should be held to a comparable bar of independent corroboration before being added to this Part rather than granted permanence on the strength of a single session's finding.

PART XII FINAL LAW

Five fixtures found CRAIF, and each carries a checkable signature independent of the narrative that produced it: invisible external activation criteria, unreachability at scale despite individual correctness, bidirectional artifact inconsistency, environment-dependent activation, and root-cause analysis that stops at the component instead of asking which institutional system should have caught it first. A future claim that does not check itself against all five has not yet earned the right to call itself complete.


PART XIII — OWNER INTERVENTION ESCAPE RATE AND OIII

13.1 The governing metric this corpus contributes to the estate is not a count of repairs performed, because a metric that rewards volume rewards exactly the failure mode Part I exists to prevent — a system that repairs many superficial symptoms while the underlying defect class that produces them goes unaddressed would score well on a repair count and score honestly on nothing else. The Owner Intervention Escape Rate is defined instead as the proportion of defects, among all defects the estate's detectors were capable of surfacing, that reached the Owner's attention before any system in the estate surfaced or resolved them.

13.2 The rate's denominator is deliberately the harder half to compute honestly, and this corpus does not pretend the difficulty away. It is not the count of defects CRAIF resolved; it is the count of defects that were, in principle, within the surfacing capability of an existing detector — liveness, SQI, the immune system, DAIF-21 — whether or not that detector actually fired in time. A rate computed only against defects CRAIF itself touched would silently exclude the defects no detector fired on at all, which is exactly the Fixture Five failure mode restated as a measurement artifact rather than closed by one.

13.3 The composite metric, Owner-Independent Institutional Integrity, combines four numerator terms — defects detected before the Owner, repairs independently verified, recurrences measurably prevented, and projects protected across the federation — divided by three cost terms — false positives, regressions introduced by a repair, and human intervention still required. Each term is drawn from objects this corpus and its sibling datasets already define: verified repairs come from Repair Certificates, recurrences prevented come from Guardian Promotion Candidates that were actually promoted and subsequently held, and human intervention required comes directly from Owner Escalation counts.

13.4 The composite is stated with its anti-Goodhart countermetrics attached at definition time rather than added later as an afterthought, because a metric introduced without its gaming vector named is a metric that will eventually be gamed exactly along that vector. The numerator term for recurrences prevented is countered by a False Healing Rate, defined as certificates later subject to Certification Revocation, because a system under pressure to show recurrence prevention can manufacture the appearance by issuing certificates that later prove unearned; the countermetric makes that manufacture visible rather than rewarding it. The denominator term for false positives is countered by an Over-Quarantine-equivalent measure — repairs proposed against Findings that a subsequent independent check showed were never real — because a system under pressure to minimize its own false-positive count can achieve a low count simply by refusing to generate candidates for ambiguous Findings, silently trading recall for a better-looking precision number, and the countermetric requires that refusal rate to be reported alongside the false-positive rate rather than in isolation.

13.5 A specific and previously observed metric-gaming pattern is named explicitly because it recurred once already in a sibling system during this session's own audit: a decision-quality classifier whose score correlated with input length rather than with the property it claimed to measure, producing an always-reject bias that was only caught when the classifier's own design was submitted to itself as a test case. The Owner Intervention Escape Rate is vulnerable to an analogous corruption if its measurement window is set by whoever benefits from a favorable reading of it — a runtime with authority over its own measurement cadence could quietly widen the window until enough LIVE-verdict noise accumulates to dilute a genuine escape. The countermeasure is structural rather than aspirational: the measurement window and cadence for this metric are set by CO-12's existing telemetry infrastructure, external to CRAIF's own runtime, per the same consultation discipline Part II establishes everywhere else in this corpus.

13.6 The target state named in the source proposal — Owner Intervention Escape Rate approaching zero — is retained as the corpus's stated ambition but is explicitly not treated as a claim this corpus can certify about itself at founding. This dataset defines the metric; it does not assert a measured value for it, because no measured value exists yet, and asserting one prematurely would itself be the exact undemonstrated-claim failure SQI's own constitution names as the first article any verification-adjacent system must refuse to commit.

13.7 A worked numeric illustration, using synthetic figures rather than any measured value this corpus does not yet possess, clarifies how the composite's terms interact rather than merely stand adjacent to each other. Suppose, across a reporting period, the estate's detectors were capable of surfacing forty defects; thirty-two were in fact detected before the Owner noticed any of them, six required an Owner Escalation, and two were the kind of ambiguous, contested-jurisdiction case Part 5.7 requires CRAIF to leave at L0. Of the thirty-two detected-first, twenty-eight repairs were issued Repair Certificates that survived their revocation window, three were later subject to Certification Revocation, and one remains within its window, not yet either confirmed or revoked. The Owner Intervention Escape Rate for the period is six divided by forty, not six divided by thirty-two, because the two contested cases correctly routed to L0 still counted as instances requiring Owner attention even though CRAIF itself never disputes that L0 was the right call for them — Escape Attribution, per Part X, asks whether the estate as a whole caught the defect before the Owner, not whether CRAIF specifically resolved it.

13.8 The three Certification Revocations in this illustration do not, by themselves, indicate the metric is being gamed; they indicate the False Healing countermetric is functioning exactly as Part 13.4 intends, surfacing that three of the twenty-eight nominally-successful repairs did not hold under later conditions. A period in which the revocation count is consistently near zero deserves scrutiny in the opposite direction from what intuition suggests: it is at least as likely to indicate that Certificates are being issued under conditions too lax to ever trigger a revocation clause as it is to indicate that every repair genuinely held, and this corpus's own adversarial review in Phase 3 treats a suspiciously low False Healing Rate as a finding requiring investigation, not a result to be celebrated uncritically.

13.9 A last scoping note distinguishes this corpus's obligation from CO-12's: CRAIF-00 defines what Owner Intervention Escape Rate and OIII mean and what evidence licenses each term; it does not define the telemetry pipeline that computes them, which belongs, per Part XI's non-duplication contract, to CO-12 as the estate's existing telemetry owner. A future implementer building the actual measurement pipeline consults CO-12's interface first and adds only the CRAIF-specific term definitions this Part supplies — the False Healing Rate, the refusal-rate countermetric, the Owner Escalation count — as new signal kinds within CO-12's existing framework, never as a parallel measurement system CRAIF maintains independently.

PART XIII FINAL LAW

A metric that rewards repair volume rewards exactly the failure this corpus exists to prevent. Every numerator term in the composite metric carries a named countermetric at the moment of its definition — false healing against recurrence-prevention claims, refusal rate against false-positive claims — because a metric introduced without its gaming vector named will eventually be gamed along precisely that vector, and the measurement window itself is set externally to the runtime it measures, for the same reason no runtime may author the evidence that authorizes its own actions.


PART XIV — FAILURE MODES OF CRAIF ITSELF

14.1 This Part exists for the same reason CPP-IAS D2's own Part XIV exists, and it borrows that Part's discipline of assembling scattered local risks into one adversarial catalogue rather than leaving each buried inside the mechanism it threatens, because a reader who encounters each risk once, in passing, may never see that every mechanism this corpus specifies has a corresponding under-eager and over-eager failure pole, and CRAIF is only honestly specified once both poles of every mechanism are named side by side, exactly as D2's own Part XIV states for the immune system it audits.

14.2 FALSE HEALING is the under-eager pole of the entire verification apparatus: a Repair Certificate issued for a transaction whose Verification Contract was satisfied on paper but whose underlying capability does not, in fact, work under every real invocation path — the structural-presence-check failure named at this corpus's founding, now recurring inside CRAIF's own certification step rather than IAS-D2's confirmation step. Its specific cause inside CRAIF is a Verification Contract drafted too narrowly relative to the Finding's actual scope, checking that a file changed rather than that the capability the file supports now activates, which is precisely why CRAIF-02's Activation Verification Contract is mandatory rather than optional for any Finding touching an activation-chain segment.

14.3 SELF-GRANTED AUTHORITY is the over-eager pole of the ladder mechanism: a transaction that acquires a higher tier than its Preconditions actually support, either because a Preconditions check was performed against stale evidence, per Part IV's staleness discipline, or because an Authority Decision's evidence field was permitted to resolve back to CRAIF's own prior output, the specific malformation Part V's validation rule exists to reject at the object level rather than merely discourage by convention.

14.4 REPAIR THRASHING is a failure mode distinct from both, occurring when a Repair Candidate is applied, verified successful, and then the same or a closely related Finding recurs shortly after, triggering a fresh transaction against what is actually one unresolved underlying condition rather than two independent ones. Its harm compounds specifically because each individual transaction in the thrash cycle can pass its own Verification Contract honestly — the candidate genuinely restored the target state at the moment it was checked — while the pattern across transactions reveals that the true root cause was never addressed, only its most recent symptom. The counter is structural: a Repair Intent's normalization step in Part III must check the target's recent Incident Closure history before opening, and a closure within a short window against the same target escalates to Owner attention rather than opening a fresh transaction silently.

14.5 SCANNER-VERIFIER COLLUSION occurs when the same underlying logic error affects both a Finding's producer and the Verification Contract's check, such that a repair appears verified precisely because the verification shares the producer's blind spot rather than because the repair actually addressed the underlying defect. This is the specific reason Part VI requires a Verification Contract to consult a check independent of the producing detector's own internal state wherever an independent check exists — SQI's reconciliation, liveness's own reachability re-scan — rather than accepting the original producer's own re-assertion that the Finding has cleared as sufficient verification of a fix.

14.6 LOCAL-TO-GLOBAL PROMOTION ERROR is Part VII's Guardian Promotion Candidate pipeline failing at its own weakest point: a Failure Pattern observed and repaired successfully within a single project's idiosyncratic configuration is proposed for global promotion before cross-project validation has actually occurred, producing a guardian that fires correctly in the project that motivated it and produces false positives everywhere else. The counter is the pipeline's own sequencing, restated here as a named failure this corpus refuses to shortcut under any pressure of urgency: local observation, local recurrence, cross-project validation, only then a promotion proposal — and the proposal remains a proposal to the owning authority, never a self-installed promotion, per Part VII's own prohibition.

14.7 EMERGENCY-STOP BYPASS is named even though L4 is not enabled at this corpus's founding, because a ladder specification that defers naming this risk until L4 is actually granted will specify the emergency stop mechanism under time pressure rather than in advance. The risk: a future L4 policy grants standing authority broad enough that the emergency stop's reach does not cover every transaction the policy authorized, leaving a subset unstoppable by the mechanism meant to halt all of them. The counter, stated now rather than deferred: any future L4 policy proposal must include, as a condition of the policy itself rather than as a separate later addition, proof that the emergency stop's reach is coextensive with the policy's authorization scope, checked before the policy takes effect, not after an incident reveals the gap.

14.8 Reading these seven failure modes together reveals the same pairing structure D2's own Part XIV names for the immune system: false healing and self-granted authority are the verification and authority mechanisms' respective under- and over-eager poles; scanner-verifier collusion and thrashing are failures of independence and of root-cause completeness respectively; local-to-global promotion error and emergency-stop bypass are failures at the two ends of the ladder's lifecycle, promotion and the ultimate authority ceiling. The discipline that follows, stated once for the whole corpus, is never to tune any single mechanism's threshold toward catching one of these failure modes more aggressively without measuring the effect on its paired mode — a Verification Contract tightened purely to catch false healing that becomes so strict it can never be satisfied by a legitimate repair has traded one failure for its opposite, not eliminated a failure.

14.9 An eighth failure mode is named separately from the seven above because it operates at a different layer than any single mechanism's under- or over-eager pole: METRIC-DRIVEN CANDIDATE AVOIDANCE, in which a runtime under implicit pressure to keep its False Positive and Refusal-Rate countermetrics both low learns, without any explicit instruction to do so, to prefer generating candidates only for Findings whose outcome is nearly certain in advance, quietly declining the harder, more ambiguous cases that would actually test the runtime's judgment. This is a corruption of Part XIII's metric design rather than of any single object in Parts III through VIII, and it is caught, when it is caught, only by comparing the distribution of Finding difficulty CRAIF actually attempts against the distribution its upstream producers actually surface — a comparison this corpus requires as a standing check precisely because the corruption produces excellent-looking numbers on every metric defined in Part XIII while the estate's harder defects quietly accumulate unaddressed.

14.10 The catalogue closes with an explicit statement of what these eight failure modes are not: they are not an exhaustive guarantee that no other failure mode exists, and any adversarial review that treats this Part as a checklist to clear rather than a floor to build from has misunderstood its purpose. New failure modes discovered through CRAIF-01 or CRAIF-02's actual operation are added here, to this Part, by the same discipline Part VII requires of any Learning Candidate — named, given a checkable signature, and paired against whichever existing mode it most resembles so the pairing discipline in 14.8 continues to apply as the catalogue grows.

PART XIV FINAL LAW

Every mechanism this corpus specifies has an under-eager and an over-eager failure pole, and CRAIF is honestly specified only when both poles of every mechanism are named beside each other rather than discovered separately, in production, sessions apart. A fix that tightens one pole without measuring its paired pole has traded one failure mode for its opposite, and trading is not the same act as closing.


PART XV — EVIDENCE AND ADMISSIBILITY FOR REPAIR CLAIMS

15.1 CRAIF does not define a new evidence-tier taxonomy; it composes the estate's existing epistemic ladder, per the consultation discipline established in Part II, and adds exactly one refinement specific to repair claims that the general ladder does not itself resolve: the distinction, named explicitly by IAS-D2's own Part XIV, between a structural presence check and a live adversarial demonstration that a mechanism actually intercepts what it was built to intercept.

15.2 For any Verification Contract concerning an activation-chain segment, presence-only evidence — a settings key exists, a hook file is registered, a module import resolves — is classified at this corpus's medium admissibility tier at best, never strong, regardless of how confidently the presence check passed, because presence evidence answers a narrower question than a repair claim requires, and treating a narrow answer as though it settled a broader one is the exact substitution CRAIF-00's founding evidence identified as unresolved inside the estate's own sealed immune-system corpus.

15.3 Strong evidence, for a repair claim specifically, requires a demonstrated before-and-after pair: a reproduction that the original Finding's condition held prior to the repair, obtained either from the Finding's own producer re-confirming immediately before execution or from a deliberate, safe reproduction the Verification Contract specifies in advance, paired with an independent re-observation after execution that the specific condition no longer holds and, where applicable, that the activation chain the repair concerned now completes end to end under a real or faithfully simulated invocation.

15.4 A specific admissibility failure is named because it defeats the strong-evidence requirement while superficially appearing to satisfy it: a Verification Contract that checks the after-state only, without ever confirming the before-state actually reproduced the Finding's condition, cannot distinguish a genuine repair from a Finding that was already stale or already resolved by an unrelated change before CRAIF's transaction ever executed. The failing-before requirement is not a formality; it is the only mechanism that rules out a Repair Certificate being issued for a problem the candidate never actually touched.

15.5 Weak evidence for a repair claim includes the executing role's own narrative account of what it did, a candidate's target-state assertion restated as though its restatement were proof of its own satisfaction, and — a corruption specific to this corpus's own object model — a Repair Intent's contributing Findings cited as ongoing proof of the repair's necessity after the repair has already been applied, which inverts the evidentiary direction entirely: a Finding proves a defect existed before the transaction; it cannot, after the fact, prove anything about whether the transaction resolved it.

15.6 Every claim this corpus's sibling datasets make about a specific mechanism working — CRAIF-01's runtime, CRAIF-02's simulation harness — is marked at authoring time with the same evidence-status vocabulary the estate already uses: VERIFIED for a claim with a reproducible demonstration on record, OBSERVED for a claim witnessed once without a controlled reproduction, INFERRED for a claim derived from related evidence rather than direct observation, HYPOTHESIS for an untested design assertion, PROPOSED for a mechanism specified but not yet built, and REJECTED for an approach this corpus's own adversarial review considered and discarded. No Part in this family may assert a mechanism's effectiveness at a VERIFIED tier without naming the specific reproduction that earns it.

15.7 The medium-tier classification for presence-only activation evidence, stated in 15.2, is not a permanent verdict on any specific transaction — it is the floor a Verification Contract must clear before its result may be treated as strong, and a transaction that only ever produces presence evidence for an activation-class Finding remains, honestly, at medium admissibility indefinitely, which is itself diagnostic information: a Failure Pattern whose repairs never accumulate strong evidence over many instances suggests either that the Activation Verification Contract required by Part VI is not actually being satisfied in practice, or that the pattern's target class genuinely cannot be verified beyond presence with the estate's current instrumentation, in which case that limitation belongs in a Learning Candidate of its own rather than being silently absorbed as an accepted ceiling.

15.8 A last admissibility distinction concerns evidence volume versus evidence independence, because the two are easy to conflate and only one of them strengthens a claim. Ten presence checks against ten different files touched by one repair do not sum to strong evidence for the repair's overall success; they remain ten pieces of medium-tier evidence about ten narrow facts, and their number does not change their tier. One independent, before-and-after, activation-verified demonstration against the single reality object the Finding actually concerned outweighs the ten presence checks combined, because admissibility tier is a property of what kind of question the evidence answers, not of how many instances of that same kind of answer have been collected — a distinction this corpus states explicitly because a runtime optimizing naively for "more evidence" will otherwise gravitate toward collecting cheap, narrow checks in volume rather than the one expensive, independent check that actually licenses the claim being made.

15.9 A final admissibility rule addresses evidence that ages between collection and use, a scenario distinct from the Finding-staleness discipline in Part III because it concerns evidence gathered specifically for a Verification Contract rather than evidence a producer originally supplied. A before-state reproduction obtained at Intake, hours before a transaction actually reaches Execution because it queued behind higher-priority work, does not automatically remain admissible as the "before" half of the before-and-after pair — if the target reality object could plausibly have changed for reasons unrelated to CRAIF's own transaction during the intervening period, the before-state must be re-confirmed immediately prior to Execution, not merely inherited from Intake, because a stale "before" measurement compared against a fresh "after" measurement can manufacture an apparent repair where an unrelated change, not CRAIF's candidate, actually produced the observed difference.

15.10 This corpus's evidence discipline closes with an explicit acknowledgment of its own limits: no admissibility rule stated in this Part can fully substitute for the estate's continuing vigilance against evidence fabrication in the strong sense — a producer or a verification method that is itself compromised, malicious, or systematically miscalibrated can, in principle, satisfy every rule this Part states while still misleading every consumer of its output. CRAIF's own defense against this deeper risk is not a rule internal to this Part but the composition discipline of Part II applied continuously: no single producer or verification method is trusted in isolation forever, and a Repair Certificate's downstream consumers — the estate's shared ledgers, the Decision Review Kernel's accountability record, the Institutional Immune System's memory — each retain the independent capacity to flag a pattern of certificates that later prove systematically unreliable, which is the estate-level check this corpus's own Part XIV names as the ultimate backstop against a compromised verification chain that no single admissibility rule alone can fully foreclose.

PART XV FINAL LAW

A repair claim requires a demonstrated before-and-after pair, not an after-state check alone — an after-only check cannot distinguish a genuine repair from a Finding that was already stale before the transaction touched it. Presence evidence for an activation-class repair is never strong evidence, only medium at best, because presence answers a narrower question than a repair claim requires, and the gap between the two questions is the exact gap this corpus's founding evidence found still open inside a sealed sibling corpus.


PART XVI — CLOSING: UNRESOLVED QUESTIONS, MATURITY MODEL, AND DONE CRITERIA

16.1 This corpus does not close every question it raises, and naming the ones it leaves open is itself a constitutional obligation rather than an admission of incompleteness, because a corpus that presents itself as having resolved every tension it touched would violate its own Part XV's prohibition on undemonstrated claims applied reflexively to itself.

16.2 The template-match blast-radius re-estimation tension named in Part IV.6 remains unresolved by design: whether an inherited blast-radius estimate from a prior confirmed instance may ever be trusted without independent re-derivation for the current target is left to accumulate evidence through CRAIF-01's own operation, with re-estimation required as the conservative default until that evidence exists, never relaxed first and measured after.

16.3 The emergency-stop-coextensive-with-policy-scope requirement named in Part XIV.7 is stated as a condition on any future L4 policy rather than as a mechanism this corpus itself builds, because L4 is not enabled at founding and specifying its emergency-stop mechanism in full, absent a concrete policy to specify it against, risks producing exactly the unfalsifiable, ungrounded specification Part IV.4 refuses to accept from a Repair Candidate — the same discipline applied reflexively to this corpus's own future extension.

16.4 A maturity model for CRAIF as a whole, rather than for any single mechanism within it, is stated in five stages: SPECIFIED, the state this corpus occupies at founding, in which every object, contract, and law is defined but none has executed against a real transaction; OBSERVED, in which the runtime described in CRAIF-01 has executed at L1 or L2 against real Findings and produced real Repair Outcomes, whether certified or failed; L3-QUALIFIED, in which at least one Failure Pattern class has accumulated sufficient confirmed L3 repairs to be considered for the evidence threshold Part VIII.5 requires; FEDERATED, in which CRAIF-02's cross-repo activation science has been exercised against at least one non-PP repository per Fixture Four's own signature; and INSTITUTIONALIZED, in which at least one Guardian Promotion Candidate produced by this corpus's own operation has completed the full local-recurrence-to-cross-project-validation pipeline and resulted in a standing guardian elsewhere in the estate. No stage may be claimed without the specific evidence Part XV requires for a VERIFIED claim at that stage.

16.5 Done criteria for CRAIF-00 specifically, as distinct from the family's overall sealing criteria stated in the family manifest, are: all sixteen Parts present in order, each closed by its own FINAL LAW; every object named in Parts III through VII cross-referenced consistently by every later Part and by both sibling datasets without contradiction; the non-duplication contract in Part XI naming every owner verified at content tier during this corpus's founding session, with no owner claimed by title alone; all five founding fixtures from Part XII represented, and confirmed present in at least one worked example in each sibling dataset; and zero executable code, zero markdown formatting, and zero contamination from any source-domain vocabulary this corpus was founded to exclude, verified by the family's contamination audit before sealing.

16.6 A third tension, not previously named, is stated here rather than left implicit: the relationship between CRAIF-00's own density and quality floor, stated in the family manifest as 1,200 to 2,500 words per Part, and the discipline against padding a Part to a word count rather than earning its length through genuine additional mechanism. This corpus was itself found, during its own founding session, to have first drafted below that floor, and every Part expanded afterward to clear it was expanded with a named worked example, a named edge case, or a named additional failure mode — never with restated summary or repeated phrasing — precisely because the same discipline this corpus applies to a Repair Candidate's falsifiability in Part IV applies reflexively to its own authoring: a Part lengthened without new mechanism has not cleared the floor honestly, regardless of its final word count.

16.7 The final word on this corpus's own scope is a reminder rather than a new rule: CRAIF-00 defines objects, laws, and a ladder; it executes nothing. Every claim of a mechanism actually working belongs to CRAIF-01 or CRAIF-02, evidenced per Part XV, and any reader who finishes this dataset believing a repair has been demonstrated has read a constitution and mistaken it for an operating system already running. The distance between the two is exactly the distance this corpus exists to make visible rather than to quietly collapse.

16.8 A last obligation belongs to whoever resumes this corpus's authoring across a future session rather than to any reader evaluating it as finished: every object, contract, and law defined across Parts III through XV is binding on CRAIF-01 and CRAIF-02 exactly as written, and a future Part that finds one of these definitions inconvenient for the mechanism it wants to specify must amend this corpus explicitly, through the same evidence-and-review discipline Part XI.9 requires for any extension, rather than silently reinterpreting a term this dataset already fixed. A Repair Intent, a Finding, a Repair Candidate, and every other named object carry exactly the fields and only the fields defined here; a sibling dataset that needs an additional field has found a genuine gap in this constitution, and the gap is closed by returning to amend CRAIF-00, not by inventing a shadow field inside CRAIF-01 or CRAIF-02 that this dataset never sanctioned.

16.9 A concluding synthesis draws the sixteen Parts together into the single operational habit an implementer or a reviewer should carry forward, because a constitution read Part by Part can lose the shape that only becomes visible when it is read as one continuous argument. Part I established that a gap exists between detection and closure that no other system in the estate was chartered to fill. Part II bound the runtime that fills it to consult rather than annex the authorities surrounding it. Parts III through VII gave that consultation a fixed vocabulary of objects so that every producer, every authority, and every consumer downstream speaks the same shape regardless of how many new detectors the estate adds in the future. Part VIII bounded how much the runtime may do without re-confirmation, and Part IX bounded who may do it, as two independent axes that do not substitute for each other. Part X named the three laws that follow from the first nine Parts read together. Part XI drew the boundary this corpus must never cross into a neighboring system's science. Part XII fixed the evidence that founded the whole undertaking as permanent, checkable cases rather than fading narrative. Part XIII defined the metric by which the whole apparatus is judged, with its gaming vectors named at birth. Part XIV catalogued how every one of the preceding mechanisms can fail, in both directions, so failure is anticipated rather than discovered fresh in production. Part XV fixed what counts as proof for any claim the corpus or its operation makes. This Part closes by admitting what remains open, because a constitution's honesty is measured as much by what it declines to claim as by what it asserts.

16.10 The single sentence a future reader should retain above all others in this dataset, more load-bearing than any individual Part's own final law: a repair is not verified because it was attempted, applied, or reported successful by the actor that performed it — it is verified when an authority independent of that actor, consulted before the fact and re-observing after, confirms that the specific condition the transaction was authorized to change has actually changed, and not before.

PART XVI FINAL LAW

A corpus that claims to have resolved every tension it touched has violated, reflexively, the same prohibition on undemonstrated claims it exists to enforce on the repairs it governs. Two tensions are named here as deliberately open — template blast-radius trust, and emergency-stop scope pending an L4 policy that does not yet exist — and both remain open until evidence, not convenience, closes them.
