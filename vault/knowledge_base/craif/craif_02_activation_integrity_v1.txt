Closed-Loop Repair & Activation Integrity Fabric — the activation axis
Version 1. Founded 2026-07-21. Composes CRAIF-00 in full; consumed by CRAIF-01 Part IX and Part XVIII as the Activation Verification Contract producer.


PART I — MISSION

1.1 Every capability in Claude Power Pack passes through a chain of stages between the moment a condition in the world makes it relevant and the moment it actually runs, and no dataset in the estate before this one specified that chain as a first-class object of study. `modules/liveness/reachability.py` answers whether a module is reachable from some live surface at all; SQI answers whether a repository's tests and executable claims correspond to reality; neither answers the narrower and, at this corpus's founding, more frequently violated question of whether a capability that is reachable, tested, and correctly implemented actually fires when the specific real-world condition it was built for occurs.

1.2 This corpus's founding evidence is CRAIF-00 Part XII's Fixture One and Fixture Four, restated here in their operational form rather than their historical form: a skill whose own activation vocabulary lived inside the skill's body, invisible to any external trigger; and a capability that fired correctly in its home repository and nowhere else, discovered only because an Owner manually checked thirty projects and counted. Both fixtures share a signature this corpus exists to make checkable in general rather than merely narratable after the fact: a gap between a capability's presence and its activation, invisible to any scanner whose own definition of reachability presupposes that a selection event already occurred.

1.3 CRAIF-02 therefore owns two things, and only two: the ontology and science of the activation chain itself — what stages it has, what can fail at each stage, and what evidence proves a stage completed — and the simulation harness that exercises that chain hermetically, without waiting for a real session to expose a defect the way Fixture One's own discovery required. Neither of these two things is owned anywhere else in the estate at this corpus's founding, verified at content tier rather than assumed from absence during this session's own audit of `modules/liveness/`, SQI's constitution, and the JIT loader's existing implementation.

1.4 A boundary stated plainly before any mechanism follows: this corpus does not own general module reachability, which remains `modules/liveness/reachability.py`'s domain exactly as CRAIF-00 Part XI fixes; does not own repository-level test or executable-claim reality, which remains SQI's; does not own drift or semantic staleness between documentation and implementation, which remains DAIF-21's; and does not own the decision of whether an activation defect, once found, warrants a repair — that decision, and the transaction that executes any resulting repair, belongs entirely to CRAIF-01, with this corpus supplying evidence and a verification contract as inputs rather than executing anything itself.

1.5 The distinction between activation and reachability deserves a concrete worked contrast, because the two are the single most common conflation a reader unfamiliar with this corpus's boundary will make on first encounter. A module can be perfectly reachable — imported cleanly, called by a live code path, covered by a passing test — while never actually activating under the specific real-world trigger condition its design intends, because the trigger-recognition logic that is supposed to invoke it along that live path contains a keyword mismatch, a priority collision with a competing skill, or a repo-profile gate that silently excludes the current project. Reachability answers "can this run if invoked correctly"; activation answers "does the correct invocation actually occur," and the second question has, at this corpus's founding, no owner anywhere else in the estate.

1.6 A second worked contrast separates activation from mere presence in a manifest or index. A skill listed in a SKILL.md catalogue, a hook registered in settings.json, and a module imported by an entrypoint file are each, individually, evidence that a capability exists in the estate's inventory — none of them, individually or combined, is evidence that the specific trigger condition the capability was built for was ever actually recognized and routed to it during a real session. CRAIF-02's entire ontology in Part II exists to give this distinction a checkable vocabulary rather than leaving it as an intuition a reviewer either applies or forgets.

1.7 This dataset's content, like its two siblings, is a specification rather than an operating system at the time of its authoring; every claim it makes about the simulation harness's behavior is marked, per CRAIF-00 Part XV's evidence discipline, as PROPOSED unless a specific reproduction is cited, and no Part in this corpus asserts the harness has executed against a real activation chain, because none had at authoring time.

1.8 The dataset's ordering follows the activation chain's own sequence rather than a topical grouping, mirroring CRAIF-01's own ordering discipline stated in its Part I.7, because a reader trying to understand why a specific cross-repo failure occurred should be able to trace forward through the exact stages the chain itself passes through before reaching the point of failure, in the same order the chain itself executes them.

1.9 A final scoping note addresses the JIT skill loader and the SessionStart / UserPromptSubmit hook infrastructure this corpus's mechanisms will repeatedly reference: CRAIF-02 does not rebuild, replace, or fork any part of that existing loading infrastructure. It specifies the ontology that describes what the loader does, the science that predicts where it can fail, and the harness that tests it hermetically — the loader itself, and any future improvement to its actual code, belongs to whichever module or skill currently owns that code, consulted and never annexed, per the same consultation discipline CRAIF-00 Part II establishes for the whole family.

1.10 A precise handoff mechanics note closes this corpus's own scope statement: the moment CRAIF-02's diagnostic procedure, specified across Parts III through IX, confirms a candidate defect matches a taxonomy member from Part IV, the object handed to CRAIF-01 is a Finding — not a Repair Intent, and not a repair candidate — because normalizing a Finding into an Intent, per CRAIF-00 Part IV, and selecting a specific candidate to address it, per CRAIF-00 Part IV and CRAIF-01 Part V, are judgments this corpus does not make. CRAIF-02's own contribution to the handed-off Finding is exactly three fields beyond what CRAIF-00's general Finding taxonomy already requires: the specific Activation Chain segment implicated, the specific Part IV taxonomy member matched, and the before-state harness run confirming the match under simulation rather than mere pattern-matching against the taxonomy's own description text. A Finding missing any of these three fields is, for an activation-class defect specifically, incomplete under this corpus's own standard, even if it would otherwise satisfy CRAIF-00's general Finding requirements — because a Finding that merely asserts "this looks like a bootstrap defect" without a harness run to back the assertion has performed a pattern match against Part IV's prose, not the diagnostic procedure Part IV.8 actually requires.

PART I FINAL LAW

CRAIF-02 owns the activation chain as a first-class scientific object and the simulation harness that exercises it hermetically — nothing else. Reachability, test reality, and semantic drift belong to their existing owners; this corpus answers the one question none of them was built to answer: does the correct invocation actually occur, and how would anyone know before an Owner had to check by hand.


PART II — ACTIVATION CHAIN ONTOLOGY

2.1 An Activation Chain is the ordered sequence of segments a capability's invocation passes through between a real-world condition and an observable, cleaned-up result: Intent, Trigger, Skill-Selection, Loader, Context-Injection, Hook, Module, Runtime-Action, Result, Evidence, and Cleanup. Each segment is a distinct point of potential failure, and a chain's overall integrity is never stronger than its weakest segment — a defect at Trigger renders every downstream segment irrelevant regardless of how correctly Module and Runtime-Action would have behaved had they been reached.

2.2 Intent is the segment representing the actual condition in the world that ought to cause activation — a user's literal words, a file-system state, a scheduled time, an error pattern in a log — and it is deliberately defined independently of any system's interpretation of it, because conflating the raw condition with a system's interpretation of that condition is exactly how a trigger-visibility defect goes undetected: the interpretation can be wrong while everyone downstream continues to reason as though the raw Intent itself had been faithfully captured.

2.3 Trigger is the segment where some mechanism — a keyword match, a regex, a semantic classifier, a file-path pattern — converts an Intent into a candidate activation signal. This is the segment Fixture One's defect lived in: a Trigger definition stored where no external mechanism could read it before the very capability it was meant to activate had already loaded, producing a Trigger that existed on paper and never actually fired for any real Intent it was designed to recognize.

2.4 Skill-Selection is the segment where, given one or more candidate Trigger matches, a specific skill, hook, or module is chosen to receive the activation — and where Part VII's competing-skills problem lives, because more than one capability's Trigger may fire on an overlapping Intent, and the selection logic's own correctness is a distinct question from whether any individual Trigger fired correctly in isolation.

2.5 Loader is the segment where the selected capability's actual content — a SKILL.md body, a module's code, a hook's script — is retrieved and prepared for execution, and it is the segment where Cross-Repository Activation Failure, Fixture Four, lives: a Loader that resolves correctly inside one repository's directory structure and fails silently, or resolves to a stale or absent artifact, inside a structurally different one.

2.6 Context-Injection is the segment where the loaded content is actually placed into the executing session's available context, subject to the budget constraints Part IX specifies — a Loader that retrieves the correct content but a Context-Injection step that truncates, deduplicates incorrectly, or drops the content under budget pressure produces exactly the same observable failure, a capability that never actually influenced the session's behavior, as a Trigger or Loader defect would have, despite both earlier segments having worked correctly.

2.7 Hook is the segment specific to event-driven activation — a PreToolUse, PostToolUse, SessionStart, or Stop event registered to fire a specific script — and it is the segment where Fixture Three, Hook and Settings Divergence, lives: a hook script that is itself correct in isolation, never actually registered in the settings artifact that would cause the runtime to invoke it on the corresponding event.

2.8 Module and Runtime-Action are the segments where the activated capability's own code actually executes and produces an effect, and this corpus treats these two segments as largely reachability's and SQI's own territory respectively — CRAIF-02's interest in them is narrow, limited to confirming that execution actually occurred as a consequence of the upstream chain, not to auditing the executing code's own internal correctness, which those sibling systems already own.

2.9 Result, Evidence, and Cleanup are the closing segments: Result is the externally observable effect the activation produced; Evidence is the durable record that the activation occurred and produced that Result, without which no later Verification Contract in CRAIF-01 Part IX could confirm the chain completed; and Cleanup is the segment ensuring any temporary state the activation created — a lock file, a session flag, a partial write — is resolved regardless of whether the activation ultimately succeeded, because an activation chain that leaves Cleanup undone corrupts the evidentiary basis for every subsequent activation attempted against the same target.

2.10 A chain's segments are not always all present for every capability class — a purely reactive hook has no meaningful Skill-Selection segment distinct from its own Trigger, for instance — and this corpus does not require every capability to instantiate all eleven segments. It requires instead that whichever segments a given capability class does instantiate be named explicitly, per capability, in the Activation Verification Contract Part XII specifies, so that a claim of "activation verified" is always scoped to the specific segments that were actually checked rather than implicitly claiming coverage of segments the check never touched.

2.11 A worked trace situates the ontology concretely: a user's literal request (Intent) is matched by a keyword pattern (Trigger) against three candidate skills, one is chosen by priority ordering (Skill-Selection), its SKILL.md body is retrieved from disk (Loader), injected into the session's available context within budget (Context-Injection), and — in this specific worked case, a skill rather than a hook — no Hook segment applies; the skill's own instructions then govern subsequent tool calls (Module and Runtime-Action), producing an observable file change (Result), a session-log entry recording the change (Evidence), and a released lock on the target file (Cleanup). Every segment in this trace is independently falsifiable, and a defect at any one of them produces a distinct, attributable failure signature rather than one generic "it didn't work."

2.12 A question this ontology resolves explicitly, because leaving it implicit invites inconsistent interpretation across future Parts: whether the eleven segments in 2.1 must execute in strict, non-overlapping sequence, or whether some tolerate partial overlap. The answer distinguishes two segment classes. Intent through Module are strictly sequential — each later segment's own definition presupposes the immediately prior segment's completion, and no capability's own design may begin Loader before Skill-Selection has concluded, for instance, without collapsing the very distinction Part VII's priority science depends on. Result, Evidence, and Cleanup, by contrast, tolerate limited overlap: an Evidence record may begin capturing a Runtime-Action's observable effect while Cleanup of an unrelated temporary resource proceeds concurrently, provided Cleanup's own completion is never treated as evidence that Evidence's capture also completed, and vice versa. This distinction matters operationally because the Simulation Harness in Part X must know, for any given corpus case, whether an observed segment ordering that deviates from the strict sequence for Intent through Module represents a genuine chain defect or, for the tolerant final three segments, an acceptable execution variance — collapsing the two segment classes into one uniform ordering requirement would cause the harness to flag legitimate concurrent Evidence-and-Cleanup execution as a false-positive ordering defect.

PART II FINAL LAW

An Activation Chain has eleven possible segments, and a chain's integrity is bounded by its weakest instantiated segment, never by the average or the majority of segments that worked. A capability that fails once, anywhere in this sequence, has failed to activate regardless of how many downstream segments would have behaved correctly had they been reached.


PART III — TRIGGER VISIBILITY SCIENCE

3.1 A Trigger is visible, in the sense this corpus requires, exactly when its definition is represented in an artifact external to the capability it activates and readable by the selection mechanism before that capability has been loaded. This single test — externality prior to loading — is the entire content of trigger visibility science, and Fixture One's defect is the concrete case of a Trigger that failed this test: its vocabulary lived inside the skill body, readable only after the skill had already been selected and loaded, producing a circular dependency no external scanner's own reachability logic could detect, because that logic itself presupposes a prior selection event.

3.2 The externality test is deliberately binary rather than graduated, because a Trigger that is "mostly" external — visible to the selection mechanism for some invocation paths but not others — behaves, for the invocation paths it is not visible on, identically to a Trigger that is not external at all. A description card, a routing table entry, or a keyword list stored in a location the selection mechanism actually reads before loading satisfies the test; the identical content restated inside the capability's own body, even verbatim, does not, regardless of how complete or well-written that restatement is.

3.3 A Trigger's visibility is distinct from its accuracy, and this corpus deliberately separates the two properties rather than conflating them under one label, because a Trigger can be perfectly visible and still fail to recognize the Intent it was built for — an externally-readable keyword list that omits a synonym a real user would plausibly use is an accuracy defect, not a visibility defect, and the two require entirely different remedies: a visibility defect requires relocating the Trigger's definition to an externally readable surface, while an accuracy defect requires expanding or correcting the Trigger's own recognition logic without necessarily moving it anywhere.

3.4 A visibility audit, as this corpus specifies it, is a mechanical check rather than a judgment call: for a given capability, locate every artifact the selection mechanism actually reads prior to loading that capability, and confirm the capability's Trigger definition appears, in full, in at least one of them. A Trigger definition split across an external summary card and additional detail inside the capability's own body passes this audit only if the external summary alone is sufficient for the selection mechanism to recognize the relevant class of Intent — a summary card that says "see the skill body for activation criteria" without stating any criteria itself fails the audit exactly as completely as no external artifact at all.

3.5 The estate's own JIT skill loader architecture, referenced but not owned by this corpus, already implements a partial answer to this science in its latent-card mechanism: a compact, always-loaded summary card per skill, with the skill's full body loaded only on a matching trigger. This corpus's contribution is not that mechanism itself but the science that explains why it is the correct shape — the card is the externally-visible Trigger surface, the full body is the Loader-segment content that only becomes relevant after Skill-Selection has already occurred — and the checkable requirement that any new skill added to the estate is audited against 3.4's mechanical test before being declared activation-safe, rather than assumed safe because the loader mechanism exists in general.

3.6 A specific corruption of visibility, distinct from the missing-external-artifact case Fixture One represents, is named because it is easy to introduce without noticing: a Trigger definition that is externally visible but conditioned on a state the selection mechanism cannot itself observe without already having loaded the capability — for instance, a Trigger stated as "activate when the project's internal configuration flag X is set," where reading flag X requires opening a file only the capability's own Loader segment knows how to parse. This is visibility in name only; the selection mechanism cannot actually evaluate the condition without performing work that belongs, properly, to a later segment, and the practical failure mode is identical to Fixture One's despite the Trigger definition technically living in an external file.

3.7 A worked negative case illustrates 3.6 concretely: a hypothetical skill whose external card states it should activate "when working with the project's custom build format," without further specifying any file extension, keyword, or directory pattern the selection mechanism can actually check. A human reading the card understands the intent; a mechanical selection process has nothing computable to evaluate, and the Trigger silently never fires for any real Intent because the selection mechanism has no way to recognize the described condition without already possessing domain knowledge the card never actually encoded as a checkable rule.

3.8 Trigger visibility science therefore requires two properties jointly, restated as the corpus's own operational definition: external representation, per 3.1, and mechanical evaluability, per 3.6 and 3.7 — a condition statable in terms the selection mechanism can actually check without first performing work reserved for a later segment. A Trigger satisfying only one of the two properties has not achieved visibility in the sense this corpus's science requires, and CRAIF-02's own audit procedure in Part V tests both properties independently rather than treating external placement alone as sufficient.

3.9 A closing distinction separates trigger visibility from trigger priority, addressed fully in Part VII: visibility asks whether a Trigger can be seen and evaluated at all; priority asks, once multiple visible Triggers evaluate positively for the same Intent, which one wins. A corpus that solved priority perfectly while leaving visibility unaddressed would still permit Fixture One's exact failure mode, because an invisible Trigger never enters the priority contest in the first place — it is simply absent from consideration, which is a strictly worse failure than losing a priority contest it was at least a candidate in.

3.10 A worked extension addresses a case this Part's examples have so far left implicit: how the externality and evaluability tests in 3.1 and 3.6 apply to a Trigger whose Intent, per Part II.2, has no natural-language form at all — a scheduled or cron-style activation, where the real-world condition is a clock reaching a specified time rather than a user's request. Externality applies identically: the schedule definition must be readable by the selection mechanism from an artifact external to the capability's own body, exactly as a keyword pattern must be. Evaluability applies with one adaptation: the mechanically checkable condition is a timestamp comparison rather than a text match, and this corpus requires the comparison's own inputs — the schedule expression and the current time source — both be resolvable by the selection mechanism without invoking any part of the capability being scheduled. A scheduled Trigger whose schedule expression is itself computed by logic inside the capability's own body — for instance, a capability that decides its own next run time based on internal state only it can read — fails evaluability by the identical mechanism 3.6's build-format example fails it, despite the schedule looking, superficially, like ordinary external configuration.

PART III FINAL LAW

A Trigger is visible only when its definition is both externally represented, readable before the capability it activates has loaded, and mechanically evaluable, statable in terms the selection mechanism can actually check without performing work reserved for a later segment. A Trigger satisfying only one of these two properties has not achieved visibility, and an invisible Trigger never enters a priority contest — it is simply absent, which is worse than losing one.


PART IV — BOOTSTRAP DEPENDENCY DEFECT TAXONOMY

4.1 Fixture One, formalized in CRAIF-00 Part XII as the Activation Bootstrap Defect, is the founding instance of a broader taxonomy this Part names in full, because a corpus that treats Fixture One as a single unrepeatable historical accident rather than the first observed instance of a general class would leave every structurally identical future defect unrecognized until it, too, required an Owner's manual discovery.

4.2 SELF-REFERENTIAL VOCABULARY is the taxonomy's first and most direct member, identical in shape to Fixture One itself: a capability's activation criteria stored exclusively inside the capability's own body, requiring the capability to have already been selected and loaded before its own activation vocabulary becomes readable. The checkable signature, restated from CRAIF-00 12.2 in this corpus's own operational terms: query every artifact the selection mechanism reads prior to loading a given capability, per Part III's visibility audit, and confirm the capability's own body is not the only such artifact.

4.3 CIRCULAR LOADER DEPENDENCY is a related but distinct member: a capability whose Loader segment itself depends on a resource that only becomes available after some other capability, which in turn depends on the first capability's own output, has activated — a mutual dependency neither side can resolve first. Unlike self-referential vocabulary, which blocks Trigger visibility specifically, a circular loader dependency can afflict a capability with a perfectly visible, externally-readable Trigger whose Loader segment nonetheless never successfully completes, because the artifact it needs to load never becomes available without the very activation it is blocking.

4.4 PHANTOM TRIGGER is a third member, distinct from both: a Trigger definition that is externally visible and superficially well-formed but whose evaluable condition can never actually be satisfied by any real Intent the selection mechanism will encounter — a keyword pattern referencing a term no user would plausibly type, a file-path pattern referencing a directory structure the estate's actual repositories never use, or a condition that depends on a state field that no producer in the estate ever actually sets. A phantom Trigger passes a superficial visibility audit while never firing in practice, and it is distinguished from a genuine visibility defect precisely because the defect is not in where the Trigger lives but in whether the condition it describes is ever actually reachable by a real Intent.

4.5 STALE BOOTSTRAP REFERENCE is a fourth member specific to multi-file activation architectures: a Trigger or Loader segment that correctly references an external artifact at the time the capability was authored, where that artifact is later renamed, relocated, or restructured by an unrelated change, silently breaking the reference without breaking anything a code-level test would catch, because the reference itself may be syntactically valid — a file path that resolves to nothing, or resolves to a different, now-unrelated file — while remaining structurally well-formed.

4.6 A worked contrast distinguishes self-referential vocabulary from stale bootstrap reference precisely, because both can present with the identical observable symptom of a capability that "used to work" or "should work" but silently does not: self-referential vocabulary was never externally visible from the moment of authoring, a defect present at birth; stale bootstrap reference was externally visible and correctly resolving at authoring time, and became broken later, through an unrelated change elsewhere in the estate, a defect introduced by drift rather than present at birth. The remedy differs accordingly — self-referential vocabulary requires relocating the Trigger's definition; stale bootstrap reference requires either repairing the broken reference or, where the drift is itself systemic, a DAIF-21 semantic-drift finding rather than a CRAIF-02 activation finding, because the underlying cause is documentation-to-implementation drift, not an activation-chain defect per se, even though its symptom is identical.

4.7 A fifth member, ORPHANED ACTIVATION SURFACE, names the inverse of the taxonomy's other members: an externally visible, mechanically evaluable, non-phantom Trigger whose Loader segment resolves correctly and whose Module segment executes correctly, but which no Skill-Selection logic anywhere in the estate actually routes any real Intent toward, because the capability was registered in a catalogue no live selection mechanism consults. This is activation's own analogue of Fixture Two's reachability debt, and this corpus's non-duplication contract requires that a finding of this shape be routed to `modules/liveness/reachability.py` as a reachability finding first, with CRAIF-02 contributing only the specific activation-chain evidence that the Trigger and Loader segments, considered alone, were not the cause.

4.8 The taxonomy's five members are not mutually exclusive in a single defective capability — a capability could plausibly exhibit a stale bootstrap reference that, once repaired, reveals an underlying phantom Trigger the stale reference had been masking — and this corpus's own diagnostic procedure, exercised through the simulation harness in Part X, is required to check each capability against all five members independently rather than stopping at the first member that produces a positive match, because stopping early risks declaring a capability activation-safe once one defect is repaired while a second, masked defect remains.

4.9 A closing note on taxonomy growth mirrors CRAIF-00 Part XII's own closing discipline on fixture growth: this taxonomy's five members are the founding minimum, drawn from the estate's own observed history at this corpus's founding session, not a claim of completeness. A future sixth member proposed for permanent status in this taxonomy must meet the same corroboration bar CRAIF-00 12.11 requires of a candidate sixth negative fixture — independent observation across more than one occurrence — before this Part is amended to include it.

4.10 This taxonomy's relationship to CRAIF-00 Part XIV's own failure-mode catalogue is one of distinct layers rather than overlap, and the distinction is worth stating explicitly because both catalogues use the word "failure" and a reader moving between the two datasets could otherwise conflate them. CRAIF-00 Part XIV catalogues failures of the repair-verification and authority apparatus itself — false healing, self-granted authority, thrashing — failures in how a Finding, once it exists, is acted upon. This Part's five members catalogue failures in how a Finding of the activation-defect class comes to exist in the first place, at the level of the chain segment itself, before any Repair Intent has been opened. A self-referential-vocabulary defect and a false-healing repair are never the same event: the former is a property of a capability's own Trigger design, discoverable by this corpus's harness before any repair is attempted; the latter is a property of a repair's own verification having been satisfied on paper without the underlying capability actually working, discoverable only after a repair transaction has already executed. A single incident can exhibit both in sequence — a bootstrap defect diagnosed correctly, and then a subsequent repair falsely certified — but they remain two distinct failures at two distinct layers, and this corpus's own taxonomy makes no claim about the second layer, which belongs entirely to CRAIF-00 and CRAIF-01.

PART IV FINAL LAW

Bootstrap dependency defects come in at least five distinct shapes — self-referential vocabulary, circular loader dependency, phantom trigger, stale bootstrap reference, and orphaned activation surface — and they are not mutually exclusive within a single capability. A diagnostic that stops at the first matching member risks declaring a capability safe while a second, masked defect of a different shape remains entirely undetected.


PART V — SYNTHETIC AND REAL ACTIVATION CORPUS DESIGN

5.1 A simulation harness specified without a concrete corpus of cases to exercise it is, per CRAIF-00 12.9's own caution, an elaborate architecture never actually tested against a real failure — the same architecture-without-exercise gap SQI's own founding evidence identified in a neighboring domain, restated here as a risk this corpus must not repeat in its own specification. This Part therefore fixes the corpus design before Part X specifies the harness that consumes it, so the harness is never described in the abstract, only ever in terms of a concrete, checkable set of cases it must handle.

5.2 The corpus has two sources, and this corpus requires both rather than either alone. The real source is CRAIF-00 Part XII's five founding fixtures, each replayed as a concrete, specific case per Part XIII of this dataset — not a paraphrase of the fixture's narrative, but a faithfully reconstructed instance with a real or precisely reconstructed Trigger definition, Loader path, and expected Result, satisfying CRAIF-00 12.8's own requirement against citing a fixture's title without exercising its signature.

5.3 The synthetic source is a generated set of cases constructed by systematically varying each segment of the Activation Chain ontology in Part II against a known-correct baseline capability, producing deliberately defective variants — a Trigger relocated inside the capability body to synthesize a self-referential-vocabulary case, a Loader path deliberately pointed at a renamed file to synthesize a stale-bootstrap-reference case — so that every member of Part IV's taxonomy has at least one synthetic exemplar the harness can replay without depending on a real historical incident having occurred for every taxonomy member.

5.4 Synthetic case generation is bounded by an explicit realism requirement: a synthetic defect must be constructible by a plausible authoring mistake, not merely a mechanically possible one, because an unrealistic synthetic case — a Trigger definition corrupted in a way no human or automated authoring process would ever actually produce — tests the harness's ability to detect an input it will never actually encounter, wasting the harness's own coverage on a case with zero probability mass in the estate's real authoring process.

5.5 The corpus's real and synthetic cases are each tagged with the specific taxonomy member or founding fixture they represent, per Part IV and Part XIII respectively, and a case belonging to more than one category — a synthetic case that happens to reproduce a founding fixture's exact structural shape — is tagged with both, because the harness's own coverage reporting in Part X depends on knowing precisely which taxonomy members and which founding fixtures a given corpus run actually exercised, not merely how many cases ran in total.

5.6 A corpus entry's minimum required fields, stated explicitly so a future case addition is unambiguous about what it must supply, are: the case's origin, real or synthetic; the specific defect or absence of defect it represents, tagged against Part IV's taxonomy or marked as a known-correct control case; the full Activation Chain trace expected under correct behavior; the specific segment or segments at which the case's defect, if any, is expected to manifest; and the evidence artifact the harness should produce to confirm whether the expected manifestation occurred.

5.7 Known-correct control cases are a required corpus category distinct from both real and synthetic defect cases, because a harness tested only against defective inputs risks a symmetric failure this corpus's own catalogue in Part XIV names directly: a harness tuned to flag every case as defective would score perfectly against a defect-only corpus while producing false positives against every legitimate activation, and control cases — capabilities the corpus asserts, with justification, activate correctly — are the only mechanism that can surface this specific failure mode.

5.8 The corpus is versioned and append-only in principle: an existing case, once added, is never silently removed or altered, because a harness whose historical coverage claims depend on a corpus that quietly shrinks or changes underfoot cannot support a durable claim about what has actually been tested over time. A case found to be malformed is marked deprecated with the reason stated, retained for audit purposes, and superseded by a corrected replacement case rather than edited in place.

5.9 A worked illustration of corpus construction for one taxonomy member, Phantom Trigger, clarifies the design concretely: the real-source case is a specific hypothetical description-card wording that references a term no observed user request in the estate's own session history ever used; the synthetic-source case is a known-correct capability's Trigger keyword deliberately replaced with a term systematically absent from any plausible request corpus; both cases share the expected-trace field showing Skill-Selection never occurring, and both are tagged PHANTOM-TRIGGER alongside their respective origin tags.

5.10 The corpus's completeness is explicitly bounded rather than claimed as exhaustive: it covers, at founding, the five real fixtures and at least one synthetic exemplar per Part IV taxonomy member, and this corpus states plainly that a taxonomy member added later, per Part IV.9's corroboration discipline, requires a corresponding corpus addition before the harness can claim coverage of it — a taxonomy entry without a corpus case is, per this corpus's own evidence discipline, a HYPOTHESIS about a failure mode, not yet a tested one.

5.11 A governance note addresses who may add a case to the corpus this Part specifies, because a corpus with no stated authorship discipline risks exactly the quality drift Part V.4's realism requirement exists to prevent. CRAIF-02 is the corpus's steward, responsible for its structural integrity — the required fields in 5.6, the tagging discipline in 5.5, the append-only discipline in 5.8 — but authorship of a new case's content is open to any capability author who has identified a plausible defect or a legitimate known-correct control case in their own capability's design, exactly as CRAIF-01's Learning Candidate pipeline is open to any producer rather than reserved to CRAIF-01's own runtime. A proposed case is admitted to the standing corpus only once it satisfies 5.6's required fields and, for a proposed defect case, once its taxonomy tag is confirmed against Part IV rather than self-assigned by the proposing author — the same consultation discipline this corpus applies everywhere else, applied reflexively to its own corpus's growth.

PART V FINAL LAW

A simulation harness is only as credible as the corpus it runs against, and a corpus built from only one source — real fixtures alone, or synthetic generation alone — leaves a gap the other source exists to close. Every taxonomy member requires at least one corpus case before the harness may claim to cover it, and a taxonomy entry without a case is a hypothesis, not yet a tested failure mode.


PART VI — POSITIVE, NEGATIVE, AND AMBIGUOUS ACTIVATION CASES

6.1 Every corpus case, per Part V, resolves to one of three verdict classes when replayed through the harness: a Positive case, in which the expected activation occurs correctly end to end; a Negative case, in which the expected activation is correctly withheld — a Trigger correctly failing to fire for an Intent it was never meant to recognize; and an Ambiguous case, in which the correct verdict genuinely depends on context the harness's own replay cannot fully reconstruct, and this Part specifies how each class is handled distinctly rather than collapsed into a single pass-fail signal.

6.2 A Positive case's evidentiary bar is the full Activation Chain trace, per Part II, completing through every segment the capability under test actually instantiates, with Evidence recorded at each completed segment — a Positive case that completes eight of eleven applicable segments and silently stops is not a passed Positive case, it is a partial-activation Negative-adjacent finding requiring its own investigation under Part XIV's failure-mode catalogue.

6.3 A Negative case's evidentiary bar is the inverse and, in a specific sense this corpus names explicitly, harder to satisfy convincingly: proving a Trigger correctly did not fire requires confirming the absence of activation was a deliberate non-match rather than a silent failure at an earlier segment that happened to produce the same observable absence. A Negative case's expected trace therefore specifies not merely "no activation occurred" but the specific segment — almost always Trigger or Skill-Selection — at which the correct non-match was determined, so a harness run that shows no activation for a different, unintended reason is distinguishable from one showing the intended non-match.

6.4 Ambiguous cases are the class this corpus's own honesty requires naming rather than silently forcing into a false Positive or Negative verdict, because some real Intents genuinely sit at a boundary a Trigger's own design was never meant to resolve crisply — a request whose wording plausibly matches two skills' Triggers with comparable strength, where either selection would be defensible. An Ambiguous case's expected outcome is not a single correct activation but a bounded acceptable set — this skill or that one, but not a third — and the harness records which member of the acceptable set actually occurred rather than scoring the run as a binary pass or fail.

6.5 The distinction between an Ambiguous case and a genuine Priority defect, addressed operationally in Part VII, matters because the two produce a superficially similar symptom — more than one plausible activation outcome — from causes this corpus treats very differently: an Ambiguous case is a property of the Intent itself, irreducible by any amount of Trigger refinement without changing what the Intent actually means; a Priority defect is a property of the selection mechanism's own resolution logic, correctable by fixing that logic without touching the underlying Triggers at all. A corpus case must be classified as one or the other before it is added, not left to the harness to disambiguate at run time, because the harness's own verdict logic for the two classes differs.

6.6 A worked Ambiguous case illustrates the distinction concretely: a request phrased in a way that plausibly invokes either a general-purpose documentation skill or a project-specific formatting skill, where a reasonable author could defend either selection depending on unstated context about what the requester actually needed. This is tagged Ambiguous, with both skills in the acceptable set, rather than treated as evidence that one skill's Trigger needs tightening — tightening either Trigger to exclude this case would only push the same underlying ambiguity onto some other, differently-worded request that shares the identical genuine uncertainty.

6.7 By contrast, a worked Priority-defect case involves two Triggers that both fire correctly and unambiguously — neither Trigger's own recognition logic is in question — but the selection mechanism's tie-breaking rule resolves inconsistently across otherwise identical runs, sometimes selecting one, sometimes the other, for the same input. This is not tagged Ambiguous; it is tagged as a Part VII priority defect, because the underlying Intent here is not genuinely uncertain, only the selection mechanism's own resolution of an uncertainty that should have been deterministic.

6.8 A harness run's aggregate report, produced after replaying the full corpus, separates its three verdict classes explicitly rather than reporting one blended pass rate, because a blended rate that averages Positive-case success against Ambiguous-case acceptable-set membership would obscure exactly the distinction 6.5 requires be kept visible — a harness that appears to be improving because its Ambiguous cases are being scored more leniently over time has not necessarily improved its actual Trigger or Priority logic at all.

6.9 A specific discipline governs reclassification: a case tagged Ambiguous at corpus authoring time may later be reclassified as a genuine Priority defect, or vice versa, only on the strength of new evidence — a repeated real occurrence that clarifies which class it actually belongs to — never merely because a harness run's current pass rate would look better under the alternate classification. Reclassifying a case to improve a reported metric without new evidence is the specific corruption CRAIF-00 Part XIV.9's Metric-Driven Candidate Avoidance names in a neighboring context, restated here as a caution against gaming this corpus's own harness output.

6.10 This Part closes with an explicit statement of proportion, because a corpus dominated by Ambiguous cases would suggest the underlying Trigger design across the estate is itself poorly specified rather than that ambiguity is an irreducible, occasional property of real requests: this corpus expects the overwhelming majority of real corpus cases, per Part V, to resolve as Positive or Negative, with Ambiguous cases remaining a small, explicitly bounded minority, and a corpus run in which Ambiguous cases exceed a small fraction of the total is itself a finding — that the estate's Trigger definitions are systematically underspecified — rather than a property of the requests being tested.

6.11 A worked numeric illustration, using synthetic figures in the manner of CRAIF-00 13.7's own worked OIII example, clarifies how the three verdict classes are expected to distribute in a healthy corpus rather than merely stating the proportion qualitatively. Suppose a corpus run of one hundred cases resolves as seventy-eight Positive, seventeen Negative, and five Ambiguous. The five Ambiguous cases, per 6.10's own proportion discipline, sit within the small-minority range this corpus expects; a subsequent run against an expanded two-hundred-case corpus that resolves forty Ambiguous rather than a proportionally consistent ten would itself be a finding under 6.10 — not evidence that the newly added cases are unusually hard, but evidence the newly authored Triggers underlying those cases are systematically underspecified, warranting a Part III visibility-and-evaluability audit across the specific capabilities the new cases target before their Ambiguous tagging is accepted as a genuine property of the Intents involved rather than a symptom of incomplete Trigger design.

PART VI FINAL LAW

Positive, Negative, and Ambiguous cases require three distinct evidentiary bars, and collapsing them into one blended pass rate hides the exact distinction this corpus exists to make visible. A case may be reclassified between Ambiguous and a genuine Priority defect only on new evidence — never to make a reported metric look better than the underlying logic actually is.


PART VII — COMPETING SKILLS, PRIORITY, AND DEDUPLICATION

7.1 Skill-Selection, per Part II.4, must resolve deterministically whenever more than one capability's Trigger fires on an overlapping Intent, and this Part specifies the science of that resolution: priority ordering, deduplication of genuinely redundant matches, and the explicit handling of the case where resolution cannot be made deterministic without violating Part VI's own distinction between Ambiguous Intents and Priority defects.

7.2 A Priority ordering, for this corpus's purposes, is any rule external to the competing capabilities themselves that resolves which one is selected when both fire — specificity of match, explicit precedence declaration, or a fixed tie-breaking convention — and the ordering's own correctness is judged by a single test: applied twice to the identical input under identical conditions, it must produce the identical selection both times. A Priority ordering that resolves inconsistently across repeated identical runs has failed this corpus's own test regardless of how sophisticated its underlying logic appears, because non-determinism at this segment makes every downstream claim about which capability actually activated unverifiable.

7.3 Specificity-based priority — preferring the Trigger match with the narrower, more specific pattern over a broader, more general one when both fire — is the ordering principle this corpus recommends as a default where no explicit precedence has been declared, because a specific match encodes more evidence about the Intent's actual nature than a general one, and a general Trigger firing alongside a specific one on the same Intent is more often evidence that the general Trigger's own pattern is too broad than that the Intent is genuinely ambiguous between the two.

7.4 Explicit precedence declaration — a capability author stating directly that this capability should be preferred over a named other when both would otherwise fire — is treated as a stronger signal than specificity when the two conflict, but this corpus requires such declarations to be externally visible per Part III's own visibility test, because a precedence rule stored only inside one of the two competing capabilities' own bodies is subject to exactly Fixture One's defect: invisible to the selection mechanism until after a selection has already, circularly, occurred.

7.5 Deduplication addresses a distinct problem from priority: two or more Triggers that fire on the same Intent and, rather than genuinely competing for a single selection slot, actually represent redundant paths to the identical underlying capability — a skill registered under two differently-worded description cards, or a hook and a skill both wired to the same effective action. This corpus requires deduplication logic to identify redundant matches by their eventual Module-segment target rather than by superficial Trigger similarity, because two Triggers with entirely different wording can still resolve to the identical downstream module, and two Triggers with superficially similar wording can resolve to entirely distinct modules that only coincidentally share vocabulary.

7.6 A worked deduplication case clarifies the distinction: a project-specific skill and a general-purpose skill both register Triggers matching the phrase "format this file," and naive similarity-based deduplication might treat them as redundant because their Trigger wording overlaps heavily. Module-target-based deduplication instead confirms the two resolve to entirely different formatting logic — one project-specific, one general — and correctly declines to deduplicate them, routing the decision instead to Priority ordering, because this is genuinely a competing-skills case, not a redundant-path case, despite the superficial wording overlap that a less careful deduplication rule would have collapsed incorrectly.

7.7 A harder case names the opposite error explicitly: a hook wired to fire on a file-save event and a skill wired to fire on a semantically related user request both ultimately invoke the identical underlying linting module, but their surface Trigger wording shares no overlap at all — one is a file-path pattern, the other a natural-language phrase — making similarity-based deduplication blind to their redundancy entirely. Module-target-based deduplication catches this case correctly precisely because it inspects the eventual downstream target rather than the surface Trigger form, confirming both paths converge on the identical module and flagging the redundancy for review rather than allowing both paths to fire independently and potentially produce a doubled or conflicting effect.

7.8 A specific failure this Part's science guards against, distinct from an incorrect Priority resolution, is SILENT MUTUAL SUPPRESSION: two capabilities whose Triggers are each individually correct and visible, but whose interaction — through an undocumented precedence rule, a load-order dependency, or an unintended deduplication false-positive — results in neither ever actually firing for an Intent that should have activated exactly one of them. This failure is distinguished from a genuine Priority defect because no selection actually occurs at all; it is distinguished from Part IV's Orphaned Activation Surface because both Triggers, examined individually, are visible and mechanically evaluable — the defect exists only in the interaction between them, discoverable only by testing the pair together, never by auditing either capability in isolation.

7.9 The simulation harness in Part X is required to include, among its corpus cases per Part V, at least one case per known pair of capabilities whose Triggers are known to overlap on any real or synthetic Intent, specifically to exercise Silent Mutual Suppression, because this failure mode is invisible to any per-capability audit and detectable only by a harness that actually replays the pair's joint behavior against a shared Intent, restated here as a concrete requirement rather than an abstract caution.

7.10 This Part's closing discipline restates Part VI's own distinction in operational terms for the priority mechanism specifically: a Priority ordering's job is to resolve competing, individually-correct Triggers deterministically; it is never the mechanism responsible for resolving genuine Intent-level ambiguity, and an implementer tempted to "fix" an Ambiguous case, per Part VI.4, by tuning Priority ordering until one member of the acceptable set always wins has not resolved the ambiguity — it has hidden it, producing a system that appears deterministic while silently discarding a legitimately valid alternative interpretation every time.

7.11 A further worked case distinguishes a specific Priority-defect subtype from the tie-breaking inconsistency 7.7 already describes: LOAD-ORDER-DEPENDENT PRIORITY, in which two Triggers' resolution order depends on the sequence in which their respective capabilities were themselves loaded into the estate's index during a given session, rather than on either Trigger's own specificity or any explicit precedence declaration. This differs from 7.7's case because no tie-breaking rule is being applied inconsistently — no tie-breaking rule exists at all, and the outcome instead depends on an incidental property, load sequence, that this corpus's own science treats as illegitimate grounds for a Priority resolution precisely because it satisfies neither the specificity nor the explicit-precedence tests of 7.3 and 7.4. The corpus case exercising this defect, added to the standing corpus per Part V, constructs two Simulated Sessions identical except for the two capabilities' own index-loading order, and a harness verdict showing different Skill-Selection outcomes between the two runs confirms the defect independent of any single run's own result.

PART VII FINAL LAW

Priority resolves competing but individually correct Triggers deterministically; it never resolves genuine Intent-level ambiguity, and tuning it to force a single winner on an Ambiguous case hides the ambiguity rather than resolving it. Deduplication must key on the eventual Module-segment target, not surface Trigger wording, because redundancy and near-identical wording are neither necessary nor sufficient for each other.


PART VIII — CROSS-REPOSITORY ACTIVATION

8.1 Fixture Four, Cross-Repository Activation Failure, establishes this Part's founding requirement: activation behavior verified inside one repository carries no evidence, by itself, about activation behavior inside a structurally different one, and a Verification Contract scoped to a single repository cannot license any claim of general, federated capability — restated here from CRAIF-00 12.5 as this corpus's own operational science rather than as historical narrative alone.

8.2 A Capability Pointer is the object this corpus defines to make cross-repo activation checkable: a record, per capability, of the specific repository profiles — repo type, directory convention, package-manager signature, or explicit repo identifier — under which the capability's Loader segment is known to resolve correctly, populated only by actual verified resolution in each named profile, never by an assumption that a Loader mechanism working in one profile implies it works in all profiles that superficially resemble it.

8.3 Loader resolution failure across repositories takes a specific, recurring shape this corpus names directly: a Loader segment that locates its target artifact via a path assumption tied to one repository's own directory structure — a fixed relative path, a specific config-file location, an assumed package-manager lockfile — silently failing, or worse, silently resolving to an unrelated artifact that happens to exist at the same relative path in the second repository, producing an activation that appears to succeed while actually invoking the wrong content entirely.

8.4 A Repo Profile is the second object this Part defines: a structured description of a repository's own relevant characteristics — the manifest files present, the directory conventions in use, whether it is the estate's home repository or a federated project — against which a Capability Pointer's known-good profiles are compared, so that a new repository can be classified against existing profiles before any activation is attempted, rather than discovered to be incompatible only after a silent Loader failure has already occurred.

8.5 The federation audit that originally surfaced Fixture Four — an Owner manually checking thirty projects and counting — is the concrete precedent this Part's science is built to make unnecessary going forward: a Capability Pointer populated with verified Repo Profiles, checked automatically against a new repository's own profile at the point that repository is first onboarded, replaces a manual, non-scalable audit with a mechanical comparison this corpus specifies but does not itself execute, consuming the estate's existing repo-detection infrastructure — referenced in CRAIF-00 Part II's consultation discipline — rather than building a parallel detection mechanism.

8.6 A specific asymmetry this corpus names explicitly: verifying that a capability activates correctly in a new repository requires actually attempting activation under realistic conditions in that repository, whether through a live session or the hermetic simulation Part X specifies; it cannot be inferred from Repo Profile similarity alone, because two repositories sharing every profile characteristic this corpus currently tracks can still differ along some unmodeled dimension that breaks a specific Loader assumption. Repo Profile comparison narrows which new repositories most urgently need direct verification; it never substitutes for that verification.

8.7 Cross-Repository Activation Rate, defined operationally in Part XI, is this Part's primary metric: the proportion of a capability's known-relevant repository population in which activation has been directly verified, as distinct from the proportion in which activation is merely assumed by Repo Profile similarity. A capability with a high assumed-compatibility count and a low directly-verified count is, per this corpus's own evidence discipline, at best OBSERVED or INFERRED for the unverified population, never VERIFIED, regardless of how confident the profile-similarity comparison feels.

8.8 A worked cross-repo trace illustrates the mechanism: a capability's Capability Pointer records verified activation in the estate's home repository profile; a new federated project is onboarded and classified against a Repo Profile that differs in package-manager signature from every previously verified profile; this classification alone does not license activating the capability with confidence — it flags the new profile as unverified, triggers a Part X hermetic simulation run scoped specifically to that profile's characteristics, and only updates the Capability Pointer to include the new profile once that simulation, or an equivalent live verification, actually confirms the full Activation Chain completes under the new profile's specific conditions.

8.9 A specific failure mode named separately from Part XIV's general catalogue because it is unique to the cross-repo dimension: PROFILE OVERFITTING, in which a Capability Pointer is populated with verified profiles narrow enough that they describe the exact repositories tested rather than the actual characteristic that determines compatibility, producing a pointer that appears well-populated while providing no genuine predictive power for any repository not in the exact tested set. The counter is requiring each verified profile entry to name the specific characteristic — not merely the specific repository — that verification actually confirmed, so a new repository sharing that named characteristic can be assessed by the characteristic itself rather than by exact repository-identity matching.

8.10 This Part's closing scoping note distinguishes its own science from CRAIF-01's cross-repo integration contract obligations, addressed in Part XV of this dataset: CRAIF-02 defines what cross-repo activation verification requires and what evidence licenses a Capability Pointer's expansion; it does not itself execute a repair to a Loader segment found broken in a new profile — that transaction, exactly as CRAIF-00 Part II requires, belongs to CRAIF-01, consuming this Part's finding as an input Repair Intent rather than acting on it directly.

8.11 A worked Profile Overfitting case, extending 8.9's definition concretely, illustrates the failure directly: a Capability Pointer records three verified profiles, each, on inspection, describing a repository using the identical package manager and an identical directory depth for its configuration file — the pointer's entries differ only by repository name, never by any characteristic distinct from the one already shared across all three. A fourth repository sharing that same package manager and directory depth is, correctly, treated as covered by the existing verification; a fifth repository using a different package manager entirely is not covered, and this corpus's own audit distinguishes the two cases by checking whether the new repository's differing characteristic was ever actually named and tested in any prior verified entry — not by counting how many prior entries exist, since three entries that never varied the relevant characteristic provide no more genuine predictive coverage than one.

PART VIII FINAL LAW

Activation verified in one repository profile licenses no claim about any other profile, however similar the two appear. A Capability Pointer's verified-profile count must reflect actual, direct verification per profile, never assumed compatibility by similarity, and a profile entry that names only the tested repository rather than the characteristic that determined compatibility has overfit to a sample of one.


PART IX — CONTEXT INJECTION AND BUDGET CONSTRAINTS

9.1 Context-Injection, per Part II.6, is the segment where a Loader's retrieved content is actually placed into a session's available context, and it is a segment with a hard constraint no other segment in the Activation Chain carries in the same form: a fixed, finite budget, such that content correctly retrieved by the Loader can still fail to influence the executing session's behavior if it is truncated, deprioritized, or never actually placed into context due to budget exhaustion — a failure this corpus treats as distinct from, and no less severe than, a Loader failure, because the observable symptom to a session's actual behavior is identical.

9.2 A specific and previously observed instance of this segment's failure, named because it recurred during this very corpus's own founding session, is a hook or context-provider emitting output large enough that the harness or session infrastructure truncates it before the relevant content is read, with the truncation itself unlogged in a way visible to the capability's own downstream consumers — producing a session that behaves as though the injected context never activated, despite the Loader and the injecting mechanism both having executed without any code-level error.

9.3 This corpus names LOADED-BUT-UNUSED as the specific waste pattern Context-Injection budget constraints make possible and Part XI's own metric tracks directly: content that is successfully retrieved, successfully injected within budget, and never actually referenced or acted upon by the session it was injected into — a distinct failure from truncation, because the content genuinely reached the session's available context and the session simply never used it, consuming budget that could have carried content the session would have acted on instead.

9.4 The distinction between truncation failure and loaded-but-unused waste matters because the two require entirely different remedies: truncation is addressed by reducing the injected content's size, restructuring the injection to prioritize the most trigger-relevant portion first, or raising the budget where raising it is actually available; loaded-but-unused waste is addressed by tightening the Trigger's own precision, per Part III, so that content is only injected when the specific Intent it addresses is actually present, rather than injected defensively on a broader Trigger match that fires more often than the content is actually relevant.

9.5 A budget-aware Loader design principle this corpus states as a general rule rather than a case-specific recommendation: content intended for Context-Injection should be structured so that its most Trigger-relevant portion appears first, with progressively less critical elaboration following, because a truncation event that cuts content mid-stream should, under this ordering, still preserve the portion most likely to actually influence the session's behavior — a design discipline the estate's own JIT latent-card architecture already partially implements through its compact-card-then-full-body structure, referenced here as a positive existing pattern rather than a novel invention this corpus originates.

9.6 A specific corruption of budget management, distinguished from both truncation and loaded-but-unused waste, is BUDGET STARVATION BY VOLUME: a session context in which many low-relevance capabilities each inject a small amount of content, individually well within any single capability's own budget allowance, but collectively consuming enough of the session's total available context that a subsequently more relevant capability's own injection is truncated or entirely displaced — a failure not attributable to any single capability's own injection behavior, only to the aggregate, and therefore invisible to a per-capability audit that examines each injection's budget compliance in isolation.

9.7 The counter to budget starvation by volume is a session-level context-cost accounting this Part requires but does not itself implement, consuming whatever session or harness infrastructure already tracks aggregate context consumption — this corpus's own contribution is the Context Cost metric defined operationally in Part XI, which makes the aggregate consumption pattern visible and attributable to specific capabilities' own injection sizes, rather than a new context-management mechanism CRAIF-02 would otherwise have to build independently.

9.8 A worked case distinguishes the three failure patterns concretely: a single large skill body that exceeds available budget and is cut mid-sentence is a truncation failure, remedied by restructuring that one skill's own content per 9.5; a well-sized skill injected on every session regardless of relevance, and never once referenced across many observed sessions, is a loaded-but-unused waste pattern, remedied by tightening that skill's own Trigger per 9.4; and a session in which fifteen small, individually-reasonable injections together displace a sixteenth, highly relevant one, is budget starvation by volume, remedied only by an aggregate accounting mechanism no single capability's own audit could have surfaced.

9.9 This corpus's own admissibility discipline, inherited from CRAIF-00 Part XV, applies directly to Context-Injection claims: a claim that a capability's content "was injected" is, absent further evidence, a presence-tier claim at best — confirming the content reached the session's context budget, not that the session actually acted on it — and a strong claim of activation requires the Result and Evidence segments, per Part II.9, to confirm the injected content actually influenced an observable downstream effect, exactly the distinction that separates a genuinely activated capability from a loaded-but-unused one.

9.10 This Part closes by naming the segment's own place in the broader chain precisely: Context-Injection is the segment most often mistaken for a purely mechanical, unremarkable pass-through step, and it is precisely that assumption — that anything successfully retrieved is thereby successfully influential — that this Part's science exists to correct. A chain audited thoroughly at Trigger, Loader, and Module but never at Context-Injection has an unexamined segment exactly as capable of silently defeating the whole chain as any other, and this corpus requires it be named, budgeted, and measured with the same rigor as every other segment in Part II's ontology.

9.11 The Context Cost metric's own measurement infrastructure is, per this corpus's non-duplication discipline restated one final time for this specific segment, consumed from whatever token-accounting or session-telemetry mechanism the estate already operates — this corpus defines what Context Cost means and what pattern, budget starvation by volume, it exists to surface; it does not build a parallel token-counting mechanism of its own. A future implementer wiring this metric into a live pipeline consults the estate's existing telemetry surface first, exactly as CRAIF-00 Part XIII.9 requires for the OIII metric's own measurement cadence, and adds only the capability-level attribution — which specific injection contributed how much to a given session's aggregate cost — as a new signal kind within that existing framework, never as an independent accounting system CRAIF-02 maintains in parallel.

PART IX FINAL LAW

Content correctly retrieved by the Loader can still fail to influence a session if Context-Injection truncates it, if the session never references it, or if its own budget footprint displaces a more relevant capability's injection elsewhere in the same session. A chain audited everywhere except this segment has an unexamined point exactly as capable of silently defeating the whole chain as any other.


PART X — THE SIMULATION HARNESS

10.1 The Simulation Harness is the mechanism that replays corpus cases, per Part V, against a candidate capability or a candidate repair, and reports a verdict per case using the classes Part VI defines, without requiring a live session to expose whatever defect the case represents — the concrete answer to CRAIF-00 12.9's own requirement that CRAIF-02 possess at least five real, previously-observed cases to replay before being asked to generalize to one nobody has seen yet, and the mechanism by which this corpus fulfills its own founding mission stated in Part I.

10.2 Hermetic execution is the harness's defining architectural property, composed by reference from SQI's own hermetic-replay discipline rather than reinvented independently: a harness run must produce an identical verdict on an identical case regardless of what else is happening in the estate at the moment it runs — no dependency on live session state, no dependency on network-reachable external services beyond what a case's own fixture explicitly stubs, and no side effect on canonical estate state that could contaminate a subsequent, unrelated run.

10.3 A harness run's basic unit is a Simulated Session: a reconstructed environment presenting a specific Intent, a specific Repo Profile per Part VIII, and a specific set of available capabilities to the selection mechanism, sufficient to exercise every Activation Chain segment the case under test actually specifies, without requiring any of those segments' underlying real infrastructure — the actual settings.json, the actual file system, the actual loader — to be mutated or even touched outside the simulated environment's own sandboxed copy.

10.4 The harness's replay architecture composes CRAIF-01's own Sandbox Execution discipline, specified in that dataset's Part VIII, by reference rather than duplication: where CRAIF-01 sandboxes a candidate repair's execution to verify it without touching canonical state, CRAIF-02's harness sandboxes an activation attempt's execution to verify the chain without touching canonical state, and the two mechanisms share the identical underlying isolation discipline — a copy-on-read, discard-on-completion execution environment — applied to two different objects, a repair candidate in one case and an activation attempt in the other.

10.5 A harness run's output, per case, records the Activation Chain trace actually observed — which segments completed, in what order, with what Evidence — compared against the case's own expected trace from its corpus entry, per Part V.6, and the comparison's granularity is per-segment rather than a single aggregate pass-fail bit, because a per-segment comparison is what makes a harness failure attributable to a specific taxonomy member per Part IV, rather than merely confirming that something, somewhere in the chain, did not go as expected.

10.6 The harness is required to run the full corpus, not a sampled subset, on any occasion this corpus's own consuming contracts require a coverage claim — specifically, CRAIF-01's Activation Verification Contract in Part XII of this dataset, and any Owner-facing claim that a capability's activation chain has been verified — because a sampled subset's pass rate cannot license a claim about the specific cases excluded from the sample, and this corpus's own evidence discipline, inherited from CRAIF-00 Part XV, does not permit a VERIFIED claim scoped more broadly than the actual cases checked.

10.7 A specific harness design requirement addresses a risk named in Part V.7's own control-case discussion: the harness's verdict logic is itself subject to periodic validation against its own known-correct control cases and known-defective real-fixture cases, because a harness whose verdict logic has drifted — through an update to its own comparison code, for instance — could silently begin reporting false Positive verdicts for genuinely defective cases, or false Negative verdicts for genuinely correct ones, and this drift would be invisible without a standing self-check the harness runs against its own founding corpus on a fixed cadence.

10.8 A worked harness run illustrates the mechanism concretely: invoked against Fixture One's own corpus case per Part XIII, the harness constructs a Simulated Session presenting the exact Intent the fixture's own historical trigger phrase represents, populates the available-capabilities set with the capability in its defective, self-referential-vocabulary configuration, and observes that Skill-Selection never occurs — the expected trace for this case — producing a verdict of DEFECT-CONFIRMED, PHANTOM-CAUSE self-referential-vocabulary, matching the case's own tagged expectation from Part V.5 exactly.

10.9 The same worked case, run a second time against a corrected configuration in which the Trigger's vocabulary has been relocated to an externally visible card, produces a Simulated Session in which Skill-Selection does occur and the full chain completes, yielding a verdict of DEFECT-RESOLVED — and this second run is the concrete before-and-after pair CRAIF-00 Part XV.3 requires for any strong repair claim, supplying exactly the Activation Verification Contract evidence CRAIF-01's own Part IX and this dataset's own Part XII require before a repair touching an activation-chain segment may close as CLOSED-REPAIRED.

10.10 The harness's own maturity is bounded honestly at this corpus's authoring: it is specified in full architectural detail in this Part, but, per Part I.7's own admission, it has not executed against a real corpus at authoring time, and this corpus marks every specific harness behavior described above as PROPOSED rather than VERIFIED, per CRAIF-00 Part XV's own evidence-status vocabulary, pending the harness's actual construction and its first real run against the corpus Part V specifies.

10.11 A boundary this Part states explicitly because the two mechanisms are easy to conflate on a first reading: the Simulation Harness and CRAIF-01's own Canary stage, specified in that dataset's Part X, are not the same mechanism operating at different scales, and the harness never substitutes for a canary nor does a canary ever call back into the harness mid-deployment. The harness exercises a candidate hermetically, entirely isolated from canonical state, specifically so a defect can be caught before any real exposure occurs at all; Canary exercises an already harness-verified candidate against a bounded slice of real conditions specifically to catch what hermetic isolation's own necessary incompleteness could not simulate. A canary failure, when one occurs, is not evidence the harness itself is unreliable — it is evidence that some real-world condition existed outside what the harness's own Simulated Session modeled, and this corpus requires such a canary failure be captured, per Part V.3's synthetic-case discipline, as a new corpus case modeling the specific condition the harness previously omitted, so the next candidate touching the same Failure Pattern is tested against it before ever reaching Canary again.

PART X FINAL LAW

The harness exists to replay a concrete, checkable corpus hermetically, never to generalize about activation in the abstract, and its own verdict logic requires periodic self-validation against known-correct and known-defective cases, because a harness whose comparison logic has silently drifted is indistinguishable, from its output alone, from one still functioning correctly.


PART XI — METRICS

11.1 This corpus defines five metrics, each scoped to a specific segment or property of the Activation Chain rather than blended into one composite activation-health score, because a blended score, as Part VI.8 already establishes for harness verdicts specifically, obscures exactly the segment-level attribution this corpus's entire ontology exists to preserve.

11.2 Activation Recall is the proportion of real Intents that should have triggered a specific capability, per that capability's own intended scope, which actually did trigger it — computed against the corpus's Positive and Negative cases per Part VI, never against Ambiguous cases, because an Ambiguous case's correct outcome is a bounded set rather than a single expected activation, and folding it into a recall computation would misrepresent genuine irreducible ambiguity as a recall failure.

11.3 Activation Precision is the inverse-facing metric: the proportion of actual activations that corresponded to a genuinely intended Intent, as opposed to a Phantom Trigger or an over-broad match firing on an Intent it was never meant to recognize. Precision and Recall are reported jointly, never singly, because a capability tuned purely to maximize Recall by broadening its Trigger indiscriminately will show a corresponding Precision collapse, and reporting only the improving metric would hide the cost the tuning imposed on the other.

11.4 False Invocation Rate is a distinct metric from the Precision shortfall, measuring specifically the rate at which an activation occurred and produced an observable Result before the capability's own downstream logic determined the Intent was not actually a genuine match for it — a cost metric capturing wasted execution and context consumption even in cases where the capability itself eventually self-corrected, distinguished from a pure Precision failure because a self-correcting false invocation still cost the session real context and time before its own correction occurred.

11.5 Cross-Repository Activation Rate, defined operationally in Part VIII.7, is the proportion of a capability's known-relevant repository population in which direct verification, not assumed profile similarity, has confirmed correct activation — reported per capability rather than as a single estate-wide average, because an estate-wide average would let a small number of thoroughly-verified capabilities mask a larger number that have never been checked outside the home repository at all.

11.6 Loaded-but-Unused Ratio, named operationally in Part IX.3, is the proportion of a capability's successful Context-Injection events across observed sessions that were never subsequently referenced or acted upon — a waste metric distinct from every accuracy metric above, because a capability can score perfectly on Recall, Precision, and False Invocation Rate while still carrying a high Loaded-but-Unused Ratio if its Trigger, though accurate, fires more broadly than the content it injects is actually needed for on each firing.

11.7 Context Cost is the fifth metric, measuring the aggregate context-budget consumption a capability's injections impose across a session population, reported specifically to make Part IX.6's budget-starvation-by-volume failure visible: a capability with a small individual footprint and a high firing frequency can impose an aggregate Context Cost comparable to a capability with a large footprint and low frequency, and this metric is the only one of the five that surfaces that equivalence directly.

11.8 These five metrics are reported per capability and, where a Failure Pattern spans multiple capabilities sharing a defect class per Part IV's taxonomy, aggregated per taxonomy member as well, so that a systemic pattern — many capabilities each individually scoring acceptably on Recall and Precision but collectively showing an elevated Loaded-but-Unused Ratio, for instance — becomes visible at the taxonomy level even when no single capability's own metrics would have triggered attention in isolation.

11.9 A specific anti-gaming discipline, mirroring CRAIF-00 Part XIII's own countermetric requirement, is stated for this Part's metrics jointly rather than per metric: no single metric among the five may be optimized in isolation without checking its effect on the other four, because Precision optimized in isolation degrades Recall, Recall optimized in isolation degrades Precision and elevates False Invocation Rate, and Loaded-but-Unused Ratio optimized in isolation — by tightening a Trigger until it almost never fires — degrades Recall for the genuine cases the tightened Trigger now misses.

11.10 This Part closes by stating explicitly what these five metrics do not measure, because a metric suite's stated boundary is as important as its stated content: none of the five metrics assess whether a capability's underlying Module logic, once activated, produces a correct Result — that assessment belongs to SQI and to the capability's own domain-specific testing, and CRAIF-02's five metrics are scoped entirely to whether the correct invocation occurred at all, never to whether the invoked capability then behaved correctly once invoked.

11.11 A worked illustration of the joint-optimization discipline 11.9 requires clarifies the tradeoff concretely rather than only asserting it exists. A capability's Trigger is tightened specifically to reduce a measured Loaded-but-Unused Ratio of forty percent, and the tightening succeeds at that goal, reducing the ratio to twelve percent. Measured in isolation, this looks like a clear improvement; measured jointly with Recall, per this Part's own requirement, the same tightening is found to have reduced Recall from ninety-four percent to seventy-one percent, because the narrower Trigger now misses a substantial fraction of genuine Intents it previously, if wastefully, caught. The joint report — both metrics, not the Loaded-but-Unused improvement alone — is what this corpus requires before any Trigger change of this kind is accepted as a net improvement, and in this specific illustration the correct conclusion is that the tightening overshot, trading an acceptable waste cost for an unacceptable coverage loss, a conclusion invisible to any report that surfaced the improving metric without its paired cost.

PART XI FINAL LAW

Five metrics — Recall, Precision, False Invocation Rate, Cross-Repository Activation Rate, and Loaded-but-Unused Ratio, alongside Context Cost — are reported jointly and never blended into one composite score, because optimizing any one in isolation measurably degrades at least one of the others, and a metric suite that hides that tradeoff behind a single number has hidden the exact information this corpus exists to surface.


PART XII — ACTIVATION VERIFICATION CONTRACT

12.1 The Activation Verification Contract is the object this corpus supplies to CRAIF-01, consumed specifically at that dataset's Part IX for any Repair Intent whose contributing Finding touches an activation-chain segment, and its entire purpose is to prove, per CRAIF-00 Part XV's admissibility discipline, that a repair restored the actual activation chain — not merely that some artifact the chain depends on was edited.

12.2 The contract's minimum required content is: the specific segment or segments, per Part II's ontology, the Finding identified as defective; the specific taxonomy member, per Part IV, or an explicit statement that no known taxonomy member matches and a new candidate member is being proposed; the before-state harness run, per Part X.9, confirming the defect actually reproduces under simulation prior to the repair; and the after-state harness run confirming the full chain, through every segment the capability instantiates, now completes where the before-state run showed it did not.

12.3 A contract missing the before-state run is rejected outright by this corpus's own discipline, restated from CRAIF-00 Part XV.4 in this corpus's specific operational terms: an after-only Activation Verification Contract cannot distinguish a genuine repair from a Finding that was already stale, or a defect that resolved itself through an unrelated change, before CRAIF-01's transaction ever executed — the identical failing-before requirement CRAIF-00 states generally, made concrete here as a specific, checkable field this contract must carry.

12.4 The contract's after-state run must be scoped to the identical Simulated Session conditions — the identical Intent, the identical Repo Profile, the identical available-capabilities set — as the before-state run, changed only in the specific artifact the repair actually modified, because a before-and-after comparison run under different conditions cannot isolate whether the repair itself, rather than some incidental difference between the two runs, produced the observed change in outcome.

12.5 For a repair touching Fixture Four's own domain, cross-repo activation, the contract additionally requires the after-state run be repeated across every Repo Profile the Finding's own scope claimed to address, per Part VIII's Capability Pointer discipline, because a cross-repo repair verified in only the profile where the defect was originally discovered has not demonstrated the general claim its own scope asserts — it has demonstrated a single-profile fix that happens to share a Finding with a broader claim.

12.6 A contract's evidence-status marking, per CRAIF-00 Part XV.6, is VERIFIED only when both before-state and after-state harness runs are on record with their full traces; it is OBSERVED if the after-state confirmation came from a live session rather than the hermetic harness, because a live-session confirmation, while genuine, lacks the harness's own reproducibility guarantee; and it may never be marked VERIFIED on the strength of the executing transaction's own narrative report that the chain "should now work," regardless of how confident that report is.

12.7 A specific rejection case this Part names explicitly: a contract presenting only a settings-file diff and a claim that the diff corresponds to a registered hook, without any harness run confirming the hook actually fires under a Simulated Session's Intent, is Fixture Three's own defect restated as an admissibility failure — the diff is presence evidence, admissible at CRAIF-00 Part XV.2's medium tier at best, and this corpus requires strictly more before permitting a CLOSED-REPAIRED verdict for any Finding this Part's contract governs.

12.8 The contract's relationship to CRAIF-01's own Verification Contract, defined generally in that dataset's Part IX, is one of composition rather than substitution: a Repair Intent touching both an activation-chain segment and some other property — a file's content correctness, say — requires both CRAIF-01's general Verification Contract and this corpus's Activation Verification Contract satisfied jointly, neither one alone sufficing for a Finding whose scope spans both concerns.

12.9 A worked contract illustrates the composition concretely, extending the worked trace from Part X.8 and X.9: the Finding identifies Fixture One's own defect pattern in a hypothetical new capability; the contract names Segment III, Trigger, and taxonomy member Self-Referential Vocabulary; the before-state harness run, per 10.8, confirms Skill-Selection never occurs; CRAIF-01's transaction relocates the Trigger definition to an external card; the after-state harness run, per 10.9, confirms the full chain now completes; the contract is marked VERIFIED, both runs attached in full, and CRAIF-01's transaction proceeds to CLOSED-REPAIRED only once this contract, alongside any other Verification Contract the Finding's full scope required, is satisfied.

12.10 This Part closes by restating a boundary already established but worth repeating at the point of consumption: this corpus's Activation Verification Contract proves an activation chain now completes; it does not, by itself, prove the resulting Result was the correct one for the Intent in a domain-specific sense — that further claim, where the repair's scope requires it, belongs to whichever domain-specific verification the Finding's full scope also implicates, composed alongside this contract rather than replaced by it.

12.11 A further rejection case, distinct from 12.7's presence-only example, addresses a subtler admissibility failure: a contract whose before-state harness run was conducted under Simulated Session conditions that do not actually match the after-state run — a different Intent phrasing, a different Repo Profile — producing two traces that differ in outcome but cannot be attributed cleanly to the repair itself, per 12.4's own identical-conditions requirement. This corpus rejects such a contract not because either individual run is invalid in isolation, but because the pairing itself fails to isolate the repair as the variable that changed, and a Verification Contract's evidentiary strength depends entirely on that isolation holding — the identical failure mode CRAIF-00 Part XV.9 names for evidence that ages between collection and use, restated here as a condition-matching requirement specific to this corpus's own before-and-after pair.

PART XII FINAL LAW

An Activation Verification Contract requires a before-state harness run proving the defect reproduces and an after-state run, under identical Simulated Session conditions, proving the full chain now completes — a settings diff or a narrative claim that the chain "should work now" satisfies neither requirement and licenses no CLOSED-REPAIRED verdict for any Finding this contract governs.

PART XIV — FAILURE MODES

14.1 This Part catalogues how CRAIF-02's own mechanisms can fail, in the same paired, under-eager-and-over-eager discipline CRAIF-00 Part XIV establishes for the family generally and CRAIF-01 Part XV and Part XVI apply to the runtime specifically, so that this corpus's own science is honestly specified rather than presented as though its harness and metrics were themselves immune to the failure modes they exist to detect in other capabilities.

14.2 FALSE POSITIVE ACTIVATION FINDING is the over-eager pole of this corpus's own diagnostic apparatus: a harness run reporting a taxonomy-member defect against a capability that, under real conditions rather than the specific Simulated Session constructed for the case, would actually have activated correctly — most often caused by a Simulated Session's own construction failing to faithfully reproduce a condition the real selection mechanism actually checks, producing a simulated failure that does not correspond to any real one.

14.3 FALSE NEGATIVE ACTIVATION FINDING is the under-eager pole: a harness run reporting a Positive verdict for a capability that, under real conditions, actually suffers from a genuine defect the Simulated Session's own construction happened not to exercise — most often because the corpus case's expected trace was authored against an incomplete understanding of the capability's actual Trigger logic, checking a narrower condition than the real Trigger actually requires to fire.

14.4 SESSION-RESTART DRIFT is a failure distinct from both diagnostic poles: an Activation Chain that completes correctly within a single continuous session but fails, silently, across a session restart or a `/compact` boundary — because some segment's state, most often Context-Injection's own budget allocation or a Loader's cached resolution, was implicitly assumed to persist across the boundary without this corpus's own ontology treating session continuity as an explicit precondition any segment is permitted to rely on.

14.5 WORKTREE DRIFT is a related but distinct failure specific to the estate's own multi-pane, shared-repository operating pattern: a Capability Pointer or Repo Profile verified against one worktree's own state silently becoming stale when a sibling pane's concurrent edit changes the repository's actual structure underneath it, producing an activation verification that was accurate at the moment it was recorded and inaccurate by the time it is next relied upon — a failure this corpus's own Part VIII does not fully resolve, because Repo Profile comparison, per 8.6, checks structural characteristics rather than a live, moment-of-use confirmation.

14.6 STALE POINTER is the specific object-level manifestation of both 14.4 and 14.5's underlying risk: a Capability Pointer's recorded verified-profile entry, once written, is never automatically re-validated on a fixed cadence by this corpus's own specification, and a pointer that was accurate at write time can silently become inaccurate as the estate's own capabilities, loaders, or repository structures evolve, with no mechanism named in this Part alone that would surface the staleness before a real activation attempt fails against it.

14.7 PARTIAL ACTIVATION is a failure this corpus names distinctly from a clean Positive or Negative verdict: a chain that completes some but not all of its applicable segments — Trigger, Skill-Selection, and Loader succeeding, but Context-Injection truncating the content before Module ever receives it, for instance — producing an observable effect that is neither the intended full activation nor a clean absence of activation, and this corpus's own harness, per Part VI.2, is required to report this outcome explicitly as its own distinct verdict class rather than rounding it to either a Positive or a Negative.

14.8 The pairing discipline this Part inherits from CRAIF-00 Part XIV.8 applies directly: 14.2 and 14.3 are the diagnostic apparatus's own under- and over-eager poles and must never be tuned against each other in isolation — a harness made stricter specifically to eliminate false positives, without checking the corresponding effect on false negatives, has traded one diagnostic failure for its opposite, not improved the harness's actual accuracy; and 14.4 and 14.5, session-restart and worktree drift, are the two faces of a single underlying risk, staleness introduced by an assumption of continuity this corpus's ontology does not actually license anywhere.

14.9 A ninth failure mode, distinguished from the eight above because it concerns this corpus's own metrics rather than its harness or ontology directly, is METRIC BLINDNESS TO INTERACTION EFFECTS: Part XI's five metrics are each computed per capability or per taxonomy member, and a systemic failure that only manifests through the interaction of multiple capabilities — Silent Mutual Suppression, per Part VII.8, being the clearest instance — can leave every individual capability's own metrics looking acceptable while the actual estate-wide activation behavior is degraded, because no single metric in Part XI is computed at the pairwise or population level Silent Mutual Suppression requires to become visible.

14.10 This catalogue closes, per the discipline CRAIF-00 Part XIV.10 states for the family generally, with an explicit refusal to claim completeness: these nine failure modes are the founding catalogue, drawn from this corpus's own design process and the estate's own observed operating patterns at founding, and any future failure mode CRAIF-02's actual operation surfaces is added to this Part by the same discipline — named, given a checkable signature, and paired against its nearest existing member — rather than treated as evidence this catalogue was already exhaustive.

14.11 A final cross-family note situates this Part's nine failure modes against CRAIF-01 Part XV and Part XVI's own runtime-failure catalogue: the two catalogues describe failures at different layers of the same overall transaction, exactly as Part IV.10 already distinguishes for the bootstrap taxonomy specifically, and a single real incident can, in principle, exhibit a failure from each catalogue in sequence — a False Negative Activation Finding, per 14.3, causing CRAIF-01 to certify a repair as CLOSED-REPAIRED when the underlying activation chain in fact still fails under a condition this corpus's own harness did not exercise, which CRAIF-01's own Part XV would separately classify as a stale-verification failure once discovered. Neither catalogue alone fully explains such a compound incident, and this corpus's own adversarial-review discipline, exercised in the family's Phase 3 review, is required to check compound, cross-catalogue failure chains explicitly rather than auditing each dataset's own failure list in isolation.

PART XIV FINAL LAW

This corpus's own diagnostic apparatus carries the identical under-eager and over-eager poles it exists to detect in other capabilities, and a fix that tightens false-positive detection without measuring its effect on false-negative detection has traded one failure for its opposite. Staleness introduced by an unlicensed assumption of continuity — across a session restart or a sibling pane's concurrent edit — is a single underlying risk wearing two names.
