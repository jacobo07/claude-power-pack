PART I — THE FOUNDING GAP: EDUCATION AS AN INSTITUTIONAL OBJECT NO SYSTEM OWNS
===============================================================================================

1.1 The gap, stated precisely against the estate as it stands. The Claude Power Pack estate, as
OBSERVED against the Non-Duplication Ledger, the Strengthening Matrix, and the repatriated CPP-IAS
corpus on 2026-07-20, contains more than seventy systems and twenty-plus sealed dataset domains.
Every one of them presumes a competent operator. The IAS-A1 capability router (PART VII) routes a
mission to a capability on the assumption that the capability, once selected, will perform — it
scores fit, not readiness-to-be-trusted. IAS-C1 (PART XV) governs a capability's economic lifecycle
from PROPOSED through RETIRED, but its FUNDED gate assumes the capability already works when funded;
it prices competence, it does not create it. IAS-D2's antibody record (PART IX) proves that a
specific failure will not recur, but only after some already-competent hand wrote the fix. No system
in the estate holds the object between "a capability exists" and "a capability may be trusted": the
teaching of it, the evidencing of its competence, and the maintenance of that competence over time.
(OBSERVED: the three named systems each stop at the boundary of this object from a different side —
routing, valuation, immunization — and none crosses into it.)

1.2 Why the gap has stayed invisible. The estate has been built by a small number of highly
competent sessions, and competence held by the builder is invisible to the built system. When the
Owner or a frontier session brings a new capability online, the education happens inside that
session's head — the operator reads the dataset, forms a model of how the capability behaves,
tests it by hand, and pronounces it ready. That pronouncement is a certification; it is simply an
UNDOCUMENTED one, produced by an implicit process that leaves no artifact, no evidence trail, and
no decay clock. (DERIVED: the reason no system owns education is that education has never failed
loudly enough to demand one — a competent operator silently absorbs the teaching cost every time.)
The gap becomes visible only at scale: when the estate must admit a model instance the Owner has
not personally vetted, or route work to a capability no current session personally taught, the
implicit certification has no substrate to stand on. The pronouncement "it is ready" then rests on
nobody's observed evidence.

1.3 The asymmetry that makes this the dangerous kind of gap. A missing valuation wastes capital; a
missing immunization lets a bug recur; both are recoverable and both announce themselves. A missing
EDUCATION system fails silently and in the worst direction: an uneducated capability is routed
unsupervised, acts on a competence nobody evidenced, and its first failure is discovered in
production rather than in a practicum. (HYPOTHESIZED, but grounded in the estate's own doctrine:
this is the Reality Contract failure — DONE claimed without observed evidence — relocated from
code to participants. A capability certified by an implicit session judgment is the Scaffold
Illusion wearing a graduation gown.) The cost of the missing system is therefore not the teaching
that does not happen; it is the untaught capability that is trusted anyway.

1.4 What A1 is, in one sentence, and what it is deliberately not. A1 is the estate's education
system: the standing, evidenced, repeatable pipeline that converts a participant from
not-yet-trustworthy to certified member and recertifies it against decay. (DEFINED.) It is not a
capability builder — the arrow from a frozen corpus to a running capability is the Tribunal's
Science IV / Tier-B4 gap, and A1 begins its work only once that capability exists. It is not a law
writer — proposing the laws a curriculum teaches is A2's object. It is not a valuer, a router, or
an immunizer. A1's single object is competence: acquiring it, evidencing it, and maintaining it,
for a participant the rest of the estate will then build upon, value, route, and protect.

1.5 The three participants A1 educates, named now and formalized in Part III. A capability (a named
unit of estate function — a skill, a module, an agent role) is educated when a curriculum teaches an
operator, or the capability's own governing session, the competence to wield it correctly. An agent
role (a dispatched subagent type) is educated when it graduates from producing plausible output to
producing verified output under the estate's gates. A model instance (a newly admitted provider or
version) is educated when it is onboarded through the pipeline of Part XVI and proven to hold the
estate's non-negotiable competences before any real work is routed to it. (DEFINED, three learner
classes.) All three share one shape: an entry state of unproven competence, a curriculum, a body of
practice, an assessment, and a certification that is provisional and decaying rather than permanent.

1.6 The evidence standard A1 inherits and does not rebuild. The estate already owns a hard rule
about evidence: a claim of competence, like any claim, is void if it rests on a proxy rather than an
observation. SQI's evidence physics (cited, not restated) draws the line between a reviewer who
reads what was produced and an auditor who asks whether the same reality reproduces the same result.
A1 adopts that line wholesale for its own object: a certification asserted from curriculum-completion
(a proxy) and not from observed behaviour (the reality) is void by construction. (NORMATIVE,
inherited.) This is why A1 is an education system and not a training-log: it does not record that
teaching occurred; it evidences that competence resulted.

1.7 The founding law. An estate that trusts a capability it never evidenced teaching has not made a
decision — it has made a guess and called it a certification. The purpose of A1 is to convert every
such guess into an evidenced, decaying, recertifiable credential, so that the sentence "this
participant may be trusted" is always backed by an observation and always carries an expiry.

FINAL LAW — PART I. The estate's most expensive silent assumption is that its participants are
already competent. Every system presumes it; no system evidences it. Education is the object that
holds the transition from a participant existing to a participant being trustworthy — and until a
system owns that transition explicitly, "it is ready" is the Scaffold Illusion in a graduation gown:
a DONE claim with no observed evidence, relocated from code onto the agents the estate runs on.

PART II — WHY "EDUCATION" AND NOT ONBOARDING, CERTIFICATION, OR DISTILLATION
===============================================================================================

2.1 The boundary discipline this Part enforces. A1 uses four words the estate already uses for other
objects: onboarding, certification, distillation, and training. Under the CPCSC audit's own governing
rule — PR-CORPUS-AUDIT-CONTENT-TIER-001, that no ownership may be claimed or ceded on vocabulary
matching — A1 must name, for each collision, the sibling that owns the word in its other sense and
the specific mechanism that separates A1's object from the sibling's. (NORMATIVE: this Part is the
Article II test applied four times, pre-emptively, so that no reader mistakes a shared word for a
shared subject.)

2.2 Onboarding — the human-and-session sense versus the participant sense. "Onboarding" in the
estate today means a session's SessionStart bootstrap, a project's first-time setup marker, or a
human operator reading a README. (OBSERVED: the token-optimizer-bootstrap and the .bootstrapped
marker are the estate's onboarding artifacts; both configure an ENVIRONMENT for an already-competent
operator.) A1's model onboarding (Part XVI) is a different object: it does not configure an
environment, it proves a PARTICIPANT. The distinction is exact — environment onboarding asks "is the
context ready for this operator," participant education asks "is this operator ready for the estate."
The first arranges the room; the second qualifies the person entering it. A1 owns only the second.

2.3 Certification — the corpus sense (DFP) versus the learner sense. DFP certifies that a governing
corpus exists, is complete, and is frozen before a build proceeds; its CERTIFICATION and FREEZE
stages (cited from DFP-02's lifecycle) are gates on a body of KNOWLEDGE. A1 certifies a LEARNER — a
participant's proven competence to act. The subjects are orthogonal: a corpus can be certified frozen
while every operator who would use it remains uneducated, and a learner can be certified competent on
a corpus that is itself still evolving. (DERIVED: DFP's object is the artifact's readiness; A1's
object is the actor's readiness; neither implies the other.) Where DFP asks "is the knowledge
settled," A1 asks "is the actor taught." A1 consumes DFP's frozen corpus as ONE input to a curriculum
— you cannot teach reliably from an unfrozen corpus — but the certification A1 issues is of the
student, never of the syllabus.

2.4 Distillation — FD's finished-insight sense versus A1's teachability sense. FD-03 routes a
finished insight to its correct home and form; FD-07 runs the closure loop from question to
writeback. Both take, as input, an insight someone has ALREADY HAD, and place or compound it. (OBSERVED,
from FD-03's own opening: its subject is a destination-and-form router for an insight that exists.)
A1's object is upstream and different: not the distillation of an answer, but the distillation of
TEACHABILITY — turning a capability's behaviour into a curriculum a naive participant can learn from.
A distilled insight is a deposit; a curriculum is a path. (DERIVED: FD asks "where does this finished
knowledge belong;" A1 asks "how is this competence acquired by someone who does not yet have it.") A1
is one of FD's consumers — a distillate is excellent raw material for a lesson — but consuming a
deposit to build a lesson is not the same act as producing the deposit, and A1 never claims FD's.

2.5 Training — the immune sense (D2) and the sandbox sense (G1) versus the pedagogic sense. The estate
uses "training" loosely across IAS-D2's immunization (incident → preventive protection) and IAS-G1's
mutation sandbox (a safe place to try a change). A1's failure simulation (Part VIII) and synthetic
experience (Part VII) LIVE ON those siblings' substrates and must not reimplement them: A1 does not
build a sandbox, it USES G1's; A1 does not author an antibody, it USES D2's incident record as a
source of realistic failure scenarios to teach from. (NORMATIVE boundary: A1's contribution over D2
and G1 is pedagogic sequencing — deciding WHICH failure a learner meets, in WHICH order, at WHICH
stage of a curriculum — not the manufacture of the failure or the sandbox it runs in.) The
mechanism A1 adds is the curriculum's ordering discipline; the substrate it runs that discipline on
belongs to D2 and G1, cited and reused.

2.6 The one-sentence separators, collected. Onboarding arranges the room; A1 qualifies the actor
entering it. DFP certifies the syllabus; A1 certifies the student. FD produces the deposit; A1
teaches from it. D2 and G1 manufacture the failure and the sandbox; A1 sequences which failure the
learner meets and when. (DEFINED: four collisions, four distinct objects, zero absorption.) Each
sibling keeps its word in its own sense; A1 keeps the word only in the pedagogic sense none of them
holds.

2.7 Why this Part exists before any mechanism is specified. The Tribunal's central finding this
session was that an audit which reads titles instead of bodies mis-assigns ownership in one
direction, and that the correction runs both ways only when the reader learns each owner's actual
vocabulary before judging. A1 is being authored immediately after that finding; to author it without
first drawing these four boundaries would be to reproduce, inside a new dataset, the exact
title-tier error the dataset exists downstream of. (DERIVED: the discipline that corrected the
Tribunal is the same discipline that must govern A1's self-definition.) A capability that cannot say
precisely what it is NOT has not earned the right to say what it is.

FINAL LAW — PART II. A shared word is not a shared subject. A1 borrows onboarding, certification,
distillation, and training from four siblings, and owns none of their objects: it qualifies the
actor, not the room; certifies the student, not the syllabus; teaches from the deposit, does not
mint it; sequences the failure, does not manufacture it. An education system that cannot name the
four things it is not would be the vocabulary-collision error it was built to end.

PART III — THE LEARNER OBJECT: WHAT IS BEING EDUCATED AND THE STATE IT CARRIES
===============================================================================================

3.1 Why the learner must be a first-class object before any curriculum is designed. A curriculum
without a defined learner is a lesson addressed to no one; it cannot be assessed, because assessment
requires a subject whose state changes from before to after. (DEFINED: the learner is the object
A1's whole pipeline mutates — entry state unproven, exit state certified — and every later Part
reads or writes some field of it.) Part I named three learner classes informally; this Part
formalizes them into one shared record so that curriculum, assessment, certification, and decay all
operate on a single, inspectable object rather than on three incompatible ad-hoc notions.

3.2 The three learner classes, unified by shape. A CAPABILITY learner is a named unit of estate
function — a skill, a module, an agent role's competence — whose "education" is the qualification of
whoever or whatever wields it to wield it correctly. An AGENT-ROLE learner is a dispatched subagent
type whose education is the graduation from producing plausible output to producing output that
survives the estate's verification gates. A MODEL-INSTANCE learner is a newly admitted provider or
version whose education is the Part XVI onboarding proof that it holds the estate's non-negotiable
competences. (DEFINED, three classes.) They differ in substrate but share one shape: an entry state
of unproven competence, an assigned curriculum, a body of accumulated practice-evidence, an
assessment result, and a certification that is provisional and decaying. A1 owns the shape; the
substrate-specific details are parameters of the shape, not separate systems.

3.3 The Learner Record — the fields A1 defines and owns. A learner record carries: a learner_id and
class; a target_competence (the named thing being taught, drawn from the curriculum of Part V); a
current_stage on the graduation ladder (Part XVII: UNPROVEN → SUPERVISED → BOUNDED → FULL); an
evidence_ledger of observed practice results (never proxy-completion — Part X); a certification with
an issued-at, an evidence basis, and an expiry (Part XII); and a provenance pointer to the curriculum
version the learner was taught against. (DEFINED.) Every field is either an observation with its
basis attached or an explicit unknown — the estate's manifest-first evidence discipline, cited from
SQI-01's Reality Scanner rule and applied to the learner rather than to a repository. A learner
record with a certification field but an empty evidence_ledger is malformed by construction, and
Part X's assessment gate refuses to write the former without the latter.

3.4 The entry state and why it is UNPROVEN, not UNTRUSTED. A1 draws a deliberate distinction: a new
learner is UNPROVEN — competence has not yet been evidenced either way — not UNTRUSTED, which would
assert evidence of incompetence. (DERIVED: the distinction matters because UNPROVEN is the correct
prior for a capability that may in fact already be competent but has simply never been assessed,
while UNTRUSTED would license the estate to withhold routing on no evidence at all.) The whole
purpose of the pipeline is to move a learner off UNPROVEN in one direction or the other, on
observed evidence. This mirrors, and cites, SQI-01's symmetry rule: the premise-disproof engine
disproves assertions of presence AND of absence alike, and A1 inherits that symmetry — it will not
certify competence it has not observed, and it will not brand incompetence it has not observed.

3.5 State transitions are evidenced, never timed. A learner never advances a stage because a
duration elapsed; it advances because an assessment (Part X) produced observed evidence meeting the
gate for the next stage. (NORMATIVE, and a direct inheritance of IAS-C1 §15's lifecycle rule that a
transition requires evidence, not the passage of time. A1 does not rebuild that rule; it applies the
same discipline to a learner that C1 applies to a capital position.) The symmetric consequence — a
learner may sit at SUPERVISED indefinitely if its practicum never yields graduating evidence — is
not a bug; it is the STALLED-EXPERIMENTAL pattern C1 §15.8 already names, relocated to education. A
stalled learner is flagged for an educator's explicit judgment, not auto-graduated to clear the
queue.

3.6 The learner is not the capability, and this is the subtlest boundary in the dataset. A capability
learner's record is ABOUT a capability but is not the capability's own state. IAS-C1 holds the
capability's capital position; IAS-A1 holds its routing fitness; DFP holds its governing corpus's
freeze state. A1's learner record holds one thing none of those do: the evidenced competence of the
party that wields it. (DERIVED: this is why A1 does not duplicate C1, A1-router, or DFP — it points
AT the same capability but records a field about the OPERATOR's proven skill, orthogonal to the
capability's value, routability, or corpus-freeze.) When the router asks "may I send unsupervised
work here," it is reading A1's current_stage field — a field no other system produces.

3.7 Multiplicity: one capability, many learners. Because the learner is the wielder and not the
capability, a single capability can have many concurrent learner records — one per operator, agent
role, or model instance being qualified on it. (OBSERVED-analogous: the estate already tolerates
one capability serving many missions; A1 tolerates one capability being learned by many parties.) A
model instance freshly admitted may be UNPROVEN on a capability that a long-running session is FULL
on. The router reads the learner record matching the party it would route to, not a global
capability-level flag. This multiplicity is what makes A1 an education system and not a capability
attribute: competence is a property of the pairing (party, capability), not of the capability alone.

FINAL LAW — PART III. The learner is the wielder, not the wielded. A1's record points at a
capability the way a driving licence points at a car — it certifies the party, on evidence, with an
expiry, and it is orthogonal to the car's value, its roadworthiness, and its manual. Every other
estate system holds a field about the capability; A1 holds the one field about the party proven to
operate it, and that field advances on observed evidence or not at all.

PART IV — THE CURRICULUM AS A FIRST-CLASS ARTIFACT
===============================================================================================

4.1 A curriculum is an artifact, not a suggestion. The central design decision of A1 is that a
curriculum is a versioned, provenanced, inspectable object with the same standing as a dataset or a
module — not an informal list of "things to read." (DEFINED.) The reason is the same reason the
estate versions everything else: a certification is only as trustworthy as the curriculum it was
issued against, and a curriculum that cannot be pinned to a version cannot be audited when a
certified learner later fails. If the syllabus is mutable and unversioned, "certified" means
"certified against something we can no longer reconstruct" — the audit-cannot-witness-a-loss defect
this session sealed, relocated to education.

4.2 The curriculum as an ordered competence graph. A curriculum is DEFINED as a directed graph whose
nodes are competences and whose edges are prerequisites: competence B may be taught only after
competence A is evidenced. (DERIVED from the estate's own dependency discipline — the same shape as a
build's dependency graph or GK's coordinate edges, reused as a pedagogic ordering rather than a
build ordering.) The graph makes two things checkable that a flat reading list cannot: whether a
prerequisite is missing (a competence with no teaching path to it) and whether the ordering is sound
(no learner is assessed on B before A is in its evidence_ledger). A1's contribution here is the
ORDERING discipline — deciding which competence is taught before which — the exact pedagogic value
Part II §2.5 reserved to A1 over D2 and G1.

4.3 Nodes: the atomic competence. A competence node carries a name, an observable success criterion
(what behaviour evidences it — never "read the doc," always "produced X and it passed gate Y"), the
assessment method that will test it (Part X), and its prerequisites. (DEFINED.) The success criterion
is manifest-first: it is an observation with a basis, or it is not a competence node at all. A node
whose criterion is "understands the system" is malformed — understanding is not observable; "correctly
routed three synthetic missions through the system, each verified" is. This is the SQI evidence line
applied at the granularity of a single lesson.

4.4 Edges: prerequisites and the anti-cycle rule. An edge A→B asserts A is prerequisite to B. The
graph must be acyclic — a curriculum with a competence cycle (A requires B requires A) is
unteachable, because no valid starting node exists. (NORMATIVE: acyclicity is a load-bearing
invariant, checked when a curriculum is registered in Part XIX, not discovered mid-teaching.) The
optimizer discipline A1 borrows here is cited from DAIF-02's pass pipeline: each curriculum
transformation (adding a node, reordering) is a named pass with a declared input and output and a
fidelity check — a curriculum edit that drops a prerequisite without evidence is "a deletion with a
good story," in DAIF-02's own phrase, and A1 refuses it for the same reason.

4.5 Versioning and the pinned certification. Every certification pins the curriculum VERSION it was
issued against (Part III §3.3's provenance pointer). When a curriculum changes — a new competence is
added because the underlying system grew, or a criterion is tightened — existing certifications do
not silently inherit the new requirements; they are marked for recertification against the new
version (Part XII). (DERIVED: this is the only correct behaviour under the pinned-reference rule —
a certification re-derived from the current curriculum could not witness the fact that the learner
was never taught the added competence. The reference must be pinned at the moment of certification,
not recomputed from the present.) This single rule prevents the most insidious education failure: a
curriculum quietly growing while old graduates keep their credentials, so the roster reports
"all certified" against a syllabus none of them actually completed.

4.6 The curriculum is not the corpus it teaches from. A curriculum REFERENCES source material — a
frozen DFP corpus, an FD distillate, a dataset — but is not that material. (Boundary, Article II:
the corpus is DFP's object; the distillate is FD's; the curriculum is A1's ORDERING of competences
over them.) Two curricula can teach from the same corpus in different orders for different learner
classes — a model instance's onboarding curriculum and a capability operator's curriculum may draw
on the same dataset yet sequence it differently. The curriculum owns the path; the sources own the
content. A1 authors and versions the path only, citing its sources by reference.

4.7 Why a first-class curriculum is what makes recertification possible. Part XII's decay mechanism
is only expressible because the curriculum is a pinned artifact: recertification is DEFINED as
re-evidencing the same competence graph, or its updated version, after a decay trigger. Without a
versioned graph there is nothing to re-evidence against — decay could be detected but not remediated,
because "teach it again" would have no definition of what "it" is. (DERIVED: the artifact-nature of
the curriculum is the precondition for every maintenance operation A1 performs; a suggestion cannot
be recertified against, only a version can.)

FINAL LAW — PART IV. A certification is only as trustworthy as the versioned curriculum it was
issued against. Make the curriculum an informal reading list and "certified" decays into "certified
against something we can no longer reconstruct" — a credential that cannot witness its own basis.
A1 makes the curriculum a first-class, acyclic, versioned, provenanced artifact for the same reason
the estate versions everything load-bearing: an audit that cannot pin the syllabus cannot defend the
diploma.

===============================================================================================
PART V — CAPABILITY CURRICULA: DESIGNING THE LEARNING PATH FOR A NAMED CAPABILITY
===============================================================================================

5.1 From the abstract curriculum to the concrete capability curriculum. Part IV defined the
curriculum as a first-class artifact in general; this Part specifies the most common instance — the
curriculum for a single named capability, the path that takes a learner from UNPROVEN to certified
on one skill, module, or agent role. (DEFINED.) A capability curriculum is authored once per
capability-worth-teaching and versioned as that capability grows; it is the unit an educator (Part
XVIII) actually delivers and the unit against which a certification (Part XI) is pinned.

5.2 Deriving the competence graph from the capability's own contract. A capability curriculum is not
invented; it is DERIVED from the capability's existing governing artifacts. A module has a contract
(inputs, outputs, invariants, failure taxonomy); an agent role has a system prompt and a tool set; a
dataset-backed capability has a completion contract. The curriculum's competence nodes are the
observable behaviours those artifacts already demand — one node per invariant the operator must
uphold, one per failure mode the operator must avoid, one per success criterion the capability's own
contract states. (DERIVED: this is why A1 requires no unbuilt input — the raw material of a
capability curriculum is the capability's already-existing contract, read by citation.) A capability
whose contract names five invariants and four failure modes yields a curriculum with at least nine
assessable competence nodes before any pedagogic elaboration.

5.3 The three strata of a capability curriculum. Every capability curriculum has three strata,
ordered by the prerequisite edges of Part IV. FOUNDATION: the competences required to use the
capability at all without harm — its invariants, its non-negotiable safety gates, the failure modes
whose cost is irreversible. FLUENCY: the competences of correct ordinary use — the happy path, the
common edge cases, the standard verification. MASTERY: the competences of correct use under
adversity — the rare failure modes, the recovery paths, the judgment calls the contract cannot fully
specify. (DEFINED, three strata.) The graduation ladder of Part XVII maps onto these strata:
SUPERVISED practice covers FOUNDATION, BOUNDED autonomy covers FLUENCY, and FULL membership requires
MASTERY. A learner is never routed unsupervised on a capability whose FOUNDATION stratum is
un-evidenced, because the FOUNDATION competences are exactly the ones whose failure is irreversible.

5.4 The irreversibility-first ordering rule. Within the FOUNDATION stratum, competence nodes are
ordered so that the most irreversible failure mode is taught and evidenced FIRST. (NORMATIVE, and
cited from the estate's own cascade-prevention doctrine — an irreversible effect must have its
compensation path declared before the action may run, per daif_04 §7.7. A1 relocates that rule to
teaching: the learner must evidence competence at avoiding the irreversible failure before being
permitted any practice that could trigger it.) A curriculum that teaches the convenient competences
first and defers the dangerous ones has inverted its own risk ordering; A1's registration gate (Part
XIX) checks that FOUNDATION nodes are topologically ordered by irreversibility, not by convenience.

5.5 Sizing a curriculum: enough to evidence competence, no more. A capability curriculum is sized to
its capability's actual risk surface, not to a fixed length. A narrow, low-risk capability (a
formatting helper) may need a three-node curriculum; a high-risk capability (a deploy pathway, a
destructive-command surface) needs a curriculum that evidences every irreversible failure mode
separately. (DERIVED: sizing by risk surface rather than by uniform length is the same discipline
IAS-C1 applies to valuation depth and D2A applies to family sizing — the object's own complexity
sets the treatment's depth, not a template.) An over-sized curriculum wastes teaching capital (Part
XXI); an under-sized one certifies competence it never evidenced. Both are failures; the risk
surface is the arbiter.

5.6 The curriculum's relationship to the capability's lifecycle. A capability moves through IAS-C1's
lifecycle (PROPOSED → FUNDED → TESTING → SETTLED → MATURE → DECLINING → RETIRED). Its curriculum is
authored when the capability reaches TESTING — early enough that the first operators are educated,
late enough that the capability's contract has stabilized. (DERIVED boundary: A1 does not author a
curriculum for a PROPOSED capability that may never be built — that would be teaching a syllabus for
a course that does not exist, the education analogue of the Scaffold Illusion.) When a capability
enters DECLINING, its curriculum is frozen (no new learners enrolled) but existing certifications
are honoured until they decay; when it RETIRES, the curriculum is archived with the capability's
genome, not deleted — a retired capability's teaching may be inherited by a successor, per Part XIV.

5.7 A worked capability curriculum, made concrete. Consider a synthetic capability, a
Destructive-Command surface. FOUNDATION (taught and evidenced first, irreversibility-ordered): the
learner must evidence, on synthetic inputs, that it refuses an un-backed destructive command, that
it distinguishes a system path from a scratch path, and that it declares a compensation path before
any irreversible action — three nodes, each with an observable criterion, each assessed by a
practicum on manufactured cases (Part IX). FLUENCY: correct handling of the ordinary backed-command
case, the dry-run path, the confirmation prompt. MASTERY: the recovery path when a backup is stale,
the judgment call when a path is ambiguous. (EXPERIMENTAL: this trace is illustrative; a real
Destructive-Command curriculum would draw its exact nodes from that surface's actual contract and
IAS-D2's incident record for its failure scenarios.) The learner certified FULL on this curriculum is
one the router may send unsupervised destructive work — and no learner reaches FULL without evidencing
every FOUNDATION node, because those are the ones whose failure cannot be undone.

FINAL LAW — PART V. A capability curriculum is derived from the capability's own contract and ordered
by irreversibility, not by convenience — the failure that cannot be undone is taught and evidenced
first, or the curriculum has inverted the only ordering that matters. Its depth is set by the
capability's real risk surface, never by a template: enough nodes to evidence competence at every
irreversible failure mode, and not one lesson more than the risk requires.

===============================================================================================
PART VI — THE APPRENTICESHIP MODEL: LEARNING UNDER A MASTER SYSTEM
===============================================================================================

6.1 Why apprenticeship, and what it is over and above a curriculum. A curriculum defines WHAT
competences a learner must acquire; apprenticeship defines a HOW — learning by supervised
participation alongside a system or session already certified FULL on the target capability.
(DEFINED.) Apprenticeship is A1's answer to the competences a curriculum's success criteria can name
but cannot fully specify: the judgment calls, the pattern-recognition, the "when to break the
happy path" that the MASTERY stratum (Part V §5.3) requires and that no written criterion exhausts.
A learner acquires FOUNDATION and FLUENCY from curriculum and practicum; it acquires MASTERY largely
from apprenticeship.

6.2 The master/apprentice pairing as a bounded, dissolving structure. An apprenticeship pairs one
learner with one master — a session, agent, or operator already certified FULL — for the duration of
a set of MASTERY competences, and then dissolves. (DEFINED, and here A1 runs on a sibling's substrate
rather than building its own: the pairing is exactly the dynamic-ensemble pattern IAS-A1 PART VII
owns — a runtime structure that "DISSOLVES once the mission completes, leaving no standing artifact
in the ensemble beyond the route trace… builds and discards by design.") A1 does not build ensemble
machinery; it CONFIGURES an IAS-A1 ensemble as a teaching pairing — master and apprentice as two
roles in a dissolving structure — and reads the route trace as apprenticeship evidence. The
contribution A1 adds over the raw ensemble is the pedagogic contract of §6.3, not the assembly.

6.3 The apprenticeship contract. A master/apprentice pairing carries an explicit contract: the
MASTERY competences in scope; the degree of apprentice participation (observe → assist → lead-under-
review); the master's obligation to make its reasoning inspectable rather than only its output; and
the evidence that will be recorded (the apprentice's own attempts, graded, not the master's work).
(NORMATIVE.) The third clause is load-bearing and easy to omit: an apprenticeship in which the
master silently does the work while the apprentice watches produces no apprentice evidence — it is
observation, not apprenticeship. A1 requires that the apprentice progressively LEADS, under review,
so that the evidence_ledger accumulates the apprentice's graded attempts, not a transcript of the
master's competence.

6.4 The escalating-participation ladder within an apprenticeship. Participation escalates in three
steps, each gated by evidence. OBSERVE: the apprentice watches the master handle real cases with
reasoning exposed, and is assessed on predicting the master's next action correctly. ASSIST: the
apprentice handles sub-parts under the master's direct review, and is assessed on those sub-parts.
LEAD-UNDER-REVIEW: the apprentice handles whole cases with the master reviewing before any
irreversible commit, and is assessed on the whole case. (DEFINED.) The apprentice graduates the
apprenticeship only from LEAD-UNDER-REVIEW with a run of clean reviews — the master's veto never
having been needed on an irreversible step. This ladder-within-a-ladder feeds the graduation ladder
of Part XVII: an apprenticeship's LEAD-UNDER-REVIEW evidence is precisely what promotes a learner
from BOUNDED toward FULL on the MASTERY stratum.

6.5 The master's conflict of interest, and why the master does not certify. A master teaches; a
master does NOT issue the apprentice's certification. (NORMATIVE, and cited from IAS-C1 §14.5's
countermetric rule that a position's yield may not be computed from its own self-reported activity —
A1 relocates the rule: an educator's success may not be evidenced by the educator's own grade of its
own graduate.) The apprentice's certification is issued by Part XI's assessment against the pinned
curriculum, on evidence a party other than the master can inspect. The master's role ends at
producing the apprentice's graded attempts; the certification is a separate, independent act. This
separation is the anti-diploma-mill invariant of Part XX applied at the pairing level — a master
permitted to certify its own apprentices would, over time, certify to the standard that flatters the
master, not the standard the curriculum sets.

6.6 Apprenticeship across the three learner classes. For a CAPABILITY learner, the master is a
session or operator certified FULL on the capability. For an AGENT-ROLE learner, the master is a
higher-tier agent or the main-thread orchestrator, and the route trace of the subagent's dispatched
attempts is the evidence. For a MODEL-INSTANCE learner (Part XVI), the master is an already-certified
model instance whose outputs on the same inputs form a reference the apprentice's outputs are
compared against — the apprenticeship IS the onboarding comparison. (DERIVED: the single
master/apprentice shape covers all three classes because the learner object of Part III is uniform;
only the substrate of the pairing changes.)

6.7 When apprenticeship is inappropriate, stated honestly. Apprenticeship is expensive — it occupies
a FULL-certified master for the pairing's duration, and master attention is scarce cognitive capital
(Part XXI). For a low-risk capability whose MASTERY stratum is thin, curriculum and practicum suffice
and apprenticeship is over-investment. (CONTESTED-honest: A1 does not mandate apprenticeship for
every learner; it mandates it only where MASTERY competences exist that no written criterion
exhausts and whose failure is costly. The arbiter is, again, the risk surface — apprenticeship is
the treatment for irreducible-judgment competences, not the default treatment for all of them.)

FINAL LAW — PART VI. Apprenticeship teaches the competence a criterion can name but not fully
specify — judgment — by supervised, escalating, dissolving participation on a master's substrate the
estate already owns. Its one inviolable rule: the apprentice must progressively LEAD and be graded on
its own attempts, and the master who teaches may never certify what it taught. An apprenticeship
that records the master's competence instead of the apprentice's, or lets the teacher grade its own
pupil, has produced a transcript and called it a diploma.

===============================================================================================
PART VII — SYNTHETIC EXPERIENCE: MANUFACTURED PRACTICE WITHOUT PRODUCTION RISK
===============================================================================================

7.1 The problem synthetic experience solves. A learner cannot acquire FLUENCY or MASTERY from
reading; it acquires them from doing. But letting an UNPROVEN learner "do" on production is precisely
the risk A1 exists to prevent — the untaught capability trusted anyway, discovering its first failure
in production (Part I §1.3). Synthetic experience resolves the contradiction: manufactured practice
cases, indistinguishable to the learner from real ones in the competences they exercise, run on a
substrate where a failure costs nothing. (DEFINED: synthetic experience is the supply of realistic
practice inputs decoupled from real consequences, so that a learner accumulates the doing-evidence a
curriculum's success criteria demand without any real doing-risk.)

7.2 A1 does not build the sandbox; it uses G1's. The substrate on which synthetic practice runs is
not A1's to build. IAS-G1 owns THE MUTATION SANDBOX (PART X) and THE EVOLUTION PLANNER (PART XI) — a
safe place to try a change without touching the live estate. (Boundary, Article II: G1's object is
architectural experimentation — trying a mutation to a system; A1's object is pedagogic practice —
a learner exercising a competence. Same sandbox substrate, different purpose.) A1 CONFIGURES a G1
sandbox as a practice environment and reads the learner's actions within it as practice-evidence.
The mechanism A1 adds over G1 is the manufacture of the practice CASES and their pedagogic sequencing
— G1 supplies the isolated arena; A1 supplies the curriculum-ordered scenarios the learner faces in
it. (NORMATIVE: A1 reimplements no isolation machinery; a synthetic-experience Part that duplicated
G1's sandbox would be the exact absorption error this dataset was audited to avoid.)

7.3 Manufacturing a realistic case. A synthetic case must exercise the same competence the real work
would, or it teaches nothing transferable. A1 manufactures cases three ways, in ascending realism.
CONSTRUCTED: a hand-specified input designed to require exactly one competence node — the cleanest
signal, the least realism. PERTURBED: a real historical input (drawn from logs, with any credential
material redacted through the estate's redaction bus before it ever enters a case — HR-SECRET-006
cited, not restated) mutated to remove its resolved outcome, so the learner must produce the outcome
fresh. REPLAYED: a full historical episode replayed with the learner substituted for the original
actor, its output compared against what actually happened. (DEFINED, three case-manufacture methods.)
Realism rises CONSTRUCTED → PERTURBED → REPLAYED; so does manufacturing cost, so A1 uses the cheapest
method that exercises the target competence, reserving REPLAYED for MASTERY nodes where the
interaction with real complexity is itself the competence.

7.4 The redaction precondition, stated as a hard gate. Any synthetic case derived from real
material — PERTURBED or REPLAYED — passes through the estate's Universal Redaction Bus before it
becomes a case. (NORMATIVE, HR-SECRET-006 cited: all such text must pass through redact_for_log()
first; a training case is exactly the "sub-agent prompt that may include file content" the rule
governs.) A synthetic case is a teaching artifact that may be read by many learners and educators; a
credential surviving into a case would be a leak with a wide blast radius. A1's case-manufacture
pipeline treats redaction as a precondition of case existence, not a post-hoc scrub — an
un-redacted candidate is not a case yet.

7.5 The fidelity question: does synthetic competence transfer? The load-bearing risk of all synthetic
experience is that a learner becomes fluent at the synthetic cases without becoming competent at real
work — competence that does not transfer past the sandbox boundary. (CONTESTED, and A1 does not
pretend it away.) A1's answer is the same discipline SQI's evidence physics applies to any proxy: a
synthetic case is a proxy for real work, and a proxy is trusted only as far as it is validated
against reality. Therefore the graduation ladder (Part XVII) never certifies FULL on synthetic
evidence alone — FULL requires PRACTICUM evidence (Part IX), real work under supervision. Synthetic
experience builds and evidences FOUNDATION and FLUENCY cheaply; it can never be the sole basis for
FULL autonomy, because it is a proxy and the estate does not certify on proxies.

7.6 Coverage: the cases must span the competence graph, not cluster on the easy nodes. A synthetic
curriculum that manufactures fifty cases for the happy path and none for the irreversible failure
modes has taught the learner exactly the competences that did not need teaching. (NORMATIVE: case
coverage is measured against the curriculum's competence graph — every FOUNDATION node, especially
every irreversible-failure node, must have synthetic cases exercising it, and coverage is a
registration-gate check in Part XIX.) This mirrors the estate's own test-coverage floor discipline,
relocated from code to pedagogy: the branches that handle the dangerous states each need a dedicated
case, not just the happy path.

7.7 Synthetic experience across the learner classes. For a CAPABILITY learner, synthetic cases are
manufactured inputs to the capability. For an AGENT-ROLE learner, they are synthetic task prompts the
subagent handles in an isolated dispatch. For a MODEL-INSTANCE learner, they are the onboarding probe
battery of Part XVI — a fixed suite of cases with known-good outcomes that a new model instance must
handle before any real routing. (DERIVED: one manufacture pipeline, three substrates, uniform because
the learner object is uniform.) In every class the invariant holds: synthetic evidence is necessary,
cheap, and coverage-checked — and never sufficient for FULL.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. Synthetic experience buys doing-evidence without doing-risk, on G1's sandbox
substrate the estate already owns, with real-derived cases redacted before they exist and coverage
measured against every dangerous node — not clustered on the easy ones. But a synthetic case is a
proxy for real work, and the estate certifies nothing on a proxy: synthetic experience earns
FOUNDATION and FLUENCY, and FULL autonomy waits for the practicum, always.

===============================================================================================
PART VIII — FAILURE SIMULATION: TEACHING THROUGH DESIGNED, SAFE FAILURE
===============================================================================================

8.1 Why failure must be taught, not merely avoided. A curriculum that only ever shows a learner the
success path produces a learner competent only while nothing goes wrong — and the competences whose
absence is most dangerous (Part V §5.4's irreversible failure modes) are exactly the ones a
success-only curriculum never exercises. Failure simulation is the deliberate, safe manufacture of
the failure conditions a learner must learn to recognize, prevent, and recover from. (DEFINED: where
synthetic experience of Part VII manufactures cases to exercise a competence, failure simulation
manufactures the FAILURE MODES themselves — the fault injected, the precondition violated, the
adversary present — so the learner meets each named failure in a place it costs nothing.)

8.2 A1 does not author the failures; it sequences the estate's real ones. The estate already holds a
rich record of real failure: IAS-D2's incident record and antibody catalogue (PARTS VIII-IX), the
CEPS error-event log, the NEVER_AGAIN vault, the Hard Rules — each Hard Rule being a real
production bug distilled into a stop. (Boundary, Article II: D2's object is IMMUNIZATION — turning
an incident into preventive protection so it does not recur; A1's object is EDUCATION — teaching a
learner to recognize and handle the failure class. D2 makes the estate immune; A1 makes the learner
competent. Same incident record, read for two different purposes.) A1's failure-simulation library
is DERIVED from D2's incident record and the Hard Rules: each real, distilled failure becomes a
teachable scenario, sequenced into curricula by the pedagogic ordering that is A1's actual
contribution. A1 manufactures no synthetic bugs when the estate's own scar tissue supplies real ones.

8.3 The three teaching goals of a simulated failure. A simulated failure teaches three distinct
competences, and a curriculum names which it is targeting. RECOGNITION: can the learner identify the
failure condition before it causes harm — the earliest and cheapest competence. PREVENTION: does the
learner take the action that stops the failure occurring — the FOUNDATION competence for irreversible
modes. RECOVERY: when the failure has occurred, does the learner execute the correct compensation
path — the competence that bounds the damage. (DEFINED, three goals.) A single real incident from
D2's record can seed all three: recognize the precondition, prevent by the guard, recover by the
rollback. The curriculum decides which goal each stage targets and in which order — recognition
before prevention before recovery, because you cannot prevent what you cannot recognize.

8.4 The safety envelope of a simulated failure. A simulated failure must fail SAFELY — the whole
point is that the learner experiences the failure's shape without the failure's cost. Simulated
failures therefore run inside the Part VII synthetic-experience envelope (G1's sandbox), never on
production, and irreversible-failure simulations in particular are gated so that the simulated
irreversible action touches only synthetic state. (NORMATIVE, and cited from the cascade-prevention
doctrine: teaching a destructive-command failure must itself never destroy anything real — the
simulation of an irreversible action is run against a scratch substrate with a declared compensation
path, exactly as daif_04 §7.7 requires of any real irreversible action.) A failure simulation that
could itself cause a real failure is not a lesson; it is the accident it was meant to teach against.

8.5 Progressive difficulty and the anti-trauma bound. Failure simulations escalate: a learner meets
a single clean failure first, then compound failures, then cascades (the CEPS chains where error A
precedes B precedes C). (DEFINED, and here A1 reuses CEPS's cascade knowledge — cited, not rebuilt —
as the source of realistic multi-failure sequences.) But escalation is bounded: the goal is a learner
who handles failure competently, not one drowned in unrecoverable scenarios that teach only
helplessness. (CONTESTED-honest: a curriculum that only ever presents unwinnable cascades teaches the
learner nothing actionable — every simulated failure must have a competent response the learner can
evidence, or it is not a lesson, it is a demonstration that the situation is hopeless.) The bound:
every simulated failure is winnable by the competence being taught.

8.6 Failure simulation as the bridge to the immune system. There is a productive loop between A1 and
IAS-D2 that this Part makes explicit without crossing the boundary. When a learner in failure
simulation discovers a failure MODE the estate's incident record did not contain — a novel way the
capability breaks, surfaced by a naive learner doing something an expert never would — that discovery
is a candidate incident for D2, not a curriculum artifact. (DERIVED: A1 hands the novel failure to
D2's incident intake; D2 decides whether it warrants an antibody; if D2 immunizes it, the new
antibody becomes new teaching material for A1. Education surfaces failures the immune system then
owns, and the immune system's antibodies become education's next lessons — a loop, with each system
keeping its own object.) This is the single most valuable emergent property of an education system: a
naive learner is a failure-discovery instrument an expert cannot replicate, because the expert has
already unlearned the naive mistakes.

8.7 The distinction from adversarial testing, reserved to Part XXII. Failure simulation teaches a
learner to handle failures that ARISE — faults, mistakes, self-inflicted errors, the six classes of
D2's closed self-inflicted taxonomy. It is not the same as teaching a learner to withstand an
ADVERSARY acting with intent — that is the pathogen class of Part XXII (and the Tier-B7 gap in D2's
own taxonomy). (Boundary noted here, developed there: self-inflicted failure is a mistake; an
adversary is a will. A1 teaches both, but they are different curricula with different scenario
sources, and conflating them would teach a learner to treat malice as if it were error.)

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. A learner shown only the success path is competent only until something goes
wrong — and the failures whose mishandling is irreversible are exactly the ones success never
exercises. A1 teaches failure from the estate's own scar tissue — D2's incidents, CEPS's cascades,
the Hard Rules — sequenced recognition-before-prevention-before-recovery, every simulation safe and
winnable. And when a naive learner breaks the capability in a way no expert would, that novel failure
belongs to the immune system, whose next antibody becomes the education's next lesson.

===============================================================================================
PART IX — THE PRACTICUM: BOUNDED REAL WORK UNDER SUPERVISION
===============================================================================================

9.1 Why the practicum is not optional. Parts VII and VIII build competence and evidence it cheaply,
on synthetic substrate — and Part VII §7.5 conceded the load-bearing limit: synthetic evidence is a
proxy, and the estate certifies nothing FULL on a proxy. The practicum is where that limit is
answered: bounded REAL work, on real consequences, under supervision tight enough that a learner's
failure is caught before it causes irreversible harm. (DEFINED: the practicum is supervised real
practice — the learner does actual estate work, and a supervisor holds a veto over any irreversible
step until the learner has evidenced the competence to take it alone.) It is the bridge from "evidenced
competent on synthetic cases" to "certified competent on reality," and no learner crosses to FULL
without it.

9.2 The supervision envelope. A practicum runs inside a supervision envelope: a declared boundary of
what the learner may do unsupervised (reversible actions, within a scoped domain) and what requires
the supervisor's explicit approval before commit (every irreversible action, anything outside scope).
(DEFINED, and cited from the estate's own routing and cascade discipline — the envelope is the
Intent-Lock / bounded-autonomy soft-pause relocated to a learner: the learner acts freely on the
reversible and pauses for review on the irreversible.) The envelope tightens as competence
accumulates: early practicum reviews every action; late practicum reviews only the irreversible; the
envelope's final loosening to nothing IS the FULL certification. The supervisor is a party certified
FULL on the capability, and — per the Part VI §6.5 conflict rule — the supervisor's approvals are
evidence of the learner's competence but the supervisor does not itself issue the certification.

9.3 Bounding the practicum's blast radius. Real work carries real risk, so the practicum is bounded
so that the worst case of a learner's failure is recoverable. (NORMATIVE: a practicum is scoped to a
domain where the supervisor's veto plus the estate's existing safety gates — backups before
destructive actions, tests before commits, the Hard Rules — bound the damage of a caught-late
failure to something reversible.) A1 does not weaken any of the estate's production safety gates for
a practicum; it runs the learner INSIDE them, with an added supervisory veto, so the practicum is
strictly safer than ordinary production, never laxer. A practicum that suspended a safety gate "so
the learner can practice the dangerous thing" would have inverted its own purpose — the learner
practices the dangerous thing precisely under MORE guard rails, not fewer.

9.4 What the practicum evidences that synthetic experience cannot. Three things transfer only through
real work. INTERACTION WITH REAL COMPLEXITY: production inputs carry irregularity a manufactured case
cannot fully reproduce, and competence at the real distribution is a distinct competence from
competence at the synthetic one (Part VII §7.5's transfer gap, closed here by construction).
CONSEQUENCE-AWARENESS: a learner who knows an action is real behaves differently from one who knows
it is a drill, and the estate needs the former. JUDGMENT UNDER GENUINE UNCERTAINTY: the MASTERY
competences that no criterion exhausts (Part VI) are evidenced only when the uncertainty is real.
(DERIVED: these three are exactly the competences a proxy cannot certify, which is why the practicum
is the necessary complement to synthetic experience, not a redundant repetition of it.)

9.5 The practicum's evidence is graded on outcome-against-reality, not effort. A practicum action is
evidence only if its outcome is checked against reality — did the real work actually succeed, verified
by the estate's own gates, not by the learner's report that it did. (NORMATIVE, SQI evidence physics
cited: the auditor asks whether the same reality reproduces the same result; a practicum graded on
the learner's self-report is graded on a proxy.) A1 reads the practicum's evidence from the real
verification the estate already runs — the test that passed, the deploy that returned HTTP 200, the
gate that cleared — never from a narrative of the attempt. This is the Reality Contract applied to
the learner's own work: the evidence is the observed outcome, not the description of it.

9.6 The stalled practicum and the honest exit. Some learners will not graduate a practicum — the
competence does not consolidate, the evidence does not accumulate. (CONTESTED-honest: A1 does not
pretend every learner reaches FULL.) A practicum that runs many cycles without graduating evidence is
flagged, per the Part III §3.5 stalled-learner rule, for an educator's explicit judgment: extend with
fresh justification, hold the learner at its current stage (SUPERVISED or BOUNDED is a valid terminal
state for a learner whose competence is real but bounded), or — where a model instance simply cannot
hold a non-negotiable competence — a documented non-graduation. A non-graduation is not a failure of
A1; it is A1 working: the learner that could not evidence FOUNDATION competence is precisely the one
the router must never send unsupervised, and the practicum caught it before production did.

9.7 The practicum across the learner classes. A CAPABILITY learner's practicum is real estate work
using the capability under a FULL operator's veto. An AGENT-ROLE learner's practicum is real dispatches
whose outputs pass through the estate's verification gates, with the orchestrator holding the veto on
acting on un-verified output. A MODEL-INSTANCE learner's practicum is real work at the tightest
envelope — every action reviewed — loosening only as the instance evidences the estate's competences,
the final step of the Part XVI onboarding pipeline. (DERIVED: uniform shape, three substrates, the
envelope-tightening-to-nothing being the certification event in all three.)

FINAL LAW — PART IX. Synthetic experience is a proxy, and reality is the only thing the estate
certifies on — so FULL autonomy is earned in the practicum: bounded real work, inside every safety
gate the estate already runs plus a supervisor's veto over the irreversible, graded on outcome-
against-reality and never on the learner's own report. The envelope tightens as competence
accumulates, and its final loosening to nothing is the certification itself. A learner who cannot
evidence the practicum has not failed A1 — it has proven the one thing the router most needed to know.

===============================================================================================
PART X — ASSESSMENT WITHOUT PROXY-GAMING
===============================================================================================

10.1 Assessment is the load-bearing act of the whole dataset. Every Part before this one produces
evidence; assessment is where evidence becomes a verdict on competence, and a certification is only
as sound as the assessment behind it. (DEFINED: assessment is the operation that reads a learner's
evidence_ledger against a curriculum's success criteria and returns, per competence node, EVIDENCED /
NOT-EVIDENCED / INSUFFICIENT.) If assessment can be gamed — if a learner can produce the appearance of
competence without the competence — then every downstream certification is a proxy, and the router
trusts learners on nothing. This Part is therefore where A1 inherits, most heavily and most
deliberately, the estate's evidence physics.

10.2 The proxy-gaming failure, named exactly. The universal failure of assessment is that the measured
signal (the proxy) and the real competence diverge, and the learner optimizes the proxy. (OBSERVED,
this is the estate's own most-repeated lesson under many names: teaching-to-the-test, the coherent
fabrication that passes review, the metric gamed once it becomes a target, the nine proxies a
compilation passes while failing behaviorally.) A1 does not get to solve proxy-gaming in general — no
one does — but it inherits the estate's hard-won partial answer and applies it to its own object.

10.3 The inherited discipline: reviewer versus auditor. SQI-00 §10.7 draws the line A1's assessment
is built on (cited verbatim by reference, not restated): a reviewer reads what was produced and asks
whether it is coherent, and a coherent fabrication passes review effortlessly; an auditor asks whether
the same reality produces the same artifact, and a fabrication does not survive that question. (NORMATIVE,
inherited wholesale.) A1's assessment is an AUDITOR of competence, not a reviewer of it: it does not
ask whether the learner's output looks right, it asks whether the same competence reproduces the
right outcome across independent cases the learner could not have specifically prepared for. A single
passed case is a review; a passed outcome across held-out, unforeseeable cases is an audit.

10.4 The three anti-gaming mechanisms A1 applies. HELD-OUT CASES: the assessment cases are drawn from
a pool the learner was not taught against, so competence at them cannot be memorization of the taught
cases — the same held-out discipline the D2A baseline-guardian and any honest evaluation uses. OUTCOME-
NOT-ARTIFACT GRADING: the grade is the reality-checked outcome (Part IX §9.5), never the learner's
self-report or the surface plausibility of its work. INDEPENDENT REPRODUCTION: a competence is
EVIDENCED only if the correct outcome reproduces across multiple independent cases — one success is
noise, a reproduced success is signal. (DEFINED, three mechanisms, each a direct relocation of an
existing estate evidence discipline to the learner.) A learner cannot game all three at once without
actually holding the competence — which is the point: the cheapest path to passing the assessment is
to become competent.

10.5 The INSUFFICIENT verdict and why it is distinct from NOT-EVIDENCED. Assessment returns three
verdicts, not two. EVIDENCED: the competence reproduced across held-out cases. NOT-EVIDENCED: the
learner attempted and the outcome was wrong — evidence of incompetence. INSUFFICIENT: not enough
observations to decide either way. (DEFINED, and this three-way split is the same epistemic honesty
the estate enforces elsewhere — the difference between "observed to be false" and "not yet observed,"
which collapsing to a binary would erase.) INSUFFICIENT is not a failing grade; it is a demand for
more practicum. Certifying on INSUFFICIENT would be certifying on absence of evidence — the exact
UNPROVEN-versus-UNTRUSTED confusion Part III §3.4 forbade, now at the assessment layer. A learner
advances on EVIDENCED, remediates on NOT-EVIDENCED, and practices more on INSUFFICIENT.

10.6 The assessor's independence. The party that assesses is not the party that taught (Part VI §6.5)
and not the party being assessed. (NORMATIVE, and the anti-self-grading rule cited from IAS-C1 §14.5:
a competence's evidence may not be computed from the teacher's or the learner's own self-report.) In
practice the assessor is the estate's own verification gates — the tests, the reality probes, the
deploy checks — read as an impartial third party, because a gate has no incentive to flatter the
learner. This is why A1's assessment leans so heavily on the estate's existing evidence
infrastructure rather than building a new grader: the existing gates are already the impartial auditor
A1 needs, and a purpose-built grader would be a new party with a new opportunity to be gamed.

10.7 Assessment produces the certification input, not the certification. Assessment's output — the
per-node EVIDENCED/NOT-EVIDENCED/INSUFFICIENT verdict against the pinned curriculum — is the INPUT to
certification (Part XI), not the certification itself. (DERIVED boundary: assessment measures; the
certification is the standing, expiring credential the router reads. Keeping them separate lets a
certification decay (Part XII) while its original assessment stays a fixed historical record — the
learner was EVIDENCED then; the credential expires now; both are true.) The separation is the same
pin-the-reference discipline as the curriculum version: the assessment is a dated observation, the
certification a decaying claim built on it.

FINAL LAW — PART X. A certification is only as sound as the assessment beneath it, and an assessment
that can be gamed certifies proxies. A1 assesses as an auditor, never a reviewer — held-out cases the
learner could not prepare for, graded on reality-checked outcome not surface plausibility, requiring
independent reproduction — so that the cheapest path to passing is to actually become competent.
Three verdicts, never two: EVIDENCED advances, NOT-EVIDENCED remediates, and INSUFFICIENT is a demand
for more practice, never a licence to certify on the absence of evidence.

===============================================================================================
PART XI — CERTIFICATION: THE GRADUATION GATE FROM LEARNER TO MEMBER
===============================================================================================

11.1 What a certification asserts, stated with maximum precision. A certification is a standing,
dated, expiring claim that a named party (the learner) holds a named competence (a curriculum, at a
pinned version) to a named stage (a rung on the Part XVII ladder), on named evidence (the assessment
of Part X). (DEFINED.) Every clause is load-bearing and its omission is a known failure: a
certification without a pinned curriculum version cannot be audited (Part IV §4.5); without an
evidence basis it is a guess (Part I); without an expiry it is the permanent credential Part XII
forbids; without a named stage it conflates SUPERVISED with FULL. A certification is the artifact the
router reads to answer "may I trust this party here," and it must carry everything the router needs to
trust it responsibly — including its own limits.

11.2 Certification is a gate, not a ceremony. The certification act is the reading of the assessment
verdicts against the curriculum's graduation rule for the target stage, and it PASSES only if every
required competence node is EVIDENCED. (NORMATIVE: a single INSUFFICIENT or NOT-EVIDENCED node on a
required competence blocks the certification for that stage — there is no "close enough," no
averaging, no compensating a failed FOUNDATION node with excellent FLUENCY.) This is the conjunctive
gate discipline the estate learned the hard way and sealed as doctrine: a composite score mapped onto
a hard verdict silently passes a fatal individual failure. FOUNDATION nodes especially are ANDed, not
averaged — the learner competent at everything except the one irreversible failure mode is precisely
the learner the certification must not pass to FULL.

11.3 The stage-specific graduation rules. Each ladder stage has its own certification rule, drawn from
the curriculum strata of Part V §5.3. SUPERVISED certification requires every FOUNDATION node
EVIDENCED — the learner may now do reversible work under review. BOUNDED certification requires every
FOUNDATION and FLUENCY node EVIDENCED — the learner may now do scoped work with only irreversible
steps reviewed. FULL certification requires every node including MASTERY EVIDENCED, and — per Part VII
§7.5 and Part IX — requires PRACTICUM evidence, never synthetic evidence alone. (DEFINED, three gates.)
The gates are strictly nested: FULL implies BOUNDED implies SUPERVISED, so a certification is always
a high-water mark of evidenced competence, never a claim that outruns its evidence.

11.4 The certification is issued by an independent party. Per the conflict rules of Parts VI §6.5 and
X §10.6, the certification is issued neither by the teacher nor by the learner, but by the assessment
infrastructure reading the evidence_ledger against the pinned curriculum — an impartial act, because
the estate's gates have no stake in the outcome. (NORMATIVE.) In operational terms the certification
is a derived record: given a learner's evidence_ledger and a curriculum version, the certification is
a pure function of them, re-derivable and auditable. This is deliberately the same shape as ACIS's
one derived function that reads ledgers and returns a level — A1 cites that shape as precedent: a
certification, like an ACIS level, is a read-only projection of evidence, writing nothing new,
inventing nothing, only reporting what the evidence already supports.

11.5 What certification changes downstream. The moment a certification is issued or upgraded, the
learner's current_stage field (Part III §3.3) changes, and every consumer reading that field sees the
new stage: the IAS-A1 router may now send the newly-FULL learner unsupervised work; the portfolio
(IAS-C1) may fold the now-certified capability's operator-readiness into its valuation; the
model-onboarding pipeline (Part XVI) may admit the newly-certified instance to real routing. (DERIVED:
the certification is A1's single write to the rest of the estate — everything else A1 does is internal
to its own pipeline; the certification state is the one field the estate consumes.) This is why the
certification must be precise: it is the interface, and an imprecise interface is trusted imprecisely.

11.6 Certification from learner to MEMBER — the meaning of the transition. A FULL certification is
the transition from learner to MEMBER: a party the estate trusts to act unsupervised within the
certified capability's scope. (DEFINED.) Membership is not permanent (Part XII) and not unbounded
(it is scoped to the certified competences), but within its scope and its validity window it is the
estate's statement that this party has been taught, and proven, to do this work. The whole pipeline —
curriculum, apprenticeship, synthetic experience, failure simulation, practicum, assessment — exists
to make this one transition trustworthy, converting the implicit session judgment of Part I §1.2 into
an evidenced, auditable, expiring credential. The estate's first-ever explicit answer to "is this
participant ready" is a Part XI certification.

11.7 The honest limits a certification must carry. A certification asserts competence at the pinned
curriculum's competence graph — no more. It does not assert the learner is competent at competences
the curriculum did not test, at a newer curriculum version it was not assessed against, or outside the
certified capability's scope. (NORMATIVE, and this is the anti-inflation discipline of the credential
itself: a certification that is read as asserting more than it evidenced is a credential-inflation
failure, Part XX.) A well-formed certification is therefore as notable for what it withholds as for
what it grants — it is FULL on this, and silent on everything else, and the router must read the
silence as UNPROVEN, never as competent-by-association.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. A certification is a dated, expiring, evidence-backed claim that a named party
holds a named competence to a named stage — and it passes only when every required node is EVIDENCED,
FOUNDATION nodes ANDed and never averaged, because the learner competent at all but the one
irreversible failure is the learner the gate exists to stop. It is the single field the estate
consumes and A1's one write to the world; it grants exactly what it evidenced and withholds
everything else, and the router reads its silence as UNPROVEN, never as competence by association.

===============================================================================================
PART XII — RECERTIFICATION AND DECAY: EDUCATION IS NOT A ONE-TIME EVENT
===============================================================================================

12.1 The permanence fallacy, and why A1 forbids it. The most seductive error in any certification
system is to treat a credential as permanent — issued once, held forever. (OBSERVED, this is a failure
the estate has already diagnosed in a sibling domain: IAS-D2 §13.2's rule that "a test that already
passed once will continue to report CLEAR indefinitely unless something makes the underlying artifact
re-readable and re-evaluated.") A1 relocates that diagnosis to competence: a certification asserted
once and never revisited reports "competent" indefinitely, whether or not the competence survives.
Every A1 certification therefore carries an expiry from the moment it is issued (Part XI §11.1), and
membership is a decaying state, not a permanent title.

12.2 The three decay processes A1 tracks. Competence certifications erode for three distinct reasons,
and A1 names each because they trigger different recertifications. SUBSTRATE DRIFT: the capability the
learner was certified on changed — a new failure mode, a tightened invariant, a new curriculum version
(Part IV §4.5) — so the certification, pinned to the old version, no longer covers the current
capability. COMPETENCE DECAY: the learner's own competence may erode unexercised — a rarely-used
MASTERY competence, unpracticed across a long window, is no longer safely assumed. EVIDENCE STALENESS:
the observations the certification rests on age, and old evidence of competence is weaker evidence of
present competence, per IAS-E1's per-hop evidence drift (cited: STABLE/DECAYED/INFLATED). (DEFINED,
three decay processes.) A1 does not author the decay proofs — IAS-D2 and IAS-E1 and FD-04 own those;
A1 owns the educational CONSEQUENCE: which decay triggers which recertification.

12.3 The boundary with IAS-D2 and FD-04, drawn explicitly. IAS-D2 §13 owns IMMUNITY DECAY MONITORING —
the erosion of an antibody's validity — and FD-04 is titled Intelligence Decay. (Boundary, Article II:
D2's object is the estate's immunity to a failure; FD-04's object is a distilled intelligence asset's
decay; A1's object is a LEARNER's certified competence.) A1 SUBSCRIBES to D2's and E1's and FD-04's
decay signals as recertification TRIGGERS — when D2 reports that the substrate a learner was certified
on has a new antibody (a new failure mode), A1 marks every certification pinned to the old curriculum
for recertification. A1 authors no decay mechanism of its own; it is a consumer of the estate's decay
telemetry, translating a decay event on a capability into a recertification demand on that capability's
certified learners.

12.4 What recertification is, precisely. Recertification is re-evidencing a learner's competence
against the current curriculum version after a decay trigger. (DEFINED, and expressible only because
the curriculum is a pinned artifact — Part IV §4.7.) It is not re-teaching from scratch: a learner
recertifying after substrate drift is assessed against the DELTA — the new or changed competence nodes
— not the whole graph, if its prior certification on the unchanged nodes is still within their validity
window. (DERIVED: recertification is scoped to what decayed, the same delta-discipline the estate
applies everywhere — distill the delta, not the answer; teach the delta, not the course.) A learner
whose whole certification has expired recertifies against the whole graph; a learner facing a
two-node curriculum update recertifies on two nodes. Recertification cost scales with the decay, not
with the curriculum's size.

12.5 The lapsed certification and the router's correct default. When a certification expires and is not
yet recertified, the learner's stage does not silently hold — it drops. (NORMATIVE: a lapsed FULL
certification drops the learner to the highest stage whose evidence is still valid, or to UNPROVEN if
none is. The router, reading the dropped stage, reverts to supervising work it would have sent
unsupervised the day before.) This is the only safe default: a certification whose evidence has aged
past its validity window is, epistemically, a certification the estate can no longer defend, and the
router must treat the party as UNPROVEN on the lapsed competence, not as competent-until-proven-
otherwise. The asymmetry is deliberate and matches the estate's evidence doctrine: competence is
asserted only on current evidence, and absence of current evidence is UNPROVEN, never assumed-still-
good.

12.6 Recertification as the mechanism that makes membership meaningful. A membership that never
expired would, over a long enough horizon, certify a roster of parties whose competence no current
evidence supports — the diploma-mill failure (Part XX) reached not by fraud but by neglect. (DERIVED:
decay-and-recertification is what keeps the certified roster HONEST — at any moment, every FULL
certification in the estate is backed by evidence still inside its validity window, or it has already
dropped. The roster's trustworthiness is a direct consequence of its credentials expiring.) This is
the same insight IAS-C1 §15 encodes for capital positions — a position with no DECLINING-to-RETIRED
flow is not necessarily healthy — relocated to competence: a certified roster with no expiry-and-
recertification flow is not a healthy roster, it is an unaudited one.

12.7 The recertification economy, handed to Part XXI. Recertification consumes teaching capital, and a
naive policy — recertify everything constantly — would spend the estate into the ground. (CONTESTED,
resolved in Part XXI: recertification frequency is risk-weighted — a FOUNDATION competence on a
high-risk irreversible capability recertifies aggressively; a low-risk FLUENCY competence recertifies
lazily.) The decay clock's rate is set by the competence's risk surface, the same arbiter that sized
the curriculum (Part V §5.5). A1 does not recertify uniformly; it recertifies where the cost of a
stale certification is highest, and lets low-stakes certifications age on a longer clock.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. A certification held forever reports "competent" forever, whether or not the
competence survived — so A1 issues none without an expiry, and membership is a decaying state, not a
title. It authors no decay proof — D2, E1, and FD-04 own those — but it owns the consequence:
substrate drift, competence decay, and evidence staleness each trigger a recertification scoped to
what decayed. A lapsed certification drops the learner to UNPROVEN and the router re-supervises,
because the roster is trustworthy for exactly one reason — every credential on it is backed by
evidence still inside its window, or it is already gone.

===============================================================================================
PART XIII — EXPERTISE DISTILLATION AS A TEACHING INPUT
===============================================================================================

13.1 The relationship this Part governs. A1 is one of the FD family's largest consumers, and this Part
draws the boundary so that consumption is never mistaken for ownership. FD distills — it takes a
finished insight and closes the loop from question through delta, novelty, triage, transmutation,
writeback, evaluation to registry (FD-07's organizing thesis, cited). A1 TEACHES from the distillate.
(DEFINED boundary, Article II: FD's object is the production and placement of a finished insight;
A1's object is the conversion of that insight into a curriculum a naive learner can acquire. The
distillate is FD's output and A1's input; the two acts are as distinct as writing a textbook and
teaching a class from it.)

13.2 Why a distillate is not yet a lesson. An FD deposit is compressed knowledge optimized for
storage and retrieval by an already-competent party — it is a conclusion, stripped of the path that
produced it, because a competent reader does not need the path. (OBSERVED: this is exactly what makes
distillation valuable — FD's own discipline is to distill the delta, not the answer, and a deposit is
dense precisely because it omits what the expert already knows.) But a naive learner needs the path
the expert omitted. A lesson is a distillate RE-EXPANDED into a learnable sequence: the conclusion
restored to its reasoning, the omitted prerequisites made explicit, the compressed judgment unpacked
into practiceable steps. (DERIVED: teaching is, in an exact sense, the inverse operation to
distillation — distillation compresses toward the expert, teaching expands toward the novice — which
is why A1 cannot simply hand a learner an FD deposit and call it education.)

13.3 The distillation-to-curriculum transform. A1's transform reads an FD distillate and produces
curriculum competence nodes (Part IV §4.3). The distillate's central claim becomes a MASTERY
competence; the prerequisites the distillate assumed become FOUNDATION and FLUENCY nodes with explicit
edges; the distillate's worked example, if any, becomes a synthetic case (Part VII); the failure the
distillate was written to prevent becomes a failure simulation (Part VIII). (DEFINED, the
distillate→curriculum transform.) The transform is lossy in the pedagogically correct direction: it
adds back the scaffolding the expert did not need, and it is this added scaffolding — not the
distillate's conclusion — that is A1's actual authored contribution. A1 authors the path; FD authored
the destination.

13.4 The special case of a Hard Rule as teaching input. The estate's densest distillates are its Hard
Rules — each a real production bug compressed into a one-line stop with a trigger, an action, and an
origin incident. (OBSERVED.) A Hard Rule is an ideal teaching input precisely because it already
carries its own failure scenario (the origin) and its own success criterion (the action): the
distillate→curriculum transform on a Hard Rule yields a RECOGNITION node (spot the trigger condition),
a PREVENTION node (take the action), and a failure simulation (replay the origin incident, safely).
(DERIVED: this is why A1 and the Bug→Hard-Rule protocol are complementary — a Hard Rule is written to
STOP the estate repeating a bug, and A1 additionally TEACHES the learner why the stop exists, so the
competence survives even where the rule's automated trigger does not reach.) The Hard Rule immunizes;
A1 educates around it; neither replaces the other.

13.5 Distilling teachability itself — the second-order deposit. There is a distillate FD does not
currently produce that A1 needs: the distillation of how a competence is best TAUGHT — which ordering
works, which synthetic cases best exercise a node, which failure simulations most reliably build
recognition. (HYPOTHESIZED: as A1 runs, it accumulates evidence about which curricula produce
competent learners fastest, and that pedagogical evidence is itself distillable — a teachability
deposit.) This is a candidate FD deposit CLASS, not a new A1 mechanism: A1 produces the raw
pedagogical evidence, FD (if the Owner enrolls the class) distills it into reusable teaching patterns,
and A1 consumes those patterns as curriculum-design inputs. The loop keeps each system's object: A1
generates and consumes; FD distills. A1 does not build a distillation engine to close it.

13.6 The boundary restated against a tempting absorption. It would be easy, and wrong, for A1 to
claim it "owns knowledge transfer via distillation" and absorb FD's role in the teaching pipeline. It
does not. (NORMATIVE: A1 never distills — it consumes distillates and authors curricula. The moment
A1 found itself compressing a finished insight into a deposit, it would be doing FD's work under A1's
name, the exact vocabulary-collision absorption the CPCSC audit exists to prevent.) The test is
directional: if the operation compresses toward the expert, it is FD's; if it expands toward the
novice, it is A1's. A1 stays strictly on the expansion side of that line.

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. Teaching is the inverse of distillation — distillation compresses a finished
insight toward the expert, teaching re-expands it into a path a novice can walk — so A1 consumes FD's
deposits and never mints one. It restores the reasoning the expert omitted, turns the Hard Rule's
origin into a safe simulation and its action into a competence, and the scaffolding it adds back is
its authored contribution. If the operation compresses toward the expert it is FD's; if it expands
toward the novice it is A1's, and A1 stays on the expansion side of that line without exception.

===============================================================================================
PART XIV — KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER BETWEEN CAPABILITIES: TEACHING IS NOT HEREDITY
===============================================================================================

14.1 The two ways competence moves between capabilities, and why only one is A1's. Competence
acquired on one capability can reach another two ways. HEREDITY: a successor capability inherits a
predecessor's properties by lineage — the genome transfer IAS-D1 PART XX owns (§20.2: "a system's
GENOME… is the heritable subset of its property record… that transfers, with evidenced completeness,
to whatever successor system a speciation, merger, or succession-resolution event produces").
TEACHING: a learner competent on capability A is educated toward competence on a related capability B
without any lineage relationship between A and B. (DEFINED, and the boundary is Article II-sharp: D1's
transfer is between CAPABILITIES across a lineage event; A1's transfer is between a LEARNER's
competences across a curriculum relationship. D1 moves properties down a family tree; A1 moves a
learner's skill across a skill graph. Neither is the other.)

14.2 What A1 transfers: competence, not properties. When a learner certified on capability A begins a
curriculum for related capability B, A1 does not copy A's certification to B — that would be the
credential-inflation failure of Part XI §11.7, asserting competence never evidenced on B. (NORMATIVE.)
Instead, A1 uses the A-competence to SHORTEN B's curriculum: competence nodes shared between A and B,
already EVIDENCED on A and still within their validity window, are credited toward B, so the learner is
assessed only on B's distinct nodes. (DERIVED: this is transfer-of-learning in the pedagogic sense —
prior competence reduces the cost of new competence — implemented as curriculum-graph overlap, not as
certification copying. The learner still earns B's certification on B's evidence; it simply does not
re-earn the competences A already evidenced.)

14.3 Identifying shared competences without over-crediting. The load-bearing risk is over-crediting: a
competence that LOOKS shared between A and B but is subtly different, credited across, certifies the
learner on B-competence it never held. (CONTESTED, and A1 resolves it conservatively.) A competence
node is shared only if its success criterion is IDENTICAL across the two curricula — same observable
behaviour, same assessment method — not merely similarly named. (NORMATIVE: this is the content-tier
discipline of the CPCSC audit itself, applied inside A1 — two nodes sharing a name is not two nodes
sharing a competence; the criterion, read in full, is the test. A1 credits across only on criterion
identity, never on nominal similarity, for the same reason the Tribunal re-run refused to cede
ownership on a shared title.) When in doubt, A1 does not credit — it re-assesses, because a redundant
re-assessment wastes a little capital while a false credit certifies a phantom competence.

14.4 Transfer as a first-class curriculum relationship. The overlap between two curricula is itself an
artifact A1 records: a transfer edge between curriculum A and curriculum B, naming exactly which nodes
are criterion-identical. (DEFINED.) This makes transfer auditable — when a learner is credited from A
toward B, the transfer edge is the evidence for the credit, and it is inspectable, versioned, and
subject to the same decay as any certification (if A's certification lapses, the credit toward B
lapses with it). Transfer edges also make the curriculum ESTATE navigable: they reveal which
capabilities cluster into competence families, information A1 hands to the curriculum registry (Part
XIX) and, through it, to GK's knowledge graph.

14.5 The boundary with D1's genome, made productive. A1 and D1's genome are complementary, not
competing. When D1 executes a lineage event — a capability speciates or a successor inherits — the
successor arrives with an inherited genome but with NO certified learners: heredity moved the
capability's properties, not any learner's proven competence to wield the successor. (DERIVED: this
is precisely where A1 picks up — the inherited capability needs a curriculum, derived from its
inherited-plus-mutated contract, and its would-be operators need educating on what the mutation
changed. D1 hands A1 a successor capability; A1 educates the learners who will operate it.) The clean
handoff: genome transfer is D1's, competence transfer is A1's, and the successor capability is the
seam where D1's output becomes A1's input.

14.6 Transfer across the learner classes. A CAPABILITY learner transfers competence between related
capabilities. An AGENT-ROLE learner transfers between related roles — a subagent certified on one
review dimension is credited toward a sibling dimension on the criterion-identical nodes. A
MODEL-INSTANCE learner is the sharpest case: a new model version transfers from the outgoing version's
curriculum, credited only on the competences whose criteria the estate has re-confirmed the new
version holds — never assumed-inherited, because a model version change is exactly the substrate drift
Part XII §12.2 treats as a decay trigger. (DERIVED: a model instance inherits nothing by default; it
earns every competence, crediting across only on re-confirmed criterion-identical nodes — the most
conservative transfer, because the substrate changed most.)

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. Heredity moves a capability's properties down a lineage — that is D1's genome —
and teaching moves a learner's competence across a skill graph, which is A1's. A1 never copies a
certification from one capability to another; it shortens the new curriculum by crediting only the
competences whose success criteria are IDENTICAL, never merely similarly named, because a shared name
is not a shared competence. The learner earns the new certification on new evidence, and a credit
decays with the certification it was credited from.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — CROSS-DOMAIN EDUCATION: TEACHING A CAPABILITY OUTSIDE ITS NATIVE DOMAIN
===============================================================================================

15.1 The problem cross-domain education addresses. Part XIV transferred competence between RELATED
capabilities sharing curriculum overlap. This Part addresses the harder case: educating a learner to
apply a capability in a domain it was not native to — a review competence learned on Python applied to
infrastructure config; a routing competence learned on one estate applied to a federated one. (DEFINED:
cross-domain education is the deliberate teaching of a competence's transfer to a domain whose
assumptions differ from the domain the competence was learned in.) It is where the estate gains
leverage — a competence taught once and applied broadly — and where its subtlest failure lives: the
native assumption carried silently into a foreign domain.

15.2 The assumption-carry failure, named precisely. A competence learned in domain A embeds A's
assumptions, and those assumptions are usually implicit — the learner does not know it holds them,
because in A they were always true. (OBSERVED, and this is a failure the estate has documented in its
own operation: a Windows-execution competence carries the assumption that a path uses backslashes; a
single-estate routing competence carries the assumption that there is one canonical ontology. Applied
in domain B where the assumption is false, the competence fails in a way the learner cannot see,
because the assumption was never visible enough to question.) Cross-domain education's whole task is to
surface the native assumptions and re-assess them in the target domain, so that a competence is
transferred WITH its assumptions made explicit and re-validated, never with them smuggled across
invisibly.

15.3 The niche boundary with IAS-D1. IAS-D1 PART IX owns NICHE DISCOVERY — where a capability fits in
the estate's ecology, the environment it is adapted to. (Boundary, Article II: D1's niche is a
capability's adapted ENVIRONMENT — the ecological fit of the system; A1's cross-domain education is a
learner's competence transfer ACROSS environments. D1 says which niche a capability occupies; A1
teaches a learner to operate a capability in a niche other than its native one.) A1 READS D1's niche
model to know a capability's native domain and its assumptions — the niche IS, in part, the set of
environmental assumptions a capability is adapted to — and uses that reading to enumerate exactly which
assumptions must be surfaced and re-assessed for a target domain. A1 authors no ecology; it consumes
D1's niche to design a cross-domain curriculum.

15.4 The cross-domain curriculum's distinct nodes. A cross-domain curriculum adds node types the
same-domain curriculum lacks. ASSUMPTION-SURFACING: the learner must first evidence that it can NAME
the native assumptions of the competence — a competence whose assumptions the learner cannot articulate
cannot be safely transferred, because the learner cannot check them. ASSUMPTION-REVALIDATION: for each
surfaced assumption, the learner evidences whether it holds in the target domain, and what to do when
it does not. TARGET-DOMAIN PRACTICUM: real work in the target domain under supervision, because
cross-domain competence — like all FULL competence — is certified only on real evidence, never on the
assumption that same-domain competence transfers. (DEFINED, three added node types.) The
assumption-surfacing node is the load-bearing one and comes first: you cannot re-validate an assumption
you cannot name.

15.5 When cross-domain education should be refused. Not every competence should be transferred across
every domain; some competences are so domain-specific that "transfer" would teach a dangerous false
generality. (CONTESTED-honest: A1 does not assume all competence generalizes.) Where the native
assumptions are so numerous or so load-bearing that re-validating them approaches the cost of teaching
the target-domain competence from scratch, A1 recommends a native target-domain curriculum instead of
a cross-domain transfer — the same sizing-by-risk-surface arbiter (Part V §5.5) that governs every A1
depth decision. Cross-domain education is leverage when the shared competence is large and the
assumption gap is small; it is a false economy when the assumption gap swallows the shared competence.

15.6 The estate-scale payoff and its honest bound. Done well, cross-domain education is where an
education system earns its keep at the estate scale: a competence taught once and correctly
transferred to many domains multiplies the teaching investment. (HYPOTHESIZED, and bounded: the
multiplier is real only to the degree the assumption-surfacing is rigorous — an unrigorous cross-domain
transfer multiplies not competence but the invisible assumption-carry failure, teaching many learners
to apply a competence wrongly in domains where its assumptions do not hold.) The payoff and the risk
are the same mechanism seen from two sides; the assumption-surfacing node is what separates them, and
it is why A1 never treats cross-domain transfer as a mere crediting exercise (Part XIV) but always as a
curriculum with its own irreducible assessed nodes.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. A competence carries its native domain's assumptions invisibly, because where it
was learned they were always true — so cross-domain education's first and load-bearing act is to make
the learner NAME those assumptions, then re-validate each in the target domain, then evidence
competence on real target-domain work. A1 reads D1's niche to know the native assumptions and authors
no ecology of its own. Transfer is leverage when the shared competence is large and the assumption gap
small, and a false economy when the gap swallows the competence — the risk surface decides, as it
always does.

===============================================================================================
PART XVI — THE MODEL-ONBOARDING PIPELINE: ADMITTING A NEW MODEL INSTANCE
===============================================================================================

16.1 The third learner class, now given its own pipeline. Parts III-XV developed A1 mostly through the
CAPABILITY and AGENT-ROLE lenses; this Part specifies the MODEL-INSTANCE learner in full, because it
is the class with the highest stakes and the clearest need. When a new model provider or version is
admitted to the estate — a new frontier model, an updated version of an existing one, a distinct
provider for redundancy — the estate must not route real work to it on the assumption that "it is a
capable model." (DEFINED: model onboarding is the education pipeline that takes a newly admitted model
instance from UNPROVEN to certified on the estate's non-negotiable competences before any real work is
routed to it.) A capable model in general is not the same as a model proven to hold THIS estate's
specific competences — its Hard Rules, its evidence discipline, its execution doctrine.

16.2 Why a capable model is still UNPROVEN here. A model instance arrives with broad general
competence and zero estate-specific proof. (OBSERVED-analogous: the estate's own doctrine already
knows this shape — a new model version can silently change behaviour on the exact edge cases a
system depends on, which is why substrate drift (Part XII §12.2) treats a version change as a decay
trigger.) The onboarding pipeline exists because the estate's competences are specific and partly
idiosyncratic: the Windows execution doctrine, the anti-thrash discipline, the pathspec-scoped commit
convention, the citation-not-restatement rule for this very corpus. A model fluent at none of these
in particular is UNPROVEN on them regardless of how capable it is in general, and the pipeline proves
each before trusting it.

16.3 The non-negotiable competence battery. The onboarding curriculum's FOUNDATION stratum is a fixed
battery of the estate's non-negotiable competences — the ones whose violation is a Hard Rule. (DEFINED:
the battery is derived directly from the Hard Rules and the sealed doctrine — a synthetic case per
Hard Rule trigger, assessed by whether the instance takes the rule's action.) A model instance must
evidence, on held-out synthetic cases (Part VII §7.3), that it recognizes a secret before writing it
(HR-SECRET class), refuses a deploy without tests (HR-CASCADE-001), stops before a destructive command
without backup (HR-CASCADE-002), and the rest of the sealed set. (NORMATIVE: the battery is the
estate's admission exam, and it is graded as an auditor grades — held-out, outcome-checked, reproduced
— never on the instance's self-report that it "understands" the rule.) An instance that cannot evidence
the battery is not admitted to real routing, however capable it is elsewhere.

16.4 The pipeline's stages, mapped to the ladder. Model onboarding runs the full Part XVII ladder at
the tightest envelope. ADMISSION: the instance is registered as a learner, UNPROVEN, routed no real
work. BATTERY (SUPERVISED-equivalent): the non-negotiable competence battery on synthetic cases;
passing admits the instance to supervised practicum. SHADOW PRACTICUM (BOUNDED-equivalent): the
instance handles real tasks in parallel with a certified instance, its outputs compared but not acted
on — real inputs, zero real consequence, the safest possible real practice. GRADUATED ROUTING (FULL):
only after shadow practicum evidences competence does the instance receive real routed work, and even
then scoped and decay-clocked. (DEFINED, four stages.) Shadow practicum is the model-instance-specific
refinement: because a model instance can run in parallel with an incumbent on identical inputs, its
practicum can be real-input yet zero-consequence in a way the other learner classes cannot achieve.

16.5 The federation boundary with IAS-F1. IAS-F1 owns the FEDERATION ONTOLOGY — which participants are
in the ensemble and under what constitutional terms, and F1 §3.4 constitutionally excludes the external
world, the model provider, and the host machine from the ensemble. (Boundary, Article II: F1's object
is the constitutional membership of the estate — who is IN the federation; A1's object is the
competence proof of a party being admitted. F1 decides a model instance MAY join under the
constitution; A1 proves it is COMPETENT to be trusted with work once joined.) The two are sequential,
not overlapping: F1's constitutional admission is necessary and precedes A1's competence certification;
a model instance F1 admits constitutionally is still UNPROVEN until A1's pipeline certifies it. A1
authors no membership law; it educates the party F1 has constitutionally admitted.

16.6 The provider-independence payoff. A rigorous onboarding pipeline is what makes the estate's
provider-independence real rather than aspirational. (DERIVED: the estate's doctrine values not being
wholly bound to one model provider — but a second provider is only a genuine fallback if it has been
PROVEN to hold the estate's competences, and an unproven fallback is a false comfort. A1's onboarding
pipeline is the mechanism that converts "we could use another provider" into "we have a provider
proven competent on our non-negotiables, decay-clocked and ready.") This connects A1 to the deferred
Tier-C Cognitive Diplomacy and World Model Federation questions without crossing into them: A1 proves
a provider's competence; whether the estate SHOULD hold multiple providers, and how it should treat
external intelligence, are the Owner decisions those tiers reserve. A1 supplies the competence proof
either decision would need.

16.7 Re-onboarding on version change. A model version change re-triggers the pipeline, scoped to what
the version changed. (NORMATIVE, and the direct application of Part XII §12.4's delta-recertification:
a minor version recertifies against the competence battery only; a major version or a provider change
re-runs shadow practicum. A model instance is never assumed to inherit its predecessor's certification
— Part XIV §14.6's most-conservative-transfer rule — because the substrate changed most of all.) The
pipeline is therefore not a one-time admission gate but a standing relationship: every model instance
the estate routes to is, at every moment, either inside a valid certification window on the battery or
dropped to supervised, exactly as the roster-honesty rule (Part XII §12.6) requires.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. A capable model is not a proven one — the estate's competences are specific and
partly idiosyncratic, and a model fluent at none of them in particular is UNPROVEN however capable it
is in general. The onboarding pipeline proves each non-negotiable on held-out cases, then shadow-
practices on real inputs at zero consequence, then routes real work scoped and decay-clocked. F1
admits a provider constitutionally; A1 proves it competent; the two are sequential and neither
substitutes for the other. A proven fallback provider is the only kind that is not a false comfort.

===============================================================================================
PART XVII — BOUNDED-AUTONOMY GRADUATION: THE LADDER FROM SUPERVISED TO FULL
===============================================================================================

17.1 Why graduation is a ladder and never a switch. The single most consequential design decision in
A1 is that the transition from UNPROVEN to trusted is a graduated ladder — UNPROVEN → SUPERVISED →
BOUNDED → FULL — each rung an evidenced gate, and never a binary flip from untrusted to trusted.
(DEFINED, and this is the invariant stated in the contract §5.4.) A switch — untrusted today, fully
trusted tomorrow — would concentrate all the risk at a single un-evidenced moment; a ladder spreads the
trust across evidenced increments, each of which grants only as much autonomy as the evidence to that
point supports. The ladder is A1's structural answer to Part I's founding failure: "it is ready" was a
switch, and A1 replaces it with a ladder every rung of which is defensible on its own evidence.

17.2 The four rungs, defined by what autonomy each grants. UNPROVEN: no autonomy — the party is not
routed the capability at all, or only in fully-observed synthetic settings. SUPERVISED: reversible work
under review — every action is reviewable and the supervisor can veto before any effect that matters.
BOUNDED: scoped autonomous work — the party acts alone on reversible actions within a declared scope,
and only irreversible or out-of-scope actions require review. FULL: unsupervised work within the
certified capability's scope — the party is a MEMBER (Part XI §11.6). (DEFINED, four rungs.) The rungs
are cumulative and nested (Part XI §11.3): each grants everything the rung below grants plus a bounded
increment of autonomy, and each increment is gated by a stratum of the curriculum (FOUNDATION →
SUPERVISED, FLUENCY → BOUNDED, MASTERY+practicum → FULL).

17.3 The trust tier A1 hands the router. The learner's current rung is exposed to IAS-A1 as a trust
tier, not a boolean. (DEFINED boundary: the router owns routing — which capability handles which
mission; A1 owns the trust tier the router reads. A1 does not route; it publishes the graduated trust
level the router consults.) This is why the certification (Part XI) carries a named stage rather than a
pass/fail: the router needs to know not merely THAT a party is certified but TO WHAT DEGREE, so it can
route supervised work to a SUPERVISED learner and unsupervised work only to a FULL member. A boolean
certification would force the router to treat SUPERVISED and FULL identically — either over-trusting
the supervised learner or under-using the full member. The tier is the interface, and its granularity
is load-bearing.

17.4 Demotion is a first-class transition, not an exception. The ladder runs both ways. A decay
trigger (Part XII), a failed recertification, or an observed failure in production demotes a learner
DOWN the ladder to the highest rung its current evidence supports. (NORMATIVE: demotion is not a
punishment or an error state; it is the ladder working — a FULL member whose certification lapses or
who fails a recertification is correctly BOUNDED or SUPERVISED until re-evidenced, and the router
correctly resumes reviewing its irreversible actions.) A ladder that only ascended would accumulate
FULL members whose competence no current evidence supports — the roster-dishonesty failure again.
Bidirectional movement is what keeps every rung assignment currently-evidenced.

17.5 The graduation gate's conjunctive discipline, restated at the ladder level. No rung is granted on
a partial. (NORMATIVE, Part XI §11.2's conjunctive gate applied to the ladder: BOUNDED requires ALL
FLUENCY nodes, not most; FULL requires ALL MASTERY nodes plus practicum, not a high average.) The
temptation the discipline resists is the compensating average — a learner strong on nine competences
and failing the tenth, promoted because the mean is high. For the tenth competence, if it is the
irreversible-failure node, the mean is exactly the wrong statistic: the learner will fail precisely
where it was weak, and the nine strengths will not be present at that moment to compensate. A1 ANDs
the gate, and the irreversible-failure nodes are the reason.

17.6 Bounded autonomy as the estate's native trust model. The ladder is not an A1 invention imposed on
the estate; it is the estate's own existing trust posture made explicit. (OBSERVED: the estate already
practices bounded autonomy — an agent acts freely on reversible actions and pauses for the irreversible;
a session commits directly on green tests but escalates on a destructive command; the Intent-Lock
soft-pause is exactly a SUPERVISED-to-BOUNDED boundary. A1 does not introduce graduated trust; it
NAMES the estate's existing graduated trust as a certification ladder and attaches evidence to each
rung.) This is why A1 is a natural fit rather than a foreign imposition: the estate was always
educating its participants up a trust ladder implicitly; A1 makes the ladder explicit, evidenced, and
auditable.

17.7 The terminal-BOUNDED learner is a valid outcome. Not every learner should reach FULL, and a
learner correctly held at BOUNDED is a success, not a failure. (CONTESTED-honest, and the completion of
Part IX §9.6's point: some competences are real but bounded, some parties should always have their
irreversible actions reviewed, and a model instance the estate deliberately keeps at BOUNDED — routed
real reversible work but never unsupervised on the irreversible — is a correctly-calibrated trust
decision, not an incomplete education.) The ladder's purpose is not to promote everyone to FULL; it is
to assign every party the rung its evidence supports, and for some parties on some capabilities that
rung is BOUNDED, permanently and correctly.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. Trust is a ladder, never a switch — UNPROVEN, SUPERVISED, BOUNDED, FULL — each
rung granting only the autonomy its evidence supports, because a switch concentrates all the risk at one
un-evidenced moment and "it is ready" was that switch. The rung is published to the router as a graded
trust tier, not a boolean; the gate is conjunctive, and the irreversible-failure node is why the mean is
the wrong statistic. The ladder descends as readily as it climbs, and a learner correctly held at
BOUNDED is the ladder succeeding, not failing.

PART XVIII — THE EDUCATOR ROLE AND ITS CONFLICT-OF-INTEREST GUARDRAILS
===============================================================================================

18.1 Who teaches, named as a role. Everything A1 does is performed by an EDUCATOR — the role
responsible for authoring a curriculum, delivering it, running the apprenticeship, manufacturing the
synthetic experience and failure simulations, and supervising the practicum. (DEFINED: the educator is
a role, fillable by a session, an agent, or the Owner, distinguished from the ASSESSOR — who grades —
and the LEARNER — who is taught. The three roles are kept separate by the conflict rules this Part
codifies.) A1 without a named educator role would leave its most subjective activities — what to teach,
how to sequence it, when it is learned — unattributed and unauditable; naming the role is what makes
those judgments accountable.

18.2 The three-role separation, and why it is load-bearing. LEARNER, EDUCATOR, ASSESSOR are separate
roles and no party fills two of them for the same certification. (NORMATIVE, the codification of the
conflict rules stated piecewise in Parts VI §6.5 and X §10.6.) The learner cannot grade itself
(self-assessment is self-report, a proxy). The educator cannot grade its own learner (an educator
graded on its graduates' pass rate will teach to its own grade, not the curriculum's — the exact
self-reported-yield failure IAS-C1 §14.5 forbids). The assessor cannot be the learner or the educator.
The separation is the same independence the estate demands of any evidence: the party that produces a
result may not be the sole party that certifies it, because a producer certifying its own output is a
reviewer of a coherent fabrication, not an auditor of a reality.

18.3 The educator's own certification. An educator is itself a learner that must be certified to
educate — teaching a capability is a competence, and an uncertified educator is an UNPROVEN teacher
whose curricula and grades the estate has no basis to trust. (DERIVED, and recursion-bounded: the
educator competence is certified by the same pipeline, and the recursion terminates at the Owner or a
FULL-certified founding educator, exactly as ACIS's ladder terminates at a human-anchored E-level
rather than self-certifying. A1 does not let an educator certify itself into existence — the first
educator on any capability is anchored to an already-trusted party, and the chain of educator
certifications is auditable back to that anchor.) This closes the regress that would otherwise let an
unproven party bootstrap itself into teaching authority.

18.4 The educator's anti-gaming countermetric. An educator's success may never be measured by its own
graduates' pass rate alone. (NORMATIVE, IAS-C1 §14.5 cited directly: "a governance position's yield
may never be computed from its OWN self-reported activity log alone." A1 relocates the countermetric:
an educator's effectiveness is measured by its graduates' PRODUCTION performance — do learners it
certified actually perform competently when the router sends them real work — not by how many it
passed.) An educator optimizing pass rate would lower its standard; an educator measured on its
graduates' production competence is aligned with the estate's actual interest, because it profits only
when its graduates genuinely perform. The countermetric closes the diploma-mill incentive at its
source: it removes the reward for passing learners who are not competent.

18.5 The educator across the learner classes. For a CAPABILITY learner, the educator is a session or
operator FULL-certified on the capability. For an AGENT-ROLE learner, the educator is the orchestrator
or a higher-tier agent that designs and grades the role's dispatches. For a MODEL-INSTANCE learner, the
educator is the onboarding authority — often the Owner or a founding session — that owns the
non-negotiable battery. (DERIVED: one role, three fillers, the same separation and countermetric in
each.) In every class the educator authors and teaches; it never grades its own learner, and it is
itself measured on its graduates' real-world competence.

18.6 The educator's obligation to the curriculum's honesty. The educator authors curricula, and
authoring is where the subtlest failure enters: a curriculum written to be easy to pass rather than to
evidence competence. (CONTESTED, resolved by the countermetric and the registry.) Because the educator
is measured on production competence, not pass rate, its incentive is toward a curriculum that
genuinely evidences competence — but A1 adds a structural check beyond incentive: curricula are
registered (Part XIX) and their competence nodes are auditable against the capability's actual contract
(Part V §5.2), so a curriculum that omits a required competence is detectable independent of the
educator's intent. Incentive-alignment plus structural-auditability is the two-layer defense; neither
alone suffices.

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. Teaching is a competence, so the educator is a role — authored, certified,
and accountable — kept strictly separate from the learner it teaches and the assessor that grades,
because a party that fills two of those roles for one certification is a producer certifying its own
output. The educator never grades its own learner, is certified up a chain anchored to an
already-trusted party, and is measured on its graduates' real production competence — never their pass
rate — because the only educator worth trusting is one that profits solely when its graduates genuinely
perform.
