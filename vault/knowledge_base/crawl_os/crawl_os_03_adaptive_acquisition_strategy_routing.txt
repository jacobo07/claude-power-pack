PART I — MISSION AND BOUNDARY: OPERATIONALIZING THE COST LADDER

1.1 Dataset #01's Part VI states a principle once, precisely, and calls it the single restatement underlying every operational rule in that Part: use the least costly strategy that can produce sufficient evidence, and escalate only upon an observed reason, never by default. A principle stated once at the constitutional level is not, by itself, a decision procedure. It tells an engine what good behavior looks like in the abstract; it does not tell that engine, standing in front of one specific frontier item at one specific moment, which of seven concrete rungs to actually select, what would count as sufficient grounds to reach for the next one, or how to notice when a rung reached for months ago has quietly become more expensive than the target now requires. This dataset exists to close that gap — to take Part VI's principle and Part V §5.4's charter and build, Part by Part, the actual mechanism a router runs to turn a frontier item into a rung selection, every time, without needing to re-derive the principle from first instinct on each individual acquisition.

1.2 The charter this dataset elaborates is stated in Dataset #01 Part V §5.4 in a single sentence: the Acquisition Strategy Router's charter is to select, for each item on the frontier, the least costly engine from the cost ladder that can plausibly satisfy the mission's evidence requirement for that item, to record the reason for its selection, and to escalate only upon an observed, stated failure at a cheaper rung, never by default and never by habit. Every Part in this dataset is, in one sense, an elaboration of some clause in that single sentence — what "least costly" actually means operationally, what "plausibly satisfy" requires as a decision criterion, what "record the reason" produces as an artifact, and what distinguishes an "observed, stated failure" from the untested prediction Part VI §6.4 already warned against at the constitutional level.

1.3 This dataset does not own the cost ladder itself. Dataset #01 Part VI already defined all seven rungs, in order, with the specific conditions under which each becomes appropriate — a valid cache hit, a basic HTTP fetch, a specialized HTTP transport, a lightweight render, a full browser, a governed browser agent, and authorized human intervention. Restating that definition here would be exactly the kind of verbatim restatement this family's own citation discipline forbids; every Part in this dataset that touches a specific rung's conditions cites Part VI's own language by section number rather than reproducing it, and where a Part's own worked example requires naming a rung's threshold condition, it does so as an application of Part VI's own definition, never as a competing one.

1.4 What this dataset does own is the decision procedure Part VI's principle implies but does not itself specify: given a frontier item, a mission's stated evidence requirement, an authorization verdict, and whatever site memory exists for the target, which specific rung does the router actually select this time, on what basis, and with what recorded justification. A constitution that says "use the least costly sufficient strategy" without a procedure for actually determining sufficiency and cost in a specific case is a principle waiting to be operationalized, and an engine without that operationalization will, in practice, fall back on whichever rung its last invocation happened to use, which is precisely the habitual-escalation failure Part VI §6.4's closing sentence names as the thing untested prediction eventually produces.

1.5 The founding resource document that names this family's nineteen engines lists the Acquisition Strategy Router in a single line, without further elaboration — the router's entire specification, prior to this dataset, lives in Dataset #01 Part V's charter sentence and Part VI's seven-rung doctrine, with no additional source material to draw from. This dataset is therefore synthesized primarily from those two already-sealed sources, extended into the operational depth those sources named but deliberately left for a dedicated dataset to elaborate, exactly as Dataset #16 was synthesized primarily from Dataset #01 Part V §5.13 and Part IX rather than from any independent source document.

1.6 This dataset's own bounding failure has three directions, mirrored from the same three-direction structure Dataset #16 Part I §1.6 already established for its own bounding failure, because the underlying shape of the risk is identical even though the domain differs. Excessive permissiveness: a router that escalates readily, on thin or predicted grounds, converging toward the most expensive rung as its default behavior because the most expensive rung is also the one least likely to produce a visible failure in the moment, exactly the drift Part VI §6.1 names as the ladder's entire reason for existing. Excessive restriction: a router that refuses to escalate even when a cheaper rung has genuinely, observably failed, starving a mission of evidence it is actually entitled to acquire because the router treats the cost ladder as a hard ceiling rather than a discipline about sequencing. Silence: a router that makes a rung selection without recording the reason, leaving no artifact by which a supervisor, an auditor, or a future session can distinguish a disciplined selection from an arbitrary one — the specific gap Part VI §6.2's own recording requirement exists to close, and a gap this dataset must never reopen by treating recording as optional overhead rather than as the mechanism that makes the entire discipline auditable.

1.7 A precise relationship to Dataset #16 must be stated at this dataset's very outset, because the two datasets sit adjacent in the family's dependency order for a reason and the boundary between them is load-bearing from the first Part rather than a detail deferred to a later composition Part. Dataset #16 decides whether an acquisition is authorized at all — whether a target may be visited, whether a credential may be used, whether the seventh rung may be invoked for a given mission. This dataset decides, for an acquisition Dataset #16 has already authorized, which of the seven rungs to actually use and how to pace it. Dataset #16's own Part XX §20.5 already states this exact boundary from its own side, in its own founding terms; this dataset restates it here because a router built without internalizing this boundary from its first Part risks the precise authorization-adjudication-inversion failure Dataset #16 Part XX §20.6 already names and forecloses.

1.8 A second precise relationship, equally load-bearing, concerns CO-01, the estate's own general operating-economics dataset. Dataset #01 Part XV §15.2 already draws this boundary at the family-founding level: CO-01 owns session-level token budgets, cross-project cost allocation, and model-class routing for the estate as a whole, territory that predates Crawl OS and would exist identically without it. This dataset's own cost ladder concerns a categorically different kind of cost — which acquisition method to use for a specific web target — and the word "cost" appearing in both datasets' own vocabulary is a coincidence of English, not a shared concern. A router that reasons about its own rung selection in terms of the mission's remaining token budget, rather than in terms of the target's own observed acquisition requirements, has crossed into CO-01's territory in a way this dataset's own Part XVI treats as a named failure in its own right.

1.9 Consistent with the honesty discipline every sealed Crawl OS dataset already applies to its own downstream state, this dataset states plainly at its outset that routing, as this dataset specifies it, is a decision made by a system that does not yet exist as running code — `modules/crawl_os/strategy_router.py` is PLANNED, per `CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md` row 3, not REACHABLE. Every procedure this dataset describes is a specification for that future module, verified today only by internal consistency and by composition with Dataset #01, Dataset #02, and Dataset #16's own already-sealed text, not by any runtime behavior this dataset's own prose can currently claim to exercise.

1.10 A routing decision, properly understood, is exactly that: a decision, made fresh for each frontier item against that item's own specific circumstances, never a default inherited automatically from the last item routed or from whichever rung happens to be most familiar to whoever configured the router. This framing echoes the precise sentence Dataset #16 Part I §1.9 uses for authorization — "a decision... never a background fact" — because the identical discipline applies here for the identical reason: a router that treats rung selection as background fact rather than as an active, justified, per-item decision has already surrendered the discipline Part VI exists to enforce, however sophisticated its underlying selection logic might otherwise be.

1.11 The remainder of this dataset proceeds in four blocks. Parts II through VI specify the routing pipeline itself — how a frontier item becomes a rung selection, the seven-rung decision procedure in full operational detail, the observed-reason discipline, site memory, and de-escalation. Parts VII through IX specify this dataset's own consumption interfaces — the fixed-constraint relationship with Dataset #16, the handoff relationship with Dataset #02, and the uniform adapter interface every rung selection actually dispatches through. Parts X through XV work through each rung's own selection mechanics in turn, plus frontier consumption and multi-mission scheduling. Parts XVI through XXII specify this dataset's boundaries with CO-01 and with the future acquisition engines, routing-table governance, supervision, and cross-mission consistency. Parts XXIII through XXV close with this dataset's own failure taxonomy, amendment discipline, and done criteria.

1.12 A worked example grounds this Part's own abstract framing before the dataset proceeds into operational detail. A mission's frontier includes a documentation page for an open-source library, previously unvisited, with no site memory on record. The router has no observed reason to reach for anything beyond the ladder's second rung; nothing about the target, its domain, or any prior interaction suggests a basic HTTP fetch would be insufficient. The router selects the second rung, records the reason — no prior observation exists warranting escalation, so the cheapest plausible rung is attempted first, precisely as Part VI §6.2 requires — and dispatches through the uniform adapter interface Part IX elaborates. Only if that dispatch returns an observed, falsifiable indication of insufficiency does Part IV's escalation discipline become relevant at all; absent that observation, the router's own job for this item is already complete the moment the second rung is selected and recorded.

PART I FINAL LAW

The cost ladder is a constitutional principle; this dataset is the decision procedure that principle requires to actually govern a rung selection. This dataset never restates Dataset #01 Part VI's own seven-rung definition, it operationalizes it. This dataset never adjudicates authorization, it consumes Dataset #16's verdict as a fixed constraint. This dataset never reasons about estate-wide token or session economics, it reasons about acquisition-method cost, a categorically different concern CO-01 alone owns. A rung selection is a decision, made fresh for each frontier item against that item's own specific circumstances, and a router that treats selection as inherited background fact rather than as an active, recorded, per-item judgment has already failed the discipline this entire dataset exists to build.


PART II — THE ROUTING PIPELINE: FROM HANDOFF TO RUNG SELECTION

2.1 This Part specifies the sequence a frontier item actually passes through, from the moment it arrives at this dataset's own routing logic to the moment a rung is selected and dispatched, in the same structural position Dataset #16 Part II gives its own four-stage adjudication pipeline — a shared vocabulary this dataset's every later Part assumes without redefining.

2.2 The pipeline has four stages, in fixed order, mirrored deliberately from Dataset #16's own pipeline shape because the underlying discipline — never skip a stage, never reorder them, never let a later stage substitute for an earlier one's own responsibility — applies here for the identical structural reason. Constraint-gathering is the first stage: before any rung is even considered, the router assembles the authorization verdict for this specific target from Dataset #16 (Part VII elaborates this consumption in full), the mission's own stated evidence requirement from the VALIDATED contract (Part VIII), and whatever site memory exists for the target or its site class (Part V). Sufficiency-assessment is the second stage: given those constraints, the router determines the cheapest rung that plausibly satisfies the evidence requirement, per Part III's own decision procedure. Selection-and-recording is the third stage: the router commits to that rung, records the selection with its basis, and dispatches through the uniform adapter interface. Outcome-integration is the fourth stage: the dispatch's own result — success, an observed insufficiency, or an observed failure — is integrated back into site memory, closing the loop for the next item that touches the same target or site class.

2.3 A precise ordering constraint binds these four stages, restated here because a router that reorders them silently produces the exact habitual-escalation or authorization-bypass failures this dataset exists to prevent: constraint-gathering must complete, specifically including confirmation of a granted authorization verdict, before sufficiency-assessment even begins. A router that begins reasoning about which rung would be cheapest before confirming the target is authorized at all has inverted the pipeline's own dependency structure, and any rung selection produced under that inversion is invalid regardless of how sound its cost reasoning might independently be — the selection was never entitled to occur in the first place.

2.4 Constraint-gathering's own sub-components deserve individual note because each failing silently produces a different downstream defect. A missing or stale authorization verdict halts the pipeline entirely for that item, per Part VII's own fixed-constraint discipline — the router does not proceed to sufficiency-assessment on the assumption that authorization will probably resolve favorably. A missing evidence requirement, which should not occur for a properly VALIDATED contract per Dataset #02's own Part XV state machine, is itself an anomaly this dataset's own Part XXIII names, since a router receiving an incomplete handoff has received something Dataset #02's own contract already promises never to produce. Absent site memory for a genuinely new target is not itself an anomaly — it is the ordinary case for a first-ever visit — and the pipeline proceeds to sufficiency-assessment with an explicitly empty site-memory input rather than treating absence as a reason to pause.

2.5 Sufficiency-assessment, the pipeline's second stage, is where Part III's own decision procedure actually executes: given the assembled constraints, walk the seven rungs from cheapest to most expensive, testing each in turn against the evidence requirement and against whatever site memory exists, until the first rung that plausibly satisfies the requirement is reached. This stage produces exactly one output — a single selected rung — never a ranked list of candidates the next stage must further narrow, because a routing decision, per Part I §1.10, is a single, committed judgment, not a shortlist deferred to a later arbiter.

2.6 Selection-and-recording, the pipeline's third stage, produces this dataset's own primary artifact: a recorded rung selection, timestamped, naming the target, the mission, the selected rung, and the specific basis for selecting it — either "no observed reason exists to escalate beyond the cheapest plausible rung" for a first attempt, or the specific observed failure that justified escalating past a cheaper rung, per Part IV's own discipline. This record is this dataset's own structural counterpart to Dataset #16 Part XV's authorization audit trail and Dataset #02 Part XXII's compilation audit trail — every sealed Crawl OS dataset that makes a consequential per-item decision maintains a comparable record, and this dataset's own version is no exception.

2.7 Outcome-integration, the pipeline's fourth and closing stage, is what distinguishes a router that merely selects rungs from one that actually learns from its own selections over time. The dispatched rung's own outcome — genuine success, an observed insufficiency triggering Part IV's escalation discipline for a subsequent attempt, or an observed failure requiring immediate escalation within the same routing pass — is written back into site memory per Part V's own population mechanics, so that the next frontier item touching the same target or site class benefits from what this item's own dispatch actually revealed, rather than the router repeating an identical cheapest-first attempt against a target site memory could already have told it would fail.

2.8 A precise distinction between an observed insufficiency requiring a fresh routing pass and an observed failure requiring immediate escalation within the same pass deserves stating here, though Part IV develops it fully: some failures are discovered only after a rung's own dispatch completes and its output is examined — a fetch that succeeds technically but returns visibly insufficient content, which the router only learns by inspecting the returned Evidence Object's own confidence signals per Dataset #01 Part VII. Other failures are discovered during the dispatch itself — an explicit HTTP error status, a rung outright refusing to execute. The pipeline treats both as triggers for the identical fourth-stage integration and, where the mission's own evidence requirement remains unmet, a return to the second stage for the same item with the newly observed failure now available as grounds for escalation, never as two structurally different pipelines requiring separate handling.

2.9 This Part's own relationship to Part I's three bounding failures deserves a direct connection before this Part closes: excessive permissiveness manifests in this pipeline as sufficiency-assessment routinely selecting an expensive rung on the first pass despite no site memory or observed reason justifying it; excessive restriction manifests as the pipeline refusing to advance past a cheaper rung even after outcome-integration has recorded a clear, observed failure at that rung; silence manifests as selection-and-recording producing a rung dispatch with no corresponding basis in the recorded artifact. A pipeline correctly implementing all four stages in fixed order, with every stage's own sub-discipline honored, is this dataset's own structural guarantee against all three failure directions simultaneously, in the same way Dataset #16 Part II's own four-stage pipeline guards against its own three bounding failures.

2.10 A worked trace closes this Part, following one frontier item through all four stages in sequence. A mission's frontier includes a government regulatory filing, hosted on a domain the mission's compiled contract lists under an explicit, request-level permitted-domains entry. Constraint-gathering confirms Dataset #16's own composite verdict for this target is granted, retrieves the mission's evidence requirement (moderate confidence, full document text), and finds no existing site memory for this specific filing though the site class — government regulatory portals — does carry a prior entry noting static HTML delivery with no client-side rendering requirement. Sufficiency-assessment, consulting that site-class memory per Part V's own site-class-fallback mechanics, determines the second rung, a basic HTTP fetch, plausibly satisfies the requirement without needing to test the first rung's cache (no prior acquisition of this specific filing exists) or reach further than the second rung (no observed reason to expect insufficiency). Selection-and-recording commits to the second rung, records "site-class memory indicates static delivery, no escalation warranted" as the basis, and dispatches. Outcome-integration, receiving a genuinely successful fetch with a high-confidence Evidence Object, updates the specific filing's own site memory to reflect a confirmed successful second-rung acquisition, available for any future mission that revisits the identical document.

PART II FINAL LAW

The routing pipeline runs constraint-gathering, sufficiency-assessment, selection-and-recording, and outcome-integration, in that fixed order, for every frontier item this dataset routes. Constraint-gathering, including confirmed authorization, always completes before sufficiency-assessment begins — a rung selected before authorization is confirmed is invalid regardless of its own cost reasoning. Selection-and-recording produces exactly one committed rung with a recorded basis, never a shortlist. Outcome-integration closes the loop into site memory, so that what one item's own dispatch reveals is available to the next item that touches the same target, and a pipeline honoring all four stages in order is this dataset's own structural guard against habitual escalation, unwarranted restriction, and unrecorded selection alike.


PART III — THE SEVEN-RUNG DECISION PROCEDURE: ATTEMPT-CHEAPEST-FIRST MECHANICS

3.1 This Part specifies the sufficiency-assessment stage Part II §2.5 names in overview, in the operational detail a router actually requires to walk the seven rungs Dataset #01 Part VI defines and arrive at a single selection, for a frontier item carrying no prior site memory at all — the genuinely first-visit case, which this Part treats as the baseline the remaining rung-specific Parts (X through XIII) then extend with each rung's own particular selection conditions.

3.2 The routing table this dataset owns, referenced by `CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md` row 3 as the artifact `modules/crawl_os/strategy_router.py` will implement verbatim, maps each of Dataset #01 Part VI's seven rungs to its own trigger condition in a fixed, ordered sequence: rung one (cache) triggers when a prior acquisition of the identical target exists within the mission's own stated freshness window; rung two (basic HTTP fetch) triggers by default for any target with no site memory indicating otherwise and no explicit contract-level signal requiring more; rung three (specialized HTTP transport) triggers only on an observed reason that a basic fetch received materially different content than a standard browser would; rung four (lightweight render) triggers only on an observed reason that meaningful content exists in the initial HTML but requires a small amount of client-side execution to surface; rung five (full browser) triggers only on an observed reason that the target is a genuine single-page application or otherwise requires substantial client-side execution; rung six (governed browser agent) triggers only on an observed reason that no simpler HTTP path reaches the content at all, typically because multi-step interaction is required; rung seven (authorized human intervention) triggers only when every automated rung has been exhausted or is inapplicable given Dataset #16's own authorization boundary, and only with Dataset #16's own operator-approval clearance already granted.

3.3 The procedure itself is a simple, disciplined walk: starting at rung one, test whether the current rung's own trigger condition is satisfied against the item's actual circumstances — the mission's freshness window, any existing site memory, any observed reason already on record from a prior attempt at this same item within the current routing pass. The first rung whose trigger condition is satisfied is the selected rung. This is a deliberately mechanical procedure, not a holistic judgment weighing all seven rungs against each other simultaneously, because a holistic judgment is exactly the kind of discretion under which habitual escalation quietly creeps in — a router permitted to weigh "this seems like it might need a browser" against "but a basic fetch might also work" on each item, rather than mechanically testing rung two's own narrow trigger condition first, will drift toward whichever rung feels safer under delivery pressure, precisely the drift Part VI §6.1 names as the ladder's own founding concern.

3.4 A precise clarification distinguishes this dataset's own procedure from a naive escalation ladder that merely retries every target at increasing cost until something works. This procedure does not attempt rung two, observe its result, then attempt rung three regardless of that result's own quality — it tests rung two's trigger condition before ever dispatching rung two, using site memory and any already-observed reason as the test's own input, and dispatches exactly one rung per routing pass unless that dispatch itself produces a fresh observed failure warranting Part IV's own within-pass escalation. A router that dispatches multiple rungs "just to compare" for a single frontier item has multiplied its own acquisition cost in the precise way the entire cost ladder exists to prevent, even if its final selection happens to land on the genuinely appropriate rung.

3.5 The trigger conditions for rungs three through six are each, by Dataset #01 Part VI's own design, conditioned on an observed reason rather than a default — meaning the procedure's own baseline, absent any such reason, always resolves to rung two for a target with no cache hit available. This is a deliberate asymmetry: the cheap rungs (one and two) trigger on affirmative, easily-checked conditions (a cache entry exists; nothing indicates otherwise), while the expensive rungs (three through seven) trigger only on the negative-then-positive pattern of first observing that a cheaper rung was actually insufficient. A procedure structured this way makes the ladder's own least-costly-first discipline the mechanical default rather than an outcome the router must actively strive toward on each item.

3.6 A precise interaction with Part V's own site memory deserves stating in this Part's own procedural terms, since site memory is what allows the procedure to skip directly to a higher rung on a subsequent visit without re-testing every cheaper rung's own condition from scratch each time. Where site memory for the specific target or its site class already records a confirmed insufficiency at a given rung — a prior visit observed that a basic fetch reliably returns bot-detection interstitial content for this specific site — the procedure's own walk treats that prior observation as satisfying the observed-reason requirement for skipping directly past the confirmed-insufficient rung, without needing to re-dispatch and re-observe the failure on every subsequent visit to the same site. This is precisely how the procedure avoids repeating an already-known-insufficient rung indefinitely, while still requiring that the underlying observation, however far in the past, was genuinely an observation and not an assumption applied preemptively.

3.7 A precise boundary applies to how far into the future a prior observation may be trusted before it requires re-verification, deferred in full to Part V's own staleness discipline but stated here in this Part's own procedural terms: the walk-the-ladder procedure consults site memory as currently valid input only when that memory has not been flagged stale by Part V's own periodic staleness check, and a stale memory entry is treated, for this Part's own procedural purposes, identically to absent memory — the procedure re-tests from rung two rather than trusting an observation old enough to no longer reliably describe the target's current posture.

3.8 The procedure's own relationship to a mission's stated evidence requirement, distinct from the target's own technical delivery characteristics, deserves a precise statement because the two considerations operate on different axes and a router that conflates them will select an inappropriately expensive rung for a mission that never needed the additional fidelity. A target's technical delivery characteristics determine which rung can technically retrieve usable content at all — a genuine single-page application simply does not return usable content through a basic fetch, regardless of what the mission needs. A mission's evidence requirement, separately, determines how much fidelity the retrieved content must carry — a mission needing only to confirm a page's existence and title, per Dataset #01 Part VI §6.3's own worked distinction, requires far less than one needing complete structural extraction. This Part's own procedure applies the technical-delivery test first, since a rung technically incapable of returning the target at all is disqualified regardless of the mission's own requirement, and then, among technically capable rungs, applies the evidence-requirement test to avoid selecting a technically-capable-but-unnecessarily-expensive rung for a mission whose own stated requirement a cheaper capable rung already satisfies.

3.9 A worked example distinguishes the two axes concretely. A mission requires only confirmation that a specific product-documentation page still exists and carries the same page title as a prior acquisition — Dataset #01 Part VI §6.3's own low-requirement case. The target happens to be hosted on a platform that, per site memory, requires a lightweight render (rung four) to fully populate its body content, because the platform loads its main content block via client-side execution. The procedure's own technical-delivery test correctly identifies rung four as the minimum rung technically capable of retrieving the page at all — a basic fetch would return an empty or near-empty shell, an observed insufficiency site memory already records. The evidence-requirement test does not further reduce this selection, because no rung cheaper than four is technically capable regardless of how low the mission's own requirement is; the procedure's own two-test structure correctly avoids escalating past rung four (the evidence requirement does not warrant a full browser, so the procedure does not reach for rung five) while still correctly refusing to select rung two or three, which site memory already confirms cannot technically deliver the content this mission needs at all.

3.10 A second worked example illustrates the reverse case, where the evidence-requirement test is the binding constraint rather than the technical-delivery test. A mission requires complete, structurally faithful extraction of every table in a lengthy technical specification document — a high evidence-requirement case per Dataset #01 Part VI §6.3. The target is a simple, static HTML page with no client-side rendering requirement at all; a basic fetch (rung two) is fully technically capable of retrieving the complete page. The procedure's own technical-delivery test identifies rung two as sufficient, and the evidence-requirement test, examining whether rung two's own delivery — complete, well-structured HTML — actually satisfies the mission's own high-fidelity requirement, confirms that it does, since the requirement concerns extraction completeness rather than rendering complexity, and a static page's own HTML already contains every table in full. The procedure correctly selects rung two despite the mission's own high evidence requirement, because a high requirement justifies escalation only when a cheaper rung's own actual delivery is observed or predicted-from-site-memory to fall short of that requirement, never merely because the requirement itself sounds demanding in the abstract.

3.11 This Part's own procedure closes with a summary restatement of its own governing discipline, because every later rung-specific Part (X through XIII) assumes this Part's own baseline procedure as settled rather than re-litigating it: walk the seven rungs from cheapest to most expensive; the technical-delivery test disqualifies any rung not actually capable of retrieving the target at all; among technically capable rungs, the evidence-requirement test selects the cheapest one whose actual delivery satisfies the mission's own stated requirement; site memory, where current and not stale, supplies the observed-reason input that allows the walk to skip directly past a confirmed-insufficient rung without re-testing it; and the entire procedure resolves to exactly one selected rung, recorded with its basis, per Part II §2.6's own selection-and-recording stage.

PART III FINAL LAW

The seven-rung procedure is a mechanical walk from cheapest to most expensive, never a holistic judgment weighing all rungs simultaneously — a procedure permitting simultaneous weighing invites exactly the discretionary drift Dataset #01 Part VI exists to forbid. A rung must first pass a technical-delivery test — can it retrieve the target at all — before an evidence-requirement test determines whether its actual delivery satisfies what the mission's own contract requires; a rung failing the first test is disqualified regardless of the second, and a rung passing both is selected regardless of how demanding the mission's requirement sounds in the abstract, because demand alone, absent an observed or memory-confirmed shortfall, never justifies escalation past a technically sufficient rung.


PART IV — OBSERVED-REASON DISCIPLINE: WHAT COUNTS AS A VALID ESCALATION

4.1 Dataset #01 Part VI §6.4 states the observed-reason requirement at the constitutional level and offers three illustrative examples — a 403 status, content far shorter than the site's typical length suggesting an interstitial, and a legitimate prior-observation-based prediction drawn from site memory rather than an untested assumption. This Part specifies the full discipline those three examples illustrate, the discipline every escalation this dataset's routing pipeline ever performs must satisfy before Part III's procedure is permitted to select any rung past the second.

4.2 An observed reason, in this dataset's own precise vocabulary, is a specific, falsifiable fact about what a cheaper rung's own dispatch actually returned, examined after that dispatch occurred, never a fact asserted about a target before any dispatch against it has been attempted. This temporal ordering is the discipline's own load-bearing element: an observed reason is, by definition, something the router learned by trying the cheaper rung and inspecting its result, not something the router inferred in advance from the target's category, domain, or superficial resemblance to a previously-difficult site.

4.3 Three categories of observed reason exhaust the legitimate grounds for escalation, restated here in this dataset's own operational terms from Dataset #01 Part VI §6.4's own three illustrative examples. An explicit failure signal — a non-success HTTP status, an explicit error response, a rung's own outright refusal to execute against the target at all — is the clearest category, requiring the least interpretation. A content-insufficiency signal — retrieved content materially shorter than the site's own typical length for that page type, a title inconsistent with the requested path, a body that inspection reveals to be a consent wall or interstitial rather than target content — requires the router to actually examine the dispatched rung's own returned Evidence Object per Dataset #01 Part VII, not merely to note that a response was technically received. A structural-incapacity signal — content that a technical inspection reveals depends on client-side execution the dispatched rung did not perform, detectable from the raw markup itself even before considering the rendered result — is the third category, distinct from content-insufficiency in that the deficiency is diagnosable from the response's own structure rather than merely from its apparent shortness.

4.4 A fact that is not, under any circumstance, a valid observed reason: a prediction about a target made before any dispatch against that specific target has occurred, however well-grounded that prediction might seem. "This platform is generally known to require heavy client-side rendering" is not, by itself, an observed reason for this specific target, even where the observation is broadly true of the platform as a category — Dataset #01 Part VI §6.4 draws this exact distinction, permitting a category-level prediction to count as legitimate only when it is itself drawn from Crawl OS's own site memory, populated by a genuine prior observed failure at a cheaper rung against that same site or site class, never from an assumption applied preemptively to a target the system has never actually attempted.

4.5 This Part's own precise contribution beyond restating Part VI §6.4's own distinction is specifying exactly how a router tells the two apart operationally, since the difference between a legitimate site-memory-derived prediction and an illegitimate preemptive assumption is not always obvious from the prediction's own surface content — both might read, in isolation, as "this site requires a browser." The operational test this dataset applies: a legitimate prediction traces, through site memory's own provenance record per Part V §5.6, back to a specific, dated, prior dispatch against that same target or site class whose own outcome was an actually-observed insufficiency or failure. An illegitimate assumption has no such trace — it exists in the router's own reasoning with no corresponding site-memory entry, meaning it was formed some other way, whether from general knowledge, a superficial resemblance to a different known-difficult site, or simple caution applied preemptively.

4.6 A worked example distinguishes a legitimate site-memory-derived prediction from an illegitimate preemptive assumption concretely, using the identical target type to make the contrast precise. Two missions, weeks apart, each include a frontier item pointing to a different page on the same interactive dashboard platform. The first mission's routing pass, having no site memory for this platform at all, correctly begins at rung two per Part III's own baseline procedure, observes a content-insufficiency signal — a near-empty HTML shell — dispatches rung four next per the within-pass escalation Part II §2.8 permits, and succeeds, writing a confirmed rung-four requirement into this platform's own site memory. The second mission's routing pass, encountering a different page on the identical platform, consults that site memory per Part III §3.6, finds a dated, provenance-traceable prior observation of rung-two insufficiency for this exact platform, and selects rung four directly without re-dispatching rung two first — a legitimate prediction, because it traces to an actual prior observation. Contrast this with a third, unrelated mission whose router, encountering a frontier item on a platform it has never visited but which shares a superficial resemblance — a similarly modern-looking interface — to the dashboard platform above, selects rung four on the reasoning that "modern-looking platforms like this usually need a browser." This third selection has no site-memory trace, is not drawn from any actual prior observation of this specific target, and is exactly the illegitimate, untested-prediction escalation Part VI §6.4 forbids, regardless of how often such predictions happen to turn out correct in practice.

4.7 The discipline's own burden of proof runs in the identical single direction Dataset #01 Part VIII §8.7 and Dataset #16 Part II §2.7 already establish for their own respective domains: the burden is on the router to demonstrate a valid observed reason before escalating, never on some downstream reviewer to demonstrate the reason's absence after the fact. A rung selection defaults to the cheapest technically-capable rung per Part III's own procedure until a valid observed reason, satisfying this Part's own three-category test, actually justifies moving past it; it does not default to whatever rung the router happens to select and require someone to catch an unjustified escalation after the fact.

4.8 A precise clarification prevents this Part's own discipline from being read as forbidding escalation on a first attempt against a genuinely novel target category, since the three-category test in 4.3 might otherwise seem to require an impossible prior dispatch against every target before any escalation is ever legitimate. Where a target's own structural characteristics are diagnosable from a lightweight, low-cost inspection performed as part of the current dispatch itself — the structural-incapacity signal named in 4.3's third category, detectable from a rung's own returned markup without requiring a full failed dispatch first — that inspection itself constitutes the observed reason, occurring within the current routing pass's own dispatch rather than requiring a separate, earlier dispatch against the identical target. This is the mechanism by which Part II §2.8's own within-pass escalation operates: rung two's own dispatch, even where it technically succeeds in retrieving something, can itself surface the structural-incapacity signal that justifies immediately proceeding to rung four within that same pass, without needing a second, entirely separate routing pass to first establish the observation.

4.9 A failure this Part specifically names, distinct from but related to Part I §1.6's own three bounding failures, is confidence laundering: a router that dispatches a cheap rung, observes a genuinely ambiguous result — content that is neither clearly sufficient nor clearly insufficient — and resolves that ambiguity toward escalation not because the ambiguity itself constitutes an observed reason but because escalating feels safer than committing to the cheaper rung's own result. Genuine ambiguity, properly handled, is examined against the mission's own stated evidence requirement per Part III §3.8's own evidence-requirement test, and where that examination does not clearly establish insufficiency, the router's own correct behavior is to accept the cheaper rung's own result rather than to treat unresolved ambiguity as though it were itself a valid observed reason to escalate — a habitual-escalation failure Part VI §6.4 already forecloses, presenting here in a specific, ambiguity-laundering disguise.

4.10 A final worked case closes this Part, applying its full discipline to a mission where the ambiguity in 4.9 is genuinely present and correctly resolved without escalation. A mission's evidence requirement is moderate — a general understanding of a product's own feature set, not verbatim extraction. A basic fetch against the product's own marketing page returns content that is shorter than some comparable pages this site's own memory records, but not dramatically so, and the retrieved text does plausibly describe the product's own features in ordinary prose. The router, applying Part III §3.8's own evidence-requirement test against this genuinely moderate ambiguity, finds the retrieved content plausibly satisfies a moderate requirement despite its somewhat-shorter-than-typical length, and correctly declines to treat that length alone as a content-insufficiency signal absent some further indication — a title mismatch, a recognizable interstitial pattern — that would elevate the observation from mere ambiguity into an actual observed reason. The routing pass concludes at rung two, and the mission's own moderate requirement is satisfied without the confidence-laundering escalation 4.9 names.

PART IV FINAL LAW

An observed reason is a specific, falsifiable fact about what a cheaper rung's own dispatch actually returned, examined after that dispatch, never a prediction asserted in advance of any dispatch against the specific target. Three categories exhaust legitimate grounds for escalation: an explicit failure signal, a content-insufficiency signal actually examined from the returned Evidence Object, and a structural-incapacity signal diagnosable from the response's own structure. A site-memory-derived prediction is legitimate only where it traces to an actual, dated, provenance-recorded prior observation; an assumption with no such trace is illegitimate regardless of how often it happens to prove correct. The burden of proof runs one direction only — a rung selection defaults to the cheapest technically-capable rung until a valid observed reason actually justifies escalating past it — and genuine, unresolved ambiguity about a cheaper rung's own result is never itself a valid reason to escalate; it is examined against the mission's own stated requirement and, absent a clear shortfall, resolved in the cheaper rung's own favor.


PART V — SITE MEMORY: CONSULTATION, POPULATION, AND STALENESS

5.1 Dataset #01 Part VI §6.4 names site memory as the specific mechanism that distinguishes a legitimate, prior-observation-grounded prediction from an illegitimate, untested assumption, and Part VI §6.5 names site memory's own periodic revisiting as the mechanism that keeps the cost ladder's discipline bidirectional rather than monotonically escalating. This Part specifies site memory's own structure, consultation interface, population mechanics, and staleness discipline in the operational depth those two constitutional references presuppose but do not themselves provide.

5.2 Site memory, as this dataset specifies it, is keyed at two distinct granularities, each serving a different purpose Part III's own procedure already relies on without fully describing the underlying structure. Target-level memory records an observation specific to one exact URL or resource — this specific document, this specific page — and is the most precise, most directly applicable form of memory where it exists. Site-class-level memory records an observation generalized across a recognizable category of target — a specific platform's own general rendering behavior, a specific type of government portal's own general delivery characteristics — and supplies a fallback prediction where target-level memory for the exact item does not yet exist, exactly as Part II §2.10's own worked trace relied on site-class memory for a specific filing it had never individually visited before.

5.3 The distinction between these two granularities carries its own precision requirement, since a site-class generalization risks exactly the illegitimate-assumption failure Part IV §4.4 forbids if applied carelessly. A site-class entry is legitimate only where it is itself derived from an aggregation of multiple actual target-level observations within that class, each individually a genuine observed reason per Part IV's own three-category test — never from a single observation hastily generalized, and never from a characteristic asserted about the class without any underlying target-level observation supporting it at all. A site-class entry's own provenance record, per 5.6 below, must be able to name the specific target-level observations it was actually built from.

5.4 Site memory's own consultation interface, invoked at Part III §3.6 and Part IV §4.5, answers a single question given a target and, where target-level memory is absent, that target's own site class: what is the most specific, currently-valid prior observation available, and what rung does it indicate. The interface returns exactly one of three answers — a target-level observation, where one exists and remains current per 5.7's own staleness test; a site-class observation, where target-level memory is absent but a current site-class entry exists; or no applicable memory, where neither exists or both have been flagged stale, in which case Part III's own procedure proceeds from its baseline, rung-two-by-default, position.

5.5 Population, the mechanism by which a dispatch's own outcome becomes a future memory entry, is Part II §2.7's own outcome-integration stage specified in this Part's own structural terms. Every dispatch this dataset's routing pipeline performs writes a target-level entry recording the rung dispatched, the observed outcome — success, insufficiency, or failure, per Part IV's own three-category vocabulary where applicable — and the timestamp of the observation. Where a sufficient number of target-level entries within a recognizable site class converge on a consistent pattern, per 5.3's own aggregation requirement, this dataset's own periodic site-class-derivation process, distinct from any single dispatch's own immediate population, produces or updates the corresponding site-class entry.

5.6 Every memory entry, target-level or site-class, carries a provenance record naming the specific dispatch or dispatches it derives from, mirroring the provenance-tagging discipline Dataset #02 Part II §2.6 already establishes for a Mission Contract's own compiled fields and Dataset #16 Part XV already establishes for its own audit trail — a discipline this family applies consistently to any record whose own legitimacy depends on being traceable back to a specific, real, examinable origin rather than standing as an unsourced assertion. A memory entry without this provenance is not a legitimate input to Part III's own procedure or Part IV's own observed-reason test, regardless of how plausible its own stated content appears.

5.7 Staleness, the discipline Dataset #01 Part VI §6.5 names at the constitutional level without specifying its own mechanics, is this Part's own closing concern. A memory entry's own currency is bounded by two distinct triggers, either of which flags the entry stale and removes it from Part III's own procedure's consultation until re-verified. A time-based trigger flags an entry stale once it exceeds an age threshold calibrated to how volatile a given site class has historically proven — a site class whose own history shows frequent rendering-strategy changes warrants a shorter threshold than one whose own history shows years of stability, and this calibration is itself a site-class-level property this dataset's own periodic review maintains. An event-based trigger flags an entry stale immediately upon a specific signal independent of elapsed time — an observed dispatch outcome that contradicts the existing entry's own prediction, which is itself evidence the underlying target or site class has changed in a way the stale entry no longer reflects.

5.8 The event-based trigger deserves particular emphasis because it is the mechanism through which Part VI §6.5's own bidirectional discipline — de-escalation as well as escalation — actually operates in practice, developed fully in Part VI of this dataset but grounded here in site memory's own mechanics. A target whose site memory records a confirmed rung-five requirement, dispatched again months later, that this time succeeds at rung two — because the site's own anti-bot posture relaxed, or its rendering strategy simplified — produces an event-based staleness trigger the moment that contradictory outcome is observed, flagging the existing rung-five entry for re-evaluation rather than allowing it to stand unchallenged simply because no scheduled time-based review has yet come due.

5.9 A precise clarification addresses what staleness does not do, since an overly aggressive staleness discipline risks discarding genuinely still-valid memory and forcing wasteful re-testing of confirmed conditions. Staleness flags an entry for re-verification; it does not delete the entry or immediately assume its own prediction is now wrong. A flagged entry's own next actual dispatch, whichever rung Part III's baseline procedure selects absent the flagged entry's guidance, produces a fresh observation that either reconfirms the flagged entry — in which case its own timestamp is refreshed and it returns to current status — or contradicts it, in which case the entry is updated to reflect the new, current reality per 5.8's own event-based mechanism.

5.10 A worked example demonstrates the full lifecycle this Part specifies, from initial population through staleness and re-verification. A specific documentation platform's site-class entry, populated from several converging target-level observations, records a confirmed rung-four requirement, timestamped at initial population. Eighteen months later, that platform's own volatility calibration — documentation platforms, per this dataset's own historical review, tend to change rendering strategy roughly annually — flags the entry stale on a time-based trigger before any contradicting dispatch has occurred. A new frontier item touching this platform arrives; Part III's own procedure, finding the site-class entry flagged stale, proceeds from its baseline rung-two-by-default position rather than trusting the eighteen-month-old rung-four prediction. The dispatch at rung two succeeds cleanly, revealing the platform has since migrated to server-rendered delivery. This fresh, contradicting observation both resolves the routing pass at the cheaper rung and triggers an update to the site-class entry itself, and the platform's own memory now correctly reflects rung two as sufficient going forward — the exact de-escalation Part VI §6.5 requires, made mechanically possible by this Part's own staleness discipline rather than depending on some separate, discretionary review to notice the change.

PART V FINAL LAW

Site memory is keyed at two granularities — target-level, the most precise; site-class-level, a fallback aggregated from multiple genuine target-level observations, never from a single hasty generalization or an unsupported assertion about a category. Every entry carries a provenance record naming its own originating dispatch or dispatches; an entry without traceable provenance is not a legitimate input to this dataset's own routing procedure. Staleness is triggered either by elapsed time, calibrated per site class, or by an observed dispatch outcome contradicting an existing entry, and a flagged entry is treated as absent for routing purposes until a fresh dispatch either reconfirms or updates it. Staleness never deletes an entry outright; it forces re-verification, which is the specific mechanism that makes the cost ladder's own bidirectional, de-escalating discipline operationally real rather than aspirational.


PART VI — DE-ESCALATION: THE BIDIRECTIONAL DISCIPLINE

6.1 Dataset #01 Part VI §6.5 states the ladder's bidirectional discipline in a single governing sentence: a mission must also be permitted, and in fact required by its own contract, to de-escalate once a cheaper rung is later confirmed sufficient for a class of source that previously required a more expensive one, because a system that only ever escalates and never de-escalates will monotonically grow more expensive over its own operational lifetime regardless of whether the underlying targets still warrant that expense. This Part specifies the mechanism by which this dataset's own routing pipeline actually performs a de-escalation, distinct from the staleness discipline Part V already specifies as the trigger that makes de-escalation possible in the first place.

6.2 A precise distinction opens this Part: Part V's staleness discipline determines when an existing memory entry can no longer be trusted as currently valid; this Part specifies what the router actually does once that determination has been made, specifically in the case where the fresh observation that follows contradicts the stale entry toward a cheaper rung rather than confirming it or contradicting it toward a more expensive one. De-escalation is therefore not a separate trigger mechanism competing with Part V's staleness discipline — it is one of the two possible outcomes staleness re-verification can produce, the other being reconfirmation or escalation to an even more expensive rung than previously recorded.

6.3 The de-escalation procedure itself is deliberately simple, mirroring the same attempt-cheapest-first discipline Part III already establishes as this dataset's own baseline behavior: once a memory entry is flagged stale per Part V §5.7, the next routing pass touching that target or site class does not automatically re-dispatch at the previously-recorded expensive rung out of habit or caution. Instead, it proceeds from Part III's own baseline procedure exactly as though no memory existed at all, walking the ladder from rung two forward and only reaching the previously-recorded rung again if the intervening rungs are actually, freshly observed to be insufficient. A router that, upon encountering a stale expensive-rung entry, simply re-dispatches at that same expensive rung "to be safe" has not performed the re-verification Part V's staleness discipline requires — it has merely renewed the stale entry's own timestamp without ever actually testing whether the underlying condition still holds, which is indistinguishable in its practical effect from never having flagged the entry stale at all.

6.4 A precise justification for why re-verification must proceed from the baseline rather than from some intermediate rung deserves stating, since testing from an intermediate point might seem like a reasonable compromise between full re-verification and blind trust in the stale entry. Testing from any rung other than the baseline presupposes exactly the conclusion re-verification exists to test — that the intervening cheaper rungs remain insufficient — and a presupposition of that kind reintroduces the untested-prediction failure Part IV §4.4 forbids, merely relocated from "will this target need escalation at all" to "how far up the ladder does this target still need." The only procedure that genuinely tests whether a cheaper rung has become sufficient is one that actually attempts that cheaper rung and observes the result, which is precisely Part III's own baseline procedure applied afresh.

6.5 A cost consideration deserves acknowledgment here, since re-testing from the baseline on every stale entry might appear to reintroduce exactly the wasteful multiple-dispatch pattern Part III §3.4 already forbids. The distinction is temporal rather than structural: Part III §3.4 forbids dispatching multiple rungs within a single routing pass for a single frontier item as a matter of course. This Part's own re-verification dispatches exactly one rung — the cheapest one, per baseline — for the specific routing pass that first encounters a staleness flag, and only escalates within that same pass if that single dispatch itself produces a fresh observed failure, per Part II §2.8's own within-pass escalation mechanism. The two disciplines are the identical single-dispatch-per-pass procedure; re-verification does not multiply dispatches, it merely resets which rung the procedure's single dispatch begins from.

6.6 A worked example demonstrates de-escalation as the specific favorable outcome of re-verification. A specific news publication's site memory records a confirmed rung-five requirement — a full browser, populated many months earlier when the publication's own article pages loaded their entire body via client-side execution. This entry is flagged stale on a time-based trigger per Part V §5.7, this publication's own site class carrying a moderate volatility calibration. A new mission's frontier item touches this publication; the routing pass, finding the entry stale, proceeds from baseline, dispatching rung two first. The dispatch succeeds cleanly, returning complete article text with no missing content — the publication has since migrated its own delivery architecture to server-side rendering, a change this dataset's routing pipeline had no way to know about until this fresh dispatch actually tested it. The routing pass resolves at rung two, and the site-class entry is updated to reflect rung two as now sufficient, the exact de-escalation Part VI §6.5 requires and the exact reason a monotonically-escalating router, one that never revisits a stale entry, would have continued needlessly dispatching a full browser against every one of this publication's pages indefinitely.

6.7 A contrasting worked example demonstrates re-verification resolving toward reconfirmation rather than de-escalation, since not every staleness flag actually produces a change in the underlying condition. The identical scenario, but the fresh rung-two dispatch instead returns a near-empty shell, the same content-insufficiency signal that originally justified the rung-five entry. The routing pass, per Part II §2.8's own within-pass escalation, proceeds to test the intervening rungs' own trigger conditions using this fresh observation, ultimately re-reaching rung five within the same pass. The site-class entry is updated with a fresh timestamp reflecting this reconfirmation, and this dataset's own re-verification discipline has correctly concluded that no de-escalation was actually warranted this time — the target's own posture has not changed, only the entry's own currency required refreshing.

6.8 A failure this Part specifically guards against, distinct from staleness itself, is de-escalation reluctance: a router that correctly flags an entry stale and correctly re-tests from baseline, but then, upon observing a genuinely successful cheap-rung dispatch, nonetheless re-escalates anyway out of an abundance of caution not grounded in any actual observed reason from the current dispatch. This is the mirror image of Part IV §4.9's confidence laundering, applied to the de-escalation direction rather than the escalation direction: a router permitted to override a genuinely successful cheap dispatch's own result, on the reasoning that the more expensive rung "felt safer" given the target's own history, has abandoned the observed-reason discipline in exactly the manner Part IV forbids, merely in the direction of unwarranted caution rather than unwarranted escalation.

6.9 This Part's own relationship to Part XXI's Crawl Cortex Supervisor interface deserves a forward note, developed fully in that later Part: a router whose own cost curve trends only upward across its operational history, with no de-escalation events ever recorded despite regular staleness flags being raised, is itself an anomalous pattern Part XXI's own supervision mechanism is positioned to surface — a router that flags entries stale correctly but somehow never actually de-escalates as a result is exhibiting exactly the reluctance 6.8 names, at a pattern level rather than a single-item level, and this Part's own individual-item discipline and Part XXI's own aggregate supervision compose to catch the failure at whichever level it first becomes visible.

6.10 A closing worked case demonstrates de-escalation applied at the site-class level rather than the single-target level, since a site-class entry's own de-escalation carries broader consequence than a single target's. A specific documentation-hosting platform's site-class entry, aggregated from several target-level rung-four observations per Part V §5.3, is flagged stale after the platform's own developers, per an external announcement this dataset's routing pipeline has no direct visibility into, migrate to a new templating engine. The site-class entry's own staleness flag fires on schedule, not from that external announcement, and the next several missions touching different pages on this platform each independently re-verify from baseline, each independently observing rung-two sufficiency. Once a sufficient number of independent target-level re-verifications converge on the new, cheaper rung — mirroring Part V §5.3's own aggregation requirement for populating a site-class entry in the first place — the site-class entry itself is updated to reflect rung two as the new baseline for this platform, and every subsequent mission touching any page on this platform benefits from the de-escalation without needing to independently rediscover it.

PART VI FINAL LAW

De-escalation is the favorable outcome of Part V's own staleness re-verification, never a separate discretionary judgment layered on top of it. A flagged-stale entry is re-tested from Part III's own baseline procedure, never from some intermediate rung presupposing the intervening rungs remain insufficient, because only a genuine baseline re-test actually establishes whether a cheaper rung has become sufficient rather than merely renewing a stale assumption's own timestamp. A router that overrides a genuinely successful cheap-rung result and re-escalates anyway, absent any observed reason from the current dispatch, has committed the identical failure Part IV forbids in the escalation direction, merely inverted — and a router whose own cost curve only ever grows, despite regular staleness flags, has abandoned this Part's own discipline at a pattern level even where no single item's own re-verification appears individually wrong.


PART VII — CONSUMING THE AUTHORIZATION VERDICT: THE FIXED-CONSTRAINT INTERFACE WITH DATASET #16

7.1 Dataset #16 Part XX §20.5 already states, from its own side, the precise boundary this Part restates and elaborates from this dataset's own side: this dataset decides whether an acquisition is authorized; this dataset decides how an authorized acquisition is actually executed and paced. This Part specifies exactly how this dataset's own routing pipeline consumes Dataset #16's composite verdict, in the operational depth the constraint-gathering stage named in Part II §2.2 presupposes but does not itself fully describe.

7.2 The composite verdict this dataset consumes, per Dataset #16 Part XIV, is a single, already-resolved judgment for a specific target and mission — granted, denied, or escalated per Dataset #16 Part II §2.4's closed three-verdict ontology — assembled from that dataset's own domain, authentication, restricted-action, and data-minimization components. This dataset receives that composite as a single, opaque input; it does not decompose the composite back into its constituent components, does not re-examine which component drove the verdict, and does not form any independent view of whether the verdict itself was correctly reached. Dataset #16's own internal reasoning is Dataset #16's own territory in full, per that dataset's own Part XIV, and this dataset's consumption interface treats the composite verdict as a black box that happens to also carry a rate-limit ceiling and seventh-rung clearance where applicable.

7.3 The word "fixed" in this Part's own title is deliberate and load-bearing: once obtained, the composite verdict is not re-requested, re-negotiated, or independently second-guessed for the duration of a single routing pass. Where a routing pass's own within-pass escalation, per Part II §2.8, moves from one rung to a more expensive one, the composite verdict already obtained at constraint-gathering continues to apply without needing re-confirmation at each escalation step, because the verdict attaches to the target and the mission, per Dataset #16 Part XX §20.9, not to any specific rung — an escalation from rung two to rung four within the same pass is still operating under the identical granted verdict obtained for this target at the pass's own outset, not a fresh authorization question the escalation itself somehow raises.

7.4 A precise exception to 7.3's own fixed-for-the-pass discipline concerns the seventh rung specifically, since Dataset #01 Part VI §6.6 and Dataset #16 Part XII both treat the seventh rung as qualitatively different from the six automated rungs. Where a routing pass's own within-pass escalation would otherwise reach the seventh rung, this dataset's own procedure does not treat the target-level composite verdict obtained at constraint-gathering as automatically covering seventh-rung invocation — Dataset #16 Part XII's own operator-approval clearance for the seventh rung specifically is a distinct authorization this dataset must confirm separately before invoking it, even where the same target already carries a granted composite verdict for its automated-rung components. Part XIII of this dataset develops this exception in full; this Part names it here because it is the one precise carve-out to the otherwise-fixed-for-the-pass discipline 7.3 establishes.

7.5 A denied or escalated verdict halts this dataset's own routing pipeline for that specific target entirely, per Part II §2.3's own ordering constraint — sufficiency-assessment never begins for a target without a granted verdict, and no rung, however cheap, is selected in its place. This dataset's own routing pipeline does not treat a denial as an invitation to select the cheapest rung "just to see," nor does it treat an escalated-for-operator-review verdict as license to proceed with automated rungs while awaiting the operator's own decision; both outcomes halt routing for that item until Dataset #16's own process resolves the verdict to granted, or the item is dropped from the mission's frontier entirely.

7.6 The rate-limit ceiling Dataset #16 Part VII hands off alongside a granted verdict, where one was discovered, is consumed by this dataset's own scheduling and pacing logic, developed fully in Part XV, but named here as part of this dataset's overall consumption interface with Dataset #16. This dataset's rung selection and this dataset's scheduling are distinct concerns consuming the identical Dataset #16 output for different purposes: rung selection asks which engine to use for this item; scheduling asks when, relative to the mission's other acquisitions against the same target, this item's own dispatch should actually occur, and a router that satisfies the former while ignoring the latter has only partially consumed Dataset #16's own handed-off constraint.

7.7 A precise worked case demonstrates the fixed-constraint discipline holding under a plausible pressure to violate it: a routing pass, having obtained a granted verdict at constraint-gathering, dispatches rung two, which returns a content-insufficiency signal strongly suggesting the target requires authenticated access this dataset's own working context happens to hold a credential for, though the granted verdict obtained at constraint-gathering did not specifically address authenticated access to this particular resource. Per Dataset #16 Part XX §20.8's own worked case addressing this exact scenario from its own side, this dataset's own router does not use the held credential on the reasoning that the target-level verdict was already granted — it halts and consults Dataset #16's own Part IV adjudication for this specific session and resource, precisely because the originally-obtained composite verdict did not itself address this newly-surfaced authentication question, and treating it as though it silently covered every subsequent authentication question this routing pass might encounter would be exactly the authorization-adjudication-inversion failure Part I §1.7 already forecloses at this dataset's own outset.

7.8 A failure this Part specifically names, extending Dataset #16 Part XX §20.9's own authorization-laundering-through-routing concern into this dataset's own operational vocabulary, is verdict scope inflation: this dataset's own routing logic treating a composite verdict granted for one specific access pattern — a straightforward, unauthenticated fetch — as though it silently extended to a categorically different access pattern against the same nominal target, such as the authenticated or restricted-action access 7.7's worked case names. A granted verdict's own scope is exactly what Dataset #16's own adjudication actually covered, per that dataset's Part XIV composite, never a license this dataset's own routing logic is entitled to read expansively in whichever direction happens to be operationally convenient.

7.9 This Part's own closing note addresses the honest current state of this consumption interface, consistent with the family-wide discipline every sealed dataset applies to its own downstream claims: `modules/crawl_os/strategy_router.py`'s own consumption of Dataset #16's composite verdict, as this Part specifies it, is PLANNED, not REACHABLE — no running code today actually queries a Dataset #16 verdict object, and this Part's specification describes the interface contract that future module will implement once Dataset #16's own future `modules/crawl_os/authz.py` exists to be queried, not a call this dataset's own text can claim currently executes.

PART VII FINAL LAW

This dataset consumes Dataset #16's composite verdict as a single, opaque, fixed constraint for the duration of a routing pass — never decomposed, never re-negotiated, never independently second-guessed. The verdict attaches to the target and the mission, not to any specific rung, so within-pass escalation across automated rungs continues under the same granted verdict; the seventh rung alone requires a separate, specific operator-approval confirmation the automated-rung verdict does not itself cover. A denied or escalated verdict halts routing for that item entirely — no rung is selected in its place, and a newly surfaced authentication or access question a dispatch itself reveals is referred back to Dataset #16 rather than assumed covered by whatever verdict was originally obtained.


PART VIII — CONSUMING THE COMPILED MISSION CONTRACT: THE HANDOFF INTERFACE WITH DATASET #02

8.1 Dataset #02 Part XXI already specifies, from its own side, the mission-to-engine handoff interface this dataset is the most direct intended recipient of: a one-time, complete transfer of a VALIDATED Mission Contract, all sixteen fields with their provenance tags, plus the full compilation audit trail, with no ongoing synchronization channel maintained afterward. PART X — RUNGS TWO AND THREE: BASIC AND SPECIALIZED HTTP TRANSPORT SELECTION

10.1 This Part is the first of four Parts (X through XIII) applying Part III's own general decision procedure to each rung's own particular selection conditions in turn, beginning with the two cheapest non-cache rungs Dataset #01 Part VI §6.2 defines: rung two, a basic HTTP fetch appropriate for static pages with no meaningful anti-automation posture, and rung three, a specialized HTTP transport such as a client-fingerprint-matched request, appropriate when a basic fetch is observed to receive materially different content than a standard browser would.

10.2 Rung two's own selection condition, per Part III §3.2's own routing table, is this dataset's own baseline — the rung selected absent any observed reason or site memory indicating otherwise, for any target whose cache (rung one) does not already hold a sufficiently fresh acquisition. This baseline status means rung two requires no additional trigger condition beyond the absence of a disqualifying signal, distinct from every rung above it, each of which requires an affirmative observed reason per Part IV before selection.

10.3 Rung three's own selection condition is the first genuinely observed-reason-gated rung in the ladder, and its own specific trigger — materially different content than a standard browser would receive — deserves operational precision beyond Dataset #01 Part VI §6.2's own brief description, since "materially different" could otherwise be read too broadly or too narrowly. The operational test this dataset applies: a basic fetch's own returned content differs from what this dataset's own site memory, or a single diagnostic comparison dispatch where no site memory exists, indicates a standard browser's own request would receive for the identical resource — a different response body entirely, a bot-detection interstitial substituted for target content, or a response status specifically associated with automated-request detection rather than a genuine resource-not-found or access-denied condition.

10.4 A precise distinction separates rung three's own trigger from rung four's, since both can present superficially similar symptoms — a basic fetch returning insufficient content in both cases. Rung three's own trigger specifically concerns anti-automation detection, where the underlying content does exist and a standard browser would receive it, but the basic fetch's own request characteristics themselves triggered a defensive substitution. Rung four's own trigger, developed in Part XI, concerns genuine client-side rendering dependency, where even a standard browser's own initial request would receive the identical incomplete markup, with the missing content only appearing after client-side execution runs. This dataset's own diagnostic distinction between the two: a specialized-transport retry per rung three, if the target's own issue is genuinely anti-automation detection, typically resolves the insufficiency; a specialized-transport retry against a target whose actual issue is client-side-rendering dependency will not resolve it, since specialized transport addresses request fingerprinting, not execution of client-side logic — and this dataset's own within-pass escalation, per Part II §2.8, proceeds to rung four rather than persisting at rung three once this diagnostic distinction becomes clear from the rung-three dispatch's own result.

PART XI — RUNGS FOUR AND FIVE: LIGHTWEIGHT RENDER AND FULL BROWSER SELECTION

11.1 This Part continues the rung-by-rung sequence Part X began, addressing the two rungs Dataset #01 Part VI §6.2 defines around genuine client-side rendering dependency rather than anti-automation detection: rung four, a lightweight render appropriate when content is present in the initial HTML but a small amount of client-side execution is needed to reveal it fully, and rung five, a full browser appropriate when the target is a genuine single-page application or requires substantial client-side execution to produce any usable content at all.

11.2 Rung four's own trigger condition, per this dataset's own routing table at Part III §3.2, fires on an observed reason that the target's initial HTML already contains the bulk of the target content, structurally present in the raw markup, but a specific, bounded piece of client-side execution is required to surface it in a form Part IX's own extract verb can actually work with — a common pattern where a page's main body is present but a specific widget, a lazily-loaded content block, or a client-side templating pass populates the final visible text from data already embedded in the page. The distinguishing diagnostic this dataset applies, restated from Part X §10.4: the raw markup itself, inspected directly rather than after execution, already contains recognizable target-content signals, distinguishing this case from rung five's own trigger where the raw markup contains essentially nothing but a loading shell.

11.3 Rung five's own trigger condition fires on an observed reason that the target is a genuine single-page application — the raw initial markup contains little beyond a bare application shell, with essentially all visible content, navigation, and structure produced by client-side execution rather than present in any recognizable form beforehand. 11.4 A precise diagnostic procedure distinguishes the two triggers operationally, extending Part X §10.4's own diagnostic pattern to this rung pair: an inspection of the raw initial markup, performed as part of the rung-two or rung-three dispatch that first surfaced the content-insufficiency signal per Part IV §4.8's own within-dispatch structural-incapacity mechanism, examines whether recognizable target-content fragments — partial text, populated data attributes, a title matching the requested resource — already exist in that raw markup despite the rendered page appearing incomplete. Their presence indicates rung four; their substantial absence, with only generic application-shell markup present, indicates rung five. This diagnostic is itself an application of Part IV §4.3's own structural-incapacity signal category, examined at a level of detail specific to distinguishing these two particular rungs from each other rather than merely from rung two.

11.7 A precise failure this Part specifically names, extending Part IV's own observed-reason discipline to this specific diagnostic boundary, is render-tier misdiagnosis: selecting rung five where rung four's own lighter execution would have sufficed, or selecting rung four where the target's own structure genuinely requires rung five's full pipeline.
