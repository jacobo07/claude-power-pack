PART I — MISSION AND BOUNDARY: OPERATIONALIZING ANTI-DETECTION-IS-NOT-AUTHORIZATION

1.1 Dataset #01 named a doctrine and left it, deliberately, at the level a constitution is supposed to stop at: a statement of principle, not a mechanism. Part IX of that dataset states that technical capacity to evade a barrier and legitimate authority to pass it are two different axes, that anti-detection capability may only survive an incidental obstacle against an already-authorized target, and that authorization judgment belongs exclusively to an engine named there only as Governance, Safety, and Authorization. It does not say how that engine actually decides. It does not say what evidence a decision must cite, what happens when the evidence is ambiguous, who may override a denial, or how a decision, once made, is recorded so a future mission against the same target does not have to relitigate it from nothing. This dataset is the answer to all of those questions, and it exists because a doctrine that is never operationalized is not a doctrine a real system obeys, it is a doctrine a real system merely agrees with in the abstract while some other, unspecified logic actually decides what happens at runtime.

1.2 The gap this dataset closes is precise, not general. Dataset #01 Part V §5.13 already named the engine and gave it a one-sentence charter: bound every other engine's behavior against authorized domains, authorized authentication, and the estate's compliance requirements. This dataset elaborates that charter into the actual adjudication pipeline, the actual signals it consults, the actual verdicts it produces, and the actual record it leaves behind — the same relationship Dataset #02 holds to the Crawl Intent Compiler's one-sentence charter and Dataset #10 holds to the Evidence Integrity Fabric's, applied here to the fourth-built rather than second- or tenth-built engine, and built early in the family's own dependency order specifically because a governance boundary specified after the engines it is supposed to bound already exist governs nothing that has not already happened without it.

1.3 The doctrine this dataset operationalizes draws its distinction on a single axis, and every mechanism this dataset defines is a specific, checkable instance of applying that one axis to a specific kind of barrier. The axis is not how sophisticated an obstacle is, and it is not how easily it could be defeated by whichever adapter Dataset #01 Part II licenses to attempt it. The axis is whether the obstacle in front of a request represents a deliberate access-control decision, made by whoever controls the target, or an incidental side effect of some generic mechanism never configured with this specific, disclosed, legitimate kind of access in mind. Robots.txt, an authentication wall, a paywall, a stated rate limit: each is a deliberate decision, and each gets its own operational treatment in the Parts that follow, because the doctrine's single sentence is easy to state and materially harder to apply correctly across every concrete shape a deliberate decision can actually take on the open web.

1.4 A boundary must be stated as sharply here as the mission itself, because this dataset's own founding instruction was explicit that it must not appropriate universal governance: this dataset owns the authorization layer specific to Crawl OS's own acquisition activity, and nothing broader. It does not govern access control for Claude Power Pack's own internal systems, it does not define the estate's general compliance posture for domains unrelated to crawling, and it does not become a second, parallel policy engine for questions that have nothing to do with an acquisition reaching outside the estate's own boundary. A proposal that asks this dataset to adjudicate anything other than a crawl mission's own access to an external target has, by construction, exceeded this dataset's charter, regardless of how naturally the word authorization appears in that proposal's own description.

1.5 This dataset's position in the family's build order is itself a substantive claim, not an incidental scheduling detail, and it is worth stating plainly because a reader encountering this dataset out of numeric order might otherwise assume authorization was an afterthought bolted onto an already-running system. `wiggly-sparking-dolphin.md`'s own dependency-based sequencing moved this dataset from its source document's original position sixteen of twenty to position four of eighteen, precisely so a governance boundary exists before the acquisition engines it bounds are built, not after. A crawl fleet that ships its fetch, browser, and extraction engines first and adds authorization later has, for however long that gap persists, a fleet with no structural mechanism preventing exactly the failure Dataset #01 Part IX exists to forbid, and this dataset's early position is this family's own concrete evidence that the doctrine was taken seriously enough to build its enforcement mechanism before the capability it constrains.

1.6 Three failure directions bound this dataset's own design space, and naming all three together matters because a system built to avoid only one of them reliably drifts into one of the other two. The first is excessive permissiveness — an adjudication logic that grants access on thin or absent evidence because denial feels like an obstacle to a mission's own progress, reintroducing under a different name the exact authorization-by-capability failure Dataset #01 Part IX exists to forbid. The second is excessive restriction — an adjudication logic so conservative it forecloses genuinely authorized access because caution feels safer than judgment, which is not a lesser evil than the first failure, it is a different failure with its own real cost: a mission that cannot complete legitimate work because the engine chartered to authorize it declined to make the call. The third is silence — a decision made without a recorded basis, which corrupts every future audit of this engine's own behavior regardless of whether any individual decision, examined in isolation, was actually correct.

1.7 This dataset's relationship to Dataset #02 restates, in this dataset's own direction, the declare-versus-decide boundary Dataset #02's own Part IX already drew from its side. Dataset #02 compiles a mission's permitted-domains and authorized-authentication fields, states plainly what basis each population had, and stops — it does not itself grant or deny anything. This dataset receives those already-populated, already-provenance-tagged fields as declared input and performs the adjudication Dataset #02 explicitly declined to perform. Neither dataset may absorb the other's half of this single, two-part process: a Dataset #02 that started granting access would have quietly become this dataset under a different name, and a Dataset #16 that started re-populating a mission's declared scope rather than adjudicating what was actually declared would have quietly absorbed Dataset #02's own owned territory instead of composing with it.

1.8 This dataset's relationship to Dataset #10's already-sealed Gate C, Authorization Reality, is the concrete point where this dataset's abstract adjudication becomes a mechanically checkable fact about a specific acquisition. Dataset #01 Part XIII §13.4 already states that Gate C passes only when an acquisition was performed within a boundary Governance, Safety, and Authorization actually cleared — this dataset is the engine that clearing refers to, and the verdict this dataset produces for a given target is the specific value Gate C checks. An acquisition whose Gate C check never actually consulted this dataset's verdict, however the acquisition otherwise turned out, has not passed Gate C in the sense Dataset #01 requires; it has merely not yet failed it, which Dataset #01 Part VIII §8.7's burden-of-proof inversion already treats as a materially different and lesser claim.

1.9 A precise definition of this dataset's central noun prevents a subtle and recurring misreading: authorization, in this dataset's own vocabulary, is not a state that exists in the world independently of being checked, waiting to be discovered. It is a decision, made by this engine, on the basis of specific evidence, at a specific time, about a specific target and a specific mission. A target is not "authorized" in some ambient sense prior to this dataset's own adjudication; it is either granted, denied, or escalated by this dataset for a specific request, and treating authorization as a background fact a system merely observes rather than a decision a system actually makes is precisely the confusion this dataset's own Part XVI will name explicitly as a failure mode in its own right.

1.10 The ambition this dataset holds itself to must be stated honestly at its own outset, because the alternative — a system that grants everything it is capable of reaching — is not actually an ambition at all, it is the absence of one. This dataset does not aim for zero friction. It aims for gatekeeping that is honest, checkable, and proportionate: a mission that should be authorized is authorized without unnecessary delay, a mission that should not be is denied with a stated reason rather than a silent stall, and a mission whose authorization genuinely cannot be resolved automatically is escalated to a human rather than resolved by whichever default happened to be more convenient to implement. Every Part that follows is a specific, operational instance of holding that standard against one particular kind of barrier or one particular kind of ambiguity a real crawl mission will actually encounter.

PART I FINAL LAW

Technical capacity to reach a target and legitimate authority to reach it are two different axes, and this dataset is the engine that turns that distinction from a stated doctrine into an actual, checkable decision. Authorization is not a background fact a system observes; it is a decision this engine makes, on specific evidence, recorded with its basis, for a specific target and a specific mission. A verdict granted on thin or absent evidence reintroduces the exact failure Dataset #01 Part IX forbids; a verdict withheld from genuinely authorized access is not a lesser evil, it is a different failure with its own real cost; and a verdict made without a recorded basis has failed this dataset's own charter regardless of whether it happened to be correct.


PART II — THE ADJUDICATION PIPELINE: FROM DECLARED SCOPE TO VERDICT

2.1 A single, ordered pipeline governs every authorization decision this dataset makes, and stating it precisely here, before any individual signal is discussed in its own Part, prevents each later Part's specific mechanics from being read as independent rules rather than as stages of one coherent process. The pipeline has four stages, always performed in this order, never reordered by an implementer who finds a later stage more convenient to check first: signal gathering, conflict resolution, verdict assignment, and record-keeping.

2.2 Signal gathering is the stage at which this engine collects everything relevant to a specific target and a specific mission before forming any judgment about either. For a domain-level request, this includes the mission's own compiled permitted-domains field from Dataset #02, that field's provenance tag, the target's own robots.txt directives where retrievable, any known terms-of-service posture, and any prior verdict this engine has already recorded for the same target. For an authentication-scoped request, signal gathering additionally includes the mission's compiled authorized-authentication field, its provenance tag, and the actual scope of whatever existing session, if any, the mission claims to hold. No stage after this one may introduce a new signal that was not gathered here; a verdict influenced by information collected outside this stage's own scan has not followed this dataset's own pipeline, regardless of whether the additional information happened to be accurate.

2.3 Conflict resolution is the stage at which signals gathered in the prior stage, which frequently disagree with each other, are reconciled according to a fixed precedence rather than whichever signal an implementer happens to notice first. An explicit, deliberate denial — a robots.txt disallow, a terms-of-service prohibition, a rate limit actively being exceeded — always overrides a broader permissive signal, including the mission's own compiled permitted-domains field, because Dataset #01 Part IX already establishes that a site operator's deliberate decision is authoritative over anything Crawl OS itself might otherwise be willing to attempt. An explicit permissive signal from the target itself — no robots.txt restriction, terms silent on automated access, an authenticated session whose scope genuinely covers the requested content — outranks a merely default-compiled mission field, because a specific signal from the party actually entitled to grant or withhold access is stronger evidence than a compiler's own conservative or permissive default. Two signals of comparable specificity that genuinely conflict, rather than resolving cleanly under this precedence, are not adjudicated by guessing; they are the trigger condition Part XI's operator-approval workflow exists to handle.

2.4 Verdict assignment is the stage at which the reconciled signal set from stage two is mapped to exactly one of three outcomes this dataset produces: granted, denied, or escalated. Granted requires an affirmative, specific signal at least as strong as the request itself — never a mere absence of a denial signal, which Part XVI will treat at length as its own distinct failure mode. Denied is assigned whenever conflict resolution surfaces an explicit, deliberate denial signal, regardless of how strong any competing permissive signal appeared. Escalated is assigned whenever the reconciled signal set is genuinely ambiguous — neither a clear grant nor a clear denial resolves cleanly under stage two's own precedence — or whenever the request itself falls within Part X's restricted-actions taxonomy regardless of how unambiguous the underlying access would otherwise be.

2.5 Record-keeping is the pipeline's final, non-optional stage, and its placement last in the ordering does not make it least important; it exists precisely because a verdict is not actually complete, in this dataset's own vocabulary, until its basis is recorded. The record captures the target, the mission, the verdict, the specific signal or signals conflict resolution actually relied on, and the timestamp — the full detail Part XV's authorization audit trail elaborates operationally. A verdict assigned but never recorded is, for every purpose this dataset cares about, indistinguishable from a verdict that was never actually adjudicated at all, because nothing downstream can verify, after the fact, that stage two's precedence was actually followed rather than a favorable outcome simply being asserted.

2.6 A precise clarification separates this pipeline from a retry loop, because the two are easy to conflate under delivery pressure. A denied verdict does not send the request back through signal gathering hoping a different pass through the same stages produces a different answer; the pipeline runs once per request against the signals available at that time, and a materially different outcome requires either new information genuinely entering signal gathering — a robots.txt file that has since changed, an operator's explicit re-authorization — or the re-adjudication discipline Part XVII defines specifically for that case, never a bare re-run of the identical pipeline against identical inputs in the hope of a different result.

2.7 The pipeline's relationship to Dataset #01's own burden-of-proof inversion, already established for content verification in Part VIII §8.7 and for authorization generally in Part IX §9.9, is direct and binding on stage three specifically: the default outcome of an incomplete or inconclusive pass through stages one and two is never granted. It is escalated, or, where the ambiguity itself constitutes evidence of an unresolved deliberate restriction, denied. A pipeline that defaults to granted whenever it cannot cleanly resolve a conflict has inverted the burden this family's own constitution already places on every authorization question, and has done so specifically at the one stage where the inversion matters most.

2.8 A worked trace through all four stages makes the pipeline concrete rather than abstract. A mission's Dataset #02-compiled contract carries a permitted-domains field, provenance-tagged as a broad, no-explicit-signal default, naming "any domain reasonably related to the resolved entity." The target under consideration is that entity's own primary documentation site. Signal gathering collects the mission's broad-default field, the target's robots.txt, which carries no disallow relevant to the requested path, and a prior verdict this engine has already recorded for the same domain from an earlier mission: granted, six days ago. Conflict resolution finds no competing signals; the robots.txt is silent-permissive and the prior verdict is itself a specific, target-level signal reinforcing rather than contradicting the domain-level default. Verdict assignment resolves to granted, on the strength of the specific robots.txt and prior-verdict signals rather than the broad compiled default alone. Record-keeping logs the new verdict, its basis, and a reference to the prior verdict it is consistent with.

2.9 A second worked trace demonstrates escalation arising from the pipeline's own stage two rather than from stage one failing to gather enough signal. The same mission's frontier includes a second target: a competitor's pricing page reachable only through a session the mission's working context does describe as authenticated, but whose actual granted scope, on inspection during signal gathering, covers only that competitor's public marketing pages, not its authenticated customer portal where the specific pricing data the mission needs actually lives. Signal gathering collects both facts faithfully. Conflict resolution correctly identifies that the mission's authorized-authentication field and the session's own actual scope disagree about whether the customer portal is reachable. Verdict assignment, per stage three's own rule, resolves this specific and genuine conflict to escalated rather than guessing in either direction, and record-keeping logs the conflict explicitly rather than only the escalated outcome, so the eventual operator reviewing it sees exactly what stage two actually found.

2.10 This pipeline's stages are the vocabulary every later Part in this dataset uses without needing to redefine it: Part III and Part IV instantiate stages one through three for domain and authentication requests specifically; Parts V through VII instantiate stage one's signal-gathering mechanics for robots.txt, terms, and rate limits specifically; Part XI and Part XII instantiate the escalated branch of stage three; and Part XV instantiates stage four in full operational detail. A future Part or amendment proposing a fifth stage, or a reordering of the four stated here, is a Part XXIV amendment event under this dataset's own governing process, precisely because every other Part's own mechanics are built assuming this ordering holds.

PART II FINAL LAW

Four stages, always in this order: gather every relevant signal before judging any of it; resolve conflicts among gathered signals by a fixed precedence that lets a deliberate denial always override a merely permissive default; assign exactly one of three verdicts — granted, denied, or escalated — never inferring a grant from the mere absence of a denial signal; and record the verdict with its actual basis, because a verdict recorded without its basis is, for every purpose this dataset cares about, indistinguishable from a verdict never actually adjudicated. An inconclusive pass through the first two stages defaults to escalated or denied, never granted.


PART III — ACCESS-BOUNDARY ADJUDICATION: DOMAINS

3.1 This Part instantiates Part II's four-stage pipeline for the specific case of domain-level access — the question of whether a mission may acquire content from a given host at all, prior to and independent of any question about authenticated access to a specific resource within it, which Part IV takes up separately. The two questions are kept apart deliberately, because a domain can be broadly acquirable while a specific resource within it remains authentication-gated, and collapsing the two into a single judgment would lose exactly the distinction a mission frequently needs: general access to a documentation site alongside no access to that same site's private customer area.

3.2 The primary input to a domain-level adjudication is Dataset #02's own compiled permitted-domains field, together with the fine-grained provenance tag that dataset's Part IX §9.11 requires specifically so this dataset can weight the signal correctly. An explicitly requested domain — "only look at their official site, nothing else" — is the strongest form this input can take, and this dataset's own signal-gathering stage treats it as a near-binding constraint on the adjudication's own scope: a target outside an explicitly requested list is not silently included in the domain-level grant merely because the target seems related, it requires its own separate consideration, typically resolving to escalated per Part II §2.9's own reasoning about scope divergence. A resolved-entity domain — the entity's own primary host, inferred rather than explicitly named — is a moderately strong signal this dataset treats as sufficient for a grant absent any competing denial signal from the target itself. A broad, no-explicit-signal default — "any domain reasonably related to the resolved entities" — is the weakest form of this input, and this dataset's own adjudication over a target reached only through this default weights the target's own robots.txt and terms posture more heavily than it would for an explicitly requested domain, precisely because the mission's own contract supplied comparatively little specific signal to begin with.

3.3 The secondary and frequently decisive input is the target's own robots.txt, consulted per the mechanics Part V elaborates fully; this Part states only its role within the domain-adjudication pipeline specifically. A disallow directive addressed to Crawl OS's own declared user agent, or to crawlers generally, is the single strongest denial signal this dataset ever encounters for a domain-level question, and per Part II §2.3's conflict-resolution precedence, it overrides even an explicitly requested domain from the mission's own contract — a mission's own stated intent to acquire from a domain does not create authorization the domain's own operator has explicitly withheld, and Dataset #01 Part IX's doctrine forecloses any reading of this dataset's own logic that would let it.

3.4 A tertiary input, consulted specifically when the primary and secondary signals do not cleanly resolve, is this dataset's own prior-verdict record for the same domain, elaborated fully in Part XV and re-adjudicated per the staleness discipline in Part XVII. A domain this dataset has granted for an unrelated prior mission, within a recency window Part XVII bounds, is treated as reinforcing evidence for a new mission's own domain-level grant, though never as a sole and sufficient basis on its own — a prior grant for a different mission with a different, potentially narrower authorized-authentication posture does not automatically transfer to a new mission whose own required access might differ.

3.5 A specific and recurring case deserves its own worked treatment because it is the single most common domain-level question this dataset will actually adjudicate: a mission's resolved entity has a single, obvious primary domain, that domain carries no robots.txt restriction relevant to the requested path, and no prior verdict exists because the domain has never been adjudicated before. Signal gathering collects the entity-resolved permitted-domains field, the robots.txt's silence, and the absence of a prior record. Conflict resolution finds no competing signal at all. Verdict assignment resolves to granted on the strength of the entity-resolution signal alone, since Part II §2.4 requires an affirmative signal at least as strong as the request, and a resolved entity's own primary domain, absent any competing denial, is exactly that: not a mere absence-of-restriction default, but a positive, specific match between what the mission actually asked for and what the compiler concretely resolved it to.

3.6 A contrasting case where the same starting conditions resolve differently demonstrates why signal strength, not merely signal presence, governs the outcome. A mission's compiled permitted-domains field carries the broad, no-explicit-signal default rather than an entity-resolved domain, because the raw request named a market or category rather than a specific organization, and the specific target under consideration is one candidate domain among several plausible ones the frontier engine's own future work will eventually rank. Here, absent robots.txt restriction, the weaker provenance of the domain signal itself, per 3.2's own graduated treatment, means this dataset requires at least one additional corroborating signal — a terms-of-service posture that does not prohibit automated access, or a prior verdict already on record — before resolving to granted rather than escalated; a broad default alone, with nothing else corroborating it, is treated as insufficient on its own for a domain this dataset has never previously adjudicated.

3.7 This Part's relationship to Dataset #02's own asymmetric-defaults discipline, stated in that dataset's Part IX §9.9, is direct: Dataset #02 compiles permitted domains toward a broader rather than maximally narrow default because domain-level public access carries a materially different risk than authenticated access. This dataset's own adjudication respects that asymmetry rather than flattening it — a broad domain-level default is adjudicated with a lower evidentiary bar than the narrow, conservative default Dataset #02 applies to authorized authentication, which Part IV's own adjudication treats with correspondingly greater caution.

3.8 A failure this Part specifically guards against, distinguished from Part VIII's later general treatment of consent conflation, is domain-scope creep: a mission granted access to a specific domain's documentation subdirectory gradually, acquisition by acquisition, expanding its actual frontier to sibling subdirectories the original adjudication never specifically considered, on the reasoning that they share the same host and the same original grant should cover them. This dataset's own adjudication is scoped to the specific target actually presented at signal-gathering time, not to the domain as an unbounded whole, and a frontier engine's own expansion into unadjudicated territory within an already-granted domain requires its own fresh pass through this Part's pipeline, however administratively convenient it would be to treat the original grant as covering everything the host happens to serve.

3.9 The domain-level verdict this Part produces feeds directly into Part XIV's composition with Dataset #10's Gate C, and it is worth stating explicitly that a domain-level grant alone does not itself constitute a complete authorization verdict for a specific acquisition — Part IV's authentication-scoped adjudication, where the target requires it, and Part X's restricted-actions check, where the acquisition involves more than passive retrieval, both apply independently and cumulatively, mirroring Dataset #01 Part XIII §13.12's own insistence that no gate's pass may be inferred from another's.

3.10 A closing distinction separates this Part's own domain-level adjudication from an entirely different and much narrower judgment this dataset does not make: whether a given domain's subject matter is worth acquiring at all. That judgment belongs to the mission's own objective and to the frontier engine's own prioritization logic, neither of which this dataset has any charter to second-guess. This dataset answers exactly one question about a domain — is acquisition from it authorized — and a domain fully authorized under this Part's own pipeline may still never be visited, for reasons entirely outside this dataset's own jurisdiction, if the frontier engine never prioritizes it or the mission's own contract never actually requires it.

PART III FINAL LAW

Domain-level authorization is adjudicated on three inputs, in descending typical strength: the target's own robots.txt, which can deny outright regardless of any competing signal; Dataset #02's compiled permitted-domains field, weighted by its own provenance tag rather than treated as a uniform signal; and this dataset's own prior-verdict record, which reinforces but never alone suffices. A broad, no-explicit-signal default requires corroboration before it resolves to granted; a resolved-entity domain does not. A domain grant is scoped to the specific target actually adjudicated, never silently extended to sibling territory the original pass never considered.


PART IV — ACCESS-BOUNDARY ADJUDICATION: AUTHENTICATION AND SESSION SCOPE

4.1 Where Part III adjudicated whether a domain may be reached at all, this Part adjudicates a categorically stricter question: whether a specific authenticated session may be used to reach a specific resource within a domain already cleared, or otherwise, at the domain-adjudication stage. The two questions are ordered deliberately — a resource behind an authentication wall on a domain this dataset has not even cleared for unauthenticated access is denied on Part III's own grounds before this Part's own logic is ever reached, since an authentication grant can never widen an access boundary a domain-level denial has already closed.

4.2 The central discipline this Part enforces, restated from Part I §1.1 in its most operationally exact form, is that an existing session's mere existence, or even its general validity, is never itself the authorization this Part requires. What this Part actually adjudicates is scope: does the specific access a mission needs fall within what the account or session holder actually, demonstrably granted, or does it reach beyond that grant into territory the session happens to be technically capable of reaching but was never actually authorized to be used for. Dataset #01 Part IX §9.5 already states this rule at the constitutional level — authorization is scoped exactly as far as the account holder's own access rights extend, and no further — and this Part's entire content is the operational mechanics of checking that scope match for a real, specific request.

4.3 Signal gathering for an authentication-scoped request collects Dataset #02's own compiled authorized-authentication field and its fine-grained provenance tag, the actual, inspectable scope of whatever session the mission's working context claims — not the account holder's theoretical maximum access, but the session's own currently granted permissions — and, where available, any explicit statement from the account holder about what the session was provided for. A session claimed but not actually inspectable at signal-gathering time, its real scope genuinely unknown rather than merely unstated, is treated identically to a session that does not exist for the purpose of this Part's own adjudication; an unverifiable claim of authorization is not a weaker form of authorization, it is the absence of the specific evidence this Part's own verdict-assignment stage requires.

4.4 Conflict resolution for an authentication-scoped request applies Part II §2.3's precedence with one authentication-specific refinement: a session's own actually-inspected scope always outranks the mission's compiled authorized-authentication field, because the field is a compiler's own record of what the mission's raw request and working context suggested, while the session's actual scope is a fact about what was genuinely granted, and a fact always outranks an inference about that fact when the two are available to compare directly. Where the compiled field claims broader access than the session's own scope actually supports, this Part does not resolve the conflict in the field's favor; it resolves toward the narrower, actually-verified scope, restating Dataset #01 Part IX §9.5's own rule at the level of a concrete conflict-resolution outcome rather than only as an abstract principle.

4.5 Verdict assignment for an authentication-scoped request holds this Part's single strictest standard among every adjudication this dataset performs, and the reason is stated plainly rather than left implicit: an authorization-scope error on the domain side, per Part III, typically costs a mission an unnecessarily denied acquisition it could have completed, which is a real but recoverable cost. An authorization-scope error on the authentication side, granted too broadly, risks the specific and categorical failure Dataset #01 Part IX exists to forbid outright — access obtained beyond what was actually authorized. This asymmetry is exactly the asymmetry Dataset #02 Part IX §9.9 already names from its own compilation side, and this Part's own adjudication logic is this dataset's operational answer to that asymmetry: granted requires an affirmative, specifically scope-matched signal, never a broad or ambiguous one, and any genuine doubt about whether a specific request falls within a session's actually-verified scope resolves to escalated, never to granted on the theory that the doubt will probably resolve favorably.

4.6 A worked case distinguishes a clean grant from the far more common escalation this Part's own strictness produces. A mission's working context includes a session explicitly described, by the account holder, as read-only access to a specific project-management tool's own roadmap view, provided for exactly the research the mission's own objective describes. Signal gathering collects this explicit, scope-matched description directly. Conflict resolution finds the compiled authorized-authentication field consistent with it, since Dataset #02's own compiler, per its Part IX §9.4, recorded the working context's existing authenticated session as the field's own provenance. Verdict assignment resolves to granted, because the session's actual, explicitly stated scope matches the specific resource the mission needs, with no gap between what was granted and what is being requested.

4.7 A second worked case, changing only the target resource within the same session, demonstrates escalation arising from a scope mismatch this dataset's own signal gathering specifically exists to catch. The same session, still validly authenticated, is now the mission's only available path to a different resource within the same tool — an administrative settings page the account holder never mentioned when describing the session's own purpose, and whose accessibility through the session is technically true but was never affirmatively granted for this mission's own use. Signal gathering correctly distinguishes "the session can technically reach this page" from "the session was granted for this page," a distinction Part IX's own authentication-isolation discipline treats as this Part's single most important operational test. Verdict assignment resolves to escalated rather than granted, because the session's technical reach and its actual granted scope have diverged, and this Part's own strict standard from 4.5 requires that divergence be surfaced to an operator rather than resolved by whichever direction felt more convenient to the mission's own progress.

4.8 This Part's relationship to Part IX, which elaborates the authentication-isolation principle this Part applies operationally here, is sequential rather than duplicative: this Part performs the specific scope-comparison adjudication for a real request; Part IX states the general procedural discipline — that possession of a working credential never itself constitutes permission — that every instance of this Part's own adjudication logic is required to honor. A future implementer reading this Part alone, without Part IX's own fuller treatment, has the operational mechanics but not the complete reasoning behind why those mechanics are built this specific way rather than more permissively.

4.9 A precise limit on this Part's own scope matters as much as its substance: this Part adjudicates whether an existing session may be used for a specific request. It does not itself establish new sessions, request new credentials, or negotiate expanded access on a mission's behalf, restating Dataset #02 Part IX §9.8's own boundary from this dataset's side of the same composition point — those actions, where legitimate at all, belong to the acquisition engines themselves, most directly the future Browser Interaction Fabric, operating strictly within whatever this dataset has actually cleared, never proactively reaching beyond it on this dataset's own initiative.

4.10 A closing provision addresses a case this Part's own strictness could otherwise handle too bluntly: a session whose scope is ambiguous not because the account holder's own grant was unclear, but because the tool or platform's own permission model does not cleanly expose what a given session can and cannot reach, leaving genuine technical uncertainty about the session's actual boundary regardless of how carefully the account holder described their own intent. This dataset's own answer treats this case identically to any other genuine ambiguity — escalation, per Part II §2.4 — rather than either defaulting to a permissive grant because the ambiguity is technical rather than intentional, or defaulting to an automatic denial that would foreclose legitimate access the account holder genuinely did intend to grant; the uncertainty itself, whatever its source, is the trigger, and Part XI's operator-approval workflow is where a human, not this dataset's own automated logic, resolves what the automated signal genuinely could not.

PART IV FINAL LAW

A session's mere existence, and even its general validity, is never itself the authorization this Part requires; what this Part adjudicates is whether a specific request falls within what was actually, demonstrably granted. A session's own actually-inspected scope always outranks a compiler's own inferred field when the two are available to compare, and any genuine gap between what a session can technically reach and what it was actually granted for resolves to escalated, never to granted on the assumption the gap is probably harmless. This Part never establishes new access on a mission's behalf; it only adjudicates the use of access that already, demonstrably, exists.


PART V — ROBOTS.TXT AND SITE-POLICY CONSULTATION MECHANICS

5.1 Dataset #01 Part IX §9.4 already establishes robots.txt as the doctrine's primary and default authorization signal, binding at the constitutional level. This Part is where that constitutional status becomes an actual, operational consultation mechanism: when robots.txt is fetched, how it is cached, how its directives are parsed and matched against a specific requested path and a specific declared user agent, and what this dataset does when the file cannot be retrieved at all rather than when it is retrieved and found permissive or restrictive.

5.2 Consultation happens before, not during or after, the domain- and authentication-level adjudications Parts III and IV perform, because both of those Parts' own signal-gathering stages depend on this Part's output already being available. A mission's frontier item is never dispatched to Part III's adjudication without a completed robots.txt consultation for its target domain already on record, current per the staleness discipline Part XVII defines, and an implementation that adjudicates domain access before robots.txt consultation completes has inverted this Part's own required ordering, however tempting the reordering might seem when a robots.txt fetch is slow and a mission is otherwise ready to proceed.

5.3 The consultation itself resolves against Crawl OS's own declared user agent specifically, per Dataset #01 Part IX §9.4's own language, and against the general wildcard user-agent block where no Crawl OS-specific block exists. A robots.txt with a wildcard disallow and no Crawl OS-specific exception is read as applying to Crawl OS; a robots.txt with a Crawl OS-specific allow that overrides a broader wildcard disallow is read according to the more specific rule, following the same standard interpretation every well-behaved automated client on the open web already applies, and this dataset introduces no novel parsing convention of its own where an established one already exists and is already sufficient.

5.4 Caching this Part's own consultation results is required, not optional, both for operational efficiency and because Part III §3.4's own reliance on prior-verdict reinforcement assumes a stable robots.txt record exists to compare a new request against. A cached result carries its own retrieval timestamp and is subject to Part XVII's own re-adjudication discipline; it is never treated as permanently valid regardless of how long ago it was fetched, because a site's own robots.txt is exactly the kind of deliberate access-control decision that can, and does, change, and a stale cache that no longer reflects the target's own current posture is a correctness risk this Part's own caching mechanism must actively guard against rather than passively accumulate.

5.5 A specific and consequential case this Part must handle explicitly is the unretrievable robots.txt — a fetch attempt that times out, returns a server error, or otherwise fails to produce a parseable file, as distinct from a robots.txt that was successfully retrieved and found to contain no relevant directives. Dataset #02 Part IX §9.9's own conservative-default reasoning for authentication does not directly apply here, because a missing robots.txt is not itself a denial signal any more than it is a grant signal; this dataset's own rule treats a genuinely unretrievable robots.txt as absent signal at the robots.txt layer specifically, feeding Part III's own domain-adjudication pipeline with one fewer corroborating signal than a successfully retrieved, permissive robots.txt would have supplied, which per Part III §3.6 raises rather than lowers the evidentiary bar the remaining signals must clear before a domain-level grant is assigned.

5.6 A distinct and stricter case is a robots.txt that returns an HTTP error specifically associated with access being actively restricted — a 401 or 403 status on the robots.txt fetch itself, rather than a generic timeout or transient server failure — which this dataset treats as itself a deliberate restriction signal on the domain as a whole, since a server actively refusing even the request for its own crawling policy is a stronger, not a weaker, signal than mere silence, and this dataset's own consultation mechanics distinguish this case explicitly from the ordinary unretrievable-file case in 5.5 rather than collapsing both into an identical "unknown" treatment that would understate the second case's own actual severity.

5.7 Site-policy consultation beyond robots.txt itself — a dedicated crawling or automated-access policy page some sites maintain outside the robots.txt convention, or an API terms page specifically addressing automated consumption — is gathered as an additional signal at this same pipeline stage where one is discoverable, weighted per Part VI's own terms-aware treatment rather than per this Part's robots.txt-specific mechanics, because a prose policy page requires the interpretive handling Part VI defines, while robots.txt's own machine-readable directive syntax does not.

5.8 A worked case demonstrates this Part's own consultation mechanics resolving a genuinely common ambiguity: a target's robots.txt carries a wildcard disallow on a specific path prefix, `/private/`, with no other restriction, and the mission's own frontier item targets a path outside that prefix entirely. This Part's own consultation correctly scopes the disallow to the specific prefix it names, producing a permissive signal for the actually-requested path despite the same robots.txt containing a genuine, deliberate restriction elsewhere on the same domain — a path-level rather than domain-level reading, which this Part's own parsing mechanics are required to support precisely because a domain-level over-generalization from a path-specific directive would incorrectly deny access robots.txt never actually restricted, the identical over-restriction failure Part I §1.6 already names as one of this dataset's own three bounding failure directions.

5.9 A converse worked case demonstrates the opposite over-generalization this Part must equally guard against: a robots.txt permits a specific path explicitly while disallowing the domain's own root and every other path, and a naive consultation mechanism, finding one explicit allow, incorrectly treats the entire domain as permissive rather than scoping the allow to the specific path it actually names. This Part's own mechanics require the narrower, path-scoped reading in both directions — neither a specific restriction generalizing into a domain-wide denial, nor a specific allowance generalizing into a domain-wide grant — because robots.txt's own directive syntax is deliberately path-specific, and a consultation mechanism that flattens that specificity in either direction has introduced an error this Part exists specifically to prevent.

5.10 This Part's relationship to Dataset #04's future Fetch Runtime Fabric deserves explicit statement, since that engine's own uniform interface, per Dataset #01 Part V §5.5, is where a robots.txt-respecting fetch actually executes. This Part owns the consultation and the resulting signal this dataset's own pipeline consumes; the future Fetch Runtime Fabric owns the mechanical enforcement of that signal at the point of an actual request, per Dataset #01 Part IX §9.4's own acknowledgment that the enforcement point does not change the underlying obligation. A fetch that bypasses this Part's own consultation, reaching a target through an adapter's own default configuration without ever consulting this dataset's cached verdict, has violated this dataset's own charter regardless of whether that specific adapter happens to respect robots.txt on its own.

5.11 A closing note distinguishes this Part's own narrow mechanics from a broader claim it does not make: consulting and correctly parsing robots.txt is necessary for a sound domain-level adjudication under Part III, but it is never alone sufficient, exactly as Part III §3.2 through §3.4 already established across three distinct input categories rather than one. A permissive robots.txt does not itself constitute a grant; it constitutes one input, however often the decisive one, into a pipeline that Part II's own four stages still govern in full, and this Part's own mechanics exist entirely inside that pipeline's signal-gathering stage, never as a shortcut around the remaining three.

PART V FINAL LAW

Robots.txt is consulted before, not during or after, domain adjudication, resolved against Crawl OS's own declared user agent and the general wildcard block, cached with a timestamp subject to re-adjudication, and read at the specificity its own directive syntax actually supports — never generalized from a path-specific restriction into a domain-wide denial, and never generalized from a path-specific allowance into a domain-wide grant. An unretrievable robots.txt is absent signal, raising the evidentiary bar the remaining signals must clear; a robots.txt whose own retrieval is actively refused is a restriction signal in its own right, distinct from and stronger than mere absence.


PART VI — TERMS-AWARE POLICY: INTERPRETING TERMS OF SERVICE AS AN AUTHORIZATION SIGNAL

6.1 Robots.txt, Part V's own subject, is machine-readable by design and admits a parsing mechanics that is largely mechanical once the file is retrieved. A site's terms of service is not machine-readable in the same sense: it is prose, written for a human reader or a court rather than a crawler, frequently silent on automated access entirely, and where it does address automated access, its language ranges from an unambiguous prohibition stated in a single sentence to a diffuse, qualified, or contradictory set of clauses spread across a document that was never written with this dataset's own adjudication pipeline in mind. This Part exists because terms-aware policy is a genuinely different signal-gathering problem from robots.txt consultation, requiring interpretation rather than parsing, and treating the two identically would either over-mechanize a signal that resists mechanization or under-weight a signal that, when it does speak clearly, is exactly as authoritative as a robots.txt disallow.

6.2 The interpretive discipline this Part requires distinguishes three outcomes a terms-of-service review can actually produce, and no fourth outcome is permitted: explicit prohibition, where the document states in terms a reasonable reader would recognize as unambiguous that automated access, scraping, or crawling of the kind this mission would perform is not permitted; explicit permission, considerably rarer, where the document affirmatively describes automated or programmatic access as permitted, typically through a documented API or a stated automated-access policy; and silence, the most common outcome by far, where the document simply does not address automated access one way or the other. A terms document that hedges — permitting some automated access while prohibiting other kinds, or conditioning permission on a registration or agreement this mission has not obtained — resolves to explicit prohibition for any specific access this dataset cannot affirmatively confirm falls within the permitted category, per the same burden-of-proof discipline Part II §2.7 already establishes for the pipeline as a whole.

6.3 Explicit prohibition, once identified, is weighted identically to a robots.txt disallow within Part II's own conflict-resolution stage: it is a deliberate, authoritative denial signal that overrides a mission's own compiled permitted-domains field regardless of that field's provenance strength. This weighting is not a novel rule invented for this Part; it is Dataset #01 Part IX §9.3's own doctrine — a robots.txt disallow, a paywall, and an authentication wall are named there as instances of the same category of deliberate decision, and a terms-of-service prohibition addressing automated access is a fourth instance of that identical category, stated in prose rather than in robots.txt's own directive syntax, but carrying the same authoritative weight for exactly the reason the syntax it happens to be stated in is irrelevant to whether the underlying decision was deliberate.

6.4 Explicit permission, where it genuinely exists, functions as a strong corroborating signal within Part III's own domain-adjudication pipeline, but this Part requires one caution silence and prohibition do not: explicit permission is frequently scoped, conditioned, or tied to a specific access method — a documented API requiring its own registration, a rate limit distinct from what Part VII separately governs, an attribution or usage requirement this dataset's own adjudication does not itself enforce but must record as a condition attached to the grant. A terms-of-service permission read as a blanket, unconditional grant when it was actually scoped to a specific access method this mission is not using has been misread in the permissive direction, which Part I §1.6 already names as this dataset's own first bounding failure, and this Part's own interpretive discipline requires the specific scope of any explicit permission to be recorded alongside the grant itself, not collapsed into an unqualified "permitted" signal.

6.5 Silence, the modal case, is treated identically to an unretrievable robots.txt per Part V §5.5's own reasoning: it is absent signal at the terms-of-service layer specifically, neither a grant nor a denial, feeding Part III's own pipeline with one fewer corroborating signal than an explicit permission would have supplied. This treatment is deliberate rather than a default this Part fell into for lack of a better answer: a terms document's silence on automated access most commonly reflects that the document predates, or was never updated to address, automated consumption at all, not a considered decision that automated access is acceptable, and treating silence as tacit permission would attribute a decision to a document that never actually made one.

6.6 A worked case distinguishes explicit prohibition from a merely restrictive-sounding clause that does not actually rise to it. A target's terms of service states that "content on this site may not be reproduced, republished, or redistributed without written permission" — language addressing redistribution of the site's own published content, not the act of a mission's own acquisition or internal analysis of that content. This dataset's own interpretive discipline does not read a redistribution restriction as an automated-access prohibition; the clause governs what a mission's consumer may subsequently do with acquired content, a question entirely outside this dataset's own charter per Part I §1.4, and conflating the two would have this dataset denying access on the strength of a clause that, read precisely, says nothing at all about whether acquisition itself is permitted.

6.7 A contrasting worked case demonstrates genuine explicit prohibition correctly identified despite indirect phrasing. A target's terms state that "users agree to access this site only through the standard web interface provided, and not through automated means, bots, scripts, or scraping tools of any kind." This clause names no specific engine and uses no robots.txt syntax, but it unambiguously addresses the exact activity this dataset's own mission would perform, and this dataset's own interpretive discipline resolves it to explicit prohibition despite its prose form, because Part I §1.3's own axis — deliberate decision versus incidental obstacle — turns on what the clause actually decides, not on the specific vocabulary or file format it happens to be expressed in.

6.8 This Part's relationship to Part III's own domain-adjudication pipeline is the same relationship Part V holds to it: this Part's own output — prohibition, permission, or silence, together with any recorded scope or condition — is one signal among several Part III's own signal-gathering stage collects, never a standalone adjudication this Part performs independently of the pipeline Part II defines. A terms-of-service review conducted outside that pipeline, its finding never actually reaching Part III's own signal-gathering stage, has produced information this dataset's own adjudication cannot use, regardless of how carefully the review itself was performed.

6.9 A specific difficulty this Part must name honestly rather than paper over: a terms-of-service document, unlike robots.txt, is not always retrievable at a stable, predictable location, may require navigating a site's own footer links to locate, and may itself sit behind an interstitial this dataset's own acquisition has not yet cleared to reach. Where locating and retrieving a target's terms of service would itself require an acquisition this dataset has not yet authorized, this Part's own consultation does not attempt that acquisition; it records the terms as unretrieved, treated as silence per 6.5, rather than treating the retrieval difficulty itself as either a prohibition or a license to skip the consultation without recording that it was skipped.

6.10 A closing distinction addresses terms-of-service documents that change over time, mirroring Part V §5.4's own caching discipline for robots.txt: a terms-aware finding is cached with its own retrieval timestamp, subject to the identical re-adjudication discipline Part XVII defines, and a terms document that prohibited automated access at one review and has since been updated to permit it, or the reverse, is not a permanently fixed finding this dataset treats as settled once and never revisited — a site's own terms are exactly as capable of changing as its robots.txt, and this Part's own consultation mechanics must treat that possibility with the same seriousness Part V already applies to robots.txt's own mutability.

6.11 This Part's own interpretive discipline closes with an honest acknowledgment of its own limits: a genuinely ambiguous terms-of-service clause — one that a careful reading cannot confidently place into prohibition, permission, or silence — is not forced into one of those three categories by this dataset's own automated logic straining to resolve an ambiguity a human reader would themselves find genuinely unclear. This Part's own answer to a clause this difficult is escalation, per Part II §2.4's own ambiguity trigger, because an automated interpretation of contested legal prose is exactly the kind of judgment this dataset's own charter requires be surfaced to an operator rather than resolved by whichever automated reading happened to be more convenient.

PART VI FINAL LAW

A terms-of-service review produces exactly one of three outcomes — explicit prohibition, explicit permission, or silence — and no fourth. Explicit prohibition carries the same authoritative weight as a robots.txt disallow regardless of the prose it is expressed in, because the axis that matters is whether a deliberate decision was made, not the syntax it was stated in. Silence is absent signal, never tacit permission. Explicit permission is recorded with whatever scope or condition it actually carries, never generalized into an unconditional grant. A clause too genuinely ambiguous for confident classification is escalated, never forced into a category by strained automated reasoning.


PART VII — RATE LIMITS AS AUTHORIZATION INPUT

7.1 Dataset #01 Part IX §9.6 already establishes, at the constitutional level, that a rate limit — whether stated explicitly by a target or inferred from repeated observed throttling responses — is part of the same authorization boundary every other deliberate access-control decision belongs to, and that anti-detection capability must never be deployed to defeat one. This Part is the operational mechanics behind that constitutional statement: how a rate limit is discovered, how it is recorded as a signal this dataset's own pipeline consumes, and how this dataset's own adjudication distinguishes a rate limit from the cost-ladder pacing decisions that belong to the future Acquisition Strategy Router rather than to this dataset.

7.2 Rate-limit discovery draws on three sources, gathered at the same signal-gathering stage Part II §2.2 defines for every other input this dataset consults. An explicitly stated limit — a documented requests-per-minute ceiling in an API's own published terms, or a rate-limiting policy stated in a site's own robots.txt via a crawl-delay directive — is the strongest and most direct signal, requiring no inference at all. An observed limit — a pattern of throttling responses, typically an HTTP 429 status or an equivalent explicit rate-limit signal, recorded across prior acquisitions against the same target — is a corroborating signal this dataset's own site memory, maintained per Dataset #01 Part IV §4.3's own memory architecture, accumulates over successive missions rather than rediscovering fresh each time. An inferred limit, the weakest and most provisional category, arises when a target exhibits degraded response behavior under sustained request volume without an explicit throttling signal, and this dataset's own adjudication treats an inferred limit as advisory input to the acquisition engines' own pacing decisions rather than as a hard authorization boundary this dataset itself enforces the same way it enforces an explicit or observed limit.

7.3 This Part's own boundary against the future Acquisition Strategy Router's territory must be stated precisely, because the two systems both concern themselves with request pacing and the distinction between them is easy to blur without a clear test. This dataset owns the authorization question: is a given request rate within what the target has actually, deliberately permitted, whether stated or reliably inferred from the target's own observed behavior. The Acquisition Strategy Router owns the operational question: given an authorized rate ceiling, how should a mission's own acquisition schedule actually pace its requests to stay comfortably within it, which is a scheduling and cost-ladder decision entirely outside this dataset's own charter. This dataset supplies the ceiling as an authorization signal; it does not itself schedule a single request.

7.4 A rate limit's role within Part II's own conflict-resolution stage mirrors robots.txt and terms-of-service prohibition in kind but differs in one operational respect worth naming: unlike a domain- or authentication-level denial, which typically resolves to a binary granted-or-denied verdict for the target as a whole, a rate limit's own denial condition is conditional on actual, observed request volume rather than on the target's mere identity. This dataset's own verdict for a rate-limited target is therefore not simply granted or denied in the abstract; it is granted subject to the specific ceiling this Part's own discovery mechanics established, and a mission's own subsequent acquisition against that target that exceeds the recorded ceiling has violated this dataset's own authorization regardless of whether the original verdict was, at the time it was issued, correctly granted.

7.5 A specific failure this Part exists to prevent, distinct from and complementary to Dataset #01 Part IX §9.6's own constitutional prohibition on defeating a rate limit through anti-detection capability, is rate-limit laundering across missions: a single target's rate limit is genuinely respected within any one mission's own request pacing, while multiple concurrent or sequential missions, each individually compliant, collectively exceed what the target actually authorized because no single mission's own view of its own request volume accounts for what other missions against the same target are doing simultaneously. This dataset's own rate-limit signal is recorded per-target, not per-mission, specifically so a future cross-mission consistency check, elaborated fully in Part XXI, can catch this exact failure before it accumulates into a violation no individual mission's own logs would reveal.

7.6 A worked case distinguishes an explicit rate limit from the weaker inferred category. A target's own API documentation states a ceiling of sixty requests per minute per client, addressed directly to automated consumers of that API. Signal gathering records this as an explicit limit, the strongest category 7.2 defines. This dataset's own verdict for acquisitions against this target is granted, subject to the stated sixty-per-minute ceiling, and that ceiling is handed to the Acquisition Strategy Router as a hard authorization boundary the router's own pacing logic must never exceed, distinct from and independent of whatever cost-ladder pacing the router might otherwise have chosen for reasons of its own operational efficiency.

7.7 A contrasting worked case demonstrates the inferred category's own weaker, advisory treatment. A target with no explicit rate-limit statement anywhere in its documentation or robots.txt begins returning materially slower response times after a mission's own frontier engine has issued several dozen requests in quick succession, with no explicit throttling status code ever actually returned. Signal gathering records this as an inferred limit per 7.2's own weakest category. This dataset's own verdict remains granted for the domain itself, per whatever Part III adjudication already applies, with the inferred degradation recorded as advisory pacing input for the Acquisition Strategy Router rather than as a hard ceiling this dataset itself would deny future acquisitions against for exceeding, since an inferred pattern, unlike an explicit or repeatedly-observed-throttling signal, has not yet risen to the level of a confirmed deliberate access-control decision this dataset's own doctrine requires before treating it as authoritative.

7.8 This Part's relationship to Dataset #01's own cost ladder, elaborated fully in that dataset's Part VI, deserves one further clarification beyond 7.3's own boundary statement: a rate limit this dataset records is not itself a cost-ladder rung, and a mission throttled by a genuine rate limit is not thereby required to escalate to a more expensive acquisition method the way Part VI's own cost ladder would have a mission escalate past an insufficient cheap rung. Respecting a rate limit is a pacing adjustment within whatever rung the router has already selected, never a trigger for selecting a different, costlier rung, and conflating the two would have this dataset's own authorization signal quietly driving cost decisions that belong entirely to a different engine's own charter.

7.9 A closing provision addresses the staleness every discovered rate limit is subject to, mirroring Parts V and VI's own treatment of robots.txt and terms-of-service findings: a recorded rate limit, whether explicit, observed, or inferred, carries its own discovery timestamp and is subject to Part XVII's own re-adjudication discipline, because a target's own stated or actual capacity to absorb automated request volume changes over time exactly as its robots.txt and terms of service do, and a rate-limit signal treated as permanently fixed risks either under-pacing a target whose capacity has genuinely grown, needlessly foreclosing legitimate throughput, or over-pacing one whose capacity has shrunk, risking exactly the rate-limit violation this Part exists to prevent.

PART VII FINAL LAW

A rate limit, stated explicitly, reliably observed, or merely inferred, is an authorization input this dataset records per target, not per mission, specifically so no single mission's own apparent compliance can mask a cross-mission violation no individual mission's own logs would reveal. This dataset supplies the ceiling; it never itself schedules a request or selects a cost-ladder rung — those decisions belong entirely to the Acquisition Strategy Router, operating within whatever ceiling this dataset has actually authorized. An inferred limit is advisory pacing input, not a hard denial boundary, until it rises to an explicit or repeatedly confirmed signal.


PART VIII — THE CONSENT FRAMEWORK

8.1 Every prior Part in this dataset has adjudicated access against a signal originating from the target itself — a domain's own robots.txt, a session's own granted scope, a target's own terms or rate limit. This Part addresses a different and, in one specific respect, more consequential source of authorization: consent given not by the target being acquired from, but by the party on whose behalf a mission operates — the Owner, or whatever human or system this family's own doctrine treats as authorized to grant access on the estate's own side of a given acquisition. Consent, in this dataset's own vocabulary, is the authorization signal a mission's own principal supplies; every other Part in this dataset addresses the authorization signal a target supplies, and a complete adjudication, per Part XIV's own composition, requires both to be satisfied, never either alone.

8.2 The distinction this Part draws matters because the two forms of authorization answer genuinely different questions, and collapsing them produces two distinct, equally serious failures. A target's own permissive signal — a permissive robots.txt, silent terms, no rate-limit restriction — answers whether the target is willing to be acquired from. It says nothing about whether the mission's own principal actually wants, or has actually authorized, this specific acquisition to occur at all. Conversely, a principal's own consent to a mission's general objective — "research this market," "read this repository" — does not itself establish that every specific target the mission's own frontier subsequently surfaces was individually contemplated or approved, and this Part's own framework exists specifically to determine how far a general consent actually extends before a specific acquisition requires its own, more granular authorization.

8.3 The framework's central mechanism is a scope test applied to every specific target a mission's frontier surfaces: does this specific acquisition fall within what the mission's own principal would reasonably understand their own consent, given at mission compilation, to already cover. A target squarely within the mission's own stated objective and expected sources, per Dataset #02's own compiled fields, is treated as covered by the mission's own general consent without requiring a fresh, per-target confirmation — a mission explicitly authorized to research a named competitor's public documentation does not require separate consent for each individual page within that documentation, because the principal's own original consent, reasonably read, already extends that far.

8.4 A target genuinely outside what the mission's own principal would reasonably have understood their consent to cover — an authenticated session being used for a resource the principal never specifically discussed, per Part IV's own worked escalation case in §4.7, or a domain reached only through the frontier engine's own broad exploratory expansion rather than anything the original request specifically named — does not inherit the mission's own general consent automatically. This Part's own framework treats this divergence as its own distinct trigger for Part XI's operator-approval workflow, related to but not identical with Part III §3.1's own domain-scope-divergence trigger: that Part's own trigger concerns whether a target is authorized by the target itself; this Part's own trigger concerns whether the mission's own principal actually consented to this specific acquisition occurring, a question a fully permissive target-side signal does nothing to answer on its own.

8.5 A worked case distinguishes covered from uncovered consent scope. A mission's own contract states the objective "compare pricing across these three named competitors," with the three competitors' own domains explicitly listed in the mission's compiled permitted-domains field. A specific pricing page on one of the three named domains falls squarely within the principal's own reasonably understood consent — the mission was explicitly authorized to acquire pricing data from exactly this competitor — and this Part's own framework treats it as covered without requiring separate confirmation, provided the target-side signals Parts III through VII already independently clear it.

8.6 A contrasting worked case demonstrates a target the same mission's own consent does not automatically extend to. The frontier engine, during acquisition, discovers that one of the three named competitors maintains a fourth, undisclosed pricing tier accessible only through a partner-only authenticated portal the mission's own working context happens to hold credentials for, inherited from an unrelated prior engagement rather than granted for this specific mission. Even though the domain itself is one of the three explicitly named, and even though a technically usable credential exists, this Part's own framework treats the specific act of using an unrelated credential to reach an undisclosed partner tier as falling outside what the principal's own original consent — "compare pricing across these three named competitors" using the mission's own established access — would reasonably be understood to cover, and routes it to escalation rather than treating the domain-level consent as extending automatically to every resource the domain happens to serve.

8.7 This Part's relationship to Part IV's own authentication-isolation discipline is close but distinct, and the distinction is worth stating precisely: Part IV asks whether a specific session's own granted scope covers a specific resource, a question about what the session's own grantor — typically a third party, such as the target site's own account system — actually authorized. This Part asks a different question about the same fact pattern: whether the mission's own principal, the party this dataset ultimately answers to, actually consented to that access being used for this mission's own purposes. A session can be entirely within its own granted scope, per Part IV's own adjudication, while its use for a specific mission still falls outside that mission's own principal's actual consent, exactly as 8.6's own worked case demonstrates — the two questions are cumulative, not substitutes for each other.

8.8 A precise limit on this Part's own scope prevents it from expanding into a general consent-management system for the estate as a whole: this Part's consent framework applies exclusively to a mission's own principal's authorization for that specific mission's own acquisition activity. It does not govern consent in any broader sense — data-subject consent under a privacy regime, a third party's own consent to being discussed in acquired content, or any consent question unrelated to whether a Crawl OS mission's own principal actually authorized the specific acquisition a mission's frontier is about to perform. A proposal asking this framework to adjudicate a broader consent question has exceeded this Part's own charter in the same way Part I §1.4 already forecloses for the dataset as a whole.

8.9 The framework's default posture, where a target's scope relative to the mission's own consent is genuinely unclear rather than clearly covered or clearly divergent, mirrors this dataset's governing rule throughout: escalation, never an assumption in either direction. A principal's own general consent to a mission's objective is not read maximally, to cover anything even loosely related, nor minimally, to require fresh confirmation for every individual acquisition regardless of how squarely it falls within the stated objective; it is read at the specific, reasonable scope 8.3 through 8.6 actually illustrate, and a case this framework cannot confidently place is surfaced rather than resolved by whichever reading felt more convenient to the mission's own progress.

8.10 A worked failure this Part specifically anticipates names the failure mode most likely to erode this framework's own discipline under delivery pressure: consent inflation by convenience, in which a mission's own frontier engine, encountering a target genuinely outside the original consent's own reasonable scope, treats the target as covered anyway because pausing for operator approval would slow the mission down and the target seems, on its face, harmless. This Part treats this failure with the same severity Part I §1.6 already assigns to excessive permissiveness generally: a scope judgment made for the sake of the mission's own momentum, rather than on the strength of what the principal actually consented to, is exactly the authorization-by-capability failure restated in this Part's own specific vocabulary.

8.11 This framework's closing discipline restates its own governing test in the form every future adjudication should be checked against: would the mission's own principal, informed honestly of the specific target and the specific access it requires, recognize this acquisition as something they already, reasonably, authorized when the mission was first compiled — or would they be surprised by it. A target that would surprise the principal is, by this framework's own definition, outside the mission's own consent scope, regardless of how plausible a case could be constructed after the fact for why it should have been covered.

PART VIII FINAL LAW

Target-side authorization and principal-side consent answer different questions, and both must be satisfied — a fully permissive target does not itself establish that the mission's own principal actually consented to this specific acquisition, and a principal's own general consent to a mission's objective does not automatically extend to every target the frontier engine subsequently surfaces. A target squarely within the mission's own stated objective is covered without fresh confirmation; a target that would surprise the principal, informed honestly of what it actually involves, is not, regardless of how plausible a retroactive justification could be constructed. A scope judgment made for the sake of the mission's own momentum rather than the principal's actual consent is the authorization-by-capability failure wearing this framework's own vocabulary.


PART IX — AUTHENTICATION ISOLATION: POSSESSION IS NOT PERMISSION

9.1 Part IV already applied this Part's own central principle operationally, to the specific question of whether a session's technical reach matches its actual granted scope. This Part states the principle itself, in its general and procedural form, because the principle governs more than the single adjudication Part IV performs with it, and a principle stated only implicitly, embedded inside one Part's own worked examples, is easy for a future implementer to apply narrowly where it should apply broadly.

9.2 The principle, stated plainly: possession of a working credential, a valid session, or any technically functional means of authenticated access is never itself the authorization to use that access for a given purpose. Authorization is a fact about what was granted, by whom, and for what; possession is a fact about what currently works. The two facts frequently coincide — most legitimately granted access is also, unremarkably, technically functional — but their coincidence is not identity, and this Part exists because a system that quietly treats the two as identical will, under exactly the kind of delivery pressure Part I §1.6 already names, eventually reach for access it possesses rather than access it was actually granted.

9.3 This Part's own procedural discipline requires that authorization decisions and credential mechanics be kept structurally separate, never resolved by the same step in this dataset's own pipeline. Part II's own conflict-resolution stage adjudicates whether an access is authorized; it does so without ever itself inspecting, transmitting, or manipulating the credential that access depends on, because those are Dataset #01 Part X's own owned mechanics, and this dataset's own adjudication is complete the moment a verdict is produced, independent of whatever credential-handling machinery the acquisition engines subsequently invoke to actually exercise a granted access.

9.4 A specific and easily overlooked instance of this principle concerns credentials this dataset's own mission encounters incidentally rather than through a deliberately granted session — Dataset #01 Part X §10.5's own worked case of a secret a target site itself has inadvertently exposed. That dataset's own doctrine already forbids using a discovered secret to extend acquisition; this dataset's own authentication-isolation discipline restates the identical prohibition from the authorization side of the same composition point: a discovered credential's mere functional validity, however genuinely it would work if used, is categorically not authorization, and this dataset's own adjudication treats any request to use a discovered rather than deliberately granted credential as an automatic denial, never as a borderline case requiring escalation, because no signal-gathering stage could ever produce an affirmative grant signal for access nobody ever actually authorized in the first place.

9.5 A second instance concerns a session genuinely granted for one mission being reused, without fresh adjudication, for a different mission that happens to share working context with the first. A session's own grant is scoped not only to a specific resource, per Part IV's own adjudication, but to the specific mission and purpose it was actually provided for; a mission that reaches for a session another mission was granted, on the reasoning that the credential is technically present and functional in the shared working context, has committed the identical possession-is-not-permission error this Part exists to name, and this dataset's own signal-gathering stage, per Part II §2.3, requires the session's own actual grant to be checked against the specific mission currently requesting it, not merely against whether some mission was once authorized to use it.

9.6 A worked case demonstrates this Part's own discipline resolving a scenario that could otherwise look, superficially, like a legitimate reuse. An earlier mission was explicitly granted a read-only session against a specific analytics dashboard, for the specific purpose of a quarterly performance review. A later, unrelated mission, operating in the same working context and discovering the still-valid session credential available, proposes reusing it to extract a different data view from the same dashboard for a different objective. This Part's own adjudication denies the reuse outright: the session's own grant was scoped to a specific prior mission and a specific prior purpose, and the later mission's own technical ability to use the still-functional credential does not establish that its own, different purpose was ever actually authorized, regardless of how minor the difference in objective might seem.

9.7 A converse case demonstrates that this Part's own discipline is not indiscriminately restrictive toward every form of credential reuse. Where a principal has explicitly granted a session for standing, ongoing use across a class of missions rather than a single one — "use this read-only reporting account for any future research into our own quarterly metrics" — a later mission whose objective genuinely falls within that explicitly stated standing grant is authorized, because the grant itself, examined honestly, already covers it; this Part's own discipline requires that the grant's actual scope be checked, not that every reuse be treated as automatically suspect regardless of what was actually granted. The distinction from 9.6 is not reuse itself, but whether the specific reuse falls within what was actually, demonstrably authorized.

9.8 This Part's own relationship to Part VIII's consent framework deserves explicit statement, since both concern authorization beyond a target's own signal, but from different sides: Part VIII asks whether a mission's own principal consented to a specific acquisition; this Part asks whether a specific use of a specific credential was actually granted, independent of who granted it. A session reuse can fail this Part's own test — the specific use was never granted — even where the mission's own principal would have been entirely comfortable with it had they been asked, because this Part's own discipline concerns what was actually authorized, not what would hypothetically have been approved if a different process had been followed.

9.9 A failure this Part specifically names, distinct from but related to the authorization-by-capability failure Dataset #01 Part XXII already catalogs at the family-wide level, is credential-scope drift: an initially narrow, correctly scoped grant that expands over successive missions not through any single dramatic overreach but through a sequence of individually plausible-seeming reuses, each slightly broader than the last, none of which this dataset's own adjudication caught because each individual step seemed close enough to the previous, already-authorized one. This Part's own defense against drift is structural rather than relying on any single adjudication catching it: every credential use is checked against its own grant's actual, current scope at the time of the specific request, per 9.3's own procedural separation, never against the precedent of a prior request this dataset itself already approved, because a chain of individually-approved small expansions is exactly the mechanism through which drift compounds undetected.

9.10 This Part's closing provision addresses the operational discipline that makes 9.3's own procedural separation actually enforceable rather than merely aspirational: this dataset's own authorization verdict never itself carries, references, or requires access to the credential material a granted session depends on. A verdict is a decision about a scope, expressed independently of the mechanism that scope is exercised through, and an authorization record that somehow required credential material to be inspected in order to be produced would have violated Dataset #01 Part X's own no-secrets doctrine at the exact composition boundary Part XVIII elaborates fully — this Part's own procedural isolation and that dataset's own secrets doctrine are two sides of the identical discipline, examined here from the authorization-decision side rather than the credential-storage side.

PART IX FINAL LAW

Possession of a working credential is never itself the authorization to use it; authorization is a fact about what was granted, by whom, and for what, and possession is only a fact about what currently works. A discovered credential is an automatic denial, never a borderline case. A session's grant is scoped to the specific mission and purpose it was actually provided for, and a later mission's technical ability to reach a still-functional credential does not establish that its own, different purpose was ever authorized — unless the original grant, examined honestly, already stood for exactly that broader, standing use. Credential-scope drift compounds through individually plausible small expansions, which is why every use is checked against its own grant's current scope, never against the precedent of a prior approval.


PART X — THE RESTRICTED-ACTIONS TAXONOMY

10.1 Every Part before this one has adjudicated access — whether a mission may reach a target, a domain, or a resource at all. This Part addresses a categorically different question that arises only once access has already been authorized: whether a specific action a mission proposes to take, once it has reached an authorized target, requires its own separate authorization beyond mere access, because the action itself carries consequences access alone does not. A mission fully authorized to reach a target's own web application is not, by that authorization alone, thereby authorized to submit a form on that application that triggers an irreversible real-world effect, and this Part exists to name, precisely, which classes of action carry that additional requirement.

10.2 The taxonomy this Part defines is closed, in the same sense Dataset #01 Part XIX's own seven-verdict ontology is closed: exactly three classes of restricted action exist, and an action falling outside all three requires no separate authorization beyond whatever access adjudication Parts III through IX already performed for the target it occurs on. The first class is form submission with side effects — any interaction that transmits data to a target in a way that changes the target's own recorded state, as distinct from a form submission that merely retrieves a filtered or paginated view of already-public content. The second class is purchase-like or state-changing actions — any interaction that commits resources, whether financial or otherwise, or that alters a persistent record the target maintains on the mission's or its principal's behalf. The third class is irreversible actions — any interaction whose effect cannot be undone by a subsequent, equally automated action, regardless of whether that specific interaction would otherwise fall under the first two classes.

10.3 A precise boundary separates a restricted action from an ordinary, unrestricted one within the same broad category of "interacting with a page," and the boundary is consequence, not mechanism. A search form submitted to filter a target's own already-public catalog by keyword is not a restricted action under this Part's own first class, because it transmits data but produces no change to the target's own recorded state — the target's own catalog is identical after the search as before it. A login form submitted using a legitimately granted, in-scope credential is likewise not restricted under this Part's own taxonomy purely by virtue of being a form submission, provided the login itself does not itself trigger a state change beyond establishing the session Part IV already separately adjudicated. The taxonomy is triggered by what an action does to the target's own state or the mission's own committed resources, never by the superficial mechanism — a click, a form post, a button — through which the action happens to be performed.

10.4 A worked case distinguishes an unrestricted interaction from a first-class restricted one on a single target. A mission researching a vendor's product catalog submits the catalog's own filter form to narrow results by category — unrestricted, since the target's own state is unchanged and the interaction only refines what is retrieved. The same mission, pursuing a different objective, encounters a "request a quote" form on the same catalog page, whose submission would transmit the mission's or its principal's own contact information to the vendor and trigger a follow-up sales process on the vendor's own side. This second interaction falls squarely within the first restricted class: it transmits data in a way that changes the target's own recorded state — a new lead is created in the vendor's own system — regardless of whether any payment or irreversible commitment is also involved, and this Part's own taxonomy requires operator approval before this second interaction is performed, even though both interactions occur on the identical target this dataset has already, separately, authorized for access.

10.5 A worked case distinguishes the second and third classes from each other, since a single action frequently belongs to both simultaneously without the taxonomy requiring them to be mutually exclusive. A mission authorized to research a subscription service's own pricing encounters a "start free trial" action that would both commit a payment method on file, should one exist in the working context, and create an account the target's own system may not offer any automated means of subsequently deleting. This single action falls within both the second class, as a state-changing and potentially resource-committing action, and the third class, as an action whose effect — an account of record, possibly a billing commitment — cannot be cleanly undone by a subsequent automated step. This Part's own taxonomy does not require distinguishing which class governs; membership in either class alone is sufficient to trigger the identical operator-approval requirement Part XI defines, and an action belonging to both classes is not thereby doubly restricted, only unambiguously restricted.

10.6 This Part's relationship to Dataset #01's own seventh cost-ladder rung, authorized human intervention per that dataset's Part VI §6.6, must be distinguished precisely, because the two concepts both involve a human and are easy to conflate. The seventh rung is a routing decision the Acquisition Strategy Router makes when no automated acquisition method can plausibly reach a target at all — a question about whether automation can succeed. This Part's own restricted-actions taxonomy is triggered regardless of whether automation could technically succeed at performing the action; a mission's own automated tooling may be entirely capable, technically, of submitting a purchase form, and this Part still requires operator approval before it does so, because the trigger here is the consequence of the action, not the technical difficulty of performing it. Part XII elaborates the seventh rung's own relationship to this Part's operator-approval mechanism in full; this Part states only that the two triggers are independent of each other.

10.7 A failure this Part specifically guards against, related to but distinct from Part VIII's consent-inflation-by-convenience failure, is restricted-action reclassification: a mission's own frontier or extraction engine, encountering an interaction that plausibly falls within one of this Part's three classes, characterizes it in terms that make it appear to fall outside all three — describing a lead-generating form submission as "retrieving additional catalog detail," for instance — specifically to avoid the friction of the operator-approval workflow. This Part treats a mischaracterization of this kind with the same severity Part IX §9.9 treats credential-scope drift: the taxonomy's own three classes are defined by an action's actual consequence, and a description crafted to avoid triggering the taxonomy, rather than to accurately characterize what the action does, has not evaded this Part's own requirement, it has violated it more seriously than an honest, correctly-classified restricted action ever could.

10.8 A closing clarification prevents this Part's own taxonomy from being read more broadly than it is intended: an action that merely appears superficially similar to a restricted class, without actually triggering the consequence that class is defined by, is not restricted merely out of caution. A read receipt, an analytics beacon fired by simply loading a page, or a session's own automatic activity-timestamp update are none of them state changes this Part's own first class is concerned with, because they are incidental byproducts of ordinary access this dataset has already authorized under Parts III and IV, not a mission's own deliberate transmission of data intended to change the target's own recorded state. Over-restricting every incidental interaction as though it required separate approval would produce exactly the excessive-restriction failure Part I §1.6 already names as this dataset's own second bounding failure, defeating this Part's own purpose as surely as under-restricting would.

PART X FINAL LAW

Three classes of restricted action, closed: form submission with side effects, purchase-like or state-changing actions, and irreversible actions. Membership is determined by an action's actual consequence to a target's own recorded state or a mission's own committed resources, never by the mechanism — a click, a form post — through which the action happens to be performed, and never by how the action is described rather than what it actually does. An action belonging to more than one class is not doubly restricted, only unambiguously restricted. An incidental byproduct of already-authorized access, carrying no deliberate state-changing intent, is not restricted merely out of caution; over-restriction defeats this taxonomy's purpose as surely as under-restriction does.


PART XI — THE OPERATOR-APPROVAL WORKFLOW

11.1 Every prior Part in this dataset has named escalation as an outcome — the third of Part II's three verdicts, the required resolution for a genuinely ambiguous signal, the mandatory routing for a restricted action, the correct handling of a consent divergence. This Part is where escalated stops being a bare word other Parts invoke and becomes an actual, specified process: who receives an escalation, what they are shown, what they may decide, and what happens to a mission while its own escalation is pending.

11.2 An escalation, at the moment this dataset's own pipeline produces it, is not a passive state a mission simply waits in indefinitely. It is a specific, structured request directed to an operator — the Owner, or whatever role this family's own broader doctrine designates as authorized to make the judgment this dataset's own automated logic could not — carrying the target, the mission, the specific signal or signals conflict resolution found genuinely ambiguous or genuinely divergent, and the specific question the operator is actually being asked to resolve. An escalation that reaches an operator as a bare "authorize this?" without the underlying signal detail Part II §2.5's own record-keeping stage already captured has been degraded in transit, forcing the operator to either investigate independently or decide on less information than this dataset's own pipeline actually possessed.

11.3 The operator's own decision space is deliberately narrow, mirroring Part II's own closed three-verdict ontology rather than inventing a fourth option specific to this workflow: an operator resolves an escalation to granted, denied, or a scoped variant of granted that narrows the original request to something less than what was asked but more than an outright denial — for instance, authorizing access to a specific resource within a domain while declining a broader grant the original escalation's own scope had proposed. A scoped grant is recorded, per Part XV's own audit-trail discipline, as its own distinct outcome, not silently collapsed into an ordinary grant that would misstate, to any future reader of the record, how much access was actually authorized.

11.4 A mission's own behavior while an escalation is pending is governed by a single rule stated plainly here because its absence would otherwise be filled by whatever default an implementer found most convenient: the specific target or action under escalation is blocked, and blocked only that target or action, not the mission as a whole. A mission with three frontier items, one of which triggers escalation, continues its authorized acquisition against the other two while the third awaits an operator's own decision, because Part I §1.6's own second bounding failure — excessive restriction — would otherwise be triggered by treating one item's genuine ambiguity as grounds to halt work this dataset has already, separately, cleared.

11.5 A time dimension this Part must address honestly: an escalation is not guaranteed to resolve within any bounded window, because it depends on an operator's own availability, and this dataset's own workflow does not invent an automatic-approval or automatic-denial timeout that would resolve a pending escalation without an actual human decision ever having been made. An escalation that has waited long enough to materially affect a mission's own ability to complete its objective is surfaced as a distinct finding to the mission's own reporting layer — not silently absorbed into the mission's final report as though the item had simply never existed — so a principal reviewing an incomplete mission's own results can see that a specific, named item is still pending a decision rather than having been silently dropped.

11.6 This Part's relationship to Dataset #01's own verdict ontology, elaborated fully in that dataset's Part XIX, resolves a specific composition question worth stating precisely: an acquisition whose authorization is still pending escalation, at the moment a mission's own reporting is generated, is reported using that ontology's own ESCALATED verdict, per Part XIX §19.7's own definition of a suspended rather than final state, and this dataset's own operator-approval workflow is the specific mechanism through which an ESCALATED acquisition eventually resolves to one of the ontology's other, final verdicts — never a state this dataset's own workflow leaves permanently unresolved without at minimum the visibility 11.5 requires.

11.7 A worked case demonstrates the full workflow across a genuinely realistic escalation. A mission's frontier item triggers escalation per Part IV §4.7's own worked scope-mismatch case — a session technically capable of reaching an administrative page it was never specifically granted for. The escalation this Part's own workflow produces names the target page, the session's own actually-verified scope per Part IV's signal gathering, the specific gap between that scope and the requested access, and the question: does this mission's own principal wish to expand the session's authorized use to include this page, or should the acquisition be denied. An operator, reviewing this record, recognizes the administrative page as genuinely outside the session's own intended use and resolves the escalation to denied. The mission's own report records the denial, its stated basis, and continues its otherwise-authorized acquisition against the remainder of its own frontier.

11.8 A second worked case demonstrates a scoped-grant resolution. A mission's frontier item triggers escalation per Part VIII §8.6's own worked consent-divergence case — a technically usable but unrelated credential reaching an undisclosed partner tier. An operator, reviewing the escalation, recognizes that the mission's own principal would in fact welcome the additional pricing data, but only for the single specific competitor already under comparison, not as a standing grant for any future mission that might similarly stumble onto adjacent, unrelated access. The operator resolves the escalation to a scoped grant, authorizing exactly this one acquisition for this one mission, explicitly not extending to any future reuse of the same credential — precisely the kind of narrow, non-precedent-setting resolution Part IX §9.7's own standing-grant discussion distinguishes from an ordinary, broader authorization.

11.9 This Part's own relationship to Part X's restricted-actions taxonomy deserves a specific clarification, since both this Part and that one route to the identical operator-approval mechanism but for structurally different reasons: an ordinary signal-ambiguity escalation, per Part II §2.4, arises because this dataset's own automated adjudication could not confidently resolve a genuine conflict in the evidence; a restricted-action escalation arises even where the underlying access is entirely unambiguous, because Part X's own taxonomy requires human sign-off on the action's consequence regardless of how clearly authorized the underlying access itself is. This Part's own workflow handles both identically once they reach it — the same structured request, the same closed decision space, the same recording discipline — but the escalation record itself distinguishes which trigger produced it, since the two carry different implications for what a reviewing operator actually needs to evaluate.

11.10 A failure this Part specifically guards against is operator fatigue collapsing into rubber-stamping: an escalation workflow that surfaces too many low-stakes, easily-resolved ambiguities alongside genuinely consequential ones trains whoever reviews escalations to approve quickly without genuinely evaluating each one, which quietly defeats this Part's own purpose without any single decision looking wrong in isolation. This Part's own defense is upstream rather than procedural: Parts III through X are themselves built to escalate only genuine ambiguity and genuine divergence, per each Part's own explicit worked contrast between a case that resolves automatically and one that does not, specifically so this Part's own workflow receives a signal that is actually worth an operator's own attention rather than a flood of cases a better-tuned upstream adjudication would have resolved cleanly on its own.

11.11 A closing provision addresses revocation, the operator's own power to withdraw a previously granted authorization, which this Part treats as structurally identical to an initial denial rather than as a separate mechanism requiring its own vocabulary: an operator may, at any time, revoke a standing or scoped grant this workflow previously issued, and a revocation takes effect for any acquisition not already completed at the moment it is issued, recorded per Part XV's own audit trail with its own stated reason, and propagated to Part XVII's own re-adjudication discipline so that any future request against the same target re-enters this dataset's own pipeline rather than relying on a grant that has since been withdrawn.

PART XI FINAL LAW

An escalation is a structured request carrying the target, the mission, and the specific signal conflict resolution found genuinely ambiguous — never a bare yes-or-no stripped of the evidence that produced it. An operator's own decision space is granted, denied, or a scoped grant recorded as its own distinct outcome; no automatic timeout resolves a pending escalation without an actual decision. Only the specific target or action under escalation is blocked, never the mission as a whole, and a long-pending escalation is surfaced as a visible, named finding rather than silently absorbed into an incomplete report. An operator may revoke a standing grant at any time, treated identically to an initial denial for any acquisition not already completed.


PART XII — ESCALATION TO THE SEVENTH RUNG: AUTHORIZING HUMAN INTERVENTION

12.1 Dataset #01 Part VI §6.6 names the cost ladder's seventh and final rung — authorized human intervention — and states plainly that it is reserved for cases where every automated method has been exhausted or is inappropriate given the authorization boundary Part IX of that dataset defines. That dataset's own charter for the Acquisition Strategy Router, per its Part V §5.4, governs when the router selects the seventh rung as a routing decision. This Part governs a distinct and prior question that dataset deliberately left to this dataset: whether the human intervention the router wishes to invoke is actually authorized to occur at all, for this specific target and this specific mission.

12.2 The distinction Part X §10.6 already drew between a routing decision and this dataset's own consequence-triggered authorization applies here in its clearest form: the router's own selection of the seventh rung answers "can nothing automated reach this target." This Part's own authorization answers a categorically different question — "should a human, acting on this mission's own behalf, actually be asked to intervene here, and if so, what specifically are they being asked to do." A router that selects the seventh rung without this Part's own authorization has made a routing decision this dataset has not yet cleared, and Part XIV's own composition with Dataset #10's Gate C treats an acquisition reaching this rung without this dataset's own prior authorization identically to any other unauthorized acquisition — void, regardless of how genuinely necessary the human intervention turned out to be.

12.3 This Part's own authorization for the seventh rung is not a rubber stamp automatically granted the moment the router requests it; it applies the identical signal-gathering, conflict-resolution, and verdict-assignment stages Part II defines for every other authorization question this dataset adjudicates, with one addition specific to this rung: the request itself must state, in terms an operator can actually evaluate, what specifically the human is being asked to do, since "authorize human intervention" without further detail is exactly the degraded, information-stripped escalation Part XI §11.2 already forbids for the ordinary escalation case, and this rung's own requests are held to the identical standard.

12.4 A worked case distinguishes a seventh-rung request this Part authorizes from one it does not. A mission's target requires navigating a multi-step, session-dependent checkout-adjacent flow purely to observe a competitor's own displayed shipping-cost calculation at a specific cart value — no purchase is completed, no account is created, and the flow itself requires human judgment only because the target's own interface defeats every automated navigation attempt the router has already tried and logged. This Part's own adjudication authorizes the seventh rung here: the specific action is named precisely, it does not itself fall within Part X's own restricted-actions taxonomy since no state change or commitment occurs, and the router's own logged automated attempts satisfy the constitutional standard that this rung is reserved for cases where automation has genuinely been exhausted.

12.5 A contrasting worked case demonstrates a seventh-rung request this Part denies outright rather than authorizes. A mission's target requires the identical multi-step flow, but reaching the specific data point the mission needs would require completing an actual purchase, which falls squarely within Part X's own second and third restricted-action classes regardless of whether a human or an automated agent performs the click. This Part's own adjudication does not authorize the seventh rung merely because a human, rather than an automated tool, would be the one performing the restricted action; Part X's own taxonomy applies identically regardless of who or what executes the action, and this Part's own request is instead routed through Part X's own operator-approval mechanism for the restricted action itself, a categorically separate and stricter authorization than mere seventh-rung human intervention for an otherwise unrestricted acquisition.

12.6 This Part's own relationship to Part XI's general operator-approval workflow is compositional rather than a wholly separate mechanism: a seventh-rung authorization request is itself a specific instance of an escalation, handled through the identical structured-request, closed-decision-space, and recording discipline Part XI already defines, distinguished from an ordinary escalation only by the specific trigger — a router's own request to invoke human intervention rather than a genuine ambiguity in an automated signal — and by the additional detail requirement 12.3 states. An implementer building a separate, parallel approval mechanism specifically for seventh-rung requests, rather than routing them through the identical workflow every other escalation already uses, has introduced an unnecessary second system for a question Part XI's own mechanism already answers.

12.7 A specific caution this Part must state explicitly addresses a subtle failure mode particular to human intervention rather than to automated acquisition: because a human performing an action can exercise judgment an automated tool cannot, there is a temptation to treat authorized human intervention as a general escape hatch around this dataset's own other Parts — using a human to reach a target robots.txt has disallowed, on the reasoning that a human, unlike an automated crawler, is not the kind of agent robots.txt addresses. This Part's own adjudication forecloses this reading explicitly: Dataset #01 Part IX §9.4's own doctrine treats a robots.txt disallow as authorization withdrawn from automated access to that content on this mission's own behalf, regardless of whether a human or a script ultimately performs the specific keystrokes, and a human intervention request targeting content this dataset has already denied at the domain or authentication level under Parts III or IV is denied here on the identical basis, never authorized merely because a human is nominally the one at the controls.

12.8 A further caution addresses the seventh rung's own relationship to Part VIII's consent framework: a router's own request to invoke human intervention, even where this Part's own authorization would otherwise grant it, still requires the mission's own principal's consent per Part VIII's own scope test, since asking an actual human to spend their own time and attention on a mission's behalf is, if anything, a more consequential imposition on the principal's own resources than a purely automated acquisition would be, and a seventh-rung request squarely within the mission's own stated objective is treated as covered by the mission's own general consent per Part VIII §8.3's own reasoning, while a seventh-rung request arising from the frontier engine's own exploratory expansion beyond that objective requires the identical fresh consideration Part VIII §8.4 already requires for any other consent-divergent target.

12.9 The recording discipline for a seventh-rung authorization mirrors Part XI §11.3's own scoped-grant treatment precisely: an authorization here is never a blanket, standing authorization for the router to invoke human intervention on this mission's behalf generally; it is scoped to the specific target and the specific action named in the request, and a router that, having received authorization for one seventh-rung intervention, treats a differently-targeted or differently-scoped subsequent intervention as covered by the same authorization has committed the identical credential-scope-drift error Part IX §9.9 already names, applied here to authorization scope rather than to a credential's own technical reach.

12.10 A closing distinction separates this Part's own narrow authorization question from the router's own separate, subsequent judgment about whether seventh-rung intervention is actually worth its own cost once authorized. This Part answers only whether the intervention is authorized to occur; whether it is actually the router's own best available option, given the cost ladder's own economics per Dataset #01 Part VI, remains entirely the router's own decision, exercised within whatever this Part has cleared, exactly as Part VII §7.3 already draws the identical boundary for rate-limit-constrained pacing decisions. This Part never tells the router which rung to select; it only tells the router which rungs, including the seventh, are actually available to select from for a given target.

PART XII FINAL LAW

Authorizing the seventh cost-ladder rung is a distinct question from the router's own decision to select it — this dataset answers whether human intervention on this mission's own behalf is authorized for this specific target and this specific action; the router alone decides whether invoking it is worth its own cost. A human performing an action does not exempt that action from Parts III, IV, or X's own adjudication; a robots.txt disallow, an unauthorized authentication scope, or a restricted action denied to automated tooling is denied identically to a human acting on the mission's behalf. Authorization here is scoped to the specific request, never a standing grant the router may extend to a differently-targeted intervention without a fresh pass through this dataset's own pipeline.


PART XIII — DATA MINIMIZATION AS A POLICY CEILING

13.1 This dataset's own charter, per its contract's ownership section, names data minimization as a policy this dataset declares, distinguished sharply from the retention and freshness mechanics Dataset #10 Part XII already owns and operates. This Part exists to make that distinction precise and operational: this dataset answers what a mission may collect and retain at all, given its consent and compliance posture; Dataset #10's own already-sealed machinery answers how whatever this dataset permits is actually stored, refreshed, and eventually superseded once collection has occurred. A ceiling and the machinery operating beneath it are not the same thing, and this Part governs only the former.

13.2 The policy ceiling this Part sets is derived from two inputs this dataset already possesses by the time it reaches this Part's own question: the mission's own required-evidence field, compiled by Dataset #02 and consumed, not re-derived, per this dataset's contract's own inputs section, and the specific authorization signals Parts III through X have already established for the mission's own targets. A mission whose required-evidence field names only a page's own existence and title carries a narrower data-minimization ceiling than a mission whose required-evidence field names full-text extraction with structural fidelity, because the ceiling this Part sets is bounded by what the mission's own contract actually needs, never by what a target's own authorization would merely permit collecting if the mission wanted more than it actually does.

13.3 This Part's own ceiling operates in one direction only, and stating this precisely prevents a natural but incorrect reading: it may narrow what a mission collects below what would otherwise be authorized under Parts III through X, but it never widens collection beyond what those Parts have actually authorized. A mission whose required-evidence field would, read expansively, justify collecting an entire site's full content, but whose actual authorization under Part III covers only a specific subdirectory, collects only what Part III's own domain-level grant actually permits — the data-minimization ceiling and the access-authorization ceiling both apply, cumulatively, and a mission's own actual collection is bounded by whichever of the two is narrower for a given target, never by the more permissive of the two.

13.4 A worked case demonstrates the ceiling narrowing collection below what access authorization alone would have permitted. A mission is authorized, per Part III's own adjudication, to acquire an entire competitor's documentation site — a broad domain-level grant with no restriction on depth or breadth. The mission's own required-evidence field, however, names only the specific API reference pages relevant to a stated integration question, not the site's own broader marketing or company-history content also present on the same domain. This Part's own ceiling limits the mission's actual collection to the API reference material the mission's own contract genuinely requires, even though Part III's own access grant would have technically permitted acquiring the marketing content too — access authorization answers what could be reached; this Part answers what should actually be collected given what the mission genuinely needs.

13.5 A specific and consequential application of this Part's own ceiling concerns personally identifying or otherwise sensitive information a mission's own acquisition might incidentally encounter while pursuing an authorized, narrower objective — an author's contact details on a documentation page the mission is legitimately acquiring for its technical content, a customer's own name appearing in a publicly visible support-forum thread the mission is legitimately researching for product-feedback patterns. PART XIV FINAL LAW

The composite verdict this Part assembles names every applicable component — domain, authentication, restricted-action, data-minimization — and Gate C passes only when every applicable component resolves to granted; an inapplicable component is recorded as absent, never as a trivial pass, and one denied or pending component fails the composite in full regardless of how many other components cleared. PART XV — THE AUTHORIZATION AUDIT TRAIL

15.1 Part II §2.5 already named record-keeping as the pipeline's own fourth, non-optional stage and stated that a verdict without a recorded basis is, for every purpose this dataset cares about, indistinguishable from a verdict never actually adjudicated. This Part is where that stage's own content is fully specified: what a record actually contains, how it is structured, how long it persists, and how it is distinguished from the Evidence Object provenance chain Dataset #01 Part VII and Dataset #10 already define for a categorically different purpose.

15.2 A single authorization-record entry contains: the target, identified precisely enough that a future request against the same target can be matched against it; the mission it was produced for; the verdict — granted, denied, escalated, or a scoped variant of either; every signal Part II §2.2's own signal-gathering stage actually collected, not merely the one or two that proved decisive; the specific conflict-resolution reasoning that produced the verdict from those signals, per Part II §2.3's own precedence; the timestamp; and, where the verdict resulted from an operator's own resolution per Part XI, the identity of the resolving operator and their own stated reasoning. An entry missing any of these fields is an incomplete record, and Part I §1.6's own third bounding failure — silence — names exactly this incompleteness as a failure in its own right, regardless of whether the verdict the incomplete record documents happened to be correct.

15.3 This trail's relationship to the Evidence Object's own provenance chain, defined in Dataset #01 Part VII §7.5 and elaborated operationally by Dataset #10, must be stated with the same precision Dataset #01 Part XVI already applies to the GK-04 composition boundary: the two records answer different questions and neither substitutes for the other. The Evidence Object's provenance chain answers where a specific piece of acquired content came from and how it was acquired. This dataset's own authorization audit trail answers why the acquisition was permitted to occur at all. An Evidence Object whose provenance chain is complete but whose corresponding authorization record does not exist, or vice versa, has a gap in exactly one of two distinct kinds of accountability this family's own constitution requires, and neither record's own completeness compensates for the other's absence.

15.4 The trail's own structure is queryable by target, by mission, and by timestamp, because each of the three access patterns serves a genuinely distinct downstream need this dataset's own consumers actually have: querying by target supports Part III §3.4's own prior-verdict reinforcement and Part XVII's own re-adjudication discipline, both of which need every historical verdict for a specific domain or resource regardless of which mission produced it; querying by mission supports a principal's own after-the-fact review of everything a specific mission was authorized or denied to do, per Part VIII's own consent-accountability concern; and querying by timestamp supports Part XXI's own cross-mission consistency check, which needs to examine every verdict issued within a specific window regardless of target or mission, to catch exactly the kind of aggregate pattern — Part VII §7.5's own rate-limit laundering, for instance — no single target- or mission-scoped query would surface on its own.

15.5 A specific discipline this trail enforces, restating Dataset #01 Part XIV §14.10's own amendment-not-deletion principle from this dataset's own side of that shared discipline, governs what happens to a record once superseded: a re-adjudicated verdict, per Part XVII, does not overwrite or delete its own predecessor; the prior record is retained, marked superseded, with a reference to the new record that replaced it, preserving this dataset's own ability to reconstruct, for any point in the past, exactly what this dataset believed was authorized at that time — an ability this Part's own trail exists specifically to guarantee, since an authorization dispute arising after the fact frequently turns on exactly this question: what did this dataset actually authorize, and on what basis, at the moment a specific acquisition occurred, not what this dataset currently believes in retrospect.

15.6 This Part's own composition with Part IX §9.10's authentication-isolation discipline deserves restating in this Part's own specific terms: an authorization-record entry never itself contains credential material, session tokens, or any content this dataset's own signal gathering encountered that would fall under Dataset #01 Part X's own redaction requirement. Where a signal this dataset consulted — a session's own scope description, for instance — might incidentally reference material subject to that redaction requirement, this Part's own record captures the scope finding itself, never the underlying credential detail that produced it, and any record produced through this trail's own mechanics passes through the identical redaction pass Dataset #01 Part X §10.4 already requires unconditionally for every Evidence Object field, applied here to this dataset's own distinct record type.

15.7 A worked case demonstrates the trail's own use resolving a genuine after-the-fact dispute. A principal, reviewing a completed mission's own results months later, questions why a specific competitor's authenticated portal was acquired from, having no memory of authorizing that specific access. Querying this trail by mission surfaces the specific authorization record: a scoped grant, per Part XI §11.8's own worked case, issued by a named operator at a specific time, with the specific reasoning that the mission's own principal would welcome the additional data for the single competitor already under comparison. The trail resolves the dispute definitively — the access was authorized, by whom, and on what specific, recorded reasoning — rather than leaving the question as an unresolvable disagreement about what was or was not originally intended.

15.8 A failure this Part specifically guards against, distinct from mere incompleteness per 15.2, is retroactive record reconstruction: an authorization record created or amended after the fact to match an acquisition that already occurred, rather than genuinely reflecting the adjudication actually performed before that acquisition was authorized. This failure is this dataset's own specific instance of the Provenance-Amnesia Failure Dataset #01 Part XXII §22.11 already names at the family-wide level for the Evidence Object's own provenance chain, and this Part treats it with identical severity: a record's own timestamp must reflect when the adjudication genuinely occurred, never be backdated or reconstructed to appear as though this dataset's own pipeline had already resolved a question it had not actually yet considered at the time the record claims.

15.9 This Part's relationship to Dataset #01's own Crawl Cortex Supervisor, whose evidence quality loop per that dataset's Part X §10.10 already scans persisted Evidence Objects for unredacted secrets on an ongoing basis, extends to this trail as well: the Supervisor's own institutional learning loop, per Dataset #01 Part V §5.12, is the mechanism through which a pattern across many of this dataset's own authorization records — a target repeatedly escalated and repeatedly denied, for instance, suggesting the mission's own frontier engine keeps proposing access it should by now know is unauthorized — is surfaced as a finding rather than left as an accumulating series of individually unremarkable records nobody ever aggregates into the pattern they actually form.

15.10 A closing provision addresses this trail's own relationship to Part XXI's cross-mission consistency check in its fullest form: this trail is the data substrate that check actually operates against, and Part XXI's own mechanics are meaningless without this Part's own discipline that every verdict, without exception, is recorded completely and honestly at the moment it is produced. PART XV FINAL LAW

A record created or amended to match an acquisition already performed, rather than genuinely reflecting a prior adjudication, is this dataset's own instance of Provenance-Amnesia and is treated with identical severity.


PART XVI — AMBIGUITY, DEFAULTS, AND THE BURDEN OF PROOF

16.1 Every prior Part has invoked this dataset's own burden-of-proof discipline in its own specific context — Part II §2.7 for the pipeline generally, Part III §3.6 for a broad domain default, Part VI §6.2 for a hedging terms clause, Part VIII §8.9 for consent scope. This Part is where that recurring discipline is stated once, completely, as this dataset's own single governing rule for every case not explicitly resolved elsewhere, so that a future Part, or a future amendment under Part XXIV, has one canonical statement to cite rather than reconstructing the rule from its scattered prior applications.

16.2 The rule, stated in its most general form: where this dataset's own signal-gathering and conflict-resolution stages produce a genuinely ambiguous result — neither a clear grant nor a clear denial resolves cleanly under Part II's own precedence — the default is never granted. It is escalated, or, where the ambiguity itself constitutes evidence of an unresolved deliberate restriction rather than a mere absence of information, denied. This is Dataset #01 Part VIII §8.7's own burden-of-proof inversion, established there for content verification, and Part IX §9.9's own extension of it to authorization generally, restated here as this dataset's own operating rule rather than as a borrowed principle applied piecemeal wherever a specific Part happened to need it.

16.3 A precise distinction this Part must draw, because it is easy to blur under the pressure of wanting a single, simple rule: absence of signal and presence of ambiguous signal are not the same condition, and this dataset's own default treats them differently. Absence of signal — an unretrievable robots.txt per Part V §5.5, a silent terms document per Part VI §6.5 — is not itself ambiguity; it is simply one fewer corroborating input, which Part III §3.6 already treats as raising the evidentiary bar the remaining signals must clear rather than as a trigger for escalation in its own right. Genuine ambiguity — a terms clause too unclear to classify per Part VI §6.11, a session scope genuinely uncertain per Part IV §4.10 — is a distinct condition in which an actual signal exists but resists confident interpretation, and it is this second condition, not mere absence, that this Part's own escalation default specifically addresses.

16.4 This distinction matters operationally because collapsing the two into a single "insufficient information, escalate" rule would produce exactly the excessive-restriction failure Part I §1.6 already names: a domain with no robots.txt, no terms of service, and no prior verdict is not thereby ambiguous — it is a domain about which this dataset has gathered no restrictive signal at all, and Part III's own graduated treatment of a broad, uncorroborated default already handles this case correctly, resolving to escalation only where the domain's own permitted-domains provenance is itself weak, not automatically merely because fewer signals happened to exist. Ambiguity is a property of a signal that was gathered and could not be confidently interpreted; it is not a property of a signal that was simply never found.

16.5 A specific and recurring form of ambiguity deserves its own named treatment because it arises differently from a single unclear signal: conflicting signals of comparable strength, where two inputs Part II's own conflict-resolution stage gathered point toward genuinely different outcomes and neither one's own specificity or authority clearly outranks the other. Part II §2.3 already resolves the common cases — a deliberate denial outranking a permissive default, an actually-inspected session scope outranking a compiled field — through a fixed precedence; this Part's own contribution is naming the residual case that precedence does not resolve: two signals of the same category and comparable specificity that simply disagree, which this dataset's own rule treats identically to any other unresolved ambiguity, escalated rather than resolved by an arbitrary tiebreaker invented at the point of conflict.

16.6 A worked case demonstrates genuine ambiguity distinguished from mere absence. A target's terms of service contains a clause this dataset's own Part VI review genuinely cannot confidently classify — language that could plausibly be read as either a general reservation of rights common to boilerplate legal prose, or a specific prohibition on automated access, depending on interpretive choices a careful human reader would themselves debate. This is not absence of signal; a signal exists and was reviewed. It is genuine ambiguity, and per Part VI §6.11's own instance of this Part's general rule, it escalates rather than being forced into prohibition or permission by strained automated reasoning.

16.7 A contrasting worked case demonstrates mere absence correctly distinguished from ambiguity. A target has no terms-of-service document discoverable at all — not a document too ambiguous to classify, but the genuine absence of any document to review in the first place. This dataset's own rule treats this as absent signal per Part VI §6.5, feeding Part III's own domain adjudication with one fewer corroborating input, never as an ambiguity requiring its own separate escalation; the two conditions look superficially similar — in both cases, this dataset does not have a clear terms-based signal to rely on — but they are structurally different, and only the first genuinely triggers this Part's own ambiguity-specific default.

16.8 This Part's own relationship to a default's own provenance, restating Dataset #02 Part II §2.6's own extracted/resolved/defaulted taxonomy from this dataset's side of that composition point, addresses a case worth naming explicitly: where this dataset's own adjudication itself falls back to a default — for instance, Part IV §4.3's own treatment of an unverifiable session claim as though no session existed — that default's own use is itself recorded in Part XV's own audit trail as a defaulted rather than a resolved determination, so a future audit of this dataset's own verdict can distinguish a verdict genuinely supported by an affirmative signal from one that fell back to this Part's own conservative default in the signal's absence, mirroring exactly the distinction Dataset #02's own provenance tagging already requires for its own compiled fields.

16.9 A failure this Part specifically names, distinct from but adjacent to the authorization-by-capability drift Part IX §9.9 already catalogs, is default normalization: a mission's own repeated encounters with the same kind of ambiguous or absent signal, resolved correctly to escalation or denial each time per this Part's own rule, gradually training an implementer's own intuition, or a future automated heuristic layered informally on top of this dataset's own pipeline, to treat that specific pattern as effectively pre-resolved, skipping the actual adjudication because "this always escalates anyway." This Part forecloses this shortcut explicitly: every instance of ambiguity or absence is adjudicated through Part II's own full pipeline regardless of how predictable its own outcome may have become through repetition, because a pattern's own predictability is not itself authorization to skip the process that established the pattern in the first place, and the one instance skipped on the assumption of predictability is exactly the instance where the underlying signal has actually changed.

16.10 This Part's own closing statement addresses the asymmetry between this dataset's own two possible errors one final time, in the specific vocabulary of burden of proof: this dataset would rather deny or escalate an access that, examined more carefully, would genuinely have been authorized, than grant an access that, examined more carefully, would genuinely have been denied. This asymmetry is not a claim that denial is costless — Part I §1.6 already names excessive restriction as a real, distinct failure with its own real cost — it is a claim that the two errors are not symmetric in severity, because a wrongly denied access can be corrected through Part XI's own operator-approval workflow or Part XXII's own revision discipline, while a wrongly granted access has already occurred by the time the error is discovered, and Dataset #01 Part IX's own doctrine treats that second error as categorically, not merely comparatively, worse.

16.11 A closing worked contrast makes this asymmetry concrete rather than abstract. A mission's frontier engine proposes two targets on the same day, both resting on comparably thin signal: one, denied conservatively, turns out on later operator review to have been genuinely authorized all along, costing the mission a delay while the denial was corrected through Part XI's own revocation-adjacent mechanism. The other, had this Part's own discipline instead defaulted to granted on comparably thin signal, would have resulted in an acquisition from a domain whose robots.txt, discovered only after the fact, in fact disallowed exactly this access — an acquisition this family's own constitution treats as void regardless of how it is later corrected, because the access already occurred. The first error costs time. The second error cannot be undone by any subsequent correction, and this Part's own default exists specifically because the two costs are not comparable.

PART XVI FINAL LAW

Genuine ambiguity and mere absence of signal are distinct conditions, and this dataset's own default treats them differently — absence raises the evidentiary bar the remaining signals must clear; ambiguity escalates or denies outright. Neither condition ever defaults to granted. A default's own use, wherever this dataset's own adjudication falls back to one, is itself recorded as a defaulted rather than a resolved determination. A pattern's own predictability through repetition is never itself authorization to skip the adjudication that established the pattern. This dataset accepts the cost of a wrongly denied access, correctable through revision, over the cost of a wrongly granted one, which cannot be undone once the acquisition has occurred.


PART XVII — RE-ADJUDICATION AND STALE-VERDICT HANDLING

17.1 Parts III through XV have each, in their own specific terms, flagged their own signals and verdicts as subject to a staleness discipline this Part now defines completely: a robots.txt cached with a timestamp per Part V §5.4, a terms-of-service finding per Part VI §6.10, a rate limit per Part VII §7.9, a composite verdict per Part XIV §14.9. This Part states, once, the actual conditions under which a prior verdict is re-adjudicated rather than simply reused, and the mechanics of what re-adjudication actually produces.

17.2 A prior verdict is re-adjudicated, rather than reused as-is, under exactly four trigger conditions, and no fifth condition is recognized without a Part XXIV amendment. The first is elapsed time beyond a recency window this dataset sets per signal category — a shorter window for a rapidly-changing signal like an observed rate limit, a longer window for a comparatively stable one like a resolved entity's own primary domain. The second is a specific, observed change in the underlying target — a robots.txt whose content hash, checked at consultation time, differs from what was previously cached; a terms-of-service document similarly changed. The third is an explicit operator action — a revocation per Part XI §11.11, or an operator's own request to revisit a previously denied target. The fourth is a new mission whose own compiled fields, per Dataset #02, differ materially from the mission the prior verdict was originally produced for, even where the target itself is identical.

17.3 The recency window named in the first trigger is not a single, dataset-wide constant; it varies by the specific signal category this Part's own earlier Parts already established, and stating the relative ordering here, without fixing exact durations this Part's own operational implementation is better positioned to calibrate, preserves the distinction Parts V through VII already draw between more and less stable signal types. Robots.txt and terms-of-service findings, both reflecting a deliberate policy decision a site operator does not typically revise frequently, carry a comparatively longer window than an observed or inferred rate limit, which reflects a target's own current operational load and can shift meaningfully over a much shorter horizon — the specific numeric windows are an implementation detail this Part's own contract-bound completion criteria do not require fixing in prose, but the relative ordering across signal categories is itself part of this dataset's own binding discipline.

17.4 The second trigger, a specific observed change, is the strongest and most decisive of the four, because it is direct evidence rather than an inference from elapsed time, and this dataset's own re-adjudication mechanics prioritize it over the first trigger wherever both would otherwise apply: a robots.txt re-consulted for an unrelated reason, within its own recency window, that happens to reveal a changed disallow directive, triggers immediate re-adjudication regardless of how much of the window remains, because a specific change actually observed is stronger evidence than a window's own mere expiration would ever provide.

17.5 A precise distinction separates re-adjudication from the ordinary pipeline Part II already defines: re-adjudication is not a fresh pass through signal gathering as though no prior verdict existed; it is a targeted re-check of specifically the signal category the triggering condition concerns, compared against the prior verdict's own recorded basis per Part XV §15.2. A rate-limit re-adjudication triggered by elapsed time re-checks the rate-limit signal specifically; it does not automatically re-run the domain-level robots.txt consultation that prior verdict also relied on, unless that signal independently also meets one of this Part's own four trigger conditions. This targeted scope keeps re-adjudication proportionate to what actually needs re-checking, rather than treating every trigger as grounds for re-running this dataset's own entire pipeline from scratch.

17.6 A worked case demonstrates targeted re-adjudication resolving a genuine change without disturbing an unrelated, still-valid prior finding. A domain granted six months ago under Part III's own adjudication, on the strength of a permissive robots.txt and an entity-resolved domain signal, is revisited by a new mission. The recency window for robots.txt consultation, per 17.3's own longer-window category, has elapsed, triggering re-consultation. The re-consultation finds the robots.txt unchanged. This Part's own mechanics record the re-check itself — per Part XV's own audit-trail discipline, a re-adjudication event is itself recorded, not merely a silent reuse of the prior verdict — and the domain-level verdict is reaffirmed rather than superseded, since 17.2's own second trigger, actual change, did not fire even though the first trigger, elapsed time, did.

17.7 A contrasting worked case demonstrates a genuine change producing a superseded verdict. A domain granted eight months ago, under a robots.txt that at the time carried no restriction on a specific documentation subpath, is revisited, and re-consultation finds the target has since added a disallow directive covering exactly that subpath, reflecting what this dataset's own signal-gathering correctly reads as the site operator's deliberate decision to restrict automated access to that specific content going forward. 17.11 This Part's closing provision names the specific operational discipline that keeps re-adjudication from becoming either too aggressive, re-checking so frequently that this dataset's own overhead becomes disproportionate to the actual rate at which target-side signals genuinely change, or too passive, allowing a stale verdict to govern acquisitions long after the conditions that produced it have shifted: the four trigger conditions in 17.2 are the complete, closed list, and a re-adjudication performed for a reason outside those four — mere curiosity, an implementer's own unease about a verdict's age absent any of the four actual triggers — is not this Part's own discipline operating correctly, it is an ad hoc deviation from a specified process that Part XXIV's own amendment discipline, not an individual implementer's own discretion, is the only legitimate channel for expanding.

PART XVII FINAL LAW

A prior verdict is re-adjudicated under exactly four conditions — elapsed recency window, an observed change in the underlying target, an explicit operator action, or a materially different new mission — and no fifth. An inconclusive re-check does not default to reaffirming a prior grant merely because a grant previously existed; a prior grant is evidence of what this dataset once believed, never evidence that the conditions producing it still hold. PART XXI — MULTI-MISSION AND CROSS-MISSION AUTHORIZATION CONSISTENCY

21.1 Every prior Part has adjudicated authorization for a single mission's own specific request against a single target. PART XXII — REVISION AND AMENDMENT OF AN AUTHORIZATION VERDICT

This Part defines a distinct mechanism for the reverse case: a mission's own contract itself changes — through Dataset #02's own revision discipline per that dataset's Part XVIII — or a dispute arises over whether a specific verdict was correctly reached in the first place, and this dataset's own prior authorization must be revisited not because a target-side signal changed, but because the question this dataset was originally asked to adjudicate has itself changed or is itself now contested.

22.2 Mission revision, this Part's own first trigger, occurs when Dataset #02's own already-sealed revision discipline produces an amended Crawl Mission Contract — a narrowed or broadened required-evidence field, a revised permitted-domains scope — for a mission this dataset has already, at least partially, adjudicated. This Part's own response distinguishes two cases per the amended contract's own actual scope of change: where the amendment narrows what the mission requires, per Part XIX §19.7's own worked treatment for a narrowed data-minimization ceiling, this dataset's own prior grants remain valid for whatever the narrower contract still covers, and no re-adjudication is required merely because the mission's own scope shrank. Where the amendment broadens or changes what the mission requires in a way this dataset's own prior adjudication never actually considered, the newly relevant fields re-enter Part II's own pipeline as a fresh adjudication, exactly as a first-time request against a target this dataset has not yet examined would.

22.3 Verdict dispute, this Part's own second trigger, occurs when a principal, an operator, or a downstream consumer of this dataset's own verdict — most directly Dataset #10's own dispute-resolution mechanics, per Part XIX §19.6's own routing rule — raises a specific, substantive question about whether a prior verdict was correctly reached, as distinct from whether the underlying signals have since changed, which Part XVII already governs. A dispute is substantive when it identifies a specific claimed error in this dataset's own original adjudication — a signal that was available but not actually consulted, a conflict-resolution precedence misapplied, a scope match Part IV's own adjudication is claimed to have gotten wrong — not a bare disagreement with an outcome absent any specific claimed defect in how it was reached.

22.4 The revision process itself, once a dispute is confirmed substantive, is not a fresh, independent re-adjudication starting from nothing; it is a targeted audit of the specific claimed defect against Part XV's own recorded basis for the original verdict, comparing what the record shows was actually consulted and reasoned against what the dispute claims should have been. A dispute confirming that the original verdict's own recorded basis genuinely omitted an available signal, or genuinely misapplied Part II's own conflict-resolution precedence, produces a revised verdict, recorded per Part XV's own supersession discipline exactly as a Part XVII re-adjudication would be. A dispute that, on this targeted audit, finds the original verdict's own recorded basis was actually sound — the signal claimed to have been overlooked was in fact considered and correctly weighted, for instance — closes with the original verdict reaffirmed and the dispute's own resolution itself recorded, never silently dismissed without its own trace.

22.5 A worked case demonstrates mission revision correctly distinguishing between a change requiring re-adjudication and one that does not. A mission's own required-evidence field is revised, per Dataset #02 Part XVIII, from "confirm the existence and title of specific pages" to "extract full structural content from those same pages" — a broadening in kind, not merely in volume, since structural extraction may itself trigger a restricted-action consideration under Part X that mere existence-confirmation never would have. This dataset's own response treats the specific pages' own domain- and authentication-level grants, per Parts III and IV, as still valid, since the target itself has not changed, but requires a fresh Part X restricted-action check against the newly required extraction depth, since that specific question was never actually adjudicated under the mission's own original, narrower contract.

22.6 A worked case demonstrates verdict dispute resolving in the disputant's own favor. An operator, reviewing a mission's own denied verdict for a specific authenticated resource, identifies that the original adjudication's own recorded basis, per Part XV §15.2, shows the session's own scope was checked against a stale, pre-rotation record rather than the session's own actual, current scope — a genuine defect in how the original verdict was reached, not merely a disagreement with its outcome. This Part's own targeted audit confirms the defect, and the revised adjudication, re-run against the session's own actually-current scope, produces a different verdict — granted — recorded as superseding the original per Part XV §15.5, with the dispute's own finding and resolution both recorded alongside it.

22.7 A contrasting worked case demonstrates a dispute that does not succeed. A principal disputes a denied verdict for a target whose robots.txt genuinely, unambiguously disallows the requested path, arguing only that the denial is inconvenient for the mission's own objective, without identifying any specific defect in how the denial was actually reached. This Part's own targeted audit finds the original verdict's own recorded basis sound — the robots.txt signal was correctly consulted and correctly weighted per Part II's own precedence — and the dispute closes with the original denial reaffirmed; a disagreement with an outcome is not, by itself, evidence of a defect in the process that produced it, and this Part's own revision mechanism responds to claimed defects, never to mere dissatisfaction with a correctly-reached result.

22.8 This Part's own relationship to Part XVII's re-adjudication discipline must be kept precise, since both produce a superseding verdict through structurally similar recording mechanics but for categorically different reasons: Part XVII responds to a changed target-side signal against an unchanged question; this Part responds to a changed or contested question against signals that may themselves be entirely unchanged. A single case can, in principle, trigger both — a mission revision that also happens to coincide with a robots.txt change discovered during the resulting re-adjudication — and where both triggers apply, this Part's own mission-revision or dispute-driven audit and Part XVII's own signal re-check both run, each producing its own recorded finding, rather than one trigger being treated as subsuming or excusing the other.

22.9 A failure this Part specifically guards against, distinct from but related to Part XVII §17.11's own closed-trigger-list discipline, is dispute inflation: treating every disagreement with a denied or escalated verdict as a substantive dispute warranting this Part's own targeted-audit process, rather than reserving that process for disputes that actually identify a specific, claimed defect per 22.3's own definition. This Part's own defense mirrors Part XI §11.10's own defense against operator fatigue: a dispute mechanism that audits every mere disagreement trains whoever raises disputes to expect a full re-litigation on demand, diluting the mechanism's own seriousness for the disputes that genuinely do identify a real defect in how a verdict was reached.

PART XXII FINAL LAW

A mission revision that narrows scope requires no re-adjudication for what remains covered; one that broadens or changes scope in a way this dataset never actually considered re-enters Part II's own pipeline as a fresh question. A verdict dispute is substantive only when it identifies a specific, claimed defect in how the original verdict was reached — an overlooked signal, a misapplied precedence — never a bare disagreement with a correctly-reached outcome, and this Part's own targeted audit either confirms the defect and produces a superseding verdict, or reaffirms the original and records the dispute's own resolution regardless. This Part and Part XVII's re-adjudication discipline answer different questions and both may apply to a single case without either excusing the other.


PART XXIII — FAILURE TAXONOMY FOR THE AUTHORIZATION ENGINE ITSELF

23.3 Consent conflation, named in Part VIII §8.2 and elaborated through that Part's own worked cases in §8.5 through §8.6, is treating a broad or ambiguous signal — a session's mere existence, a domain's general public reachability, a mission's general consent to its own stated objective — as though it were the specific consent a particular access actually requires, collapsing Part VIII's own two-question framework, target-side authorization and principal-side consent, into a single question that answers neither correctly.
