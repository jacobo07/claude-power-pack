PART I — MISSION, AND THE FAILURE MODE THIS CONSTITUTION EXISTS TO KILL

1.1 The system this constitution governs is not a scraper, and it is not a directory of scrapers. A scraper answers a narrow question: given a URL and a set of instructions, what bytes came back. That question is cheap to answer and has been answered well by a dozen mature open-source projects for a decade. The question this constitution exists to answer is categorically different: given an intention expressed in ordinary language, what is the smallest, cheapest, most defensible sequence of actions that produces demonstrable, provenance-carrying, institutionally reusable knowledge about the external world, and how does the system that performed that sequence prove, after the fact, that it did what it claims. No scraper answers that question. No orchestration layer that merely chooses between scrapers answers it either, because choosing an engine is not the same as owning the consequences of the choice.

1.2 Crawl OS is that something. Its jurisdiction is a single question that nothing else in Claude Power Pack answers: when Claude Power Pack needs to know something that lives outside its own repository, on the open web, behind a login, inside a PDF, inside a repository it does not own, how does that need become verified, provenance-carrying, reusable institutional knowledge, and how is every step of that becoming made accountable. The knowledge distillation systems govern what happens to a fact once it is inside the estate. The decision-review systems govern whether an action was justified. The verification systems govern whether an internal claim about the estate's own code is proven. None of them looks outward. None of them owns the moment a Claude Power Pack agent reaches past its own boundary and touches something it does not control. That reaching-outward moment, and everything that must happen around it to make the result trustworthy, is the territory this corpus governs.

1.3 The failure mode this constitution exists to kill has a precise shape, and the source document that founded this family named it exactly once, in five words that this constitution treats as load-bearing rather than decorative: a system must not accept Markdown as proof of success. The shape of the failure is this. An acquisition is attempted. Some bytes are returned. Those bytes are converted to clean, readable, well-formatted Markdown. The Markdown looks like a successful extraction because Markdown is what a successful extraction is supposed to look like, and a reader, whether human or agent, forms a belief that the underlying page was retrieved, that its content is representative, and that whatever claim was built on top of that content is grounded. At no point in that sequence did anything lie. The renderer rendered truthfully what it was given. The defect lives entirely in the gap between what was retrieved and what the clean output implies was retrieved, and a login wall, a cookie-consent interstitial, a soft-404 that returns HTTP 200, a paywall preview, and a genuinely successful extraction of the target article can all produce Markdown that is, to a downstream reader, visually indistinguishable.

1.4 This is not a hypothetical failure invented to justify a governance apparatus. It is the single most common failure mode of every crawling and scraping system this constitution's founding resource document surveyed, and it recurs precisely because every one of those systems was built to solve the retrieval problem, not the trust problem, and clean output was always the retrieval problem's own definition of success. A crawler that returns clean Markdown for a consent wall has done its job by its own internal standard. It fetched a page. It converted the page. The conversion succeeded. That the content is worthless is a fact about the world the crawler was never designed to notice, because noticing it requires a second, independent judgment about whether the content that came back is the content that was actually wanted, and that judgment is precisely what an engine, by itself, cannot supply about itself.

1.5 A second, structurally identical failure compounds the first, and it concerns not a single acquisition but a fleet of them. Eleven capable, independently maintained tools exist, each excellent within its own scope, and the naive response to their existence is to expose all eleven directly to an agent and let the agent choose per turn, by whatever intuition the prompt happens to encode that moment. This produces a system whose behavior is not policy but vibes: the same research request, asked twice, may route through a heavyweight browser agent once and a bare HTTP fetch the next time, for no reason traceable to anything more principled than which tool's name appeared more recently in the agent's context. A fleet without a router is not eleven capabilities. It is one capability, chosen at random, wearing eleven costumes, and the randomness is invisible to everyone downstream who only sees that an answer arrived.

1.6 From these two failures follows the mission, stated once and never softened. Crawl OS exists to make it structurally difficult to confuse: bytes retrieved with content wanted; a rendered page with a fetched one; a login-gated preview with the article behind it; a browser action taken with a browser action that succeeded; a page visited with a page whose relevant section was actually read; a crawl that ran to completion with a crawl that produced anything of value; a tool chosen with a tool chosen for a defensible reason; and, above every other confusion, clean Markdown with verified success. Each of these pairs has a real, named instance among the eleven surveyed resource projects or in the general literature of production crawling, and none of them was caused by a careless implementer. All of them were caused by a pipeline that had no organ whose sole job was to notice the difference.

1.7 The constitution is therefore not a style guide for writing crawlers, and it does not compete with the eleven resource projects on execution quality. It has exactly one form of authority, and the authority is precisely delimited: it may refuse to accept an acquisition as evidence. It cannot fetch a page faster than curl-impersonate fetches one. It cannot render a page more faithfully than a real browser renders one. It cannot extract a schema-shaped record more reliably than a purpose-built extractor tuned on that exact site. What it can do is decline to certify that an acquisition produced usable evidence, and require the acquiring engine, or the router that chose it, to either produce stronger proof or escalate to a costlier method that can. In an ecosystem where nothing may be institutionalized as knowledge without that certification, the power to withhold certification is sufficient to enforce every other rule in this document without Crawl OS ever needing to out-engineer a single one of the eleven adapters it governs.

1.8 The scope boundary must be stated with the same sharpness as the mission, because a governance layer that quietly expands into execution becomes a bottleneck and is then, correctly, routed around by whoever it is slowing down. Crawl OS does not decide what Claude Power Pack should research. It does not rank which external sources matter to a given project. It does not own general reasoning about the material once it has been acquired, and it does not own the institutional memory of the estate as a whole, only the portion of that memory that traces back to something acquired from outside the estate's own boundary. Its product is narrower and, for exactly that reason, achievable: given a research intention and a target outside the estate, produce provenance-carrying evidence sufficient to license the claim that was asked for, or produce an honest, specific statement of why sufficient evidence could not be obtained.

1.9 A final framing must be earned before the rest of this Part can be trusted, because the ambition stated so far could be misread as a demand for perfection. The ambition is not a corpus with zero failed acquisitions. That is unreachable against a hostile, constantly changing open web, and pursuing it produces theatre: a system that quietly narrows what it is willing to attempt until failure becomes rare because opportunity became rare with it. The actual ambition is a system in which failure is visible rather than silently converted into a plausible-looking success, in which the cost of acquisition is proportional to what the acquisition is actually worth, in which every engine choice is traceable to a stated reason, and in which each failed or degraded acquisition makes the next attempt, on that source or a similar one, measurably cheaper or more likely to succeed. The measure of Crawl OS is not how rarely it reports failure. A system that rarely reports failure on the open web is either extraordinarily disciplined about what it attempts, or it is not looking closely enough at what came back, and from outside its own reports those two conditions are indistinguishable — which is exactly why the reports themselves, and what licenses them, are this constitution's central concern.

1.10 The objection that will be raised at the first sign of friction deserves an answer here rather than later, because it is correct as far as it goes. The objection is that all of this — a router with a cost ladder, evidence objects with a dozen required fields, ten reality gates, a supervisor with five loops — is a large apparatus to erect around the act of downloading a web page, and that the apparatus will slow research down. The first half is true without qualification. The second half fails on arithmetic, not on principle. The apparatus is paid for once per acquisition, in a small number of extra fields and one router decision. Its absence is paid for every time a downstream claim is built on evidence that was never actually there, and that cost is not paid by the acquiring engine or the router; it is paid later, by whoever trusted the clean Markdown, discovers the error under pressure, and has no record of which acquisition, which engine, which moment produced the claim that turned out to be false. The apparatus is not free. It is cheaper than a corpus nobody can audit.

PART I FINAL LAW

The danger is never the failed acquisition. It is the acquisition that returns clean, readable, plausible Markdown for a consent wall, a paywall preview, or a soft-404 — because clean output converts an absence of retrieved content into a confident belief that content was retrieved, and no downstream reader can see, from the Markdown alone, the page the system never actually got. A system must not accept Markdown as proof of success. Everything else in this constitution is a consequence of that sentence.


PART II — THE ADAPTERS-ARE-NOT-IDENTITY DOCTRINE

2.1 The founding resource document that this corpus elevates into constitutional law states its central architectural claim as a single opposition, and this Part exists to make that opposition binding rather than aspirational. Firecrawl, Crawl4AI, Crawlee, Scrapy, Browser Use, Auto Browser, Scrapling, AutoScraper, MarkItDown, and curl-impersonate are engines of execution. Crawl OS decides which engine to use, why, under what restrictions, how to verify the result, and what knowledge from that result deserves to persist. The opposition is not a preference for one architectural style over another; it is a claim about where identity lives. An engine's identity is its execution technique — how it fetches, how it renders, how it extracts. Crawl OS's identity is never an execution technique. It is a set of decisions, a schema for evidence, a governance boundary, and a memory. Those four things do not change when curl-impersonate is replaced by a successor project; an execution technique changing is precisely the event this doctrine exists to make survivable.

2.2 The doctrine has an operational test that resolves any ambiguity about where a given piece of logic belongs, and the test is asked of every new capability before it is built: if this project disappeared tonight, would Crawl OS's decisions, evidence schema, governance rules, or accumulated memory need to change. If the honest answer is no — if only the adapter implementation would need to change, swapped for a successor with an equivalent capability declaration — the capability belongs in the execution plane and must be built as a thin, replaceable adapter. If the honest answer is yes — if the router's cost ladder, the evidence object's required fields, an authorization boundary, or a piece of institutional memory would need to be rewritten — the capability has leaked into the identity layer and the design is wrong, regardless of how convenient the leak was to implement. This test is the doctrine's entire enforcement mechanism, and it costs one sentence of reflection per new capability, which is precisely why it is stated first among this Part's provisions rather than left implicit.

2.3 The doctrine's most concrete expression is the adapter capability declaration, required of every engine before Crawl OS's router is permitted to route to it. Each adapter must declare its capabilities, its limitations, its cost, its latency profile, its dependencies, the content types it can meaningfully handle, its level of determinism, whether it supports persistent sessions, whether it can execute client-side script, its retry policy, and the health signals it can emit about its own operation. This is not documentation for a human reader. It is the interface contract the router consults at decision time, and it is the single artifact that makes an adapter replaceable: a successor project that can produce an equivalent declaration is, from Crawl OS's perspective, the same adapter with a new name, and swapping it requires touching exactly one configuration surface rather than every place in the codebase that once assumed a specific project's API.

2.4 The doctrine forbids a specific and tempting shortcut, named explicitly because it is the shortcut every fast-moving build eventually takes under time pressure: exposing an engine's native API surface directly to an agent, on the theory that the agent can be trusted to use it wisely turn by turn. An agent handed eleven tool names and told to pick one is not exercising judgment; it is exercising whichever tool's description happened to be most salient in that turn's context, and the resulting behavior is unauditable by construction, because there is no single place where the decision was made according to a stated rule — the decision was made eleven times, independently, inside eleven different invocations, each locally plausible and collectively incoherent. The doctrine requires the opposite: an agent requests a capability at the level of research_topic, or extract_dataset, or capture_evidence, and the mission compiler and strategy router, both owned by Crawl OS and neither an adapter, decide internally which of the eleven engines the request will actually invoke.

2.5 A second forbidden shortcut concerns vendoring. The doctrine does not permit Crawl OS to fork any of the eleven surveyed projects into its own codebase, however tempting a small patch might be in the moment, because the moment the fork happens, every upstream security fix, every upstream compatibility update, every upstream improvement in evasion-of-nothing-illegitimate anti-bot handling becomes a manual merge that Crawl OS's maintainers must perform forever, on a piece of code they did not design and whose internals they do not deeply understand. The doctrine instead requires that any patch a Crawl OS use case genuinely needs be either contributed upstream, worked around at the adapter boundary through the capability declaration's documented limitations, or accepted as a known limitation of that specific engine, routed around by the strategy router rather than fixed by forking. This provision is elaborated fully in Part XI; it is asserted here because it is a direct corollary of the identity boundary this Part establishes, not an independent rule.

2.6 The doctrine's positive obligation, as distinct from its prohibitions, is that Crawl OS must be able to state, for any acquisition it performed, which engine executed it and why, in language that references the mission's requirements rather than the engine's marketing description. "Crawl4AI was chosen because the target was identified as technical documentation, for which its LLM-oriented Markdown extraction and content-aware chunking were judged sufficient at a lower cost than a full browser render" is an admissible justification. "Crawl4AI was chosen because it is generally the best tool" is not, because it does not reference a property of the target, a property of the mission, or a comparison against the cost ladder's cheaper alternatives, and a justification that would be equally true regardless of what was being acquired is not a justification at all — it is a default masquerading as a decision.

2.7 The doctrine extends to a subtler case that the founding resource document raised about one specific candidate engine, and the case is worth preserving here because it demonstrates the doctrine functioning under genuine uncertainty rather than only under clean cases. The Cortex project's actual internals could not be reliably verified from public sources at the time this constitution was founded. The doctrine's answer to an unverifiable engine is not to exclude it and not to adopt its claimed capabilities on faith; it is to provisionally assign it a role consistent with what can be inferred from context — in that specific case, persistent supervision and quality assurance rather than another extraction engine — and to mark that assignment explicitly as inferred pending verification, so that no downstream decision is built on an unverified capability claim without the inference being visible and revisitable. An engine whose capabilities cannot be verified is not thereby exempt from the capability declaration; it is required to earn one before Crawl OS routes anything of consequence through it.

2.8 The doctrine's final provision concerns the router's own posture toward its adapters, and it is the provision most often violated by systems that otherwise respect this boundary correctly. The router must never develop a implicit favorite. A router that defaults to Crawl4AI for every ambiguous case because Crawl4AI is the team's personal preference, or because it was the first adapter built and integration testing concentrated there, has silently re-collapsed eleven engines into one, exactly as surely as an agent handed all eleven tools directly. The cost ladder defined in Part VI, not familiarity, not integration maturity, and not developer preference, is the only legitimate source of a routing decision, and any deviation from the cost ladder's stated logic is itself a finding the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's Loop Four is obligated to surface, not a private optimization the router is permitted to make quietly.

PART II FINAL LAW

An engine's identity is its execution technique; Crawl OS's identity is its decisions, its evidence schema, its governance boundary, and its memory, and those four things must never change when an execution technique does. The test is one sentence, asked before every new capability is built: if this adapter vanished tonight, would anything Crawl OS itself owns need to change? If the answer is yes, the design has leaked identity into an adapter, and the leak is the defect, however convenient it was to write.


PART III — OWNERSHIP BOUNDARY: WHAT CRAWL OS OWNS

3.1 A constitution that states what a system does without stating, with equal precision, what it does not do, has stated half a boundary, and half a boundary is indistinguishable from no boundary at all the first time an adjacent team needs to know whether a capability belongs to them or to Crawl OS. This Part and the one that follows it exist as a pair for that reason, and neither is complete without the other. This Part states the affirmative territory: the specific, nameable responsibilities that belong to Crawl OS and to nothing else in Claude Power Pack, verified at STOP #1 against every plausibly overlapping system already sealed in this estate before a single one of these claims was allowed to stand.

3.2 Crawl OS owns the compilation of a research intention into a Crawl Mission Contract. When an agent or a human expresses a need in ordinary language — read this repository, investigate this market, extract every function signature, compare these competitors, watch for when this changes, build a dataset from all these sources — that expression is underspecified by construction, and turning it into an executable, boundable, auditable unit of work is Crawl OS's first and most foundational owned responsibility. The resulting contract must state an objective, the entities involved, the sources anticipated, a target depth and breadth, a freshness requirement, the evidence required to satisfy the mission, the domains authorized, any authentication authorized, a maximum cost, a maximum time, explicit stop conditions, an output schema, a required confidence level, an update policy, and a preservation obligation. No other system in this estate compiles this specific contract shape, and Part XV of this dataset will show precisely why the nearest apparent neighbor, the decision-review family's evidence-burden machinery, is adjacent rather than identical.

3.3 Crawl OS owns the selection and operation of the appropriate acquisition runtime for a given mission, which is a narrower and more specific claim than "owns web automation in general," a claim this dataset explicitly disclaims in the Part that follows. Selecting between an HTTP fetch, a specialized transport, a lightweight render, a full browser, a governed browser agent, and human-authorized intervention, according to a stated cost ladder and the properties of the specific target rather than by habit, is owned territory, and it is owned precisely because nothing else in the estate has ever needed to make this particular class of decision. General task routing among AI models is a different decision, owned elsewhere, with a different cost structure and different failure modes; this constitution does not claim it and does not need to.

3.4 Crawl OS owns the preservation of provenance for anything it acquires. A canonical URL, the URL actually requested, the chain of redirects between them, the instant of acquisition, the apparent date of the content itself, the engine and version that performed the acquisition, the session profile under which it acquired the content if any, a hash of the raw content and a separate hash of the normalized content, and a record of the confidence and any anomaly signals observed during acquisition, together constitute a category of record that exists nowhere else in Claude Power Pack, because nothing else in the estate reaches outside its own boundary to touch content it does not control. This is elaborated fully as the Evidence Object in Part VII; it is named here as owned territory because provenance for externally acquired evidence is Crawl OS's single most defensible and least contestable claim in this entire constitution.

3.5 Crawl OS owns the verification of the integrity of what it acquired, as a distinct responsibility from the acquisition itself. A page can be successfully fetched, in the narrow sense that bytes arrived and no transport error occurred, while nonetheless failing to contain the content that was sought — Part I's central failure mode restated as a positive obligation rather than a prohibition. Determining whether a page's structure survived extraction, whether tables and lists and headings remained coherent, whether the extracted text corresponds to the primary content rather than to navigation chrome or an error page rendered with an HTTP 200 status, is Crawl OS's owned responsibility, discharged through the Production Reality Gates defined fully in Part XIII and enforced continuously by the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's Loop Three.

3.6 Crawl OS owns the detection of change in previously acquired sources, and the specific claim is narrower than general monitoring: it is the detection of semantic change in content the system itself previously acquired and recorded, distinguished explicitly from a raw byte-level diff, which is cheap to compute and routinely wrong, because a page can change every byte through a template update while its substantive content is untouched, or change one number in an otherwise identical page in a way that invalidates a previously institutionalized claim. Owning this distinction, and the Temporal Truth Engine that operationalizes it, is Crawl OS's territory because it requires the same provenance record that only Crawl OS maintains for externally acquired content.

3.7 Crawl OS owns the delivery of Evidence Packages to systems above it in the estate's architecture — the knowledge distillation, dataset construction, and institutional memory systems that consume acquired external evidence but do not themselves acquire it. This is stated as a delivery obligation rather than a production obligation to make the boundary exact: Crawl OS is responsible for the package arriving in the schema those downstream systems expect, carrying the provenance those systems require to cite their sources honestly, and it is not responsible for what those downstream systems subsequently do with the package, which decision belongs to them.

3.8 Crawl OS owns the router's cost discipline as an operational responsibility distinct from its architectural existence, which was established in Part II. This distinction matters because a cost ladder that exists on paper but that the router silently ignores under pressure — reaching for a full browser out of habit rather than escalating only on an observed, stated reason — is not owned territory that is being exercised; it is owned territory that is being abandoned while its documentation remains intact. The obligation is continuous and operational: every routing decision above the cheapest rung must carry a reason traceable to a property of the target or a prior failure at a cheaper rung, recorded at the moment the decision is made, not reconstructed afterward if someone asks.

3.9 Crawl OS owns institutionalization judgment specifically for externally acquired evidence — the decision about which acquired content, having passed the Production Reality Gates, deserves promotion into the estate's permanent memory versus remaining a transient artifact of a single mission. This is not a claim to own institutional memory in general, which belongs to the estate's broader knowledge and memory systems; it is the narrower and prior claim that only Crawl OS is positioned to judge whether a specific piece of externally sourced content has earned that promotion, because only Crawl OS holds the provenance record that makes the judgment auditable.

3.10 A final, cross-cutting owned responsibility closes this Part: Crawl OS owns explaining its own provenance on demand. When any downstream system or human asks where a specific claim originated, Crawl OS must be able to reconstruct which mission requested it, which URL produced it, which version of that URL was consulted, which engine performed the extraction, which transformation was applied to the raw content, and which specific fragment of the resulting evidence supports the claim in question. This obligation is not a convenience feature bolted onto the architecture; it is the direct, load-bearing consequence of Part I's mission statement, and a Crawl OS implementation that cannot discharge it in practice has failed regardless of how many pages it has successfully retrieved.

PART III FINAL LAW

Crawl OS owns the compilation of intention into a bounded mission, the selection and operation of the acquisition runtime, the preservation and verification of provenance, the detection of semantic change, the delivery of evidence to systems above it, the operational discipline of its own cost ladder, the institutionalization judgment for externally acquired content, and the on-demand reconstruction of any claim's provenance back to its source. Each of these is owned because nothing else in this estate reaches outside its own boundary to acquire content it does not control — and ownership here means the obligation is continuous and auditable, not merely documented once and assumed thereafter.


PART IV — OWNERSHIP BOUNDARY: WHAT CRAWL OS DOES NOT OWN

4.1 The affirmative territory described in Part III is defensible only because it is bounded, and the boundary must be drawn with the same precision applied to the territory itself, because an ownership claim that silently expands under the pressure of a convenient feature request is not a constitution, it is a starting position in a negotiation that Crawl OS will eventually lose to the systems it should have deferred to from the outset. This Part exists to make deferral automatic rather than negotiated, by naming the specific neighboring territories STOP #1 examined and confirming, for each, that Crawl OS does not own it and must compose with the system that already does.

4.2 Crawl OS does not own general reasoning. Once evidence has been acquired, verified, and delivered as an Evidence Package, any inference drawn from that evidence, any synthesis across multiple pieces of evidence, any judgment about what the evidence means for a broader question, belongs to the reasoning capacity of whatever system or agent consumes the package, not to Crawl OS. Crawl OS's involvement ends at the boundary of demonstrable acquisition; it does not extend into interpretation, and a Crawl OS implementation that begins forming opinions about the significance of what it retrieved, rather than simply retrieving it with maximal fidelity and provenance, has stepped outside its jurisdiction.

4.3 Crawl OS does not own the estate's institutional memory in general. It owns, per Part III, the narrower judgment of whether a specific piece of externally acquired content deserves promotion, and it owns several purpose-built memories of its own — source memory, site memory, extraction memory, session memory, evidence memory, change memory, negative memory, and procedural memory, elaborated fully in a later dataset in this family — but the estate's broader knowledge base, its cross-project institutional learning, and its long-term memory architecture belong to systems that predate Crawl OS and were confirmed at STOP #1 to already own that territory. Crawl OS's memories are specialized instances that feed into, and compose with, that broader architecture; they do not replace it or compete with it.

4.4 Crawl OS does not own dataset creation in general. It owns, specifically, the compilation of research findings into structured, provenance-carrying institutional datasets when that compilation is the explicit product of a crawl mission — the Web-to-Dataset Compiler defined in a later dataset in this family — but the broader discipline of dataset construction, dataset quality standards, and the corpus conventions this very document is written under belong to the dataset-first protocols already sealed elsewhere in this estate. Crawl OS's Web-to-Dataset Compiler is a producer that feeds those protocols; it is not a second, competing implementation of them.

4.5 Crawl OS does not own universal quality assurance. The Crawl Cortex Supervisor, defined in Part XIII and fully specified in a later dataset, performs quality assurance specifically over crawl missions — evidence integrity, extraction fidelity, coverage sufficiency, and the health of the crawling runtime itself. It does not audit code quality, does not review architecture decisions unrelated to acquisition, and does not perform the estate's general verification function, which belongs to the verification axis already sealed elsewhere and which this constitution explicitly composes with in Part XVII rather than duplicates.

4.6 Crawl OS does not own general automation. A scheduled task that restarts a server, a deployment pipeline that ships code, a background job that reorganizes files, none of these fall within Crawl OS's jurisdiction merely because they are automated and unattended. Crawl OS's automation is specifically the automation of external evidence acquisition; the estate's general automation and orchestration capabilities, wherever a given automated task does not involve reaching outside the estate's boundary to acquire externally sourced content, belong to other systems entirely.

4.7 Crawl OS does not own browser automation as a universal capability. It owns browser automation specifically in service of acquisition missions — navigating a site to reach content, authenticating into a legitimately accessible session, interacting with a dynamic interface to surface information that has no simpler HTTP path. A browser automation need that has nothing to do with acquiring external evidence, such as automated testing of Claude Power Pack's own interfaces, is not Crawl OS's territory even though the underlying technique, driving a browser programmatically, is superficially similar. The distinguishing question, always, is whether the target of the automation is external content the estate does not control, or the estate's own surface.

4.8 Crawl OS does not own agent orchestration in general. The Crawl Intent Compiler and Acquisition Strategy Router coordinate the specific sequence of engines and steps within a single crawl mission, but the broader discipline of coordinating multiple agents across multiple concurrent workstreams, and the parallel session scheduling that governs how many such workstreams may run at once, belong to systems already sealed elsewhere in this estate, confirmed at STOP #1, and Crawl OS's own missions are themselves scheduled and budgeted by those systems rather than by a competing scheduler of Crawl OS's own invention.

4.9 Crawl OS does not own domain knowledge unrelated to acquisition. Nothing in this constitution licenses a Crawl OS dataset to accumulate substantive expertise about commerce operations, product research, advertising, or any other domain the acquired content happens to touch. The prohibition against domain contamination, stated in the founding resource document as a hard boundary against a specific set of adjacent quality benchmarks used only for structural calibration, is restated here as a constitutional matter: Crawl OS's datasets speak only of acquisition, evidence, provenance, extraction, normalization, crawl memory, change intelligence, and the governance of those activities, regardless of what subject matter a given crawl mission happened to be about.

4.10 The boundary drawn across this Part and the one preceding it is not a static map to be consulted once and forgotten. It is a live discipline: every proposed new Crawl OS capability must be checked against it before being built, using the same test articulated in Part II — does this capability require Crawl OS itself to own something new, or does it belong, once examined honestly, to a system that already owns adjacent territory and with which Crawl OS should compose rather than compete. The Duplicate-to-Advantage Engine's Family Sizing Mode, extended specifically during this corpus's own STOP #1 to answer exactly this question for a proposed family rather than one proposal at a time, is the mechanical instrument that discipline is enforced with, and it is binding on any future proposed addition to this family, not only on the founding twenty.

4.11 A worked example closes this Part, because the test articulated so far is easy to state and easy to misapply under pressure, and a single concrete case makes its application legible. Suppose a proposal arrives to build a "Source Reliability Scorer" that watches every domain Crawl OS has ever acquired from and maintains a running trust rating for each. Applied naively, this looks like Crawl OS territory — it concerns sources, it concerns acquisition, and the founding resource document names source memory as an owned responsibility. Applied against the test in 4.10, the honest question is different: does scoring a domain's general trustworthiness require anything Crawl OS uniquely holds, or is it a generic reputation and risk-scoring capability that happens to be fed acquisition events as one of several possible input streams. Examined this way, the proposal decomposes into two pieces with different owners. The narrow piece, recording per-domain acquisition outcomes, extraction success rates, and anomaly frequencies as they specifically pertain to Crawl OS's own missions, is genuinely site memory, already owned per Part III. The broad piece, a general trust or reputation score usable by any estate system regardless of whether the underlying event came from a crawl mission, a manual review, or an external report, belongs to whatever risk or trust infrastructure already exists elsewhere, or, if none exists, is a proposal for a new system entirely separate from Crawl OS, to be evaluated on its own merits rather than built quietly inside this family because a plausible-sounding name made it feel adjacent. The decomposition, not a verdict on the proposal's name, is what this Part's boundary actually produces.

PART IV FINAL LAW

Crawl OS does not own general reasoning, the estate's broader institutional memory, dataset creation as a universal discipline, universal quality assurance, general automation, universal browser automation, general agent orchestration, or any domain knowledge unrelated to acquisition. Every one of these boundaries was confirmed by evidence at STOP #1, not assumed, and every future proposed capability must be checked against the same test before it is built: does this genuinely require new ownership, or does it belong to a system that already owns it, with which Crawl OS should compose rather than compete.


PART V — THE FOURTEEN-ENGINE CONSTITUTIONAL ARCHITECTURE

5.1 A constitution that states ownership without naming the structures through which ownership is exercised has described a jurisdiction with no government. This Part names the fourteen constitutional engines through which Crawl OS discharges the ownership established in Part III, in the order the founding resource document itself specifies, and states for each engine a one-sentence charter binding on every later dataset in this family that elaborates it further. No engine listed here may be elaborated in a way that contradicts its charter as stated in this Part; an elaboration that requires a different charter has discovered that the architecture itself needs amendment, which is a Part XXIV event, not a silent drift permitted to a downstream dataset.

5.2 The Crawl Intent Compiler's charter is to convert an underspecified research request, expressed in ordinary language, into the bounded Crawl Mission Contract described in Part III, and to do nothing else — it does not execute acquisition, it does not choose engines, and its output is a contract, not a result.

5.3 The Resource Discovery and Frontier Engine's charter is to identify and prioritize the specific sources a mission should visit, ranking candidate URLs and sources by their expected contribution of new information relative to what the mission has already acquired, rather than by raw depth or arrival order, and to hand a prioritized frontier to the strategy router rather than acquiring anything itself.

5.4 The Acquisition Strategy Router's charter is to select, for each item on the frontier, the least costly engine from the cost ladder defined fully in Part VI that can plausibly satisfy the mission's evidence requirement for that item, to record the reason for its selection, and to escalate only upon an observed, stated failure at a cheaper rung, never by default and never by habit.

5.5 The Fetch Runtime Fabric's charter is to expose a single, uniform interface — discover, map, fetch, render, interact, extract, normalize, validate, persist, replay — behind which any of the eleven adapter engines may sit interchangeably, per the doctrine established in Part II, so that a mission's logic never needs to know which specific engine is executing a given step.

5.6 The Browser Interaction Fabric's charter is to govern the subset of acquisitions that genuinely require a browser — dynamic interfaces, authenticated sessions, multi-step navigation with no simpler HTTP path — through a governed, session-aware, human-interruptible control plane, and to be invoked only when the strategy router has determined that no cheaper rung of the cost ladder can plausibly satisfy the mission.

5.7 The Extraction and Rule Induction Fabric's charter is to convert raw fetched or rendered content into structured, LLM-ready representations, using schema-based extraction where a schema is known, content-aware chunking where structure must be inferred, and page-type classification to distinguish an article from a reference page from a listing from an error page rendered with a success status code.

5.8 The Document Normalization Fabric's charter is to convert any non-HTML document — PDF, DOCX, PPTX, XLSX, an image carrying meaningful metadata — encountered during acquisition into the same canonical intermediate Markdown representation used for web content, so that document ingestion and web crawling are unified at the representation layer rather than treated as two separate systems that happen to share a vault.

5.9 The Evidence Integrity Fabric's charter is to construct, for every acquisition, the Evidence Object described fully in Part VII, and to refuse to pass an acquisition downstream as institutionally usable evidence until that object's required fields are populated with genuine, non-fabricated values, including an honest confidence score and any observed anomaly signals.

5.10 The Knowledge Distillation Fabric's charter is to consume verified Evidence Packages and produce the compact, cited, reusable knowledge artifacts that downstream estate systems consume, and to do so only after the Production Reality Gates in Part XIII have been satisfied, never before.

5.11 Crawl Memory and Change Intelligence's charter is to maintain the eight specialized memories named in Part IV — source, site, extraction, session, evidence, change, negative, and procedural — and to detect semantic rather than byte-level change in previously acquired sources, feeding the Temporal Truth Engine that distinguishes publication date, event date, update date, acquisition date, and current validity.

5.12 The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's charter is to run continuous, non-executing oversight across five loops — mission watch, runtime health, evidence quality assurance, knowledge quality assurance, and institutional learning — detecting degradation and closing incomplete loops without itself performing the acquisitions it supervises, per the full specification in a later dataset in this family.

5.13 Governance, Safety, and Authorization's charter is to bound every other engine's behavior against authorized domains, authorized authentication, and the estate's compliance requirements, positioned early in this constitution's own build order specifically because a governance boundary asserted after eight engines are already specified governs nothing that has not already happened.

5.14 Evaluation and Reality Gates' charter is to operationalize the ten Production Reality Gates defined in Part XIII as the mechanical, checkable criteria a mission must pass before any of its output may be considered complete, composing explicitly with the decision-review family's evidence-burden vocabulary per Part XVII rather than inventing a parallel one.

5.15 Skills, MCP, and Agent Interfaces' charter, built last among the fourteen precisely because it cannot be honestly specified until the other thirteen exist to be described, is to expose Crawl OS's capabilities to Claude Power Pack exclusively at the institutional level named in Part XXI — research_topic, extract_dataset, verify_claim, and their siblings — never at the level of raw browser or fetch primitives.

5.16 These fourteen engines are not independent modules that happen to share a repository. They form a single pipeline whose stages were named in the founding resource document and are restated here as the architecture's spine: discovery, selection, acquisition, interaction, extraction, normalization, verification, traceability, and reusable knowledge. Each engine above sits at exactly one stage of that spine, and an engine's charter, restated once more, is the boundary of what it may do at its stage — no engine may reach forward into a later stage's responsibility or backward into an earlier one, because a pipeline whose stages silently absorb each other's work is not an architecture, it is a monolith wearing fourteen labels, and the entire justification for naming fourteen engines rather than building one undifferentiated crawler collapses the moment that absorption is tolerated even once.

5.17 A worked example traces a single mission through all fourteen engines, because the charters stated above are easy to read as fourteen independent job descriptions rather than as one pipeline, and only a trace makes the handoffs concrete. A mission arrives as the request "find out whether this open-source project's documentation still matches its current API." The Crawl Intent Compiler produces a Mission Contract naming the project's documentation site as the target, a moderate freshness requirement, a bounded depth of one documentation tree, and an evidence requirement of extracted function signatures with page-level provenance. The Resource Discovery and Frontier Engine, consulting site memory, prioritizes the API reference pages over the project's blog or changelog, which the mission's contract does not need. The Acquisition Strategy Router, finding this specific site previously recorded in site memory as requiring only a basic fetch, selects the first rung of the cost ladder rather than defaulting to a browser. The Fetch Runtime Fabric executes that fetch through its uniform interface, invisibly to every later stage, against whichever specific adapter the router named. Governance, Safety, and Authorization confirms the domain is within the mission's authorized scope before the fetch is permitted to proceed at all, a check that in this constitution's own build order happens before, not after, the engines that follow. The Extraction and Rule Induction Fabric applies schema-based extraction tuned for API reference pages rather than generic article extraction, because the page-type classification correctly identified the target as reference material. The Document Normalization Fabric takes no action in this particular mission, since no non-HTML documents were encountered, which is itself a legitimate and common outcome for an engine whose charter is conditional on content type. The Evidence Integrity Fabric constructs an Evidence Object per page, populating a genuine confidence score derived from whether the extracted signatures parse as syntactically valid code in the target language. Evaluation and Reality Gates checks the resulting objects against Gates A through D before anything is permitted downstream. The Knowledge Distillation Fabric, only once those gates pass, produces a compact comparison between the extracted signatures and the documentation's claimed signatures. Crawl Memory and Change Intelligence records the outcome in extraction memory, improving the schema's confidence for this exact site on any future mission. The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's five loops ran throughout, not at the end, watching for a stalled mission, a zombie browser process that never spawned because none was needed, and evidence anomalies that in this mission never fired. The Skills, MCP, and Agent Interfaces engine is what the original request entered through and what the final comparison exits through, the only engine touched at both ends of the trace and the only one whose charter is to be invisible to every engine between those two ends.

PART V FINAL LAW

Fourteen engines, one pipeline: intent compilation, resource discovery, strategy routing, fetch execution, browser interaction, extraction, document normalization, evidence integrity, knowledge distillation, memory and change intelligence, supervision, governance, evaluation, and interfaces. Each engine's charter is the boundary of what it may do at its stage of the pipeline, and no engine may absorb a neighboring stage's responsibility, because a pipeline that tolerates that absorption even once has already become the monolith this architecture exists to prevent.


PART VI — THE COST LADDER AND THE LEAST-COSTLY-SUFFICIENT-EVIDENCE PRINCIPLE

6.1 Every acquisition Crawl OS performs costs something — time, tokens, external API spend, browser compute, proxy bandwidth, or storage — and a system that does not discipline that cost against what the acquisition is actually worth will, left unmanaged, drift toward the most capable and most expensive method available for every request, because the most capable method is also the one least likely to produce a visible failure in the moment, and visible failure avoidance is a powerful, mostly invisible force shaping engine selection under any kind of delivery pressure. The cost ladder exists to make that drift structurally difficult rather than merely discouraged in a document nobody consults at decision time.

6.2 The ladder has seven rungs, ordered from cheapest to most expensive, and the Acquisition Strategy Router, per its charter in Part V, must attempt the cheapest rung that can plausibly satisfy the mission's stated evidence requirement before escalating, and must record, at the moment of escalation, the specific observed reason the cheaper rung was judged insufficient. The first rung is a valid cache hit — content the mission or a prior mission already acquired, within the freshness window the current mission's contract specifies, requiring no new acquisition at all. The second rung is a basic HTTP fetch, appropriate for static pages with no meaningful anti-automation posture. The third rung is a specialized HTTP transport, such as a client-fingerprint-matched request, appropriate when a basic fetch is observed to receive materially different content than a standard browser would. The fourth rung is a lightweight render, appropriate when content is present in the initial HTML but a small amount of client-side execution is needed to reveal it fully. The fifth rung is a full browser, appropriate when the target is a genuine single-page application or requires substantial client-side execution to produce any usable content. The sixth rung is a governed browser agent capable of multi-step interaction — form-filling, multi-page navigation, session persistence — appropriate when no simpler HTTP path exists to the content at all. The seventh and final rung is authorized human intervention, reserved for cases where every automated method has been exhausted or is inappropriate given the authorization boundary defined in Part IX.

6.3 The principle underlying the ladder is stated once, precisely, because every operational rule in this Part is a restatement of it under a different name: use the least costly strategy that can produce sufficient evidence, and escalate only upon an observed reason, never by default. Sufficiency is defined relative to the mission's own stated evidence requirement, recorded in the Crawl Mission Contract per Part III, not relative to some abstract standard of maximal fidelity; a mission that only needs to confirm a page's existence and title requires far less than a mission that needs to extract every table on the page with perfect structural fidelity, and the ladder must be consulted against the specific requirement in front of it, not against a generic notion of "getting the best possible result" that would justify escalating every single time.

6.4 An observed reason for escalation is a specific, falsifiable fact about what the cheaper rung actually returned, not a prediction made in advance. "The basic fetch returned a 403 status" is an observed reason. "The basic fetch returned content, but its length is far below what a typical page of this type contains, suggesting a bot-detection interstitial rather than the target content" is an observed reason. "This site is known to use client-side rendering" is a legitimate reason only if it is drawn from Crawl OS's own site memory, populated by a prior observed failure at a cheaper rung on that same site, not from an unverified assumption applied preemptively to a site the system has never actually attempted. The distinction matters because a router permitted to escalate on prediction rather than observation will, over time, accumulate predictions that were never actually tested, and those untested predictions become a silent, unaudited default exactly as surely as always reaching for the same familiar engine.

6.5 The ladder's discipline is bidirectional, and the direction less often enforced is the more important one: a mission must also be permitted, and in fact required by its own contract, to de-escalate once a cheaper rung is later confirmed sufficient for a class of source that previously required a more expensive one. Sites migrate rendering strategies, anti-bot postures change, and a site that once required a full browser may, months later, be servable by a basic fetch. Crawl OS's site memory must be revisited periodically rather than treated as a permanent verdict, because a system that only ever escalates and never de-escalates will monotonically grow more expensive over its own operational lifetime regardless of whether the underlying targets actually still warrant that expense, and a monotonically growing cost curve is itself evidence that the ladder's stated discipline has quietly stopped being enforced.

6.6 The seventh rung, authorized human intervention, requires its own discussion because it is qualitatively different from the six that precede it: it is not a more powerful automated method, it is the deliberate suspension of automation in favor of a human decision, and it exists specifically because the alternative to a well-designed seventh rung is not a system that never needs one, it is a system that either fails silently when automation is genuinely insufficient or, worse, attempts an unauthorized workaround to avoid admitting that automation reached its limit. A mission that reaches the seventh rung and is not authorized to use it must fail with an honest, specific statement of what was needed and why automation could not supply it, rather than silently downgrading its evidence requirement to something automation happens to be able to satisfy.

6.7 The cost ladder is not, and must never be treated as, a ranking of engine quality. The seventh rung is the most expensive, not the best; a governed browser agent invoked for a static documentation page is not a superior acquisition to a basic HTTP fetch of the same page, it is a wasteful one, and the ladder's entire purpose is to prevent exactly that category of waste from becoming the system's default behavior under the pressure of wanting to be maximally certain about every acquisition regardless of what the mission actually required. Certainty beyond what a mission's contract calls for is not diligence; it is unaccounted cost, and the ladder exists to make that cost visible and justifiable rather than invisible and habitual.

6.8 The router's cost ladder decisions are themselves subject to the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's Loop One, mission watch, which specifically monitors for anomalous cost patterns — a mission whose per-item acquisition cost is trending upward without a corresponding trend in target difficulty, or a router that is escalating at a rate inconsistent with its own historical baseline for a given site class — and surfaces these patterns as findings rather than allowing them to accumulate silently across missions. The ladder's discipline is therefore not solely the router's own responsibility to self-enforce; it is a continuously supervised property of the system, because a discipline that depends entirely on the disciplined component never failing to discipline itself is not a discipline, it is a hope.

PART VI FINAL LAW

Use the least costly strategy that can produce sufficient evidence for the specific mission's stated requirement, and escalate only upon an observed, falsifiable reason recorded at the moment of escalation — never by default, never by habit, and never on an untested prediction. The ladder is bidirectional: a site that once required an expensive rung must be re-evaluated for de-escalation as conditions change, or the system's cost curve will monotonically grow regardless of whether the underlying targets still warrant the expense. The most expensive rung is not the best rung. It is the most expensive one.


PART VII — EVIDENCE OBJECTS AS THE CONSTITUTIONAL UNIT OF WORK

7.1 Most crawling systems treat their unit of output as text: a string of retrieved and cleaned content, handed to whatever consumes it next. Crawl OS's constitutional unit of output is not text. It is the Evidence Object, a structured record in which retrieved content is exactly one field among many, and the remaining fields are what make the content usable as evidence rather than merely available as prose. This distinction is the single most consequential architectural choice this constitution makes, because every downstream guarantee this corpus offers — provenance, verifiability, honest confidence, traceability back to a source — depends on the Evidence Object existing and being populated honestly, and none of those guarantees are recoverable after the fact from text alone, however clean that text appears.

7.2 The Evidence Object's required fields, restated here from the founding resource document as binding rather than illustrative, are: the canonical URL of the content, the URL actually requested if it differs from the canonical one, the full chain of redirects traversed between them, the instant of acquisition, the apparent date of the content itself as distinct from the acquisition date, a title, an author or responsible entity where determinable, a MIME type, a detected language, the specific acquisition method used, the engine and version that performed the acquisition, the session profile under which the acquisition occurred if any, a hash of the raw retrieved content, a separate hash of the normalized content, visual evidence where the mission's contract requires it, the specific fragments of the content actually used to support a downstream claim, the outbound links discovered, a provenance chain back to the mission that requested the acquisition, a confidence score, any anomaly signals observed during acquisition, any known rights or access restrictions, and a change history where the same source has been acquired more than once.

7.3 No field in this list is decorative, and the ones most often treated as optional by systems built under delivery pressure are precisely the ones this constitution insists on hardest. The confidence score is not a courtesy; it is the field that makes Part I's central prohibition enforceable, because a confidence score that is honestly low for a suspected consent wall is the mechanism by which clean Markdown stops being able to masquerade as proof of success. Anomaly signals are not a courtesy either; they are the field in which the Evidence Integrity Fabric records exactly the observations — unusually short content relative to the page's apparent type, a title inconsistent with the requested URL's path, a redirect chain terminating somewhere unrelated to the original domain — that a downstream reader would need in order to independently judge whether to trust the acquisition, rather than trusting it by default because nothing about the output looked obviously wrong.

7.4 The distinction between a raw content hash and a normalized content hash is deliberate and serves two different purposes that would be lost if only one hash were kept. The raw hash proves, byte for byte, what was actually received from the source at the moment of acquisition, and licenses a genuine replay or dispute about what the source originally contained. The normalized hash identifies the content after Crawl OS's own transformation into canonical Markdown, and is what downstream deduplication and change-detection systems actually compare against, because two acquisitions of substantively identical content can differ in raw bytes over irrelevant details — whitespace, a session token embedded in a query parameter, a timestamp printed in a page footer — while being, for every purpose that matters to the mission, the same evidence. Keeping only one hash collapses two distinct questions, what did the source actually send and has the substantive content changed, into one answer that cannot honestly answer either.

7.5 The provenance chain field is what makes Part III's final owned obligation, explaining any claim's provenance on demand, mechanically possible rather than aspirational. An Evidence Object's provenance chain must be able to answer, without requiring any external reconstruction, which mission requested this acquisition, which specific item in that mission's frontier this acquisition corresponds to, and which prior acquisition, if any, this one supersedes in the change history. A system that can answer "here is the content" but not "here is the mission that asked for this content and here is why" has produced a record, not evidence, and the difference between those two things is the entire subject matter of this Part.

7.6 The relationship between an Evidence Object and the Production Reality Gates defined in Part XIII must be stated precisely to avoid a common misunderstanding: an Evidence Object being fully populated with all required fields is a necessary but not sufficient condition for that evidence being accepted as gate-passing. A fully populated Evidence Object with an honestly low confidence score and multiple recorded anomaly signals is a well-formed object that correctly reports a probable failure; it is not evidence that has earned institutionalization, and the Evidence Integrity Fabric must never conflate structural completeness of the record with success of the underlying acquisition. The object's job is to report the truth about what happened, including when what happened was a failure worth recording precisely as a failure.

7.7 Evidence Objects for content acquired via the Document Normalization Fabric, per Part V's charter for that engine, carry the same required fields as web-acquired evidence, with the acquisition method field distinguishing a document conversion from a web fetch and the canonical URL field, where the source is a local or attached document with no meaningful URL, replaced by an equivalently canonical identifier for the document's origin. This uniformity is deliberate and is the mechanism by which Part III's claim that Crawl OS acquires digital evidence, not merely web pages, is made structurally true rather than rhetorically true: a PDF converted through MarkItDown and a web page fetched through Crawl4AI produce the same shape of record, differing only in the specifics of how each field was populated, and any downstream consumer of Evidence Objects can treat both uniformly without needing to know which acquisition path produced a given piece of evidence.

7.8 The Evidence Object schema is versioned, and a version change is a Part XXIV amendment event, not a silent field addition performed by whichever engine happens to need a new field first. A schema that drifts field by field, engine by engine, without central governance produces exactly the kind of incoherent, unauditable corpus this entire constitution exists to prevent — a corpus in which asking "what does a Crawl OS Evidence Object actually contain" no longer has one answer, because the true answer depends on which engine, on which date, produced the specific object being examined. The schema is owned centrally, by the Evidence Integrity Fabric, precisely so that this question always has exactly one current answer.

7.9 A final provision closes this Part and anticipates a failure mode this constitution's authors judged likely enough to name explicitly: an Evidence Object must never be constructed retroactively from memory or inference after an acquisition, populated with plausible-looking values for fields the acquiring engine did not actually observe. A confidence score is either derived from real signals the Evidence Integrity Fabric actually examined, or it is absent and the object is marked as such; a hash is either computed from actual retrieved bytes, or the object records that no hash could be computed and why. A fabricated field is, in every consequence that matters, indistinguishable from Part I's clean-Markdown failure mode wearing a more sophisticated disguise, and this constitution treats it with exactly the same severity.

PART VII FINAL LAW

The Evidence Object, not raw text, is Crawl OS's constitutional unit of output. Every required field exists to answer a specific question a downstream reader will eventually need answered — what was actually received, when, from where, through what chain, with what confidence, and with what observed anomalies — and no field may be populated with a plausible-looking value that was not actually derived from the acquisition it describes. A fabricated field is Part I's failure mode in a more sophisticated disguise, and this constitution treats it with the same severity.


PART VIII — THE PROHIBITION OF MARKDOWN-AS-PROOF

8.1 Part I named the failure mode this constitution exists to kill. This Part elevates that naming into an enforceable article, in the same structural position the verification axis elsewhere in this estate gives to its own first article, because the parallel is exact and deliberate: both articles exist to prevent the same underlying confusion, applied to a different surface. There, the confusion is between test output and verified behavior. Here, it is between clean rendered content and verified acquisition. The mechanism of enforcement is different because the surfaces are different, but the article's force is identical: a claim of success unaccompanied by the specific evidence that would license it is not permitted to stand.

8.2 The article's precise statement is this: no Crawl OS engine, router, or supervisor may treat the mere existence of clean, well-formatted Markdown output as sufficient evidence that an acquisition succeeded in retrieving the content the mission actually sought. Markdown quality is a property of the rendering step. Acquisition success is a property of whether the underlying content matches what was wanted. These two properties are produced by different parts of the pipeline, they can vary independently, and a system that allows the former to stand in for the latter has made exactly the substitution Part I identified as the founding failure this entire corpus was written to prevent.

8.3 The article's enforcement mechanism is the Production Reality Gates, specifically Gate B, Content Reality, defined fully in Part XIII: a mission's output is not permitted to be treated as complete on the strength of Gate A, Acquisition Reality, alone. A page can genuinely have been fetched — no transport error, a 200 status, real bytes received — while still failing Gate B, because the bytes received were a consent wall, a paywall preview, or a soft-404 rendered with a success status. Gate A passing is necessary. It has never been sufficient, and this article exists specifically to prevent any implementation detail, shortcut, or delivery-pressure decision from quietly treating it as though it were.

8.4 A specific corruption of this article deserves naming because it defeats the article while appearing, superficially, to comply with it: the substitution of a length or structure heuristic for genuine content verification. A system that checks only whether the returned Markdown exceeds some minimum character count, or contains at least one heading and one paragraph, has built a test that a well-formed consent wall or a well-formed paywall preview will pass just as easily as genuine target content, because consent walls and paywall previews are themselves professionally designed pages with real headings, real paragraphs, and often quite a lot of legally-mandated text. A heuristic of this shape does not detect the failure mode; it detects the absence of the most extreme, laziest version of the failure mode, and mistaking that for genuine content verification is itself a form of the exact confusion this article prohibits.

8.5 Genuine content verification, as this article requires it, examines properties that a consent wall or paywall preview will not coincidentally share with the target content: whether the extracted text's topical content, assessed against the mission's stated objective, plausibly matches what was sought; whether the page's title and the requested URL's path are mutually consistent; whether the content length is consistent with the typical length of genuine content at that site and page type, drawn from site memory; and, where the mission's contract calls for it, whether a visual capture of the rendered page is consistent with genuine target content rather than an interstitial. None of these checks is infallible in isolation. Their combination, and the honest confidence score that results from weighing them, is what Gate B actually requires, not a single cheap proxy standing in for the whole judgment.

8.6 The article extends explicitly to a case the founding resource document raised as a specific, named risk: a page that returns an HTTP 200 status while its actual content is an error message, a "content not found" notice, or an empty template, a pattern common enough on the modern web that treating status code alone as evidence of success is independently disqualifying, entirely separate from the consent-wall and paywall cases already discussed. A soft-404 of this kind will often render as perfectly clean, well-structured Markdown, because the site's error page was designed with the same care as its content pages, and an implementation that stops checking once it observes a 200 status and readable output has failed this article regardless of how sophisticated its Markdown renderer is.

8.7 The article's burden of proof runs in one direction only, and this direction is the article's single most important operational consequence: the burden is on the acquisition to demonstrate that it retrieved genuine content, not on a downstream reviewer to demonstrate that it did not. A mission's output defaults to UNVERIFIED content status, per the verdict ontology elaborated in Part XIX, until the specific checks in 8.5 have actually been performed and have actually passed; it does not default to verified and require someone to catch the failure after the fact. This is the same inversion the estate's verification axis makes about its own absence-of-failure-signal reasoning, applied here to acquisition rather than to test execution, and it is inverted for the identical reason: a system defaulting to success is a system that will, under any kind of pressure, quietly stop checking, because the checks only ever subtract from an already-assumed positive result rather than being required to produce one.

8.8 A final clarification prevents this article from being over-read into a prohibition it does not actually impose. The article does not require that every acquisition achieve certainty, and it does not forbid a mission from proceeding on evidence the Evidence Integrity Fabric honestly scored at moderate rather than high confidence, where the mission's own contract, per Part III, has stated that moderate confidence is sufficient for its purposes. What the article forbids is the silent conversion of unverified or low-confidence acquisition into an unqualified claim of success, whether that conversion happens through an omitted confidence score, an unexamined status code, or the simple, human, and entirely understandable temptation to trust output that merely looks right.

8.9 A worked example closes this Part. A mission targets a news article behind a metered paywall that renders three paragraphs of genuine article text before the paywall interstitial replaces the remainder, all inside a single HTTP response with a 200 status. A naive acquisition returns this response, converts it to Markdown, observes real headings, a real byline, and three real paragraphs of prose, and every length- or structure-based heuristic in 8.4 passes cleanly, because three paragraphs of genuine, well-written text is not a short or malformed response by any structural measure. Genuine verification under 8.5 catches what the heuristic cannot: site memory for this specific publication records that its articles typically run twelve to twenty paragraphs, and three paragraphs followed by a component recognizable in the raw HTML as a subscription prompt is a length anomaly relative to that site's own baseline, not relative to some generic notion of a short page. The Evidence Integrity Fabric records this anomaly, assigns a confidence score reflecting partial rather than complete content, and the Evidence Object is honestly marked as a partial acquisition rather than a successful one, which is the correct outcome whether or not the mission's contract judges three paragraphs sufficient for its purposes. The article was never about preventing partial acquisitions; it is about preventing a partial acquisition from being reported as though it were complete.

PART VIII FINAL LAW

Clean Markdown is a property of rendering. Successful acquisition is a property of whether the content retrieved matches what the mission actually sought. These two properties vary independently, and no Crawl OS component may treat the first as proof of the second. The burden of proof runs one direction only: an acquisition defaults to unverified until Gate B's specific checks have actually been performed and have actually passed, never the reverse. A status-200 soft-404 and a professionally designed consent wall will both produce clean, well-structured Markdown, and a system that stops checking once output looks right has already failed this article.


PART IX — THE ANTI-DETECTION-IS-NOT-AUTHORIZATION DOCTRINE

9.1 Several of the fourteen engines this constitution names, and several of the eleven adapters they route through, possess genuine technical capacity to evade automated detection: TLS and JA3 fingerprint matching that makes a request indistinguishable from a mainstream browser's, human-plausible timing and mouse-movement synthesis in a governed browser session, header ordering and casing that defeats naive bot-signature matching. This Part exists because technical capacity and legitimate authority are two different axes, and a system that lets the first stand in for the second has committed a category error with consequences far more serious than any single failed acquisition — it has built a machine whose default posture, wherever it is technically capable of proceeding, is to proceed, regardless of whether proceeding was ever actually permitted.

9.2 The doctrine's precise statement is this: anti-detection capability exists to let a legitimately authorized acquisition survive an incidental, non-adversarial technical obstacle, and for no other purpose. It does not exist to let Crawl OS reach content that a site operator has deliberately placed behind an access-control decision. The distinction the doctrine turns on is not how sophisticated the obstacle is, and not how easy or hard it would be to defeat technically; it is whether the obstacle represents a decision, made by whoever controls the target, that this content is not to be accessed by automated means, or by this specific requester, or at all without further authorization.

9.3 The operational test that resolves the distinction in practice asks a single question of every case where an acquisition meets resistance: is the barrier in front of this request a deliberate access-control decision by the site operator, or an incidental side effect of a generic anti-automation system that was never configured with this specific, disclosed, legitimate kind of access in mind. An authentication wall is a deliberate decision. A paywall is a deliberate decision. A robots.txt disallow directive addressed to crawlers generally, or to Crawl OS's own declared user agent specifically, is a deliberate decision, stated in exactly the channel the web's own conventions provide for stating it. A content-delivery network's generic bot-mitigation layer blocking every request that lacks a modern TLS fingerprint, applied indiscriminately to legitimate and illegitimate automated traffic alike with no awareness of which is which, is an incidental side effect, and surviving it with a matched fingerprint is not a circumvention of anyone's decision, because no decision about this specific case was ever actually made.

9.4 Robots.txt is the doctrine's primary and default authorization signal, and Crawl OS must consult it before any acquisition proceeds against a domain it has not already cleared through Governance, Safety, and Authorization. A disallow directive is not an obstacle to be routed around by whichever adapter happens to ignore it; it is authorization explicitly withdrawn by the party entitled to withdraw it, and the doctrine treats defeating it, by any technical means, identically to acquiring content from a domain never authorized at all. This holds regardless of whether a specific adapter's default configuration happens to respect robots.txt automatically or requires the Fetch Runtime Fabric to enforce it at the uniform-interface layer; the enforcement point does not change the obligation.

9.5 Authentication introduces a case that must be handled with more precision than a blanket prohibition, because Part III already establishes that Crawl OS legitimately operates within sessions a human has explicitly authorized — the human's own credentials, provided for a specific mission, used to reach content that human account is genuinely entitled to see. The doctrine's rule here is that this authorization is scoped exactly as far as the account holder's own access rights extend, and no further. A mission operating inside a legitimately provided session may retrieve what that account can see; it may not use the session as a foothold to probe for adjacent content the account holder was never granted access to, on the theory that the session's mere existence constitutes a broader authorization than the one actually granted.

9.6 Rate limits, whether stated explicitly by a target or inferred from repeated observed throttling responses, are part of the same authorization boundary rather than a separate courtesy this doctrine treats more lightly. Anti-detection capability must never be deployed to defeat a rate limit, because a rate limit is itself a site operator's stated decision about how much automated access it is willing to permit, and evading it technically is authorization circumvention regardless of how the evasion is technically classified by whichever engine performs it. The doctrine draws no distinction between defeating a rate limit through raw request-volume tricks and defeating one through fingerprint rotation that makes throttled requests appear to originate from many different, unthrottled clients; both are the same violation wearing different technical clothing.

9.7 A worked pair of cases makes the distinction concrete where abstract statement leaves room for motivated misreading. A research site's content-delivery network blocks any client whose TLS handshake does not resemble a current mainstream browser, applied uniformly to all automated traffic with no per-target authorization logic behind it, and the target pages themselves carry no robots.txt disallow, no authentication wall, and no terms-of-service prohibition on automated access; escalating from a basic fetch to curl-impersonate's fingerprint-matched transport here survives an incidental technical barrier and is fully consistent with this doctrine. A second site's robots.txt explicitly disallows automated access to its research archive, and a governed browser agent, technically capable of navigating that archive exactly as a human visitor would, is routed through it anyway on the reasoning that the capability exists and the content is otherwise public; this is authorization circumvention wearing a technical justification, and it is prohibited without exception, regardless of the browser agent's fidelity or the archive's public visibility to human readers.

9.8 The doctrine assigns final authorization judgment exclusively to the Governance, Safety, and Authorization engine named in Part V, and forbids every other engine, including the Fetch Runtime Fabric, the Browser Interaction Fabric, and any adapter possessing anti-detection capability, from making an independent authorization determination of its own. An adapter that observes it is technically capable of proceeding past an obstacle is not thereby licensed to proceed; capability is a fact about the adapter, authorization is a fact about the mission, and only the engine whose charter is authorization may resolve the second fact.

9.9 Where an authorization signal is ambiguous, absent, or simply unknown — a domain with no robots.txt at all, a site whose terms of service are silent on automated access, a newly encountered target with no prior record in site memory — the doctrine's default is that the target is not authorized until Governance, Safety, and Authorization has affirmatively cleared it, mirroring exactly the burden-of-proof inversion Part VIII establishes for content verification. A system that defaults to permitted-until-shown-otherwise will, under delivery pressure, quietly stop asking the question at all, for precisely the reason Part VIII's 8.7 already names for the content case.

9.10 The doctrine's final provision closes the loop back to Part I's founding failure mode: an acquisition obtained by defeating a deliberate authorization decision must never be institutionalized as legitimate evidence, however clean, complete, or well-formed the resulting Evidence Object appears. The provenance chain required by Part VII must record whether an authorization check was performed and what it found, and an object whose authorization check failed, or was never performed, is void as evidence regardless of the content quality it happens to carry — a technically successful but unauthorized acquisition is not a lesser-confidence success. It is not evidence at all.

PART IX FINAL LAW

Technical capacity to evade a barrier and legitimate authorization to pass it are two different axes, and this constitution never lets the first substitute for the second. Anti-detection capability may only be used to survive an incidental, non-adversarial technical obstacle against an already-authorized target; it may never be used to defeat a deliberate access-control decision — a robots.txt disallow, an authentication wall, a paywall, a stated rate limit — regardless of how easily it could be defeated technically. Authorization judgment belongs exclusively to Governance, Safety, and Authorization, defaults to withheld when ambiguous, and an acquisition obtained by circumventing it is void as evidence no matter how clean the result looks.


PART X — THE NO-SECRETS-IN-CORPUS DOCTRINE

10.1 A meaningful fraction of the acquisitions Part IX licenses as legitimate happen inside authenticated sessions, and every one of those sessions is reached through some operational secret — a password, a bearer token, an API key, a session cookie — that made the authentication possible. These secrets are operational instruments for reaching content. They are never themselves the content, and this Part exists because the two are dangerously easy to conflate the moment a debugging capture, a replay log, or a raw header dump gets written to the same persistent store the corpus itself lives in.

10.2 The doctrine's precise statement is this: no raw credential, token, cookie value, or API key may appear in any Evidence Object, knowledge distillation artifact, memory record, or supervisor log that Crawl OS persists, under any circumstance, regardless of whether its presence was deliberate or an accidental byproduct of capturing something else. The Evidence Object's session profile field, defined in Part VII, exists precisely so that a mission can honestly record that an authenticated session was used without ever requiring the credential itself to appear anywhere in the record; a session profile is an opaque reference, never a container for the secret it refers to.

10.3 The doctrine names the specific fields where a secret most often leaks in by accident, because a prohibition stated only in the abstract is easy to satisfy in the fields anyone was already thinking about and violate in the ones nobody was watching: raw HTTP request and response headers captured for later replay or debugging, full request-response dumps kept to diagnose an extraction failure, screenshots taken for visual verification that may incidentally show a logged-in username or an unmasked field, and authentication-failure error messages that sometimes echo back a fragment of the credential that was rejected. Every one of these is a legitimate, useful artifact in its unredacted-adjacent form; none of them is safe to persist without a redaction pass first.

10.4 The doctrine's enforcement mechanism mirrors, and is bound by, the Secret Firewall discipline already governing this entire estate: redact before persisting, never persist raw and clean up later. The Evidence Integrity Fabric must run every field of every Evidence Object it is about to write through a redaction pass before that write occurs, unconditionally, whether or not the specific mission's contract mentions authentication at all — because the cost of a false negative in this specific check, a secret that reaches a corpus other systems already trust and quote from, is categorically higher than the cost of running the pass on missions that never needed it.

10.5 A related obligation covers a different and rarer case: a crawl mission that incidentally discovers a secret the target site itself has exposed — an API key committed into a public repository, a token left visible in unminified page markup, a credential pasted into a public forum post. The doctrine's answer is narrow and firm. The discovery is recorded as a governance finding, routed to Governance, Safety, and Authorization, described in the corpus only in redacted form — that a secret of a given type was found at a given location, never the secret's value. The discovered secret is never used to extend or deepen the current or any future acquisition, and its value is never written anywhere Crawl OS itself persists, because using a site's own exposed secret to reach further content is a Part IX authorization violation wearing a Part X secrets violation's clothing, and this constitution treats it as both at once.

10.6 Session memory, one of the eight specialized memories named across Parts III and IV, may legitimately record that a working session profile exists for a given domain, may record its observed expiry behavior and renewal cadence, and may record which acquisition method successfully authenticates against it. It may never store the credential material that session profile refers to. That material lives exclusively in whatever secret-storage substrate the wider estate already maintains for exactly this purpose, referenced by Crawl OS only through an opaque identifier, and Crawl OS's own memory store is constitutionally forbidden from becoming a second, shadow copy of secrets the estate already manages elsewhere.

10.7 A worked example traces the doctrine through a full mission. A research mission authenticates into a project-management tool using owner-provided credentials to extract a public-facing roadmap page available only to logged-in users. The resulting Evidence Object correctly records session_profile: "pm-tool-readonly-2026-07" and nothing more about the session. The redaction pass, run before any header dump captured for replay purposes is persisted, strips the bearer token that appeared in the captured Authorization header, replacing it with a marked redaction notice recording that a credential was present and was removed. Extraction memory records that this domain requires OAuth-bearer authentication and that this specific session profile satisfies it successfully. At no point does the token's actual value exist anywhere Crawl OS itself writes to disk.

10.8 The doctrine forbids a specific rationalization that recurs whenever this Part meets delivery pressure: that persisting a secret is harmless because the corpus is internal, not exposed to the open internet. An internal corpus is read by future sessions, indexed by graphify per the Tier 1 consumer contract in CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md, and quoted by agents composing answers for a human reader who may not be the mission's original author. There is no reliable boundary inside this estate that guarantees an internal secret never surfaces in an external-facing output once written, and the doctrine treats "internal only" as no defense whatsoever, for the identical reason the estate's own Secret Firewall doctrine treats it as no defense in any other context.

10.9 A precise clarification prevents this doctrine from being misread as conflicting with Part VII's demand for genuinely populated Evidence Object fields: a redacted field, honestly marked as having contained a credential that was removed, is a complete and truthful field. What Part VII forbids, and what this doctrine forbids identically, is a fabricated absence — a record that pretends no credential was ever involved in an acquisition that in fact required one. Redaction with an honest marker satisfies both Parts at once; silent omission satisfies neither.

10.10 Enforcement does not end at the moment of ingestion. The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's evidence quality loop scans persisted Evidence Objects on an ongoing basis for patterns consistent with an unredacted secret slipping through — a field matching a known credential shape, an unusually long opaque string in a field that should be short, a header capture that was never routed through the redaction pass at all. A positive finding halts institutionalization of the affected record immediately and escalates to governance; it is never quietly patched in place as though the leak's exposure window had not already occurred the moment the record was first persisted.

PART X FINAL LAW

Operational secrets reach content; they are never themselves the content, and no raw credential, token, cookie, or key may appear in anything Crawl OS persists, deliberately or by accident, regardless of how internal the corpus appears to be. Every field of every Evidence Object passes through redaction before it is written, unconditionally. A discovered secret is reported in redacted form and never used to extend acquisition. A redacted field honestly marked as redacted is complete; a fabricated absence of a credential that was actually used is a Part VII violation and a Part X violation at once.


PART XI — THE NO-MEGAFORK DOCTRINE

11.1 Part II's adapters-are-not-identity doctrine already named, in passing, the prohibition against vendoring one of the eleven surveyed engines wholesale into Crawl OS's own codebase. This Part exists to give that prohibition the full elaboration a temptation this persistent and this reasonable-seeming in the moment requires, because a rule stated once in a single sentence inside a broader doctrine is exactly the kind of rule that gets quietly set aside the first time a deadline makes the shortcut look cheap.

11.2 The temptation has a precise, recurring shape, and naming it precisely is most of this doctrine's enforcement value: a specific target site defeats an adapter's default configuration in some narrow way, the adapter's own upstream project has no immediate fix, and the fastest available remedy is to copy the adapter's source into Crawl OS's own repository, patch the one function that is wrong, and ship the patched copy going forward. Every step of that sequence up to the last is reasonable engineering triage. The doctrine forbids the terminal step, and only the terminal step.

11.3 Three responses are permitted, in a firm order of preference, mirroring the resolution Part II's 2.5 already sketched. First, contribute the fix upstream to the adapter's own project, so every user of that project, not only this estate, benefits from the correction and the estate's own copy stays current with upstream by construction. Second, where an upstream contribution is not immediately practical, work around the limitation entirely at the adapter boundary — a pre-processing or post-processing wrapper that never touches the adapter's own internals and would need to change only if the adapter's public interface itself changed. Third, where neither of the first two is available, accept the limitation as a documented fact in that adapter's capability declaration and let the Acquisition Strategy Router route around it, to a different rung of the cost ladder or a different adapter entirely, exactly as Part VI's cost-ladder discipline already anticipates for any other observed limitation.

11.4 The third response is not a defeat dressed up as acceptable, and stating this plainly matters because it is the response most likely to feel like giving up under delivery pressure. Part VI established the cost ladder specifically so that alternatives always exist; a documented, observed limitation in one adapter is exactly the kind of falsifiable, recorded reason Part VI's 6.4 requires for a routing decision, and routing around a known limitation to a different engine or a different cost-ladder rung is the system functioning exactly as this constitution designed it to function, not a failure that a fork would have avoided.

11.5 The megafork's cost is not paid once, at the moment of the copy-and-patch, and stating the cost only at that moment is how the shortcut keeps winning the argument against better judgment. It is paid continuously, forever, in three specific and compounding ways: every future upstream security patch becomes a manual diff review against a codebase that has already begun to diverge from its origin; every future upstream feature or performance improvement that this estate might otherwise have adopted for free becomes a merge conflict against a patch this estate's own maintainers wrote under time pressure and may no longer remember the full reasoning behind; and the fork's presence inside Crawl OS's own repository creates a standing, false impression, to any future auditor, that this estate's maintainers understand and are responsible for maintaining code they did not design, tested against edge cases they did not anticipate, in a project whose broader architecture they did not build.

11.6 The doctrine's exception is stated precisely enough that it cannot be stretched to cover the case it exists to forbid: a thin translation shim, converting Crawl OS's own uniform Fetch Runtime Fabric interface into a given adapter's native calling convention, is not a fork. It contains no copied internals from the adapter's own source, and it would need to change only if the adapter's public API itself changed — which is precisely the adapter-replaceability behavior Part II already licenses and requires. The dividing line the doctrine draws is copied internals versus a translation layer sitting entirely outside them; the former is forbidden without exception, the latter is required of every one of the eleven adapters.

11.7 A related and equally forbidden shortcut shares the megafork's underlying economics without literally copying any source: pinning an adapter dependency indefinitely to an old version because a newer release broke a Crawl OS-specific workaround built against the old one's quirks. This is the megafork's economic twin. The maintenance burden of tracking upstream is avoided the identical way, by simply declining to move, and the identical cost accrues — a silently widening gap between what this estate actually runs and what the wider ecosystem, including its security fixes, has already moved past.

11.8 The doctrine's positive obligation is that any limitation serious enough to genuinely tempt a fork must be recorded, with the specific observed failure that provoked the temptation, in that adapter's capability declaration under Part II's 2.3. This is not paperwork for its own sake; it is what makes the temptation and its resolution visible to every future maintainer who encounters the same site or the same class of limitation, so the same triage decision is made once, deliberately, and documented, rather than silently re-litigated under pressure every time the underlying limitation is independently rediscovered.

11.9 A worked example makes the doctrine's application concrete. A specific site's markup structure defeats Crawl4AI's default content-boundary heuristic, misidentifying a wide sidebar of related links as the article's main body and returning that sidebar's link list as the extracted content instead of the actual article text. The forbidden response patches Crawl4AI's internal heuristic function inside a vendored copy of its source, now permanently diverged from upstream. The doctrine's required response records the specific failure pattern in site memory, applies a Crawl OS-side correction entirely at the extraction adapter boundary using a site-specific selector override that the Fetch Runtime Fabric's uniform interface already supports without touching Crawl4AI's own code, and separately files the underlying heuristic gap with the Crawl4AI project upstream, where a permanent fix benefits every user of that project rather than only this one estate's forked copy of it.

11.10 The doctrine closes with the same test Part II established for the identity boundary generally, restated here for the specific case of touching an adapter's own source: before a single line of any of the eleven engines' own code is modified, ask whether Crawl OS's own decisions, schema, or memory genuinely require the change, or whether only the adapter's behavior needs to change. If only the adapter's behavior needs to change, the fix belongs upstream, at the adapter boundary as a wrapper, or accepted as a routed-around limitation — never inside a fork this estate has now committed to maintaining for the rest of that adapter's operational lifetime.

PART XI FINAL LAW

Never fork. Contribute upstream first; work around at the adapter boundary second; accept a documented limitation and route around it third. A thin translation shim that touches no adapter internals is not a fork and is required of every adapter; copying internals to patch them, however narrow the patch, creates a permanent maintenance liability this estate did not design and will pay for indefinitely. Pinning a dependency indefinitely to dodge an upstream break is the same failure by a different name. The test is the same one Part II already established: if only the adapter's behavior needs to change, the fix does not belong inside this repository.


PART XII — THE NO-DEFAULT-BROWSER DOCTRINE

12.1 Part II's 2.8 already forbade the router from developing an implicit favorite among its eleven adapters, and this Part exists because one specific favorite is dramatically more common, and dramatically more dangerous, than any other a router could silently fall into: a full browser render. A full browser works, in the narrow sense of successfully returning usable content, on almost every site on the open web, including the overwhelming majority of static and lightly dynamic pages that never needed one, and it is exactly this universal applicability that makes it the single easiest default to slide into without ever consciously deciding to.

12.2 The doctrine's operational rule is precise: the Acquisition Strategy Router may select a browser rung — the fifth or sixth rung of Part VI's cost ladder — only upon an observed, recorded failure at a cheaper rung against this specific target, or a positive, recorded classification already established in site memory that this site or site class requires it. It may never select a browser rung as the initial default for a site the router has no acquisition history against, regardless of how plausible browser necessity might seem from the site's category, its domain name, or its apparent subject matter.

12.3 The economics that make this doctrine necessary are concrete and worth stating in the same register Part VI used for the cost ladder generally: a full browser render consumes an order of magnitude more compute, memory, and wall-clock time than a basic HTTP fetch for the overwhelming majority of the open web's static and lightly dynamic content, and a system that defaults to a browser converts what could be a cheap, fast, high-throughput acquisition fleet into a slow, expensive, low-throughput one — silently, one individually reasonable-seeming decision at a time, in a way that never appears as a single dramatic failure a supervisor would immediately catch, only as a cost curve that has quietly become far steeper than the actual target mix ever required.

12.4 A second cost sits beside the raw compute cost and is easy to underweight because it rarely appears in a simple cost comparison: a browser session is stateful, materially harder to sandbox cleanly than a stateless fetch, more likely to leave an orphaned process behind if a mission terminates abnormally mid-render, and represents a larger attack surface and a larger set of things that can silently go wrong mid-acquisition than a bare HTTP request ever can. The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's runtime health loop must specifically watch for exactly this class of failure — an accumulating count of orphaned browser processes across missions is itself a finding, independent of whether any individual mission using those processes ever reported an error.

12.5 The triggers that do legitimately warrant escalation to a browser rung, restated here from Part VI to make this doctrine self-contained rather than requiring a cross-reference for its own core rule, are: an initial HTTP fetch returning content materially shorter than this site's typical baseline as recorded in site memory; a target confirmed, by observation rather than assumption, to be a genuine single-page application whose initial HTTP response carries no meaningful server-rendered content; a target requiring multi-step interaction — form submission, multi-page navigation, session-dependent state — for which no simpler HTTP-only path exists; or an explicit requirement in the mission's own contract for visual capture, which only a rendered session can produce.

12.6 The doctrine's hardest test arrives with a genuinely unfamiliar site, precisely because this is where the temptation to default is strongest and the router has the least evidence to resist it with. A mission encountering a site with no prior entry in site memory must still attempt the cheapest plausible rung first and observe what actually comes back before escalating, resisting the intuitive but untested belief that an unfamiliar site is more likely than a familiar one to require a browser. Unfamiliarity is not evidence of dynamic rendering; treating it as such is precisely the untested-prediction failure Part VI's 6.4 already names and prohibits, applied here to the single rung where the temptation to make that exact mistake is strongest.

12.7 The doctrine makes an explicit corollary of its own core rule: once a site has been correctly classified through actual observation as requiring a browser rung, site memory should record that classification, so that future missions against the same site are not forced to re-litigate the question at the cheap rung's expense on every single occasion. The doctrine's target is unjustified, unobserved defaulting, not the elimination of legitimate, memory-informed fast-pathing directly to a browser rung once real evidence already supports that classification for a given site.

12.8 A worked example distinguishes the forbidden pattern from the required one at the scale where the doctrine actually matters most, a multi-site mission rather than a single acquisition. A mission targets fifty pages spread across ten previously unknown documentation sites. The forbidden pattern routes all fifty acquisitions through a governed browser agent from the outset, on the generalized and untested reasoning that "documentation sites sometimes use client-side rendering frameworks." The doctrine's required pattern attempts a basic fetch against each of the ten sites' first page, observes that eight return full, well-structured, server-rendered content while two return near-empty shells consistent with a genuine client-rendered single-page application, escalates only those two specific sites to a browser rung, and records both classifications, the eight fetch-sufficient sites and the two browser-required sites, in site memory for every future mission against any of the ten.

12.9 A limit on the doctrine's own scope must be stated to prevent it from being over-read: this Part does not forbid browser use, and it does not treat a correctly escalated browser acquisition as any kind of violation. A mission whose own contract explicitly requires visual verification, or whose target is genuinely and observably a single-page application, is using the browser rung exactly as Part VI's cost ladder intends it to be used, and nothing in this doctrine penalizes that acquisition or treats its cost as waste. What this doctrine penalizes is exclusively the unexamined, unobserved default — browser use as habit rather than browser use as a justified, evidence-backed escalation.

12.10 Enforcement mirrors Part VI's 6.8 precisely, applied to this single most common failure point: the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's mission-watch loop specifically flags any mission, or any router configuration across missions, whose browser-rung usage rate is inconsistent with the historical baseline for its actual target mix, surfacing the pattern as a finding for review rather than permitting an unaudited habit to compound silently, mission after mission, until the entire fleet's cost profile has drifted far past anything the underlying targets ever actually required.

PART XII FINAL LAW

A full browser works on almost every site, which is exactly why it is the single most dangerous default a router can silently fall into. The router may select a browser rung only upon an observed failure at a cheaper rung or a memory-recorded classification already established through real observation — never as the initial default for an unfamiliar target, and never because a site's category merely seems likely to need one. Unfamiliarity is not evidence of dynamic rendering. A correctly escalated browser acquisition is never a violation; an unexamined one, compounding silently across a fleet, is exactly the failure this doctrine exists to catch before it becomes the system's unaudited normal.


PART XIII — THE TEN PRODUCTION REALITY GATES

13.1 Parts I, VIII, and IX have each, in isolation, argued that a single dimension of apparent success is insufficient on its own: clean output is not acquisition success, per Part I; acquisition is not content success, per Part VIII; technical success is not authorization success, per Part IX. This Part collects every dimension of what sufficient evidence actually requires into one ordered, mechanical, checkable sequence — the ten Production Reality Gates. A mission's output is complete only when every gate its Mission Contract requires has actually been checked and has actually passed; no gate may be skipped because an earlier gate passed, and no earlier gate's pass licenses skipping a later one.

13.2 Gate A, Acquisition Reality: did the acquisition attempt complete without a transport-level failure — no connection error, no timeout, a status code actually recorded, real bytes actually received. This is the narrowest gate, established already in Part VIII's 8.3, and it is necessary but never alone sufficient; a fully successful HTTP transaction can fail every gate that follows it.

13.3 Gate B, Content Reality: does the retrieved content, examined per the specific checks Part VIII's 8.5 requires — topical match, title-path consistency, length-baseline consistency, and where required a visual check — plausibly match what the mission actually sought, rather than a consent wall, a paywall preview, or a soft-404 rendered with a success status.

13.4 Gate C, Authorization Reality: was the acquisition performed within a boundary Governance, Safety, and Authorization actually cleared — robots.txt respected, any authentication scoped to what the account holder is genuinely entitled to see, any stated or inferred rate limit honored — per the full doctrine in Part IX. An acquisition that fails this gate is void as evidence regardless of how cleanly it passes every other gate.

13.5 Gate D, Extraction Fidelity: did the structured extraction preserve the page's actual structure — tables remaining tabular, lists remaining ordered or unordered as the source intended, headings remaining hierarchical — rather than collapsing structured content into an undifferentiated wall of prose a downstream reader cannot distinguish from freeform text.

13.6 Gate E, Provenance Completeness: are every one of the Evidence Object's required fields from Part VII genuinely populated with values actually derived from this specific acquisition, with no field silently omitted and no field carrying a plausible-looking but non-derived value — the fabrication prohibition of Part VII's 7.9 restated here as a checkable gate rather than only as a prose rule.

13.7 Gate F, Secret Hygiene: has every field passed the redaction pass Part X requires, with no raw credential, token, or cookie value surviving anywhere in the object being passed downstream.

13.8 Gate G, Confidence Honesty: does the object's confidence score actually derive from the specific signals the Evidence Integrity Fabric examined for this acquisition — the Gate B checks, any anomaly signals, any partial-content indicators — rather than a default, an optimistic round number, or a value copied forward from a previous, different acquisition of the same source.

13.9 Gate H, Freshness Sufficiency: does the content's apparent date, as distinct from its acquisition date, satisfy the freshness requirement the mission's own contract stated, or, where it does not, has that gap been honestly flagged rather than silently accepted as though the content were current.

13.10 Gate I, Coverage Sufficiency: did the mission actually acquire the depth and breadth of sources its own contract specified, or did the acquisition silently truncate — stopping partway through a longer result set, sampling a handful of sources where the contract specified an exhaustive sweep — without that truncation being recorded as a deviation the mission's consumer must be told about.

13.11 Gate J, Reusability Readiness: is the evidence, having passed every gate before it, actually in a schema and format the downstream Knowledge Distillation Fabric and the estate's broader consuming systems can genuinely ingest, rather than technically complete but structurally incompatible with what its declared consumers expect.

13.12 The gates are cumulative, not independent, and this Part's single most important operational rule is that no gate's pass may be inferred from another gate's pass. Gate A passing does not imply Gate B; Gate B passing does not imply Gate C; a mission is not permitted to check only the gates its author found convenient and treat the unchecked remainder as passed by default, because a default-pass posture on any gate is the identical failure Part VIII's 8.7 already named for content and Part IX's 9.9 already named for authorization, generalized here to every one of the ten.

13.13 A worked example traces one acquisition through all ten gates to make the cumulative requirement concrete rather than abstract. A mission acquires a vendor's pricing page. Gate A passes: a 200 status, real bytes, no transport error. Gate B passes: the content's topic matches, the title is consistent with the URL path, and the length is consistent with this site's typical pricing-page baseline in site memory. Gate C passes: the page carries no robots.txt disallow and required no authentication. Gate D passes: the pricing table's rows and columns are preserved as a genuine table in the extracted representation, not flattened into a paragraph. Gate E passes: every Evidence Object field is populated with values actually derived from this acquisition. Gate F passes trivially, since no credential was ever involved. Gate G passes: the confidence score reflects the specific signals actually examined, scored high because every check above passed cleanly. Gate H fails: the mission's contract required pricing data no older than seven days, and the page's apparent last-modified date is forty days old, with no fresher version reachable through any rung of the cost ladder. The honest outcome is not a silent pass; it is a mission output reporting nine of ten gates passed, Gate H failed with the specific reason recorded, and a confidence-qualified claim rather than an unqualified one — precisely the outcome Part VIII's 8.8 already described as the correct handling of a real, honestly reported limitation.

13.14 The gates compose explicitly with the verdict ontology defined fully in Part XIX: a mission's final verdict is derived mechanically from which gates passed and which did not, rather than assigned by whichever engine happens to produce the mission's final summary, and Part XIX's ontology is the vocabulary in which a gate failure like Gate H's in 13.13 is actually reported to a downstream consumer.

13.15 Not every gate is equally load-bearing for every mission, and this Part's closing provision states precisely how that variability is handled without reopening the default-pass loophole 13.12 already forbids. A mission's own contract may declare a specific gate not applicable to its stated evidence requirement — a mission needing only to confirm that a canonical reference page still exists has no meaningful freshness requirement for content that rarely if ever changes, and Gate H may be marked not applicable rather than passed. Not applicable is a distinct, honestly recorded status, never silently collapsed into passed, and it may only be declared in the mission's own contract at compilation time, per Part III, never invoked after the fact by whichever engine finds a given gate inconvenient to satisfy for the acquisition actually performed. A mission report listing nine gates passed and one gate marked not applicable is claiming something different, and independently checkable as such, from a report listing all ten gates passed outright, and the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's evidence quality loop audits not-applicable declarations exactly as it audits passes, watching specifically for a pattern of missions marking the same gate not applicable across a target class where the gate would, if actually checked, plausibly fail.

PART XIII FINAL LAW

No acquisition is complete on the strength of one gate's pass, and no gate's pass may be inferred from another's. Ten gates — Acquisition Reality, Content Reality, Authorization Reality, Extraction Fidelity, Provenance Completeness, Secret Hygiene, Confidence Honesty, Freshness Sufficiency, Coverage Sufficiency, and Reusability Readiness — are checked independently and reported cumulatively, and a mission that reports nine gates passed and one gate failed, with the specific failure named, has done its job correctly. A mission that silently treats an unchecked gate as passed has not.


PART XIV — INSTITUTIONALIZATION IS NOT AUTOMATIC

14.1 Passing every Production Reality Gate in Part XIII establishes that a piece of evidence is trustworthy. It does not establish that the evidence is worth keeping forever. This Part exists to separate two judgments that are easy to collapse into one under the pressure of a pipeline that already did the hard work of verification and would rather not do the additional work of deciding what to do with the result: verification answers whether a claim is true, institutionalization answers whether it is worth remembering, and this constitution requires both, in that order, decided by different logic.

14.2 The default posture is stated as plainly as this constitution states anything: passing all ten gates makes a piece of evidence eligible for institutionalization. It does not make institutionalization automatic. A gate-passing acquisition that answers a narrow, one-time question relevant only to the single mission that requested it — confirming a specific page still returns a success status, checking today's specific numeric value on a page that changes hourly — is fully verified and correctly disposable once its mission concludes, and institutionalizing it anyway does not make the corpus more valuable; it makes the corpus larger and less signal-dense. A corpus that grows by accumulating everything ever verified, whether or not it will ever be consulted again, is not the institutional memory Part III describes. It is a log.

14.3 The judgment of what earns promotion belongs, per Part III's 3.9, to Crawl OS specifically and to no other system, because only Crawl OS holds the provenance record that makes the judgment auditable — but the judgment itself is distinct from the question the Evidence Integrity Fabric already answered. It asks whether this evidence, or a claim built on it, will plausibly be needed again, by this mission's own consumer or by some future mission this constitution cannot yet name, in a form durable enough to be worth the corpus's ongoing maintenance cost.

14.4 Four criteria operationalize that question, none individually decisive, considered together. Reusability: does the evidence answer a question with a shelf life longer than the single mission that asked it, rather than a transient, timestamped fact useful only in the instant it was acquired. Generality: does the evidence generalize beyond the specific mission's narrow framing, in a form a differently framed future mission could still make use of. Provenance strength: does the evidence's Evidence Object carry a complete, high-confidence provenance chain, since institutionalizing a weakly provenanced object compounds a small present uncertainty into a durable, harder-to-audit future liability. Redundancy: does the corpus already hold equivalent evidence from an equally or more authoritative source, in which case institutionalizing a duplicate adds maintenance cost without adding signal.

14.5 A worked distinction separates two acquisitions from the same mission to make the criteria concrete. A mission researching a software project's licensing terms acquires the project's license file, an artifact that changes rarely, generalizes well beyond this single mission's question, carries strong provenance, and has no existing equivalent already in the corpus — a clear institutionalization candidate by all four criteria. The same mission also acquires the project's current public star count, an artifact whose value is accurate only at the instant of acquisition, generalizes to nothing beyond that instant, and would be actively misleading if institutionalized and later read as though still current — a piece of evidence that correctly passes every Production Reality Gate and just as correctly is never institutionalized.

14.6 The doctrine's relationship to failed or gate-failing acquisitions must be stated with the same precision Part VII's 7.6 already applied to the Evidence Object itself: a well-formed record of a failure — an honestly low confidence score, a specific recorded anomaly, a Gate H freshness failure precisely reported — is not disqualified from institutionalization merely because it reports a failure rather than a success. Negative memory, elaborated fully in Part XXIII, is itself an institutionalized category, and a well-documented failed acquisition against a specific site can be exactly as valuable to a future mission as a well-documented successful one, because it prevents that future mission from repeating an attempt this one already knows will not work.

14.7 The doctrine forbids a specific failure mode, named because it is the natural resting state of a pipeline under no active governance: institutionalization by default, in which every gate-passing Evidence Object is promoted simply because promoting it requires no additional decision and declining to promote it would require someone to actively choose not to. This is precisely the corpus-as-log failure named in 14.2, and it is the institutionalization judgment's own version of Part VI's cost-ladder drift — not a single dramatic mistake, but an accumulating, unaudited default that silently degrades the corpus's signal density one convenient non-decision at a time.

14.8 The doctrine equally forbids the opposite failure, institutionalization withheld by excessive caution, in which a maintainer under corpus-quality pressure declines to promote genuinely reusable, well-provenanced evidence because the promotion decision itself feels risky to make. A Crawl OS implementation that institutionalizes nothing is not disciplined; it has quietly abandoned the owned obligation Part III's 3.9 assigns it, producing missions that verify evidence correctly and then let every verified fact evaporate the moment the mission concludes, forcing every future mission to re-acquire and re-verify the identical evidence from scratch.

14.9 The Knowledge Distillation Fabric, per its Part V charter, is the mechanism through which an institutionalization judgment is actually executed once made: it consumes the specific Evidence Package marked for promotion and produces the compact, cited artifact that actually enters the estate's durable memory, and this sequencing — judgment first, distillation second — matters because a distillation process applied to evidence that was never actually judged worth keeping produces a compact, well-cited, and entirely unnecessary artifact, compounding the corpus-as-log failure rather than correcting it.

14.10 A final provision ties institutionalization back to the amendment discipline of Part XXIV: an institutionalization judgment, once made and acted on, is not permanent in the sense of being unrevisitable. A Temporal Truth Engine finding of semantic change, per Part III's 3.6, can require an institutionalized fact to be superseded, corrected, or explicitly retired, and the judgment that something was worth institutionalizing when it was acquired does not obligate the corpus to preserve it unchanged forever once the world it described has moved on.

14.11 The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's fifth loop, institutional learning, per its Part V charter, closes this Part's own accountability gap: it periodically re-examines a sample of past institutionalization judgments against outcomes actually observed since — was a promoted fact ever subsequently queried by another mission, did a piece of evidence declined for promotion turn out to be needed again and require costly re-acquisition, did a promoted fact go stale without its supersession being caught. This is not a re-litigation of every individual judgment; it is a standing check on whether the four criteria in 14.4 are being applied with the discipline this Part requires or have quietly drifted toward either of the two failure modes named in 14.7 and 14.8, and a drift finding from this loop is itself the trigger for revisiting how institutionalization judgments are being made across the family, not only which specific one was wrong.

PART XIV FINAL LAW

Verification answers whether a claim is true. Institutionalization answers whether it is worth remembering. Passing every Production Reality Gate makes evidence eligible for promotion; it never makes promotion automatic. Reusability, generality, provenance strength, and redundancy against what the corpus already holds decide the second question, and a well-documented failure can pass all four criteria as surely as a well-documented success. A corpus that promotes everything it verifies is a log, not a memory, and a corpus that promotes nothing has abandoned the obligation Part III assigned it.


PART XV — THE COMPOSITION BOUNDARY WITH CO-01

15.1 Every engine in this family consumes tokens, compute, and time, and every one of those resources is finite and already governed, estate-wide, by the Cognitive OS economics layer, sealed as CO-01 before this family existed. This Part exists to state precisely where Crawl OS's own cost logic ends and CO-01's begins, because the two are adjacent enough, and both concerned enough with the idea of cost, that conflating them was flagged as a live risk at this family's own STOP #1 sizing pass, not a hypothetical one.

15.2 CO-01 owns general operating economics for the estate as a whole: session-level token budgets, cross-project cost allocation, the routing of a task to a cheaper or more capable model class based on the task's nature, and the estate-wide accounting that answers how much a given session, project, or day has cost against what ceiling. This is estate-wide territory that predates Crawl OS and would exist identically if Crawl OS did not.

15.3 Crawl OS's own Part VI cost ladder owns something narrower and specific to acquisition alone: given a single acquisition target, which of seven rungs — cache hit through authorized human intervention — is the least costly one plausibly sufficient for this specific mission's evidence requirement. This is not a budget. It is a routing decision, made once per acquisition, answering a question CO-01 was never built to answer, because CO-01 has no visibility into whether a specific web page requires a browser render or a basic fetch.

15.4 The fold decision, made explicitly at this family's Fase 0 and binding on every future Crawl OS build, resolves the overlap by elimination rather than by negotiated boundary: the originally proposed dataset governing cost, performance, and resource discipline does not exist as a separate Crawl OS dataset. Its substantive content — the general principle that acquisition cost must be proportional to acquisition value, restated for CO-01's own audience — becomes a new Part authored inside CO-01 itself, and no Crawl OS dataset, including this one, may reintroduce general budget, cost-ceiling, or token-accounting language that duplicates CO-01's own territory under a different name.

15.5 The composition point that survives the fold is a data relationship, not a shared vocabulary: every Crawl OS mission's actual resource consumption — tokens spent compiling its contract, compute spent on each rung of the cost ladder it actually climbed, wall-clock time consumed — is reported upward as input to CO-01's own estate-wide accounting, exactly as any other estate subsystem's consumption is reported. Crawl OS is a cost center CO-01 tracks; it is never a second, competing cost-governance authority operating in parallel to CO-01's own.

15.6 A precise test resolves any future ambiguity about which side of this boundary a new proposal belongs on, mirroring the ownership test Parts II and IV already established: does the proposal concern a decision specific to a single acquisition — which rung, which adapter, which target — or does it concern resource allocation across missions, across projects, or across the estate as a whole. The former is Crawl OS's cost ladder. The latter is CO-01's operating economics, regardless of how naturally the idea of cost appears in a description of either.

15.7 A worked case demonstrates the test resolving a genuinely plausible-sounding proposal correctly. Suppose a future contributor proposes a governor that caps total spend across all concurrent Crawl OS missions estate-wide and reallocates budget between missions by priority. Applied against the test in 15.6, this is not a single-acquisition routing decision; it is cross-mission resource allocation, which is CO-01's territory by definition, and the correct disposition is not to build a Crawl OS-owned governor but to extend CO-01's existing operating-economics logic to treat Crawl OS missions as a governed cost center alongside every other estate workload it already governs.

15.8 A second worked case demonstrates the opposite resolution. Suppose a proposal names a mechanism that, given a single mission's already-allocated budget, orders that mission's own frontier of candidate sources by expected information gain per unit of remaining budget. This concerns one mission's own internal sequencing of its own already-allocated resources, never reaching into cross-mission or cross-project allocation, and belongs to the Resource Discovery and Frontier Engine's Part V charter, composing with, but never absorbing, CO-01's separate allocation decision that set the budget this sequencing merely spends within.

15.9 The composition boundary is bidirectional in one further respect worth naming explicitly: CO-01 does not reach downward into Crawl OS's own routing logic any more than Crawl OS reaches upward into CO-01's allocation logic. CO-01 may set the ceiling a mission operates under; it does not decide which rung of the cost ladder a specific acquisition should use to stay under that ceiling, exactly as Crawl OS does not decide how much of the estate's total budget a given project deserves relative to another project's needs.

15.10 This Part's governing discipline, restated once for permanence, is the same discipline Part IV closed with for every other neighboring system: a live boundary, re-checked against every future proposal, not a static map. Any future Crawl OS proposal whose description leans on cost, budget, spend, or token language must be run through the test in 15.6 before it is built, and a proposal that fails the test belongs, by construction, inside CO-01, never inside this family under a name chosen to make it sound like acquisition-specific routing when it is not.

15.11 The fold's original composition point named three concerns together — cost, performance, and resource governance — and this Part closes by treating the latter two with the same discipline already applied to cost, rather than leaving them as an implied afterthought. Performance, in the estate-wide sense of latency budgets, throughput targets, and concurrency ceilings across every workload the estate runs, is CO-01's territory for the identical reason cost is: it concerns the estate as a whole, not one acquisition. Crawl OS's own performance concerns are narrower and already owned elsewhere in this constitution — a single mission's own latency, governed by the cost ladder's rung selection in Part VI, and the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's runtime health loop watching a single mission's own execution per Part V's charter for that engine. Neither is a claim on estate-wide performance governance.

15.12 Resource governance, the fold's third named concern, resolves identically once the same test is applied. General compute allocation, proxy bandwidth budgeting across every estate workload, and storage quota management belong to whatever estate-wide infrastructure governance CO-01 or its siblings already provide; Crawl OS's own resource footprint — how many concurrent browser sessions a single mission may hold open, how much proxy bandwidth one acquisition may consume before the strategy router is required to de-escalate — is a mission-scoped operational parameter the Acquisition Strategy Router enforces directly, reported upward as consumption exactly as 15.5 already describes, never governed by a second, Crawl OS-owned resource authority operating beside CO-01's.

PART XV FINAL LAW

CO-01 owns estate-wide operating economics; Crawl OS's cost ladder owns a single acquisition's routing decision, and neither reaches into the other's territory. The composition is a data relationship, not a shared vocabulary: Crawl OS reports its consumption upward as one governed cost center among many, and never becomes a second, competing cost authority. No Crawl OS dataset may reintroduce general budget or token-accounting language under a name chosen to make it sound like routing when it is allocation.


PART XVI — THE COMPOSITION BOUNDARY WITH GK-04

16.1 Provenance, Part VII's central obligation, does not terminate inside a Crawl OS-only data store forever. Once a specific piece of institutionalized evidence is woven into the estate's broader knowledge graph, Crawl OS's provenance chain field has a natural counterpart already living elsewhere in this estate — the typed edges the graph-writing engine, GK-04, maintains for every other kind of estate-internal relationship. This Part draws the boundary between Crawl OS's own provenance record and GK-04's own graph, the composition CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md already commits dataset ten to honoring.

16.2 GK-04 owns the estate's typed knowledge graph write-back: the general mechanism by which any estate subsystem registers a durable, queryable relationship — one artifact depending on another, one decision superseding another, one dataset composing with another — as a typed edge between two graph nodes, independent of which subsystem produced the relationship being recorded.

16.3 Crawl OS does not own a second, parallel graph. Its Evidence Object's provenance chain, per Part VII's 7.5, is the source-of-truth record for a single acquisition's own lineage — which mission requested it, which frontier item it satisfies, which prior acquisition it supersedes — and that record is Crawl OS's own, held in Crawl OS's own evidence memory. What Crawl OS composes with GK-04 for is narrower: once a piece of evidence earns institutionalization per Part XIV, the relationship between that evidence and whatever estate node it now informs deserves to exist as a typed, graph-queryable edge, not only as a fact buried inside a Crawl OS-internal record no other estate subsystem's graph traversal would ever discover.

16.4 The interface, precisely: an institutionalized Evidence Object triggers a single typed edge write, through GK-04's own writer, naming the evidence's origin as crawl_os, referencing the specific Evidence Object by identifier, and naming whatever estate node — a dataset, a decision record, a module — the evidence now supports. Crawl OS constructs the edge's content. GK-04 owns the writing, the storage, and the graph's own query surface; Crawl OS never writes directly into the graph's own storage layer, exactly as no other estate subsystem does.

16.5 CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md already states the honest current status of this interface precisely, and this Part restates it as binding rather than aspirational: today, the composition point is declared and the edge shape is specified, but the writer does not yet exist, so no crawl_os-origin edge has yet been written. This Part does not claim otherwise. Its obligation is that when the writer exists, Crawl OS's institutionalization judgment from Part XIV is the trigger, and the edge it produces carries an origin marker distinguishing Crawl OS-sourced graph knowledge from every other subsystem's contribution to the same graph.

16.6 The doctrine forbids Crawl OS from reimplementing any portion of GK-04's own responsibility under a different name, a temptation this constitution treats with the same severity Part XI treats forking an adapter's source: no Crawl OS-internal graph, no Crawl OS-specific query layer duplicating what GK-04 already provides, no shadow index of estate-wide relationships maintained separately because waiting for GK-04's writer to exist felt slower than building a smaller, narrower version inside this family. A narrower, Crawl-OS-only need is served by Crawl OS's own evidence memory; an estate-wide, graph-queryable relationship is GK-04's territory without exception.

16.7 The composition is genuinely narrow, not full subsumption, and stating the limit precisely prevents this Part from being misread as ceding provenance itself to GK-04. Crawl OS's own provenance chain remains the authoritative, complete record of a single acquisition's full lineage — GK-04's typed edge is a compact, graph-queryable pointer into that record, not a replacement for it, and a future query against GK-04's graph that finds a crawl_os-origin edge is expected to follow that edge back into Crawl OS's own Evidence Object for the acquisition's full provenance detail, exactly as Part III's 3.10 already obligates Crawl OS to supply on demand.

16.8 A worked example anticipates the composition once the writer exists. A mission institutionalizes a project's documented rate limits, extracted and verified per every gate in Part XIII, as durable knowledge feeding a future Crawl OS mission against the same project. The institutionalized fact enters the estate's broader documentation memory as a node. GK-04's writer records a typed edge from that node back to the specific crawl_os Evidence Object that produced it, tagged with its origin. A future estate query traversing that node, performed by a system with no knowledge of Crawl OS's own internals, discovers the edge, follows it, and can reconstruct exactly which mission, which URL, which acquisition method produced the fact it is now relying on, without that querying system needing to understand any of Crawl OS's own architecture to do so.

16.9 This Part's relationship to the broader Tier 1 graphify consumption already verified for every Crawl OS dataset, per CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md's invocation model, must be kept distinct from the GK-04 composition described here: graphify's indexing of this dataset's own text file as a node is a general mechanism applying uniformly to every dataset in this estate, sealed and operating today; GK-04's typed edge write-back for institutionalized evidence is a narrower, still-planned mechanism specific to the content Crawl OS's missions produce, not to the dataset text describing Crawl OS's own architecture. Citing the first as evidence for the second would misstate what is actually live.

16.10 The boundary this Part draws closes with the same discipline every composition boundary in this constitution shares: Crawl OS interfaces with GK-04 by producing well-formed edge content and handing it to GK-04's own writer; it does not, at any point, absorb GK-04's storage, query surface, or general graph-maintenance responsibility, and any future proposal that would have Crawl OS maintain its own competing graph structure fails this Part's test on inspection, regardless of how convenient a self-contained graph might seem while GK-04's own writer remains unbuilt.

16.11 The interim state, before GK-04's writer exists, is not a silent gap this constitution leaves unaddressed; it is handled by exactly the two-tier invocation model CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md already establishes for the whole family. Tier 1, graphify's own indexing of this dataset's text as a node, is live today and requires nothing further to hold true. The GK-04 typed-edge composition described in this Part is recorded, honestly, in the PLANNED vocabulary that document already uses, naming the future writer's module path as the downstream consumer rather than asserting a REACHABLE status the repository's own liveness gate, scoped only to modules/*.py, has no mechanism to certify for a prose dataset in the first place. A future contributor who builds the writer inherits an obligation from this Part, not a discovery: the edge shape in 16.4 is already specified, and building the writer to match that shape, rather than designing a new one at that time, is what makes this Part's declaration today a binding commitment rather than an empty aspiration with nothing behind it.

16.12 A final distinction closes this Part and prevents its own worked example in 16.8 from being misread as evidence the composition already operates: 16.8 describes what the composition will do once built, in the same anticipatory register Part XVIII's own worked cases will use for capabilities this family has not yet reached in its build order. A dataset that could not distinguish between a composition it has verified and a composition it has only designed would itself have committed the exact confusion Part I's founding failure mode names for acquisition, applied here to the corpus's own claims about itself, and this constitution holds its own self-description to the identical standard it holds every acquisition to.

PART XVI FINAL LAW

Crawl OS's provenance chain is the authoritative record of a single acquisition's lineage; GK-04's typed edge is a compact, graph-queryable pointer into that record, never a replacement for it. Crawl OS constructs edge content; GK-04 owns the writing, storage, and query surface, without exception. The interface is declared and specified today; the writer is not yet built, and this constitution states that gap honestly rather than claiming a composition that does not yet operate.


PART XVII — THE COMPOSITION BOUNDARY WITH DRK-03

17.1 Part V's charter for Evaluation and Reality Gates already commits that engine to composing with the decision-review family's evidence-burden vocabulary rather than inventing a parallel one, and CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md already names the specific artifact that vocabulary lives in: DRK-03. This Part makes that composition precise enough to build against, because a charter that merely says "compose, don't duplicate" without naming which side owns which piece of the shared territory is not yet a boundary, only an intention.

17.2 DRK-03 owns general decision-review across the estate: the confidence and sufficiency logic that determines, for any estate decision in any domain, how much evidence of what quality justifies what level of confidence in a claim, expressed in a shared vocabulary — named CONNECT in that family's own terms — that every estate subsystem citing a confidence or sufficiency requirement is expected to use rather than reinvent.

17.3 Crawl OS's own Reality Gates, defined fully in Part XIII, own something narrower and domain-specific: the ten acquisition-specific checks that determine whether a given piece of externally acquired content is trustworthy at all. These checks produce raw signals — a topical match, a title-path consistency finding, a length-baseline comparison — that are inputs to a confidence judgment, not themselves a confidence judgment expressed in the estate's own shared vocabulary.

17.4 The composition point, stated as an architectural fact rather than an aspiration: Gate G's confidence score, per Part XIII's 13.8, is computed using DRK-03's own scoring logic, fed the acquisition-specific signals only Crawl OS can supply, with DRK-03 remaining the sole owner of how those signals map to a final confidence number expressed in CONNECT's own terms. Crawl OS never defines its own, second confidence scale running in parallel to the one the rest of the estate already uses.

17.5 A precise test resolves any future ambiguity about which side of this boundary a given piece of logic belongs to, in the same form Parts II, IV, and XV already established for their own boundaries: does a proposal concern the domain-specific signals an acquisition produces, or does it concern how those signals, once produced, are weighed into an overall confidence or sufficiency verdict. The former belongs to Crawl OS's Reality Gates regardless of which family eventually consumes the signal; the latter belongs to DRK-03 regardless of which family produced the raw input feeding it.

17.6 A worked example applies the test to a concrete mission requirement. A mission's own contract states that a claim may only be institutionalized at ninety percent confidence or higher. The number ninety percent, what it means, and the logic determining that a claim scored below it must be marked insufficient rather than merely lower-confidence, all come from DRK-03's own sufficiency-threshold model, shared identically with every other estate decision that cites a confidence requirement in any domain. Crawl OS supplies the Gate B, D, and H signals as inputs to that shared model and never redefines, for its own convenience, what ninety-percent sufficiency actually means.

17.7 This family's own STOP #1 sizing pass already resolved a case worth preserving here as evidence that composition claims in this constitution are audited rather than assumed: the originally proposed Crawl Intent and Mission Compilation dataset's suggested composition with DRK-03 turned out, on inspection, to be a false positive triggered by the shared word evidence rather than any genuine shared territory, and was correctly rejected rather than built. Not every family that uses a word DRK-03 also uses is thereby composing with it.

17.8 The doctrine forbids Crawl OS from silently drifting its own Gate G computation away from DRK-03's vocabulary over time, mirroring the drift concern Part VII's 7.8 raises for the Evidence Object schema generally. A change to how Crawl OS computes or expresses confidence is itself a Part XXIV amendment event, precisely because it risks breaking a shared vocabulary that every other estate consumer of confidence scores, not only Crawl OS's own downstream systems, already relies on holding a stable meaning.

17.9 The relationship this Part draws is deliberately asymmetric, and the asymmetry is the point rather than an oversight: DRK-03 needs to know nothing about acquisition, adapters, or the cost ladder to perform its own general function, while Crawl OS needs DRK-03's vocabulary specifically so that its own Gate G output can be compared, on equal footing, against every other domain's confidence claims across the estate. Composition runs in one direction, Crawl OS importing DRK-03's logic, and never the reverse, because only one of the two families has a genuine need for the other's specific machinery. A future proposal that has Crawl OS export its own acquisition-specific signals as a generic input format DRK-03 must learn to consume, rather than Crawl OS calling into DRK-03's own existing interface, has inverted the asymmetry for no defensible reason and should be read as a sign the proposal misunderstands which side of this composition actually depends on the other.

17.10 CONSUMER_DECLARATIONS.md's own evidence standard for this composition, once the downstream Reality Gates module is built, is stated precisely so a future maintainer knows exactly what to verify: its Gate G implementation must literally import the confidence-computation logic from the decision-review family's own module rather than defining an equivalent function locally, and a diff between the imported logic and any hypothetical Crawl-OS-local reimplementation is, by this Part's own design, the concrete test that the composition point is holding in practice rather than merely declared in prose, exactly as GK-04's edge shape in Part XVI's 16.4 supplies its own equivalent concrete test for a different composition boundary.

17.11 A worked failure this doctrine specifically anticipates makes the drift risk concrete rather than hypothetical. A future contributor, finding DRK-03's own confidence function's calling convention momentarily inconvenient to integrate against under delivery pressure, writes a local approximation inside Crawl OS's own Evidence Integrity Fabric that produces similar-looking numbers for the common case, intending to swap in the genuine import later once time permits. This is the identical temporary-special-case failure Part XI's no-megafork doctrine names for an adapter's source, applied here to a shared vocabulary rather than to vendored code: the approximation drifts from DRK-03's genuine logic exactly where the two disagree, silently, at precisely the edge cases where the difference actually matters to a downstream decision. It is caught only if the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's evidence quality loop specifically samples Gate G scores against DRK-03's own genuine computation and flags the divergence — the same enforcement pattern Part VI's 6.8 already applies to cost-ladder drift, applied here to vocabulary drift instead, and a contributor who ships the approximation without also wiring that comparison has shipped exactly the kind of unaudited default this constitution treats as a defect regardless of how close the approximation's numbers happened to land.

PART XVII FINAL LAW

DRK-03 owns general confidence and sufficiency logic, expressed in a shared vocabulary the whole estate uses; Crawl OS's Reality Gates own the acquisition-specific signals that feed it, never a second, parallel confidence scale of their own. The test is constant across every boundary this constitution draws: does the proposal concern the raw signal, or the judgment made from it. A shared word is not evidence of a shared territory, and this family's own STOP #1 already rejected one composition claim that turned out to be exactly that.


PART XVIII — THE COMPOSITION BOUNDARY WITH DEEP RESEARCH AND AUTORESEARCH

18.1 Two existing capabilities already live in this estate and already perform actions that sound, at a description level, like what Crawl OS claims for itself: Deep Research fetches external content and synthesizes a cited report from it, and Autoresearch runs recurring competitive-intelligence sweeps that fetch and summarize competitor activity. This Part exists because a family whose own founding complaint against the systems it surveyed was mistaking resemblance for identity cannot itself be built on an unexamined resemblance to a neighboring system already sealed in this same estate.

18.2 Deep Research owns a specific, narrower thing than acquisition in general: given a research question, fan out web searches, fetch a bounded set of sources, adversarially verify specific claims against those sources, and synthesize a cited report — a single-shot, question-to-report pipeline with its own internal verification loop, already reverse-engineered from a specific external workflow and already sealed as its own module in this estate, independent of this family.

18.3 Autoresearch owns something narrower still and temporally distinct: scheduled, recurring competitive-intelligence sweeps for a specific class of project, producing a standing intelligence feed rather than a one-shot report, on a fixed daily cadence rather than a mission-triggered one.

18.4 Crawl OS owns neither a report-synthesis pipeline nor a recurring-schedule capability. It owns the acquisition substrate underneath both: the fourteen engines that turn a request for a specific piece of verified, provenance-carrying content into a completed Evidence Object. The test that separates the three, restated once more in this family's now-familiar form: does a capability decide what question to ask and how to synthesize an answer, which is Deep Research's and Autoresearch's territory, or does it decide how a single piece of external content gets acquired and verified once the question already knows what it needs, which is Crawl OS's territory.

18.5 The honest current relationship, stated per this constitution's own discipline against overclaiming a composition that does not yet operate: Deep Research and Autoresearch today implement their own acquisition logic independently, predating this family, and do not yet call through Crawl OS's fourteen engines. This Part does not claim otherwise. Its obligation is to state the target composition precisely enough that a future integration has somewhere honest to aim — Deep Research's and Autoresearch's own fetch steps eventually becoming callers of Crawl OS's Skills, MCP, and Agent Interfaces engine at the institutional level Part XXI requires, rather than either system indefinitely maintaining a second, parallel acquisition stack.

18.6 The doctrine forbids two symmetric failures. Crawl OS absorbing Deep Research's or Autoresearch's own synthesis and question-framing logic, on the theory that acquisition and synthesis are naturally one system, would violate Part IV's 4.2 prohibition on Crawl OS owning general reasoning. Deep Research or Autoresearch continuing to maintain independent acquisition logic indefinitely, once Crawl OS's own acquisition substrate is mature enough to serve them, would be exactly the kind of redundant, unaudited parallel capability Part IV's 4.10 test already forbids for a proposal inside this family, applied here across a family boundary instead of within one.

18.7 A worked case distinguishes the boundary at the level a future integration would actually need to reason about. Autoresearch's recurring sweep decides which competitor domains to check today and what questions to ask about each — squarely its own territory. Once it has decided, fetching each competitor's pricing page with full provenance, verified against the ten Production Reality Gates, is squarely Crawl OS's territory, and Autoresearch's own synthesis of what changed since the last sweep operates on Crawl OS's Evidence Packages as input, never on raw, unverified fetch output Autoresearch acquired through a parallel path of its own.

18.8 The doctrine's provenance implication deserves separate statement because it is the concrete benefit a real integration would realize, not an abstract tidiness argument: a Deep Research report or an Autoresearch sweep finding, once actually routed through Crawl OS's acquisition substrate, inherits every Evidence Object field Part VII requires for every claim in the resulting report — the exact URL, the exact acquisition instant, the exact confidence score — which neither system's own independent fetch logic was ever built to provide with the same rigor, because provenance at this depth was never either system's own owned responsibility to begin with.

18.9 This Part's relationship to the family's own eighteenth dataset, Claude Code Skills, MCP and Agent Interfaces, must be kept distinct from what this Part describes: that dataset governs how any consumer, including Deep Research and Autoresearch, invokes Crawl OS's capabilities once built, at the interface level. This Part governs which system owns which piece of territory in the first place. The two compose — this Part decides what crosses the boundary, the family's dataset eighteen decides the calling convention it crosses through — but neither substitutes for the other.

18.10 The boundary closes with the same honesty discipline Part XVI already applied to its own composition: today, Deep Research and Autoresearch remain independent of this family, and this Part states the target composition as a future obligation rather than a present fact, in the identical vocabulary this family already commits to using for every downstream claim it cannot yet verify empirically.

18.11 The doctrine's enforcement is not a one-time boundary check performed only at this family's own founding. The Duplicate-to-Advantage Engine's Family Sizing Mode, extended during this corpus's own STOP #1 specifically to evaluate a proposed family's boundary against neighboring systems rather than one proposal at a time, remains available as a standing instrument any future contributor may re-run against Deep Research or Autoresearch specifically, whenever a proposed Crawl OS capability's description begins to resemble either system's own territory closely enough to raise the same question this Part answers today. A boundary drawn once at founding and never re-checked degrades exactly as silently as the cost-ladder and confidence-vocabulary drifts this constitution names elsewhere, and this Part's own boundary claims no exemption from the discipline it demands of every other one in this family.

18.12 A second worked case sharpens the boundary at the specific point a future integration engineer would actually need it. Suppose Autoresearch's own daily sweep, once integrated, needs to check a single competitor's pricing page that its schedule has never encountered before. The correct call crosses the boundary exactly once, at the institutional level Part XXI requires: a single request naming the target and the evidence needed, handled entirely by Crawl OS's own Crawl Intent Compiler and Acquisition Strategy Router internally. The incorrect pattern has Autoresearch itself deciding which rung of the cost ladder to use, or maintaining its own site memory for that competitor's domain, because both decisions, however small they look in the moment, are Crawl OS's owned territory under Part III regardless of which system's schedule triggered the need for them.

PART XVIII FINAL LAW

Deep Research owns question-to-report synthesis; Autoresearch owns recurring competitive sweeps; Crawl OS owns neither and owns instead the acquisition substrate both would eventually stand on. The test is constant: deciding what to ask and how to synthesize belongs to the two neighboring systems; deciding how a single piece of content is acquired and verified once the question is already known belongs to Crawl OS. Today the two systems run independently, and this constitution says so honestly rather than describing a composition already operating that is not.


PART XIX — THE VERDICT ONTOLOGY FOR ACQUISITION

19.1 Parts VIII and XIII have both, at different points, used specific verdict words — unverified, partial, void — without yet defining them as a closed, binding vocabulary. This Part supplies that vocabulary, because a family whose central complaint against the systems it surveyed is that success was never precisely defined cannot itself leave its own success language informal. Seven verdicts, and only these seven, may be assigned to a Crawl OS acquisition or mission; no engine may report an outcome for which this Part provides no word.

19.2 UNVERIFIED is the default state of every acquisition from the instant it completes Gate A until the remaining gates its contract requires have actually been checked, per Part VIII's 8.7 inversion of the burden of proof. It is not a final verdict; it is the honest starting condition every acquisition occupies before this Part's remaining six verdicts can be considered, and a mission report that lists an item as UNVERIFIED at its conclusion, rather than resolving it to one of the six that follow, has an incomplete pipeline, not a legitimately final outcome.

19.3 VERIFIED is assigned when every gate the mission's own contract requires has either passed or been honestly marked not applicable per Part XIII's 13.15, and the resulting Evidence Object is eligible, though never automatically promoted, for institutionalization under Part XIV.

19.4 PARTIAL is assigned when at least one required gate failed, but the sufficiency threshold DRK-03's own vocabulary computes for this specific mission's contract, per Part XVII's composition, is still met by what did pass. The Gate H freshness failure worked through in Part XIII's 13.13 resolves here to PARTIAL if the mission's own confidence requirement tolerates forty-day-old pricing data, and to INSUFFICIENT if it does not; the verdict is not determined by the gate failure alone, it is determined by that failure measured against the specific mission's own stated tolerance.

19.5 INSUFFICIENT is assigned when one or more required gates failed and the mission's stated sufficiency threshold is not met. Unlike VOID, described next, an INSUFFICIENT acquisition was honestly attempted and honestly evaluated; its Evidence Object is retained, feeding the negative memory this family's Part XXIII elaborates, because a well-documented insufficient attempt is exactly the kind of failure record Part XIV's 14.6 already establishes as legitimately institutionalizable in its own right.

19.6 VOID is categorically distinct from INSUFFICIENT and must never be conflated with it: it is assigned specifically when Gate C, Authorization Reality, fails, per Part IX's 9.10, regardless of how every other gate resolved. A VOID acquisition is not a weak or partial piece of evidence graded down for its shortcomings; it is not evidence at all, and no downstream system may cite a VOID record as support for any claim under any confidence level, however the remaining nine gates happened to resolve.

19.7 ESCALATED is assigned when the Acquisition Strategy Router determines, per the cost ladder's own seventh rung in Part VI's 6.6, that no automated method can plausibly satisfy the mission's evidence requirement and authorized human intervention is required but not yet granted. It is a suspended, not a final, state: an ESCALATED item resolves to one of this Part's other verdicts once human intervention occurs or the mission's contract is amended to accept a lower automated tier.

19.8 SUPERSEDED is assigned to a previously VERIFIED or PARTIAL record when the Temporal Truth Engine, per Part III's 3.6, detects that the underlying source has since undergone semantic change. The original record is not deleted or silently overwritten; it is marked SUPERSEDED, its change history field per Part VII's 7.2 pointing to whatever newer acquisition replaces it, preserving the corpus's own ability to reconstruct what was known at any prior point in time, not only what is currently believed.

19.9 A worked example distinguishes all seven verdicts operating together across a single multi-source mission, because their differences are easy to state in isolation and easy to blur in practice. A mission acquiring five competitor pricing pages produces: one VERIFIED page, all required gates passing cleanly; one PARTIAL page, Gate H failing on a stale but within-tolerance cache; one INSUFFICIENT page, Gate B failing on content that turned out to be a regional redirect rather than the target page, with no sufficiency threshold met; one VOID page, Gate C failing because the page sat behind a robots.txt disallow the mission's frontier had not checked before attempting it; and one ESCALATED item, the fifth competitor's pricing requiring an authenticated partner portal no automated credential was authorized to enter. The mission's honest final report names all five verdicts individually rather than averaging them into a single undifferentiated confidence number, because the five items are not five samples of the same underlying quality, they are five categorically different outcomes that happen to have been produced by the same mission.

19.10 The ontology's closure is itself a binding property: an engine that produces an eighth word, or a compound state this Part does not name, has introduced ambiguity this constitution exists to eliminate, and any future dataset in this family proposing a new verdict must amend this Part explicitly under Part XXIV's process rather than introduce a parallel, undeclared vocabulary at the point of use.

19.11 The seven verdicts carry an implicit but binding severity ordering that any consumer aggregating results across a mission must respect rather than treat as seven unordered labels. From least to most concerning for a downstream claim's own trustworthiness: VERIFIED, PARTIAL, ESCALATED, SUPERSEDED, INSUFFICIENT, VOID — with UNVERIFIED excluded from the ordering entirely because, per 19.2, it is never a legitimate final state to aggregate over in the first place. This ordering matters concretely when a mission's own contract requires a single summary verdict across many acquired items rather than an itemized list: the summary is the least favorable verdict actually present among the items the contract deems required, never an average, a majority vote, or a most-common value, because a single VOID item among nine VERIFIED ones does not average out to mostly trustworthy — it means one specific claim in the set has no evidentiary support at all, and the summary must say so.

19.12 This ordering also resolves a question Part XIV leaves implicit: which verdicts are even eligible for the institutionalization judgment that Part describes. VERIFIED and PARTIAL are the only two verdicts a mission may submit for the reusability, generality, provenance-strength, and redundancy assessment Part XIV's 14.4 defines; INSUFFICIENT and VOID never enter that judgment as candidates for positive institutionalization, though both remain eligible for the distinct negative-memory institutionalization Part XXIII describes, which asks a different question entirely — not whether a claim is worth remembering as true, but whether a failure is worth remembering as a lesson.

PART XIX FINAL LAW

Seven verdicts, and only seven: UNVERIFIED, VERIFIED, PARTIAL, INSUFFICIENT, VOID, ESCALATED, SUPERSEDED. UNVERIFIED is a starting condition, never a legitimate final report. VOID is categorically distinct from INSUFFICIENT — an authorization failure is not evidence gradable on a confidence scale, while an honestly attempted and honestly insufficient acquisition remains a legitimate, institutionalizable record of what did not work. A mission with five different outcomes reports five different verdicts, never one averaged number standing in for all five.


PART XX — DEPLOYMENT PLANES AS SEPARATION OF POWERS

20.1 Part V's charter for the Crawl Cortex Supervisor states, in one clause easy to read past, that it performs continuous oversight without itself performing the acquisitions it supervises. This Part elevates that clause into a structural principle governing not only the Supervisor but every relationship in this constitution between an engine that acts and an engine that judges the action: the two must run on separated deployment planes, separated enough that the acting engine cannot alter, suppress, or starve the judging engine's ability to observe what it did.

20.2 The doctrine's precise statement, borrowing its name deliberately from a governance concept older than software: acquisition execution, evidence judgment, and authorization are three powers, and this constitution requires them to be exercised by components that cannot control each other's ability to function, exactly as a legislature that could unilaterally silence a court would not meaningfully be checked by that court's rulings.

20.3 The acquisition plane comprises the Fetch Runtime Fabric, the Browser Interaction Fabric, and every adapter they route through — the engines that actually reach outside the estate's boundary and touch external content. This plane's failure modes are the ones every other Part in this constitution already names: a hung browser process, a rate-limited domain, a malformed extraction. This plane must never be positioned such that its own failure or misbehavior can silence the plane that is supposed to detect that failure.

20.4 The evidence judgment plane comprises the Evidence Integrity Fabric and Evaluation and Reality Gates, the engines that examine what the acquisition plane produced and assign the Production Reality Gate outcomes and verdict ontology terms this constitution's Parts XIII and XIX define. Its defining property is read-only access to what the acquisition plane produced, and no write access whatsoever back into the acquisition plane's own execution — it may report a Gate C failure; it may never itself intervene to force a retry, escalate a rung, or otherwise act on its own finding, because an engine that both judges and acts on its own judgment has collapsed the separation this Part exists to preserve.

20.5 The authorization plane comprises Governance, Safety, and Authorization alone, and its separation from the other two is the most consequential of the three, because it is the one plane whose entire purpose is to say no to the other two. An authorization plane running inside the same process, the same failure domain, or the same resource pool as the acquisition plane it governs would be exactly as compromised, in a crisis, as a regulator who reported to the entity being regulated: its ability to say no, at the precise moment saying no matters most — a runaway mission attempting more acquisitions than it was authorized for, a router quietly reaching for an unauthorized domain under load — depends on continuing to function when the plane it is refusing is itself under load or behaving badly.

20.6 The Crawl Cortex Supervisor occupies a fourth position this Part must state precisely because it touches all three planes without belonging to any of them: it observes the acquisition plane's runtime health, the evidence judgment plane's gate outcomes, and the authorization plane's own decisions, across all five of its loops per Part V's 5.12, but it does so entirely as a read-only observer whose own findings feed back into the system only as surfaced reports, never as direct control actions on any of the three planes it watches. A Supervisor that could itself pause a mission, override a router decision, or force an authorization grant would have quietly become a fourth power indistinguishable in kind from the three it exists to keep separated from each other.

20.7 A worked failure this doctrine specifically prevents makes the abstraction concrete. A mission's acquisition plane, under load, begins silently timing out its own health-check reporting to save resources, rather than reporting genuine failures honestly. In a system without deployment-plane separation, this starves the evidence judgment plane of the very signals it needs to detect the degradation, and starves the Supervisor's runtime health loop identically, producing a silent, corpus-wide degradation that looks, from outside, like nothing is wrong, precisely because the plane responsible for reporting that something is wrong is the same plane that is failing. With genuine separation, the evidence judgment and authorization planes' own health is independently observable, and their silence about the acquisition plane's own reporting gap is itself a detectable anomaly, not an invisible one.

20.8 The doctrine does not require three literal, physically separate machines or processes in every deployment of this architecture — it requires that the three planes' failure domains be independent enough that a failure or compromise originating in one cannot, by construction, prevent the other two from continuing to observe, judge, or refuse. A single-host deployment satisfies this doctrine if the three planes run as genuinely isolated processes with independent resource allocations and no shared mutable state the acquisition plane could corrupt to blind the other two; it fails this doctrine if all three share a single process whose failure takes down oversight and enforcement along with execution.

20.9 The doctrine's relationship to Part IX's authorization boundary and Part XIII's gate discipline is now precise rather than merely conceptual: Part IX assigns authorization judgment exclusively to Governance, Safety, and Authorization, and this Part is the structural guarantee that the assignment means something under adversarial conditions, not only under the well-behaved conditions where every plane is functioning normally and separation was never actually tested.

20.10 A final provision extends the doctrine to future engines this family has not yet built: any new capability proposed for this family must declare, at design time, which of the three planes it belongs to, or whether it occupies the Supervisor's fourth, observer-only position, and a proposal that cannot be cleanly assigned to exactly one of these four positions has identified a design that has not yet resolved its own separation-of-powers question, which must be resolved before the capability is built, not discovered afterward when two planes turn out to share a failure domain neither designer anticipated.

20.11 A final clarification distinguishes this doctrine from mere redundancy or high availability, concepts it superficially resembles but is not. Redundancy protects against a plane's outright failure by duplicating it; this doctrine protects against a plane's continued, misbehaving operation being able to compromise the other two even while it appears to be running normally. A redundant pair of acquisition-plane workers both silently suppressing their own health signals under the identical load condition would satisfy an availability requirement while still failing this doctrine completely, because availability was never the property at risk — the acquisition plane's ability to blind its own overseers was, and no amount of redundancy within a single plane addresses a failure mode that operates across the boundary between planes rather than within one.

PART XX FINAL LAW

Acquisition execution, evidence judgment, and authorization are three powers, and none may control whether the other two can continue to function. The evidence judgment plane may report a failure; it may never act on its own report. The authorization plane's ability to refuse must survive the exact conditions under which refusal matters most — a plane it governs behaving badly under load. The Supervisor watches all three as a read-only fourth position and never becomes a fourth power. A capability that cannot be assigned to exactly one of these four positions has not yet resolved its own design.


PART XXI — THE INTERFACE DISCIPLINE

21.1 Part II's 2.4 already forbade exposing an engine's native API surface directly to an agent, and Part V's 5.15 charter for Skills, MCP, and Agent Interfaces commits that engine to exposing Crawl OS's capabilities exclusively at the institutional level, never at the level of raw browser or fetch primitives. This Part is where that commitment becomes a concrete, closed vocabulary rather than a principle awaiting definition.

21.2 Five institutional verbs, and only these five, form Crawl OS's entire external calling surface. research_topic, given an underspecified question, compiles and executes a full mission through the Crawl Intent Compiler. extract_dataset, given a schema and a bounded source set, produces structured records via the Web-to-Dataset Compiler. verify_claim, given an existing claim and its stated source, re-acquires and re-checks that source against the Production Reality Gates to confirm or refute the claim. capture_evidence, given a single specific target, produces one Evidence Object without the broader mission-compilation overhead research_topic invokes. monitor_source, given a previously acquired source, registers it with the Temporal Truth Engine for ongoing change detection.

21.3 No sixth verb at a lower altitude is permitted — no direct fetch, no direct render, no direct interaction primitive exposed to a caller. Any capability request expressed at that granularity is, by construction, a request for an adapter's own native primitive, and Part II's 2.4 already explains why an agent handed that primitive makes an unauditable per-turn choice rather than an institutional decision. A caller that believes it needs a primitive-level verb has, in every case examined at this family's own design time, actually needed one of the five institutional verbs applied more narrowly, not a new sixth verb at a lower altitude.

21.4 The five verbs are strictly typed at both boundaries. What a caller supplies is constrained: research_topic requires an objective, an evidence requirement, and a freshness bound at minimum, mirroring the Mission Contract fields Part III's 3.2 already defines, because the verb is literally a thin front door onto contract compilation rather than a separate capability with its own separate input shape. What a caller receives is equally constrained: never raw text alone, always one or more Evidence Objects, or, for research_topic and extract_dataset specifically, the compact synthesized or structured artifact together with the Evidence Package supporting it, per Part III's 3.7 delivery obligation.

21.5 Every one of the five verbs' return values must report per-item verdicts drawn from Part XIX's seven-word ontology rather than a single boolean success. A caller receiving a capture_evidence response receives a verdict-tagged object, never an untagged one, because Part I's entire founding complaint is exactly what happens when a caller is handed something that looks complete without the tag that says whether it actually is.

21.6 The five-verb surface is versioned identically to the Evidence Object schema per Part VII's 7.8: adding a sixth verb, or changing a required field of an existing verb's own contract, is a Part XXIV amendment event, and no engine may quietly extend the calling surface's shape to accommodate a convenient one-off need.

21.7 A worked example distinguishes correct from incorrect interface use. A caller wants to compare a competitor's current pricing to what it was six months ago. The correct pattern issues two calls — one capture_evidence against the current page, one retrieval through monitor_source's own change history for the same source — and compares the two results itself. The incorrect pattern has the caller directly instructing a browser adapter to navigate to the page and capture it, bypassing the institutional verbs entirely because it felt faster for a one-off check; this is precisely the shortcut Part II's 2.4 already forbids, restated here as an interface-level violation rather than only an internal-architecture one.

21.8 This Part's relationship to the family's own eighteenth dataset, Claude Code Skills, MCP and Agent Interfaces, is narrower than that dataset's own eventual scope: this Part defines the calling contract itself, independent of transport, while that future dataset specifies how the contract is exposed through a given transport — a skill definition, an MCP tool schema, a direct function call. A skill definition, an MCP tool schema, and a direct call are three different transports for the identical five-verb surface this Part defines, and none of the three may define a sixth verb or a differently shaped contract for the same underlying capability.

21.9 A verb call that cannot be satisfied returns an ESCALATED- or VOID-carrying result through the same typed return path, never an exception whose message a caller must parse to learn what actually happened, because Part XIX's ontology exists precisely so failure is a first-class, structured outcome rather than an out-of-band signal a caller might not even be checking for.

21.10 The discipline test restated once for permanence: any future capability's exposure to the wider Claude Power Pack estate must map onto one of the five verbs or justify, explicitly and through the Part XXIV amendment process, why a sixth is genuinely required — not because the five are sacred by assumption, but because a sixth verb proposed casually, outside that process, is indistinguishable in kind from the router-favorite and megafork failures this constitution names for other unaudited defaults.

21.11 This Part's own relationship to Part XX's separation of powers must be stated, because the five verbs sit at a boundary the three planes described there do not obviously map onto. The interface layer itself is not a fourth plane and not an exception to that doctrine; it is the single narrow gate through which every plane's combined output reaches a caller, implemented as a thin translation over whichever plane actually produced the specific field a caller asked for. A research_topic call's returned Evidence Objects still carry gate outcomes computed entirely on the evidence judgment plane; an authorization failure inside a research_topic call is still VOID because Gate C failed on the authorization plane; the interface layer changes nothing about which plane produced which fact, it only presents the combined result through one of five stable shapes.

21.12 A final provision addresses concurrency at the interface boundary specifically, since it is the layer every external caller actually touches. A single caller may hold multiple verb calls in flight against different targets simultaneously, but the interface discipline does not itself grant any caller a resource allocation beyond what CO-01's own operating economics, per Part XV, has allocated that caller's session. The five-verb surface is a calling contract, not a scheduling guarantee, and a caller issuing more concurrent calls than its own allocated budget supports receives ESCALATED or ordinary queuing behavior at the scheduling layer CO-01 already owns, never a Crawl OS-invented concurrency policy layered on top of it. This is the same asymmetry Part XV's own 15.9 already draws between the two families' allocation authority, restated here at the one point where a caller could otherwise mistake a scheduling delay for a Crawl OS-specific limitation rather than the estate-wide budget it actually is.

PART XXI FINAL LAW

Five verbs — research_topic, extract_dataset, verify_claim, capture_evidence, monitor_source — form Crawl OS's entire external calling surface, and no caller is ever handed a raw fetch, render, or interaction primitive. Every return is verdict-tagged from Part XIX's own ontology, never a bare boolean. A sixth verb requires the Part XXIV amendment process; a sixth verb introduced casually is the same unaudited-default failure this constitution names everywhere else.


PART XXII — FAILURE MODES AND ANTI-PATTERNS CATALOG

22.1 This Part catalogs, by name, every failure mode this constitution has identified across Parts I through XXI, plus two additional patterns not yet individually named, so that a single Part serves as the fast, checkable reference a mission author or reviewer consults rather than re-reading twenty other Parts to recall which specific failure a given symptom matches.

22.2 The Clean-Markdown Failure, named in Part I, is the root failure this entire constitution exists to prevent: mistaking well-rendered output for verified content, present as the shared root cause beneath every other failure this catalog names even where its exact clean-Markdown form does not literally appear.

22.3 The Router-Favorite Failure, named in Part II's 2.8 and elaborated for its single most common instance in Part XII's no-default-browser doctrine, is a system silently collapsing many available engines or rungs into one habitual default, chosen by familiarity rather than by a stated, falsifiable reason.

22.4 The Megafork Failure, named fully in Part XI, is copying an adapter's own internals to patch a narrow limitation, converting a one-time convenience into a permanent, compounding maintenance liability this estate did not design.

22.5 The Authorization-by-Capability Failure, named fully in Part IX, is treating technical capacity to evade a barrier as license to pass it, when the barrier in fact represents a deliberate access-control decision this constitution requires be respected regardless of how easily it could be defeated.

22.6 The Secret-Leak-by-Convenience Failure, named fully in Part X, is persisting an operational credential into a corpus artifact because redacting it felt like unnecessary overhead for a mission that obviously did not need it, when the redaction pass's unconditional application is precisely what this constitution requires regardless of any individual mission's apparent need.

22.7 The Default-Pass Failure, named across Part VIII's 8.7, Part IX's 9.9, and generalized fully in Part XIII's 13.12, is treating an unchecked gate, an unconfirmed authorization, or an unexamined content check as though it had passed, because checking would have required an action nobody took rather than a check that genuinely occurred.

22.8 The Corpus-as-Log Failure, named fully in Part XIV, is institutionalizing every gate-passing acquisition by default because declining to promote something requires an active decision nobody wants to make, degrading the corpus's signal density one convenient non-decision at a time until institutional memory describes a log nobody can usefully query.

22.9 The Confidence-Vocabulary-Drift Failure, named fully in Part XVII's 17.11, is a local approximation of a shared estate vocabulary — DRK-03's confidence and sufficiency logic specifically — quietly substituted for the genuine imported logic under delivery pressure, diverging silently and specifically at the edge cases where the divergence matters most.

22.10 The Plane-Collapse Failure, named fully in Part XX, is running acquisition execution, evidence judgment, and authorization in a shared failure domain such that one plane's misbehavior can silence the very oversight meant to catch it. This is the single most structurally dangerous failure this catalog names, because every other failure in this list depends on some oversight mechanism actually functioning in order to be caught, and this is the one failure capable of disabling that mechanism itself.

22.11 Two failure modes not yet individually named elsewhere in this constitution close the catalog. The Provenance-Amnesia Failure is producing a technically correct piece of content whose provenance chain was partially or wholly reconstructed rather than genuinely observed — a URL recalled from memory rather than recorded at acquisition time, a confidence score estimated after the fact rather than derived from the Evidence Integrity Fabric's actual examination. It is indistinguishable in its consequences from Part VII's 7.9 fabrication prohibition but worth naming separately because it typically arises from a well-intentioned attempt to backfill a record after the fact rather than from any dishonesty at the moment of acquisition. The Verdict-Flattening Failure is collapsing Part XIX's seven-term ontology into a single boolean or a single number when reporting to a downstream consumer, discarding exactly the distinctions — VOID versus INSUFFICIENT, PARTIAL versus VERIFIED — that make the ontology worth having in the first place, and it is the specific failure Part XIX's 19.11 severity ordering exists to prevent at the aggregation step.

22.12 This catalog's own governing rule closes this Part: every failure named here traces to a single shared root, a system optimizing for looking correct in the moment over being verifiably correct on inspection, and a mission author who cannot immediately place a specific symptom into one of the eleven named patterns above should treat that difficulty itself as a signal — either a genuinely new failure mode this catalog must be amended to include under Part XXIV's process, or a symptom that, examined more carefully, resolves into a pattern already named here under an unfamiliar description.

22.13 The catalog's practical use is as a review checklist rather than only a reference glossary. A reviewer examining a completed mission's report should be able to name, for each item whose verdict fell short of VERIFIED, which of the eleven patterns, if any, explains why, and a mission report whose author cannot map an INSUFFICIENT or VOID outcome to a named pattern, or to an honestly novel failure warranting a Part XXIV amendment, has not yet explained its own result completely enough to be considered a finished report regardless of how correctly its individual gate checks were performed.

22.14 The catalog deliberately excludes failure modes belonging to neighboring systems this family composes with rather than owns, restated once more from Part IV's boundary discipline. A DRK-03 sufficiency-threshold miscalibration is not a Crawl OS failure pattern even though it can produce a Crawl OS verdict that looks wrong, and a CO-01 budget misallocation starving a mission of the resources its own contract needed is not a Crawl OS failure pattern either, even though its symptom surfaces inside a Crawl OS mission report. Attributing a neighboring system's own failure to this catalog would misdiagnose the root cause exactly as surely as failing to recognize a genuine Crawl OS pattern when one is actually present, and a reviewer applying this catalog must first confirm the failure originates inside one of the fourteen engines Part V names before reaching for a pattern here.

22.15 This Part's two newly named patterns, Provenance-Amnesia and Verdict-Flattening, were identified during this dataset's own construction rather than inherited from the founding resource document's original survey, and their presence here is itself a small instance of the institutional learning loop Part XXIII describes operating one level up — on this constitution's own text rather than on acquired external content. A corpus that can name a failure mode it discovered about itself, mid-construction, and fold that discovery back into its own governing text before declaring itself sealed, is doing exactly what Part XXIII asks every future mission to do with what it learns, and a future dataset in this family that discovers its own new pattern owes this catalog the identical honesty.

PART XXII FINAL LAW

Eleven named failures, one shared root: a system optimizing for looking correct over being verifiably correct. Clean-Markdown, Router-Favorite, Megafork, Authorization-by-Capability, Secret-Leak-by-Convenience, Default-Pass, Corpus-as-Log, Confidence-Vocabulary-Drift, Plane-Collapse, Provenance-Amnesia, and Verdict-Flattening are the closed catalog until amended. A symptom that fits none of them is either a genuinely new failure awaiting amendment, or an old one wearing an unfamiliar description.


PART XXIII — THE INSTITUTIONAL LEARNING LOOP AND NEGATIVE MEMORY

23.1 Part IV's 4.3 named eight specialized memories Crawl OS maintains as its own: source, site, extraction, session, evidence, change, negative, and procedural. Six of the eight have already appeared throughout this constitution in the specific contexts where they matter — site memory recording cost-ladder classifications in Part VI, session memory recording authenticated session profiles in Part X, evidence memory implicit in every Evidence Object this constitution requires. This Part gives negative memory, the seventh, the dedicated treatment its distinct character requires, and closes with how the Crawl Cortex Supervisor's fifth loop keeps all eight honest over time.

23.2 Negative memory's precise content is a record of what did not work, specifically enough that a future mission encountering the same or a similar target does not repeat an attempt this constitution's own accumulated experience already knows will fail. This is not merely a copy of INSUFFICIENT or VOID Evidence Objects, per Part XIX's ontology, though those feed it — it is a distilled, queryable form: this domain requires authentication no available credential satisfies, this site's robots.txt disallows the entire path an intended mission would need, this specific extraction schema fails against this site's markup structure in a specific, recorded way.

23.3 Negative memory's institutionalization criteria differ from Part XIV's four positive criteria in one specific respect worth naming: redundancy, which penalizes a positive record for duplicating existing corpus content, does not apply the same way to a negative record, because a repeated confirmation that a known failure still holds is itself informative. A site whose authorization posture has not changed in six months is different information from a site checked once and never revisited, and negative memory's own freshness, not its uniqueness, is the property most worth tracking.

23.4 A worked distinction shows negative memory doing real work a purely positive-only memory could not. A mission targets a documentation site behind an authentication wall no available credential satisfies. Without negative memory, a future mission targeting the same site re-attempts the identical acquisition, re-discovers the same VOID or ESCALATED outcome, and burns a full round of cost-ladder escalation before reaching the same conclusion the estate already reached months earlier. With negative memory, the Crawl Intent Compiler consults it during mission compilation itself, per Part III's 3.2, and the mission's own contract is compiled honestly from the start with the authentication gap already named as a known constraint rather than rediscovered as a surprise partway through execution.

23.5 Negative memory's relationship to Part IX's authorization doctrine deserves explicit statement because the two are easy to conflate: a negative memory record describing that a domain's robots.txt disallows a given path is not itself a second authorization check substituting for the live check Part IX requires before every acquisition attempt. Site conditions change; a disallow directive recorded six months ago may have been lifted since. Negative memory informs mission compilation and cost-ladder decisions with a strong prior; it never substitutes for the live Governance, Safety, and Authorization check Part IX requires at the moment of acquisition itself.

23.6 Procedural memory, the eighth and final specialized memory, is named here rather than left entirely implicit because its relationship to negative memory is close enough to require distinguishing. Procedural memory records how a successful acquisition against a specific site or site class was actually accomplished — which extraction schema, which specific selector override per Part XI's 11.9 worked example, which session-renewal cadence — while negative memory records what did not work. The two are complementary registers of the same underlying discipline, one archiving working procedures for reuse, the other archiving failed ones for avoidance, and a site's full memory profile is genuinely incomplete without both.

23.7 The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's fifth loop, institutional learning, already introduced in Part XIV's 14.11 for its role auditing institutionalization judgments specifically, has a broader charter this Part now completes: it is the single mechanism responsible for keeping all eight memories honest over time, not only the promotion judgment. It periodically re-validates a sample of negative memory records against current conditions, per 23.5's warning that stale negative memory can itself become a false constraint baked into every future mission's contract; it audits procedural memory's continued accuracy against a site's own observed drift, per Part VI's 6.5 bidirectional cost-ladder discipline; and it surfaces, as a finding rather than a silent correction, any memory record whose confidence in its own continued accuracy has degraded below a threshold the mission compiler should be warned about before relying on it uncritically.

23.8 A worked failure this loop specifically catches: a site's authentication requirements changed eight months ago, becoming open access, but negative memory's original record, that this domain requires authentication no available credential satisfies, was never revisited, and every mission compiled since has silently deprioritized or excluded this source from its frontier based on a constraint that no longer holds. The institutional learning loop's periodic re-validation is what surfaces this specific staleness as a finding, rather than the constraint persisting indefinitely simply because nothing ever prompted anyone to check whether it still held.

23.9 The doctrine's relationship to Part XIV's institutionalization judgment closes a loop opened in that Part: negative memory is not exempt from institutionalization discipline merely because it records a failure. It passes through the identical judgment — is this reusable beyond the single mission that discovered it, does it generalize, is its provenance strong enough to trust, is it redundant with what the corpus already records — with redundancy weighted differently per 23.3, but never waived. 23.10 This Part closes by naming the specific value negative memory delivers to the family's own founding mission, restated from Part I: a system that never remembers what did not work is condemned to rediscover every failure at full cost, on every mission, forever, and the difference between that system and one with genuine institutional memory is not merely efficiency. It is the difference between a system that learns and one that only executes.

23.11 A final distinction closes this Part: the eight memories are Crawl OS's own, populated and consulted exclusively by Crawl OS's fourteen engines, and this Part's discipline governs their internal maintenance, never their external exposure. A caller reaching Crawl OS through the five-verb interface Part XXI defines never queries site memory, negative memory, or any of the other six directly; it receives their effects only indirectly, folded into a verb call's own returned Evidence Objects and verdicts, exactly as a caller of any well-designed system receives the benefit of that system's internal caching and learning without ever needing to address the cache by name.

PART XXIII FINAL LAW

Eight memories, not six: source, site, extraction, session, evidence, change, negative, and procedural. Negative memory is redundancy-tolerant where positive memory is not, because a repeated confirmation of a known failure is informative rather than wasteful, and it must never substitute for the live authorization check Part IX requires at the moment of acquisition. The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's fifth loop keeps all eight honest over time by re-validating, not by trusting a record's age. A system that never remembers what did not work relearns every failure at full cost, forever.


PART XXIV — EVOLUTION, VERSIONING, AND THE AMENDMENT PROCESS

24.1 Every constitution that forbids silent drift in the systems it governs must hold itself to an equal standard for its own text, or the prohibition against silent drift becomes a rule this document exempts itself from precisely where exemption would be most convenient. This Part defines the amendment process binding on this dataset and on every other dataset in the Crawl OS family, closing the loop opened by every prior Part's forward reference to a Part XXIV amendment event.

24.2 This dataset's own version number, stated in its front matter, increments on any amendment. A Part's internal subsection numbering is stable once sealed, and a new provision within an existing Part is appended with the next available decimal rather than renumbering existing subsections, so that a cross-reference such as Part VII's 7.9, made anywhere in this family, remains valid indefinitely rather than silently drifting when Part VII is later amended.

24.3 What counts as an amendment event is restated here as the closed list this constitution has referenced piecemeal throughout: a change to the Evidence Object schema per Part VII's 7.8, a change to the confidence-vocabulary computation per Part XVII's 17.8, a new or removed verdict in the ontology per Part XIX's 19.10, a new or removed verb in the interface discipline per Part XXI's 21.6, a change to any engine's charter stated in Part V, and any change to an ownership boundary drawn in Parts III, IV, XV, XVI, XVII, or XVIII.

24.4 The amendment process itself proceeds in three steps. A proposed amendment is stated in writing against the specific Part and subsection it modifies. It is subjected to the same ownership test this constitution applies to every new capability proposal — does this genuinely require the change, or does existing text already cover the case under a different framing. Where the amendment touches a composition boundary with a neighboring family, it requires that neighboring family's own review before this dataset's version increments, because Crawl OS may not unilaterally redefine a boundary a neighboring system also depends on holding stable.

24.5 This doctrine's relationship to the Duplicate-to-Advantage Engine's Family Sizing Mode, already cited in Part XVIII's 18.11 as a standing re-audit instrument, is sequential rather than redundant: Family Sizing Mode is the tool that detects when a proposed change might duplicate or conflict with existing territory. The amendment process this Part defines is what happens after that detection — the actual mechanism by which a genuinely warranted change is incorporated rather than merely flagged.

24.6 A worked distinction separates an amendment from a mere clarification. Adding a worked example to an existing Part that illustrates a rule already stated, without changing what the rule actually requires, is not an amendment and does not require this process. Parts XIII through XX's own strengthening passes, adding subsections that elaborated already-stated rules with additional worked cases rather than changing any Final Law's substance, are examples of clarification, not amendment, and this Part's own retroactive classification of that work confirms the distinction it draws is one this family's own construction already relied on in practice, not only in theory.

24.7 A Part or provision this process determines should be retired is never silently deleted. It is marked deprecated with a citation to the amendment that superseded it and the specific reason, because a constitution that quietly deletes what it once required makes it impossible for anyone auditing an old mission's compliance to know what rule that mission was actually built against at the time.

24.8 An amendment to this dataset that changes an ownership boundary or a shared vocabulary binding on other Crawl OS datasets propagates as an obligation on those datasets to update their own citations, tracked the same way any cross-dataset dependency in this family is tracked, and a sibling dataset citing a superseded provision without updating is itself now out of compliance with this family's own internal consistency standard.

24.9 A worked amendment scenario closes the abstraction. Suppose a future mission reveals that Part XIII's ten gates are insufficient for a newly encountered acquisition category — video content requiring a distinct fidelity check no existing gate captures. The correct response is not a mission-specific workaround invented in isolation; it is a proposed Gate K, evaluated against this Part's process, and, if adopted, incorporated with the same rigor applied to every other provision this constitution defines, including an update to Part XIX's verdict ontology if the new gate changes how sufficiency is computed.

24.10 This Part's own amendment status closes the loop on itself: this Part is itself amendable under the exact process it defines, and a future revision to the amendment process is the one case in this constitution where the process must be applied to its own text — a recursion this Part accepts explicitly rather than declaring itself exempt from the discipline it imposes on every other Part.

24.11 This Part's relationship to Part XX's separation-of-powers doctrine deserves explicit statement, because an amendment process is itself a kind of power over the rest of this constitution's text, and a process that could be invoked unilaterally by whichever engine found a given provision inconvenient would reproduce, at the level of governing text rather than runtime execution, the exact plane-collapse failure Part XX names for execution, judgment, and authorization. The amendment process is therefore deliberately outside all three runtime planes: it is authored and reviewed by whoever maintains this family's own governance, never triggered automatically by an engine's own runtime behavior, and no engine may amend this constitution to excuse its own observed non-compliance rather than correcting the non-compliance itself.

24.12 A worked case distinguishes a legitimate amendment from an illegitimate one attempting to use this process as cover. Suppose the Acquisition Strategy Router is observed, over several missions, defaulting to a browser rung more often than Part XII's doctrine permits. The illegitimate response proposes amending Part XII to loosen its threshold until the router's actual behavior falls back within compliance. The legitimate response fixes the router's own logic to match the doctrine already stated, because the observed drift is a Part XXII Router-Favorite Failure in the router's implementation, not evidence the doctrine itself was wrong. This Part's amendment process exists to incorporate genuinely new requirements this constitution did not anticipate, never to retroactively legalize an engine's own observed non-compliance with a requirement it always should have met. The Crawl Cortex Supervisor's own institutional learning loop, per Part XXIII's 23.7, is what should surface the drift in the first place, well before any amendment proposal, correctly framed or not, ever reaches a maintainer's desk.

PART XXIV FINAL LAW

A constitution that forbids silent drift everywhere else and exempts itself is not a constitution, it is a convenience. Every amendment is stated in writing against the specific Part and subsection it modifies, tested against the same ownership logic this document applies to every new capability, and, where it touches a shared boundary, cleared with the neighboring family that boundary also binds. Nothing is silently deleted; deprecation always cites its own replacement. This Part governs its own amendment identically to every other Part's, and no engine may ever invoke this process to excuse its own observed non-compliance rather than to incorporate a genuinely new requirement.
