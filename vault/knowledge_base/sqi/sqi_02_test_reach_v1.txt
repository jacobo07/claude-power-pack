PART I — MISSION: EXISTENCE DOES NOT IMPLY CONNECTION

1.1 The constitution of this corpus establishes a law and does not build the organ that enforces it. The law is that a test which is not executed protects exactly what a non-existent test protects, which is nothing, and that its existence makes the situation strictly worse because it manufactures a confidence that a bare absence could never have produced. That law was derived from observation, it was stated once, and it was left standing. This dataset is the organ. Its entire subject is the measurement, detection, baselining, and defence of the distance between what a repository has authored and what a repository actually runs, and its jurisdiction ends precisely where that distance ends.

1.2 The question this dataset exists to answer can be written in one line and cannot be answered by any runner. What fraction of the protection that was authored is reached by the invocation that anyone will actually type. Every word in that sentence is load-bearing. Authored, because the denominator is the tests that exist on disk, not the tests that some configuration file believes exist. Reached, because collection is not execution and execution is not assertion. Anyone, because the invocation that matters is not the one documented in a file that nobody opens but the one a human, a continuous-integration job, or an agent with no prior context would run on first contact. And actually, because the entire failure class lives in the gap between a command's declared intent and its observed behaviour.

1.3 The runner cannot answer this question, and the reason is structural rather than incidental. A test runner reports on what it collected. Its report is a complete and truthful description of its own activity, and it contains no field, and can contain no field, describing the tests it never saw. A report generated from inside the collection boundary cannot describe the boundary. This is not a deficiency to be fixed by a better runner; it is a property of any instrument that measures only what it touches. The measurement of reach must therefore be performed by a second instrument, standing outside the runner, holding an independent census of what exists, and comparing that census against the runner's own account of what it executed. That second instrument is the reconciliation engine, and the remainder of this dataset specifies it.

1.4 The evidence that the gap is real, large, and invisible was produced by the audit that founded this corpus, and it was produced in the most uncomfortable possible place, which is this system's own body. Claude Power Pack carries seventy-six test files. Its canonical invocation collects six of them, yielding forty-three tests, and those forty-three pass. The suite is green. The green is arithmetically true. It also describes under ten percent of the test files the repository has authored, and nobody had noticed, because nothing in the pipeline was responsible for noticing and no report generated by the pipeline could have contained the observation. The self-described most audited repository in the estate was running a minority of its own protection and reporting success.

1.5 The second observation came from a sibling repository. TUA-X carries a configuration line that scopes collection to a single subtree. The default invocation collects four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight tests, all green. Outside that subtree sit three hundred and ninety further tests, in two directories, and they are not broken, and they are not stale, and they are not aspirational. Invoked explicitly they pass: one hundred and fifty-seven in one root, two hundred and thirty-two in the other. They are green tests protecting nothing, held one configuration line away from irrelevance, and the runner's default report does not mention them, because from inside the collection boundary they do not exist.

1.6 The third observation moved the law off the test surface entirely, which is what proved it was a law rather than a runner quirk. CostaLuz Lawyers declares a static security scanner in its dependency set and carries its configuration in the repository. No job invokes it. The tool is installed. It is configured. It has never once been executed. Its presence in the dependency manifest and its configuration file together form the strongest available evidence of deliberate intent, and that evidence is exactly what makes the artifact dangerous: a reader encountering it concludes that the security surface is scanned, and the conclusion is false, and the artifact that produced the false conclusion is the very thing that was supposed to prevent it.

1.7 Three findings, three stacks, three mechanisms, one law. Existence does not imply connection. A test file is not protection; a test file reachable from an invocation that is actually run is protection. A scanner is not a control; a scanner whose non-zero exit fails a build is a control. The artifact and the connection are separate objects, they are recorded in separate places, they decay independently, and every quality instrument in ordinary use measures the first and none measures the second. The mission of this dataset is to make the second measurable, baselined, defended against silent decay, and impossible to fake without the fake being visible.

1.8 The scope boundary must be stated as sharply as the mission, because a reach engine that expands into test-quality adjudication becomes a second reviewer and will be routed around. This dataset does not judge whether a test is good. It does not decide whether an assertion is strong, whether a fixture is representative, or whether a mock is placed at the right boundary, except at exactly one point: where a weakening of those properties is the mechanism by which protection is silently withdrawn while the count holds steady, which is Part XV's subject and is measured as a loss, not as a code review. It does not own the evidence ladder; the levels a reach claim may carry are ACIS's E0 through E7 and are cited, never redefined. It does not compute blast radius; that is the decision kernel's. It does not route an insight into its destination form; that is FD-03's, and building a second router is forbidden by name.

1.9 What this dataset does own is a small set of objects and the mechanisms that produce them: a topology map of every test artifact that exists, an executed manifest harvested from the runner's own machine-readable output, a reconciliation diff between them, three reach metrics and three loss metrics computed from that diff, a per-root baseline that fails on a silent decrease, a protection-surface baseline that counts what can break rather than what guards it, a drift detector that refuses to let code outgrow its protection, a weakening detector that catches the withdrawal of protection at constant count, a five-verdict classifier for any green signal, and a five-state model for every non-test guard in the estate. Each is specified operationally in the Parts that follow, and each exists because a real observation demanded it.

1.10 The obvious objection is that this is counting, that counting is cheap, and that a large apparatus has been erected around an arithmetic operation. The objection misreads what is being counted. Counting tests is trivial and is the thing every existing instrument already does; it is precisely the instrument that reported forty-three passing and told nobody about the seventy unread files. What this dataset counts is not tests but the edges between artifacts and invocations, and edges are exactly the thing that no artifact-level instrument can see, because an edge is a relationship and every tool in the ordinary pipeline is a census of nodes. The reconciliation is arithmetic. Knowing which two censuses to subtract, and knowing that neither may be produced by the party being audited, is the entire discipline.

PART I FINAL LAW

Existence does not imply connection. A test that exists but is never executed protects exactly what a non-existent test protects — nothing — and its existence makes matters strictly worse, because it manufactures the false confidence a bare absence never could. No report generated inside a collection boundary can describe that boundary; the runner's account of its own activity is complete, truthful, and structurally incapable of naming what it never saw. Reach must therefore be measured by a second instrument, holding an independent census, standing outside the runner it audits.


PART II — THE THREE COSTUMES OF ONE LAW

2.1 A law observed once is an anecdote, observed twice is a coincidence, and observed three times in three unrelated stacks by three unrelated mechanisms is a law. The three findings of the founding audit qualify, and the purpose of this Part is to strip each of its local costume until the shared skeleton is visible, because the skeleton is what transfers and the costume is what makes each instance look like somebody else's problem. Every practitioner reading the scope-line finding concludes that they do not use that runner. Every practitioner reading the inert-scanner finding concludes that their scanners run. The generalization is what defeats that reflex.

2.2 The first costume is the out-of-tree suite. Claude Power Pack authored seventy-six test files over time, in the places where the work was: beside the module they exercised, under a tools directory, inside a subsystem, wherever the author was standing when the need arose. The canonical invocation was fixed early, pointed at a single directory, and never revisited, because there was never a moment at which revisiting it was anybody's task. The authored surface grew organically. The invocation stayed where it was planted. Six files remained inside it. The mechanism is not a configuration error and nobody made a mistake; it is the ordinary consequence of a growing numerator and a frozen denominator, and it will occur in every repository where the two are permitted to move independently, which is every repository that does not measure the gap.

2.3 The second costume is the scope line. TUA-X sets a collection path in its project configuration that names one subtree. The line is correct, it was written deliberately, and at the moment it was written it was almost certainly the right line — it made the default run fast and focused. Then two more test roots came into existence, one hundred and fifty-seven tests in one and two hundred and thirty-two in the other, and the line did not move, because a configuration line has no owner and no expiry and nothing in any pipeline anywhere asks whether it still describes the repository. Three hundred and ninety green tests now sit outside the default collection. They pass on demand. They are invoked by nobody. Their protective value is exactly zero and their reported value is exactly zero, and the second zero is why nobody knew about the first.

2.4 The passing orphan is more dangerous than the failing orphan, and the asymmetry is worth dwelling on because it inverts the intuition. A failing orphan is a nuisance that will eventually be discovered, because sooner or later somebody runs it directly, sees red, and either fixes or deletes it. A passing orphan is stable. It survives refactors, it survives reviews, it survives audits, and it will keep passing until the day the code it guards is broken — at which point it will still not go red, because nothing invokes it. Its entire lifecycle is silent. It contributes a file to the repository's test-file count, it contributes a favourable impression to every reader who greps for coverage of a module and finds a test file named after it, and it contributes nothing whatsoever to protection.

2.5 The third costume abandons tests altogether, and that is what proves the law is not about testing. CostaLuz Lawyers declares a static security analyser as a dependency and carries its configuration. No job ever invokes it. There is no scope line to blame and no test root to widen; there is simply no edge in the invocation graph between the tool and any process that runs. The tool is a node with no inbound edge. Its protective value is zero. Its evidentiary value is negative, because the presence of a configuration file is read by every subsequent reader as proof of deliberation, and deliberation is read as protection, and so the artifact actively transmits a belief that its own inertness disproves.

2.6 Strip the costumes and the skeleton is a graph. The nodes are protective artifacts: test files, test cases, scanners, linters, type checkers, schema validators, hooks. The edges are invocations: a runner collects a file, a job runs a scanner, a harness loads a hook. Protection is not a property of a node. It is a property of the path from the canonical invocation root to that node. A node with no path is inert regardless of its content, its quality, its author's intent, or the number of hours spent on it. This is the generalized statement of the law, and it is what makes the three findings one finding: an out-of-tree test file, a scope-excluded test root, and an uninvoked scanner are the same topological object, which is an unreachable node, arrived at by three different routes.

2.7 The graph framing immediately produces a fourth costume that the audit also observed and that is easy to misfile. InfinityOps carries one hundred and twenty-two ExUnit files and CostaLuz carries twenty-seven, and the audit could not verify a single one of them, because the compiler refuses to run: dependencies are unlocked and compilation fails before a test is reached. No configuration line excludes these tests. No directory is out of tree. The edge from the invocation to the node exists in the configuration and is severed at runtime by the environment. The protective value of those one hundred and forty-nine files, today, in the audited environment, is zero — the same zero as the out-of-tree file and the uninvoked scanner. The mechanism is different, the record is different, the remediation is different, and the reach is identical.

2.8 That fourth case forces the law's most important refinement, which is that reach is never a property of an artifact. It is a property of the pair: artifact and invocation, evaluated in an environment. The same test file has reach one under an invocation that collects it and reach zero under one that does not. The same Java suite has reach one under the correct toolchain and reach zero under a toolchain one major version behind, which the audit demonstrated by losing an entire run to exactly that mismatch before retrying under the correct version and observing one thousand five hundred and ninety-six passing assertions in one module and ten in another. A reach figure quoted without naming its invocation and its environment is not a weak measurement. It is not a measurement at all, in the same way that a temperature quoted without a scale is not a temperature.

2.9 A fifth costume is worth naming because it is the one the estate is least equipped to see, and it does not involve reach at zero. KobiiCraft's audited suites are green and substantial — one thousand five hundred and ninety-six passing assertions in the map engine, ten in the core module — and its economy service, the surface on which a player's money lives and therefore the single most abusable surface a server exposes, has zero test references anywhere in the entire plugin tree. Every test that exists is reached. Every reached test passes. And the protection over the money surface is nil. Reach at one hundred percent over the authored set says nothing about coverage of the risk set, and a dataset that measured only the first would report perfect health over this repository. That is why the reach metrics of Part VII are insufficient on their own and why Part XIII exists.

2.10 The final observation of this Part is about discovery rather than mechanism. Every one of these findings was invisible until somebody asked a question that no pipeline asks. Not a better question — the same question, asked by a party standing outside the instrument. How many test files exist, and how many did the runner touch. Which suite roots exist, and which did the default invocation enter. Which scanners are declared, and which are invoked. The questions are embarrassingly simple, they take minutes, and the reason they had never been asked is that asking them is nobody's job by default, and everything in the pipeline is generating a green signal that makes asking feel unnecessary. The engine specified in this dataset is, in the end, nothing more than the institutionalization of those three questions, asked mechanically, on a schedule, by a party that did not write the code.

PART II FINAL LAW

An out-of-tree test file, a scope-excluded test root, an uninvoked scanner, and a suite blocked by an unqualified environment are one topological object reached by four routes: an unreachable node. Protection is never a property of an artifact; it is a property of the path from the canonical invocation to it, in a named environment. A reach figure quoted without its invocation and environment is not a weak measurement — it is not a measurement. And the passing orphan is the dangerous one, because its silence is stable forever.


PART III — TEST TOPOLOGY CARTOGRAPHY: WHERE TESTS ACTUALLY LIVE, VERSUS WHERE THEY ARE ASSUMED TO LIVE

3.1 The reconciliation engine subtracts two censuses, and this Part specifies the first of them. The topology map is an inventory of every protective artifact that exists on disk at a named commit, produced without reference to any runner, any configuration file, or any belief about where tests are supposed to be. Its defining property is independence: it must be capable of finding a test file that every configuration in the repository denies the existence of, because that file is precisely the object the engine was built to surface. A census produced by asking the runner what it knows about is not a census. It is the runner's opinion, restated.

3.2 The circularity trap is the first thing a naive implementation walks into and it is worth naming explicitly. It is tempting to enumerate tests by invoking the runner in a listing mode, because every runner offers one and the output is clean and structured. That output is the second census, not the first. It is the executed manifest's precursor, and using it as the authored census guarantees a reach of one hundred percent in every repository forever, because the numerator and denominator would be produced by the same instrument, filtered through the same configuration, bounded by the same collection path. The topology map must be built from the filesystem, by a process that has never read the project configuration, and the fact that this feels crude is the point: crudity is what buys independence.

3.3 Discovery proceeds by convention per language, and the conventions are cheap to enumerate and must be recorded rather than assumed. Python tests are files matching the test-prefixed or test-suffixed module patterns anywhere beneath the repository root. Elixir tests are files with the script extension ending in the test suffix, conventionally under a test directory but not necessarily. Java tests are source files under a test source root, conventionally named with a Test suffix, and the audit's own experience shows that the location is defined per build descriptor and therefore multiplies with the number of build descriptors. JavaScript and TypeScript tests are files carrying a spec or test infix. C harnesses follow no convention at all and must be found by their inclusion of an assertion header or by a name that a human chose. The engine ships one discovery rule per stack, each with its pattern set committed as data, because a discovery rule that lives in code is a rule nobody can audit.

3.4 Naming-based discovery produces false positives, and the exclusion list that suppresses them is the most dangerous artifact in the entire engine, because it is the one an adversary would edit. Vendored dependencies contain thousands of test files belonging to other projects. Virtual environments, build output directories, package caches, and compiled artifact trees contain more. Fixture modules whose names begin with the test prefix are data, not tests. Helper modules that a test imports are not themselves tests. Every one of these must be excluded, every exclusion narrows the denominator, and a narrowed denominator inflates the reach ratio. The engine therefore treats its exclusion list as a governed artifact: committed, diffed, reviewed as a change to the measurement instrument rather than as a change to a configuration file, and never editable by the same task whose reach figure it improves.

3.5 Naming-based discovery also produces false negatives, and these are worse, because a false negative shrinks the denominator invisibly and the shrinkage looks like health. Documentation tests embedded in source files are executable assertions that no test-file pattern will find. Property-based checks defined beside the code they exercise are tests living in a source file. A Java suite compiled into a non-standard source root is a suite the convention will miss. The KobiiSports C harnesses that include the graphics engine's headers are tests by intent and integration drivers by construction, and no pattern-based sweep will classify them correctly. The engine's answer is not to solve the classification problem, which is unsolvable in general, but to make its own recall auditable: it emits its pattern set, its exclusions, its hit count per rule, and — the load-bearing field — a count of files that matched no rule but live in a directory whose name suggests testing. That last count is the engine's own uncertainty, reported rather than concealed.

3.6 Counting must occur at two granularities and they answer different questions. The file granularity is cheap, is stable across runners, and is what makes the Claude Power Pack finding expressible: seventy-six authored, six reached. The case granularity is expensive, requires parsing rather than globbing, and is what makes the TUA-X finding expressible: four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight collected by default, three hundred and ninety green outside it. Neither granularity substitutes for the other. A repository can reach every file and a minority of cases if a scope line excludes a marker; it can reach every case in a minority of files if the unreached files are large. The engine reports both and never averages them, and where the case census cannot be produced — because the parse is unreliable for the stack — it reports the case reach as unknown rather than assuming it equals the file reach.

3.7 The project axis is the dimension that pattern-based discovery misses entirely, and it is the one that scales worst. A repository is not one suite. KobiiCraft carries ninety build descriptors, which is ninety potential suites, each with its own source roots, its own dependency set, its own toolchain assumptions, and its own reachability from whatever command the top-level build runs. TUA-X carries an internal subproject with its own test root. The topology map must therefore enumerate build descriptors as first-class nodes before it enumerates files, and it must record, for each descriptor, whether the repository's canonical invocation enters it at all. A module that the top-level build does not include is a module whose every test is orphaned, and no file-level sweep will reveal that, because the files are all present and all correctly named.

3.8 The map's schema is dictated by what the reconciliation will need, and every field earns its place by being subtracted against something. Each entry carries the artifact path, the artifact kind (test file, build descriptor, scanner configuration, hook, linter configuration), the stack, the discovery rule that matched it, the case count where parseable, the enclosing project node, a content hash, and the commit at which it was observed. The map as a whole carries the repository, the commit, the exclusion list applied, the per-rule hit counts, and the engine's own uncertainty count. That last field is what distinguishes a census from a claim: a census that cannot state what it might have missed is asserting completeness it has not earned.

3.9 Anchoring the map to a commit is what makes drift detectable and is not an administrative nicety. A topology map is a photograph. Two photographs at two commits produce a difference, and the difference is the raw material of the drift detector in Part XIV and the deletion detector in Part XV. Without the anchor, every measurement is an instant with no derivative, and every derivative in this dataset — the loss ledger, the baseline guardian, the drift integral, the weakening catalogue — is computed against a prior map. The content hash serves the same purpose one level down: a file whose path is unchanged and whose hash moved is a candidate weakening event, and it is retrieved by a comparison that costs nothing.

3.10 The map is an input and it is never a verdict, and this constraint deserves stating because it is the discipline the engine most easily abandons. A topology map showing seventy-six test files says nothing about protection. It does not say the repository is well tested, it does not say the repository is badly tested, and it does not license any claim whatsoever about the code. It is one half of a subtraction. Quoted alone it is exactly the artifact this dataset exists to discredit — a count of nodes presented as though it described a graph — and an engine that emits its topology map as a headline figure has become the thing it was built to detect.

PART III FINAL LAW

The authored census must be built from the filesystem by a process that has never read the project configuration, because a census produced by asking the runner what it knows is not a census but the runner's opinion restated, and it yields a reach of one hundred percent forever. Discovery is per-stack, its pattern set and its exclusions are committed and governed artifacts, and it must report its own uncertainty — the files that matched no rule but live where tests live. Build descriptors are enumerated before files: a module the build never enters orphans every test inside it, and no file sweep will show it.


PART IV — THE NON-STANDARD LOCATIONS: CO-LOCATED SUITES, OUT-OF-TREE FILES, INTERNAL SUBPROJECTS, SEPARATE FRONTEND SUITES, MANUAL TESTS, HARDWARE TESTS

4.1 Every non-standard location is a legitimate engineering decision and a reach liability at the same time, and holding both halves of that sentence simultaneously is the whole of this Part's discipline. The reflex of a governance layer confronted with a test file in an unexpected place is to declare the placement wrong and demand relocation. That reflex is a mistake: the placements enumerated below exist for good reasons, the reasons persist, and a policy that forbids them will be ignored, and an ignored policy governs nothing. The correct posture is that a non-standard location is permitted and must be connected — or, where connection is genuinely impossible, declared, owned, and expiring. Location is free. Disconnection is the defect.

4.2 The co-located suite places tests beside the code they exercise rather than in a mirrored tree. It is the dominant pattern in several ecosystems, it shortens the distance between a change and its guard, and it makes a module self-contained and movable. It is also the single most common producer of the out-of-tree orphan, because the canonical invocation almost always names a directory, and a co-located test is by definition not in that directory. Its signature in the topology map is unmistakable: test files whose enclosing directory is a source directory. Its detection is a one-line predicate. Its remediation is either to widen the invocation to the repository root, which is usually correct and usually cheap, or to add the co-located roots to the collection path explicitly, which is correct and requires that somebody maintain the list forever.

4.3 The out-of-tree file is the co-located suite's less principled cousin and is what Claude Power Pack accumulated to the tune of seventy of seventy-six files. It is not a pattern; it is a residue. A test was written next to a tool, or inside a module directory, or in a scratch location that became permanent, or at the top level during an investigation that never got cleaned up. Each individual placement had a local justification that has long since evaporated, and no placement was ever wrong enough to be worth an argument. The aggregate is a repository whose canonical invocation reaches under ten percent of its authored test files. The detection is the reconciliation itself and requires no special rule. The remediation is a single decision — widen the invocation, or enumerate the roots — and the reason it had never been taken is that nobody had ever seen the number.

4.4 The internal subproject is the structural version of the same failure and is more resistant to remediation, because it is deliberate. TUA-X contains a subsystem with its own test root holding two hundred and thirty-two tests, and that subsystem is a coherent unit with its own boundaries. It was not misplaced. The default collection path names a sibling subtree and therefore never enters it, and the two hundred and thirty-two tests pass on demand and run in no automated context. The detection requires the project axis of the topology map: a subproject is a node with its own descriptor, and the question is whether any canonical invocation enters it. The remediation is a matrix rather than a widening — each subproject gets its own invocation, and each invocation gets its own baseline, because merging them into one command produces a single number that hides which half died.

4.5 The separate frontend suite is the most systematically unmeasured surface in the estate and the audit was explicit about not having executed it. TUA-X carries twenty-nine specification files, KobiiCraft carries forty-one, and InfinityOps carries thirteen, and the audit counted them and did not run them, and recorded them as unverified rather than as passing, which is the correct verdict and is the honest one. The pattern is universal: a repository whose primary stack is one language acquires a second stack for its interface, the second stack brings its own runner, the runner is never wired into the repository's canonical invocation because the canonical invocation belongs to the primary stack, and the specification files accumulate. Their reach is unknown, which is worse than zero, because zero is a fact and unknown is an absence of measurement that will be read as zero risk.

4.6 Manual tests are the category that governance layers pretend does not exist and that every real estate carries. A script that a human runs before a release. A harness that requires a credential nobody wants in continuous integration. A probe that talks to a live service. These are real protection, they are frequently the only protection over the integration seams that matter most, and they have reach zero by construction, because no automated invocation reaches them and no automated invocation should. The engine's obligation here is not to force them into automation. It is to register them as a distinct class — protective artifacts with a declared human invocation — and to require of each an owner and a cadence, so that the question of when it was last run has an answer. A manual test with no record of its last execution is not a manual test. It is an orphan with a story.

4.7 Hardware tests are manual tests whose manual step is a machine. KobiiSports Resort carries C drivers that include the console's graphics headers; they are not host-runnable, they cannot be made host-runnable without a rewrite, and no amount of governance will change the fact that a frame-budget assertion requires a frame. Their reach on any host invocation is zero and will always be zero, and reporting them as orphans forever would train every reader to ignore the orphan list, which is an attack on the detector's credibility and therefore on its power. The correct handling is a declared exclusion carrying an owner, a reason, and an expiry, which removes them from the orphan class and places them in a ledger that ages — and the aging is what preserves the pressure, because an exclusion that has been renewed four times is a finding of a different kind.

4.8 The remediation ladder that these six categories share is short and is ordered by cost. First, widen the canonical invocation until it reaches the artifact, which is the correct answer for co-located suites and out-of-tree residue and costs a configuration line. Second, add an invocation, which is the correct answer for internal subprojects and separate frontend suites and costs a job. Third, register a declared human invocation with an owner and a cadence, which is the correct answer for manual tests and costs a record. Fourth, declare a bounded, owned, expiring exclusion, which is the correct answer for hardware tests and costs a waiver. There is no fifth rung. An artifact that fits none of the four is an orphan, and it is reported as one, and reporting it is the engine's entire job.

4.9 The classification itself must be mechanical, because a classification that requires judgement will be performed by the party with an interest in its outcome. The engine assigns each authored artifact to exactly one of six classes by predicate: reached by the canonical invocation; reachable by an existing invocation that the canonical set does not include; co-located with source and unreached; inside a project node the build does not enter; carrying a declared human invocation; carrying a declared expiring exclusion. Anything that satisfies none of the six is an orphan. The predicates are evaluated in order, the assignment is deterministic, and the engine never asks an agent to decide which class an artifact belongs to, because the decision is exactly where the pressure to launder would land.

4.10 The final point of this Part is that the categories are not a taxonomy of misbehaviour. They are a taxonomy of ordinary engineering, observed in real repositories, all of which were built competently by people who were doing sensible things. The co-location was sensible. The subproject boundary was sensible. The frontend runner was sensible. The hardware coupling was unavoidable. Not one of the six categories is a mistake, and the aggregate of all six, unmeasured, is a repository in which most of the authored protection is inert. That is the shape of this entire failure class: it is not built out of errors. It is built out of reasonable decisions whose interaction nobody was assigned to observe.

PART IV FINAL LAW

Every non-standard location is a legitimate engineering decision and a reach liability at once. Location is free; disconnection is the defect. Six classes, assigned by deterministic predicate and never by judgement: reached, reachable-but-unwired, co-located, inside an unentered project node, declared-human-invoked, declared-excluded-with-expiry. Anything satisfying none of the six is an orphan. And the categories are not a taxonomy of misbehaviour — every one of them arose from a sensible decision, and the failure lives entirely in the interaction that nobody was assigned to observe.


PART V — THE AUTHORED-VERSUS-EXECUTED RECONCILIATION ENGINE

5.1 The engine is a subtraction performed between two censuses that must not share a producer, and every design decision in it follows from that single constraint. Census A is the topology map of Part III, built from the filesystem, blind to configuration. Census B is the executed manifest, harvested from the runner's own machine-readable account of what it did. The diff between them is the reach report. The engine has no model, no heuristic, and no judgement; it is a join on two independently produced sets, and its entire correctness rests on the independence of the producers and the fidelity of the join key.

5.2 The executed manifest must be harvested from the runner's structured output and never from a summary, and this is the operational form of the constitution's prohibition on substituting a summary for an output. A runner that reports forty-three passing has told you a count. A runner that reports the node identity of each of the forty-three tests it collected has told you a set, and a set can be subtracted and a count cannot. Every runner in the estate can be made to emit the set: a collection-only listing mode, a machine-readable report file, a trace flag, a structured result document. The engine consumes identities. A pipeline that discards them at the point of measurement has destroyed the only signal reconciliation can eat, and it has destroyed it invisibly, because the count it retained is accurate.

5.3 The manifest is harvested at collection, not at execution, and the distinction is what makes the engine affordable. Collecting four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight tests costs seconds. Executing them costs minutes and side effects. The reach question — which artifacts does this invocation reach — is answered entirely at collection, and therefore the engine can run on every commit, in a hook, as a pre-flight, without ever paying the cost of a suite. Execution-stage losses, which are skips and deselections and fixture errors, require a run and are harvested from the run when one happens. The engine is therefore two-tiered by construction: a cheap collection-time reach check that can run constantly, and a richer execution-time loss check that runs when the suite runs.

5.4 The join key is the engine's hardest engineering problem and its most common source of a false finding. A filesystem census produces absolute paths. A runner produces node identities that may be relative to a root the runner computed, may be module paths rather than file paths, may use platform-specific separators, may differ in case on a case-insensitive host, and may carry a parameterization suffix that must be stripped to reach the file. A join that fails on normalization reports every test as an orphan, which is a catastrophic false positive that destroys the engine's credibility on first contact. The engine therefore normalizes both sides to a repository-relative, forward-separated, case-folded path before joining, and — the load-bearing safeguard — it asserts that the intersection is non-empty. An intersection of zero is not a finding of total orphanhood. It is a normalization bug, and the engine says so and refuses to emit a verdict.

5.5 The diff produces three sets and each carries a distinct meaning. Authored-and-executed is the reach: the artifacts that are actually protecting something. Authored-not-executed is the orphan set, which is the finding this entire dataset was built to surface. Executed-not-authored is the surprise set, and it is the one every implementation forgets, because it looks like an impossibility and it is not. A runner that executes a node the filesystem census never found means one of three things: the discovery rule has a recall gap, the exclusion list is over-broad, or the tests are generated at runtime and exist on disk only during the run. Each is a real condition and each is a defect in the measurement instrument, and the surprise set is the only place any of them will ever appear.

5.6 The surprise set is therefore the engine's self-audit and must be treated as an error rather than as a curiosity. A non-empty surprise set means the authored census is incomplete, and an incomplete authored census means the denominator of every reach metric is too small, and a denominator that is too small inflates the reach ratio in the flattering direction. The engine's own most likely failure mode is silently reporting a better reach than reality, and the surprise set is the single signal that detects it. It is therefore a first-class output, it is non-empty exactly when the instrument is broken, and its correct handling is to fix the discovery rule and recompute rather than to explain the surprise away.

5.7 The engine never trusts a count and this rule must be enforced at the type level rather than by discipline. Counts are lossy summaries of sets, they are what every existing tool emits, they are what every human quotes, and they are exactly what cannot be reconciled. Two counts that match may describe disjoint sets. Seventy-six authored files and seventy-six executed files is not reach of one hundred percent if they are different seventy-six files. The engine's internal representation is sets of identities from end to end, counts are derived at the reporting boundary and nowhere else, and any input arriving as a count is rejected as inadmissible for reconciliation purposes even though it may be perfectly true.

5.8 The blocked case must be handled explicitly, because it is the case that will occur in every estate and the case a naive implementation gets exactly backwards. When the runner cannot be invoked at all — the compiler refuses, the dependencies are unlocked, the toolchain is absent, which is precisely the state the audit found in the two Elixir repositories carrying one hundred and forty-nine ExUnit files between them — the executed manifest is empty. An engine that computes reach as executed over authored will report reach of zero, which is arithmetically defensible and epistemically wrong, because a reach of zero is a measurement and this is the absence of one. The correct output is that reach is unknown, that the environment is not qualified, and that no reach claim of any strength may be issued. Reporting zero would attribute to the repository a defect that belongs to the environment, and that is the canonical fault-attribution error the constitution's attribution law exists to forbid.

5.9 The engine's outputs are a reach report and a set of records, and they are separate objects with separate lifetimes. The reach report is a per-invocation, per-root, per-environment table of the three sets and the metrics derived from them, and it is ephemeral: it describes one commit under one invocation and expires the moment either changes. The records are durable. Each orphan class produces a FailureRecord carrying the F5 classification from the sealed taxonomy, an origin axis of config or ci or environment, a detectability axis of misleading-green, and a root cause that is demonstrated rather than hypothesized. Each remediation produces a RegressionRecord whose preventing test is a reach assertion, and whose red-before-green is observed by running that assertion against the unremediated configuration. The report is what the engine says. The records are what the estate keeps.

5.10 The engine's final and most easily forgotten obligation is to itself. It is a program, it lives in a file, and that file is subject to the exact law it enforces: it can be authored and never executed. An engine that is not reached by the canonical invocation is an inert node in the graph, indistinguishable from the uninvoked scanner it was built to find, and its report — which nobody runs — will be as green and as meaningless as everything else. The engine therefore emits its own file's reach status as a mandatory field of every report it produces, and a report that does not contain a positive self-reach assertion is inadmissible. An auditor exempt from its own audit is not an auditor.

PART V FINAL LAW

The engine is a subtraction between two censuses that must not share a producer: a filesystem map blind to configuration, and an executed manifest harvested as node identities from the runner's own structured output. Counts cannot be reconciled — two matching counts may describe disjoint sets — so identities are the only admissible input. The surprise set, executed-but-not-authored, is the instrument's self-audit and is non-empty exactly when the denominator is too small and the reach is flatteringly wrong. A blocked runner yields reach unknown, never reach zero: zero is a measurement, and this is the absence of one.



PART VI — THE RECONCILIATION CHAIN: AUTHORED, DISCOVERED, COLLECTED, EXECUTED, PASSED, EVIDENCE-PRODUCING

6.1 Reach is not a single boundary. It is a chain of six stages, each of which is a filter, each of which drops artifacts, and each of which drops them by a different mechanism that requires a different detector and yields a different record. The chain runs authored -> discovered -> collected -> executed -> passed -> evidence-producing. A protective artifact is genuinely protecting only when it survives all six, and the estate's instruments measure the fifth stage exclusively. Every finding of the founding audit is a loss at a stage nobody was watching, and the reason those losses were invisible is that the one number everybody reads is computed at stage five and is arithmetically correct about the population that reached it.

6.2 The first transition, authored -> discovered, is a loss in the measuring instrument rather than in the pipeline, and it is the only stage where the loss inflates the reported reach. A test that exists and that the topology sweep does not find is subtracted from the denominator, and a smaller denominator makes the ratio better. This is why Part III insists that the discovery rules, the exclusion list, and the per-rule hit counts are governed artifacts, and why the engine reports its own uncertainty count. Discovery loss is the only stage at which the engine can lie to itself in the flattering direction, and it is therefore the stage that the surprise set of Part V exists to police.

6.3 The second transition, discovered -> collected, is the stage at which the founding findings live and is the one the engine measures most cheaply. An artifact is collected when the runner, executing its own configuration, accepts the file into its run set. Losses here are configuration losses: a collection path scoping to a subtree, which is the TUA-X mechanism that orphaned three hundred and ninety green tests; a file living outside every configured root, which is the Claude Power Pack mechanism that left seventy of seventy-six files unreached; an ignore directive; a recursion exclusion; a module absent from a multi-project build's member list. The detector is the reconciliation diff and it costs a collection-only run, which is seconds. The record is a FailureRecord of class F5 with an origin axis of config.

6.4 The third transition, collected -> executed, is the stage that the runner itself reports and that no human reads. A test can be collected and then not run: a skip decorator, an expected-failure marker, a marker-based deselection in the default arguments, a fixture that errors during setup and aborts the test without failing the run, a collection error that the runner prints as a warning and does not propagate to the exit code. Every one of these appears in the runner's own output, in a line that names a number, and the number is not zero, and nobody looks at it. A suite reporting two hundred passed and forty skipped is universally read as two hundred passed. The forty are the interesting ones, because a test does not acquire a skip decorator by accident: somebody put it there, under pressure, intending to return, and the return did not happen.

6.5 The fourth transition, executed -> passed, is the only one the estate instruments and it is the one that carries the least information, because it is conditional on everything before it. A pass rate is a statement about the population that survived four prior filters, and it is arithmetically true of that population and silent about every artifact the filters removed. This is the precise mechanism by which a green signal describes a minority of authored protection while presenting as a description of the whole: the numerator and the denominator of the pass rate are both drawn from the survivors, and the survivors are exactly the set the reader believes to be the total. Forty-three of forty-three passing is a true statement, a complete statement about its population, and a description of six of seventy-six files.

6.6 The fifth transition, passed -> evidence-producing, is the stage that no repository in the audit instrumented at all and that this dataset asserts is a genuine stage rather than a bookkeeping formality. A test can pass and produce no durable artifact: the result exists in a terminal buffer, is read by a human, is summarized into a sentence, and is gone. Nothing downstream can compare against it. No baseline can detect that the count fell, because no baseline was recorded. No certification can cite it, because a citation requires an artifact with a hash and a timestamp and an environment. A pass that leaves no evidence has protected the code on exactly one occasion, for exactly one observer, and has contributed nothing to the estate's memory. The chain's last link is the one that makes every subsequent Part in this dataset possible, and it is the link every pipeline omits.

6.7 The chain composes multiplicatively and the arithmetic is brutal. A repository that discovers ninety-five percent of its authored tests, collects eighty percent of those, executes ninety percent of those, passes one hundred percent of those, and durably records the result of half of them, has an end-to-end protective yield of roughly thirty-four percent, and its dashboard reports one hundred percent because the dashboard reads stage five. Each individual stage looks acceptable. No single stage is a scandal. The product of five acceptable stages is a repository in which two thirds of the authored protection is doing nothing, and the fact that no stage is individually alarming is exactly why the aggregate went unobserved for the entire life of the estate.

6.8 Each stage requires its own detector and its own record, and collapsing them is the design error that makes a reach engine useless. A single number labelled reach, computed as passed over authored, conflates a configuration exclusion with a skip decorator with a discovery gap, and the three have entirely different owners, costs, and remediations. Widening a collection path is a configuration line. Removing a skip is an engineering decision about a test somebody deliberately disabled. Fixing a discovery gap is a change to the audit instrument. The engine therefore reports the chain as a chain: five transition losses, each with its own set of identities, each producing its own record class, and never summed into a headline figure that hides which link broke.

6.9 The chain also explains why the environment sits outside it rather than inside it. An unqualified environment does not cause a loss at one stage; it severs the chain before the first transition can be evaluated. The one hundred and forty-nine ExUnit files across the two Elixir repositories are authored and discovered and are then met by a compiler that will not run because dependencies are unlocked. There is no collection stage, no execution stage, and no pass stage to measure. The chain does not report a loss of one hundred percent at the collection boundary; it reports that the chain could not be traversed, that reach is unknown, and that the environment record carries a qualification of blocked. A severed chain and an empty chain are different objects and must never share a verdict.

6.10 The chain's practical value is that it turns a vague anxiety into an ordered list of cheap questions, and the ordering matters because each question is only meaningful if the previous one was answered. Did the sweep find every test file, and what is its own uncertainty count. Did the runner's collection accept every file the sweep found, and if not, which ones and by what configuration mechanism. Did every collected test run, and what does the skip line say. Did every executed test pass. And did the result land in an artifact that a baseline can compare against tomorrow. Five questions, each answerable in minutes, each producing a distinct record, and together they exhaust the space in which a green signal can be lying. The estate had never asked question one.

PART VI FINAL LAW

Reach is a chain of six stages — authored, discovered, collected, executed, passed, evidence-producing — and the estate instruments exactly one of them. The chain composes multiplicatively: five individually unalarming stages at ninety-five, eighty, ninety, one hundred and fifty percent yield a third of the authored protection and a dashboard reading one hundred percent, because the dashboard reads the pass rate, whose numerator and denominator are both drawn from the survivors. Discovery loss is the only stage that flatters the instrument. A severed chain reports unknown, never zero.


PART VII — THE REACH METRICS: TEST FILE REACH, TEST CASE REACH, SUITE ACTIVATION RATIO

7.1 Three metrics measure the positive side of the reconciliation and each answers a question the other two cannot. Test File Reach is the fraction of authored test files that the canonical invocation executes. Test Case Reach is the fraction of authored test cases that it executes. Suite Activation Ratio is the fraction of discovered suite roots that any canonical invocation enters at all. They are computed from the same diff, they diverge in informative ways, and the divergence between them is frequently a more valuable finding than any of their individual values.

7.2 Test File Reach is the cheapest of the three, is computable in seconds on any stack, and is the metric that made the founding finding expressible. Claude Power Pack authored seventy-six test files. Its canonical invocation reaches six. Test File Reach is six over seventy-six, which is under eight percent, and the number is not an estimate or a projection but a subtraction of two observed sets. The metric's virtue is that it requires no parsing, no language-specific case counting, and no runner cooperation beyond a collection listing, which means it can be deployed across a heterogeneous estate on day one and will produce a comparable figure for a Python repository, an Elixir repository, and a Java repository without any per-stack modelling.

7.3 Test File Reach's weakness is that files are not equal, and the weakness is symmetric. A repository that reaches ninety percent of its files may be missing the one file containing four hundred cases. A repository that reaches ten percent of its files may be missing seventy files containing one trivial assertion each. The metric is therefore a sentinel and never a certificate: a low value is a strong signal that something is wrong and demands investigation, and a high value licenses no conclusion at all. This asymmetry — informative when bad, uninformative when good — is characteristic of every cheap metric in this dataset and is the reason each is paired with a more expensive one.

7.4 Test Case Reach is the expensive metric and it required, in the founding audit, an explicit invocation of every orphaned root to establish. TUA-X's default invocation collects four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight cases. The two orphaned roots, invoked explicitly, yield one hundred and fifty-seven passing in one and two hundred and thirty-two passing with one skipped in the other, which places at least three hundred and ninety green cases outside the default collection and gives a default case reach in the region of ninety-two percent against the union of the three roots. That figure is instructive precisely because it is high. A ninety-two percent case reach reads as healthy on any dashboard, and it conceals three hundred and ninety tests that protect nothing, and the file-level and root-level metrics both scream while the case-level metric murmurs.

7.5 The divergence between file reach and case reach is therefore a finding in its own right and the engine reports it as one. A case reach far above the file reach means the unreached files are small, which usually means they are the specialized ones — the regression pins, the edge-case suites, the security deny-cases — because those are the files that hold few tests each. A file reach far above the case reach means one large suite is excluded, which is usually a subproject or a marker deselection. Neither is worse than the other. Both are invisible to anyone reading a single number, and the engine's obligation is to publish both and to name the divergence when it exceeds a recorded threshold.

7.6 Test Case Reach also carries a denominator problem that the founding audit exposed by failing to solve it, and honesty about that is more useful than a fabricated figure. Claude Power Pack's canonical invocation executes forty-three cases from six files. The authored case count across all seventy-six files was never established, because establishing it requires parsing seventy files that nothing in the pipeline had ever parsed. The correct report is therefore that Test File Reach is six over seventy-six and Test Case Reach is unknown, and the unknown is itself the finding: a repository that cannot state how many test cases it has authored cannot compute the metric that would tell it how many are protecting anything. An engine that filled the gap with an estimate would have manufactured the exact false confidence it exists to destroy.

7.7 Suite Activation Ratio operates on the project axis and is the metric that catches the failures the other two structurally cannot. It is the fraction of discovered suite roots — build descriptors, test roots, subprojects, secondary-stack packages — that any canonical invocation enters. TUA-X has three test roots and its default invocation enters one, giving an activation ratio of one third. KobiiCraft carries ninety build descriptors, of which the audit observed executed suites in two, and carries its mocking framework in one of the ninety, which is a statement about activation rather than about testing. The metric's value is that a root with zero activation contributes zero to every file-level and case-level numerator while contributing its full weight to both denominators, and so a repository can watch its file reach decay for years without ever seeing the structural cause.

7.8 All three metrics are reported per invocation, per root, and per environment, and never as a single repository-wide scalar, and this prohibition is the direct descendant of the constitution's refusal to average a confidence vector. A repository-wide reach figure is a mean, a mean conceals its minimum, and the minimum is the whole finding. A repository with one root at ninety-eight percent and one root at zero reports a comfortable aggregate that describes neither root and licenses a decision that is wrong for both. The engine emits a vector. The vector's minimum is the number that gates. Any consumer that reduces the vector to a mean before acting on it has reintroduced the failure the vector exists to prevent.

7.9 The metrics are weighted only at the point of decision and never at the point of measurement, and keeping those two operations separate is what preserves the raw signal. The measurement is a count of identities and is objective. The weighting — that a reach gap over a permission surface matters more than a reach gap over a formatting utility — is a risk judgement, it belongs to the risk ladder, and it composes the decision kernel for anything at the higher tiers. Fusing them produces a single risk-adjusted reach figure that cannot be audited, because its inputs are no longer separable, and it produces an instrument whose value can be moved by adjusting a weight rather than by connecting a test.

7.10 The three metrics together are still insufficient and the dataset states this plainly rather than leaving the reader to discover it. All three measure the reach of tests over tests. None of them measures the reach of tests over risk. KobiiCraft, whose reached suites pass one thousand five hundred and ninety-six assertions and ten more, and whose economy service — the surface on which player money lives — carries zero test references anywhere in its tree, would score well on every metric in this Part. The metrics in this Part are necessary, they are the foundation of everything that follows, and a system that stops here has built an instrument that reports perfect health over an unprotected money surface. That is why the loss metrics of Part VIII exist, and why the protection-surface baseline of Part XIII is the metric that actually gates.

PART VII FINAL LAW

Three reach metrics, each blind where the others see: Test File Reach is cheap, cross-stack, and made the six-of-seventy-six finding expressible; Test Case Reach is expensive and reported ninety-two percent over a repository holding three hundred and ninety orphaned green tests; Suite Activation Ratio catches the unentered root that drags both denominators while contributing to neither numerator. All are informative when bad and uninformative when good. All are vectors per root, never a mean, because a mean conceals its minimum and the minimum is the finding. And all three measure tests over tests, never tests over risk.


PART VIII — THE LOSS METRICS: ORPHANED TEST COUNT, SILENT COLLECTION LOSS, EXECUTED PROTECTION RATIO

8.1 The reach metrics measure what survived. The loss metrics measure what did not, and they are not the arithmetic complement of the reach metrics, because a complement would be a single quantity and the losses occur at three different stages by three different mechanisms with three different owners. A system that reports only reach reports a number that a reader can feel good or bad about. A system that reports the losses reports a list of identities, each attached to the mechanism that dropped it, and a list of identities is actionable in a way that a percentage never is.

8.2 Orphaned Test Count is the count of authored artifacts that no canonical invocation collects. It is an absolute number, not a ratio, and the choice of an absolute is deliberate: a ratio can be improved by growing the denominator and an absolute cannot. Claude Power Pack's orphaned test file count is seventy. TUA-X's orphaned test case count is three hundred and ninety. CostaLuz's orphaned scanner count is one. The orphan set is the engine's headline output, it is a set of paths rather than a figure, and its correct presentation is the list itself, because a reader shown seventy paths reacts differently from a reader shown a percentage, and the difference in reaction is the entire point of publishing it.

8.3 Silent Collection Loss is categorically distinct from orphanhood and is the metric that the runner itself already computes and that nobody consumes. It is the count of artifacts that the runner collected and then did not execute: skipped, marked as expected failures, deselected by a marker expression in the default arguments, aborted by a setup error that did not fail the run, or dropped by a collection error that the runner reported as a warning while exiting zero. The runner prints every one of these. The number appears in the last line of the output. It is the most-printed and least-read number in software engineering, and it is silent not because the runner conceals it but because the pipeline downstream of the runner discards everything except the pass count.

8.4 The two losses have opposite discovery properties and this is why they must be counted separately. An orphan is invisible to the runner and visible to the reconciliation. A silent collection loss is visible to the runner and invisible to the pipeline. Fixing the first requires an instrument the estate does not have. Fixing the second requires only that somebody read a line that is already being printed, and the fact that the second is nonetheless universal tells you something important about how quality signals actually decay: they do not decay by concealment, they decay by non-consumption, and a number that is emitted and never read is functionally identical to a number that was never computed.

8.5 Silent Collection Loss carries an age dimension that the orphan count does not, and the age is the finding. A test skipped yesterday is a decision. A test skipped for eleven months is a deletion that nobody had to justify. The engine therefore records, for each skip and each expected failure, the commit at which the marker was introduced, and it reports the age distribution rather than the count alone. The countermeasure to skip accumulation is not a prohibition, which would merely relocate the pressure into outright deletion where it is harder to see. It is a standing, owned, visible inventory in which every skip has a name attached and an age that grows in public.

8.6 Executed Protection Ratio is the third loss metric and it is the one that changes the subject from tests to risk. It is the fraction of the protection surface — the enumerated set of things that can break, specified in Part XIII — that is reached by at least one executed test. It is the only metric in this dataset whose denominator is not made of tests, and that property is what makes it the only metric that could have caught the KobiiCraft finding. One thousand five hundred and ninety-six assertions pass. Ten more pass. Every one of them is reached. And the economy service, on which player money lives and which is the single most abusable surface a server exposes, has zero test references anywhere in the entire plugin tree, which places its Executed Protection Ratio at zero regardless of what the other metrics report.

8.7 The three loss metrics are non-overlapping by construction and must never be summed, and the temptation to sum them is strong because a single total loss figure would be convenient. An orphaned file, a skipped case, and an unprotected surface element are three different kinds of object, measured in three different units, dropped at three different stages, owned by three different parties. Adding them produces a number with no unit and no owner. The engine reports three numbers and three identity sets, and it accepts that three numbers are harder to put on a dashboard than one, and it regards that difficulty as a feature, because the pressure to collapse a vector into a scalar is precisely the pressure that produced every finding in this corpus.

8.8 The Loss Ledger is the durable substrate beneath the three metrics and it is append-only for a reason that Part XVIII will make sharp. If the loss figures are recomputed fresh on every run with no memory, then the cheapest way to drive the orphan count to zero is to delete the orphans, and the ledger would show an improvement. An append-only ledger records that seventy files were orphaned at a named commit, and records at a later commit that sixty-eight of them were connected and two were deleted, and the deletion is a line in the ledger rather than an absence in a recomputation. The ledger's job is not to store the current number. It is to make the history of the number un-erasable, because every gaming strategy against this dataset is an attempt to change a number without leaving a trace.

8.9 Each loss metric emits a record and the record classes differ, which is what makes the losses routable. An orphan emits a FailureRecord of class F5 with an origin axis of config, ci, or environment depending on the severing mechanism, a detectability axis of misleading-green, and a cross-repository exposure field naming every repository in the estate that uses the same runner — because the correct response to finding a scope line hiding tests in one repository was never to fix that line, it was to examine every repository with the same runner for the same class of exclusion. A silent collection loss emits a record with an origin axis of test and an owner and an age. An unprotected surface element emits a record of class F1 or F8 depending on whether the gap is over logic or over an authorization boundary, which is exactly the double classification the sealed taxonomy assigns to the economy finding.

8.10 The loss metrics complete the reconciliation engine's output and they are the half of it that anybody acts on. Nobody remediates a reach percentage. People remediate a list of seventy paths, a list of forty skips with an owner and an age, and a named service with zero test references. The metrics exist to make the lists comparable across repositories and across time; the lists exist to make the metrics actionable. An engine that emits only the metrics has produced a dashboard. An engine that emits only the lists has produced a nuisance. The pairing — a figure that trends and a set of identities that can be worked — is what makes the difference between an instrument that changes behaviour and one that decorates a wall.

PART VIII FINAL LAW

Three losses at three stages, never summed: the orphan, invisible to the runner and visible only to reconciliation; the silent collection loss, printed by the runner in a line nobody reads and therefore decayed by non-consumption rather than by concealment; and the Executed Protection Ratio, the only metric whose denominator is risk rather than tests, and therefore the only one that could see one thousand five hundred and ninety-six green assertions standing beside a money surface with zero test references. The Loss Ledger is append-only, because every attack on this dataset is an attempt to move a number without leaving a trace.


PART IX — CANONICAL INVOCATION DISCOVERY: WHAT A HUMAN, A CONTINUOUS-INTEGRATION JOB, OR AN AGENT WOULD CONSIDER THE OFFICIAL TEST COMMAND

9.1 Every metric in this dataset is a function of two arguments and one of them has been assumed so far. Reach is reach with respect to an invocation, and the entire apparatus collapses if the invocation is chosen badly or chosen by the party being measured. This Part specifies how the canonical invocation is discovered, which oracles are consulted, how their disagreement is resolved, and why the disagreement itself is frequently the most valuable output of the procedure. An engine that lets an agent nominate its own canonical invocation has handed the agent control of the denominator and has ceased to measure anything.

9.2 Three oracles exist and each is consulted independently. The first is the continuous-integration configuration: whatever command a job actually executes is a fact about the estate's automated behaviour and is not subject to interpretation. The second is the documentation: a readme, a contributor guide, a project file's script section, a task runner's default target. The third is the zero-argument default: what the stack's standard runner does when invoked with no arguments from the repository root. Each oracle answers a different question. The first answers what runs. The second answers what somebody intended to run. The third answers what will run when a party with no context arrives.

9.3 The third oracle is the one that matters most and is the one that governance layers systematically undervalue, and the reason is that the estate is increasingly operated by parties with no context. An agent dispatched to verify a repository will type the stack's default command. A new engineer will type the stack's default command. A hook, a pre-commit gate, a done-gate, and a session-start check will all type the stack's default command, because the default command is the only one that generalizes across a heterogeneous estate. Whatever that command reaches is what the estate's automated conscience will see, and everything it does not reach is, for every practical purpose, invisible. The zero-argument default is therefore not one oracle among three. It is the de facto canonical invocation, and the other two exist to be compared against it.

9.4 The precedence rule resolves disagreement mechanically and without negotiation. Where a continuous-integration job exists, its command is authoritative for the question of what the estate actually runs, because it is the only oracle backed by an observation rather than by an intention. Where no such job exists, the zero-argument default is authoritative, because it is what will be run by whoever arrives next. Documentation is never authoritative. It is evidence of intent, it is frequently correct, it is frequently a description of a command that stopped working two years ago, and it is the oracle most likely to describe a repository that no longer exists — which is exactly the failure the constitution's supremacy doctrine addresses by placing the disk above the plan.

9.5 Disagreement between the oracles is a first-class finding and is reported as such rather than being silently resolved. A readme that documents a command reaching every suite root, beside a job that runs a command reaching one, is a repository whose authors believe they are protected and whose automation disagrees. That gap is the belief-versus-behaviour gap, it is the precise psychological substrate of every finding in this corpus, and it is measurable: the engine computes reach under each oracle's command and reports the vector. The difference between documented reach and actual reach is the size of the estate's collective misapprehension, expressed as a number, which is a thing no other instrument produces.

9.6 The canonical invocation is frequently a set rather than a single command, and modelling it as a scalar is a design error that produces false orphans. A repository with a primary-stack suite, a secondary-stack interface suite, and a security scanner has three legitimate invocations, and a test reached by the second is not an orphan merely because the first does not collect it. The engine therefore discovers a CanonicalInvocationSet, computes reach as the union over the set, and — critically — also computes and reports the per-invocation reach, because a set whose union covers everything while one member covers nothing has a dead invocation that will be discovered on the day somebody relies on it.

9.7 The absence of any canonical invocation is a valid discovery outcome and must be reported as a finding rather than as a measurement failure. KobiiSports Resort's C drivers include the console's graphics headers and are not host-runnable; there is no command on any host in the estate that executes them, and the audit said so, and saying so was correct. A repository with no canonical invocation has a reach that is undefined rather than zero, its every protective artifact falls into the declared-exclusion class of Part IV or into the orphan class, and the decision between those two is a governance decision with an owner, not a measurement the engine can make.

9.8 Scanners, linters, type checkers, schema validators, and hooks have canonical invocations exactly as tests do, and extending the discovery procedure to them costs nothing and is what generalizes this Part beyond testing. The question for a declared security analyser is identical in form to the question for a test file: which job invokes it. CostaLuz declares one and configures one and no job invokes it, which means its canonical invocation set is empty, which means its reach is zero, which means it protects nothing — and the entire chain of reasoning is the same chain, applied to a different node class, producing the same verdict by the same procedure. That the procedure transfers without modification is the strongest available evidence that the law it enforces is a law.

9.9 The discovery procedure emits a CanonicalInvocationRecord and the record's fields are chosen so that a reach figure can never be quoted without them. Each record carries the command verbatim, the oracle that supplied it, the working directory, the environment record under which it was observed, whether it was actually executed by the engine or merely read from a configuration, the suite roots it entered, and the collection manifest it produced. A reach figure that does not cite such a record is inadmissible, in exactly the way that a temperature without a scale is inadmissible, and the engine refuses to emit one.

9.10 The final discipline of this Part is that the canonical invocation must be discovered before the reach is computed and never adjusted afterwards, and the ordering is a firewall rather than a convention. An engine that computes a reach, finds it embarrassing, and then reconsiders which invocation was really canonical has performed scope laundering — the retrospective narrowing of an objective so that the work performed becomes the work intended — and it has performed it against itself, which is the hardest version to detect because there is no adversary to blame. The invocation is discovered, recorded, hashed, and then the reach is computed against it. Changing the invocation after seeing the number is a governance event, it is recorded as one, and it does not silently produce a better figure.

PART IX FINAL LAW

Reach is a function of two arguments and the second is the invocation, so an engine that lets the measured party nominate its own invocation has handed over the denominator and measures nothing. Three oracles: the job, which observes what runs; the documentation, which is evidence of intent and never authoritative; and the zero-argument default, which is what a party with no context will type and is therefore the de facto canon. Their disagreement is the belief-versus-behaviour gap made numeric. The invocation is discovered, recorded, and hashed before the reach is computed — reconsidering it afterwards is scope laundering with no adversary to blame.


PART X — CANONICAL INVOCATION FAILURE PATTERNS PER RUNNER

10.1 The law generalizes and the mechanisms do not, and an engine that ships only the law will find nothing. Each runner in the estate severs the invocation-to-artifact edge by its own means, each means has a distinct signature in a distinct file, each requires a distinct probe, and this Part enumerates the observed ones. Every pattern below was seen in the founding audit; none is hypothetical; and the enumeration is deliberately incomplete, because a catalogue that claimed completeness would be asserting a coverage of a space nobody has bounded.

10.2 The Python collection-path pattern is the archetype and is what orphaned three hundred and ninety green tests in TUA-X. The project configuration declares a collection path naming one subtree, the default invocation therefore never descends into sibling roots, and the sibling roots accumulate tests that pass on demand and run in no automated context. The signature is a single key in a single configuration file. The probe is to parse that key and compare its subtree against the topology map's set of directories containing test files, and any directory in the second and not under the first is an orphaned root. The probe costs a file read and it would have found this defect on the day the second root was created.

10.3 The Python out-of-root pattern is the same class by a different mechanism and is what left seventy of Claude Power Pack's seventy-six test files unreached. Here there is no exclusion at all: the invocation names a directory, the tests were authored elsewhere, and the configuration is innocent. The distinction matters operationally because the remediations differ — the first is fixed by widening a declared path, the second by widening the invocation itself or by moving files — and it matters diagnostically because an engine that only parses configurations will find the first and miss the second entirely. The probe for the second is the reconciliation, and only the reconciliation, which is why configuration parsing can never substitute for it.

10.4 The Elixir pattern observed in the audit does not sever the edge at collection; it severs it before compilation. InfinityOps and CostaLuz both fail to compile, with the toolchain reporting that dependencies are not locked, and one hundred and twenty-two ExUnit files in the first and twenty-seven in the second are therefore unverifiable — not failing, unverifiable, and the distinction is the whole of the attribution law. The signature is a compiler error naming unlocked dependencies. The probe is to attempt the compile and to classify a failure at that stage as an environment qualification failure rather than a product failure. The verdict is blocked, the reach is unknown, the environment record carries the blocker verbatim, and no engineer is dispatched to repair product code that was never broken.

10.5 The Java pattern severs the edge at the toolchain boundary and is the most expensive failure in the catalogue because it costs an entire run before producing any signal. KobiiCraft's prebuilt test classes target a major version that a runtime one version behind refuses to load, and the audit lost a run to exactly that mismatch before pinning the correct version and observing one thousand five hundred and ninety-six passing assertions in the map engine and ten in the core module. The signature is a class-file version error. The probe is to assert the toolchain version before invoking anything, and to record it in the environment record, because a baseline recorded under one toolchain and compared under another is comparing two different repositories.

10.6 The Java multi-project pattern is a second, independent severing mechanism in the same stack and is the one that scales worst. KobiiCraft carries ninety build descriptors. A top-level build enters the modules its descriptor names, and a module absent from that list is a module whose every test is orphaned regardless of how well written it is and how correctly it is named. The signature is a descriptor present on disk and absent from its parent's member list. The probe is a graph traversal from the root descriptor, and any descriptor not reachable from the root is an unentered project node — the fourth class of Part IV's classification, and one that no file-level sweep can see because every file in it is present and correctly named.

10.7 The interface-stack pattern is the one the estate has never measured. TUA-X carries twenty-nine specification files, KobiiCraft forty-one, and InfinityOps thirteen, and the audit counted them and did not execute them and recorded them as unverified, which was the honest verdict. The severing mechanism is that the repository's canonical invocation belongs to the primary stack and the specification files belong to a runner the canonical invocation has never heard of. The signature is a package manifest declaring a test script that no job invokes. The probe is the same probe used for scanners: search the job graph for an edge to that script, and where none exists, the entire secondary suite has reach zero and is recorded as such rather than as passing or as absent.

10.8 The scanner pattern severs the edge with no configuration at all and is therefore the hardest to see, because there is nothing to parse and nothing to misread. CostaLuz declares its static security analyser and configures it and no job runs it. The signature is a declared tool with no inbound invocation edge — an absence, and absences do not appear in diffs, do not appear in configurations, and do not appear in any report generated by any tool in the ordinary pipeline. The probe is the only one available: enumerate every declared guard from the dependency manifests and the configuration files, then enumerate every command executed by every job, and subtract. The subtraction is trivial. Nobody had performed it.

10.9 The exit-code pattern is the meta-failure that can defeat every probe above and deserves its own treatment. A runner that encounters a collection error, prints it as a warning, and exits zero has produced a green build from a broken suite. A job step marked to continue on error has an invoked guard whose findings are advisory and will be ignored within two iterations. A scanner invoked with a scope that matches no files exits zero, having scanned nothing, and reports success. In each case the edge exists, the invocation happened, and the protection is nonetheless zero, and the only probe that catches it is to assert that the run's exit code is load-bearing and that its file count is non-zero. An invoked control whose failure cannot fail anything is not a control.

10.10 The unifying observation is that nine distinct mechanisms across five stacks all reduce to three probes, and reducing them is what makes the engine buildable rather than an endless catalogue. The first probe parses configuration and compares its declared scope against the topology map, which catches collection paths, member lists, and scope patterns. The second probe reconciles the collection manifest against the topology map, which catches everything the first probe misses, including the mechanisms nobody has enumerated yet. The third probe interrogates the invocation itself — its exit code, its toolchain, its file count, its inbound edges — which catches the failures where the edge exists and is inert. Three probes, mechanically applied, would have found every one of the nine.

PART X FINAL LAW

Nine severing mechanisms across five stacks: a collection path scoping to a subtree; files authored outside every configured root; a compile blocked by unlocked dependencies, which is unverifiable and never failing; a toolchain a major version behind, which loses the run before a test executes; a build descriptor absent from its parent's member list; an interface suite whose runner the canon has never heard of; a declared scanner with no inbound invocation edge; and an exit code that cannot fail. All nine reduce to three probes — parse the declared scope, reconcile the manifest, interrogate the invocation — and three probes, applied mechanically, would have found every one.



PART XI — THE COLLECTION-TRAP CATALOGUE AND HOW EACH IS DETECTED

11.1 A trap, in this dataset, is a mechanism by which a protective artifact is separated from its invocation without anybody performing an act that looks like removing protection. The definition is deliberately narrow and it excludes deletion, which is loud, and it excludes failure, which is loud, and it includes only the quiet mechanisms — the ones whose signature in a diff is a configuration line, a decorator, a directory move, or an absence. Twelve are catalogued here. Each was either observed in the founding audit or is the direct structural neighbour of one that was, and each is specified with the artifact it lives in, the reason it is invisible, and the probe that finds it.

11.2 The scope trap lives in a project configuration and declares which subtree the runner may descend into. It is invisible because it is correct: on the day it was written it described the repository exactly, it made the default run fast, and it was the right decision. It becomes a trap through the passage of time and the arrival of a second root, and there is no moment at which anybody is prompted to reconsider it, because a configuration line has no owner, no expiry, and no test. Probe one, the configuration parse, finds it by comparing the declared subtree against the set of directories the topology map found tests in.

11.3 The out-of-root trap has no configuration at all and is therefore invisible to probe one entirely. The invocation names a directory, tests were authored elsewhere, and nothing anywhere declares that the elsewhere is excluded — it simply was never included. This is the trap that consumed seventy of seventy-six files in the repository that audits everything else, and it is the reason that configuration parsing can never be the engine's primary probe. Probe two, the manifest reconciliation, is the only thing that finds it, and it finds it without knowing anything about the stack, the configuration format, or the runner's internals.

11.4 The marker trap lives in the runner's default arguments and deselects a class of tests by a label rather than by a location. Its signature is a default-argument line containing a marker expression, most often excluding a category somebody once found slow or flaky. It is more insidious than the scope trap because the deselected tests can live inside the collection path, can be discovered by the topology sweep, and can even appear in a collection listing, and are then removed from the run set by an expression that reads as a performance optimization. Probe one finds it in the configuration; probe two confirms the identities it removed.

11.5 The skip trap and the expected-failure trap live in the test files themselves and are the only traps whose author knew, at the moment of authoring, that they were removing protection. They are also the only traps that are universally regarded as legitimate, and they are legitimate — a test disabled during an investigation is a reasonable thing. The trap is not the skip. The trap is the absence of any force that would ever cause anybody to revisit it, which is why Part VIII insists that the skip inventory carries an age and an owner. Probe three, the invocation interrogation, harvests these from the runner's own output, which has been printing them all along.

11.6 The import-error trap is the one that produces the most dangerous artifact in the catalogue, because it removes an entire directory of tests and produces a warning rather than a failure. A shared fixture module raises during import; every test file in that directory becomes uncollectable; the runner reports a collection error, exits zero under some configurations, and the pipeline reports success. The count drops silently. Probe two catches it as a mass orphaning event, probe three catches it as a non-zero collection-error count, and the baseline guardian of Part XII catches it as a decrease — which is three independent detections of one trap, and the redundancy is deliberate, because this trap can remove hundreds of tests in a single commit that touches no test file.

11.7 The unentered-project trap lives in a build descriptor's member list and is the trap that scales with the size of the repository. A module exists on disk, contains a well-formed suite, and is not named by its parent, and so the top-level build never enters it and every test inside it is orphaned by omission rather than by exclusion. In a repository carrying ninety build descriptors this trap can be present many times over without any individual instance being noticeable, and no file-level instrument will ever see it, because every file is present, correctly named, and syntactically perfect. Probe one finds it by traversing the descriptor graph from the root and reporting the descriptors it never reaches.

11.8 The toolchain trap and the dependency-lock trap sever the edge before collection begins, and they are grouped because they share a verdict rather than a mechanism. A runtime one major version behind the compiled test classes loses the entire run. An unlocked dependency set stops the compiler before it reaches a test, which is what left one hundred and forty-nine ExUnit files across two repositories unverifiable. In both cases the correct output is blocked, the reach is unknown, and the environment record carries the blocker verbatim, and the single most important property of the detection is that it must not attribute the failure to the product. Probe three establishes the environment's qualification before any measurement is attempted, and a measurement attempted against an unqualified environment is discarded rather than recorded.

11.9 The no-job trap has no artifact to inspect and is detected only by subtraction, and it is the trap that proves the catalogue must extend beyond tests. A declared guard with no inbound invocation edge is inert, and the absence of an edge is not visible in any file, any diff, or any report. The probe enumerates the declared guards from the dependency manifests and configuration files, enumerates every command every job executes, and subtracts. Its near neighbour, the workspace trap, is the same absence in a monorepo: a root-level test script that runs one package while a dozen packages carry their own suites, so that the union of the declared invocations covers a fraction of the declared packages.

11.10 The exit-code trap and the vacuous-scope trap close the catalogue and they are the two that defeat every other probe, because in both the edge exists and the invocation happens. A step that is configured to continue on error has invoked its guard and disarmed it. A scanner pointed at a scope matching no files has run, scanned nothing, and exited successfully. A runner that prints a collection error and exits zero has run and hidden the fact that it ran less. In each case, reach as measured by the manifest is perfect and protection is zero. Probe three is the only defence and its two assertions are simple: the exit code must be capable of failing the build, and the run must report a non-zero count of things it actually examined.

11.11 The twelfth trap is the one aimed at this dataset and it is named the census trap. It is the widening of the discovery exclusion list so that authored artifacts vanish from the denominator, which improves every ratio in this corpus without connecting a single test. It is detected by the surprise set of Part V, by the governance treatment of the exclusion list as a committed and diffed instrument, and by the append-only Loss Ledger of Part VIII, and the fact that it requires three independent defences is a measure of how attractive it is. Every other trap in this catalogue is an accident. This one is the only one that can only be deliberate.

11.12 The catalogue's operational punchline is its collapse. Twelve traps, five stacks, and four artifact kinds reduce to three probes: parse the declared scope and compare it against the topology map; reconcile the collection manifest against the topology map; and interrogate the invocation for its exit-code semantics, its toolchain, its examined-file count, and its inbound edges. Probe two is the universal one and would find eleven of the twelve without knowing anything about the stack. Probe one is fast and finds the traps that a manifest cannot, because a manifest of a run that never happened is empty. Probe three is the one that catches the traps in which everything looks connected and nothing is armed. An engine that implements all three is complete against the observed catalogue and, more importantly, is not blind to the traps that have not yet been catalogued, because probe two does not enumerate mechanisms — it enumerates outcomes.

PART XI FINAL LAW

Twelve traps, one definition: a mechanism that separates a protective artifact from its invocation without anybody performing an act that looks like removing protection. Deletion is loud, failure is loud, and every trap here is quiet — a scope line, a marker expression, a skip decorator, an unentered descriptor, a missing edge, a disarmed exit code. Eleven are accidents and one, the widening of the census exclusion list, can only be deliberate. All twelve fall to three probes, and the universal probe enumerates outcomes rather than mechanisms, which is why it also finds the traps nobody has catalogued yet.


PART XII — THE TEST COUNT BASELINE GUARDIAN: FAILING ON A SILENT DECREASE

12.1 The reconciliation engine measures a state. The baseline guardian measures a derivative, and the derivative is where the estate's most dangerous events live, because the states are frequently acceptable and the transitions between them are frequently not. A repository whose executed test count is four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight is neither healthy nor unhealthy on that number alone. A repository whose executed test count was four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight last week and is four thousand and ninety today, with a green build, has lost two hundred and seventy-eight tests to a mechanism nobody chose and nobody noticed, and the loss is invisible in every artifact the pipeline produces, because the pipeline reports pass rate and the pass rate is one hundred percent of a smaller population.

12.2 The guardian's contract is a single asymmetry: an increase requires nothing, and a decrease fails the build. This is deliberately not a symmetric constraint, and the asymmetry is what makes the guardian cheap enough to be tolerated. Adding tests is the behaviour the estate wants and gating it would be perverse. Losing tests silently is the behaviour that produced every finding in this corpus, and gating it costs a comparison against a recorded number. The guardian therefore does exactly one thing: it refuses a build in which the executed count fell without an accompanying, explicit, reasoned baseline update. It has no opinion about whether the tests were good, whether the count is high enough, or whether the coverage is adequate. It cares only that protection is not withdrawn in silence.

12.3 The baseline is recorded per suite root and never as a repository total, and this is the guardian's single most important design decision. A repository total is a sum, a sum permits redistribution, and redistribution is exactly what a refactor produces: two hundred tests leave root A and one hundred and eighty arrive in root B, the total falls by twenty, and a tolerance of one percent swallows it. Worse, a root can die completely — an entire directory dropped from the collection path — while a growing sibling absorbs the difference and the total rises. A per-root baseline makes that impossible: root A's count fell to zero, and no arithmetic anywhere else can conceal it.

12.4 The baseline is keyed by environment and this is what prevents the most common false alarm and the most effective attack simultaneously. The same Java repository yields one thousand six hundred and six passing assertions under the correct toolchain and zero under a runtime one major version behind, because the run is lost before a test executes. A baseline recorded under one and compared under the other reports a catastrophic decrease that is entirely an artifact of the harness, and an engineer dispatched by that alarm would go looking for deleted tests that nobody deleted. The baseline therefore carries the environment record — the runtime versions, the toolchain, the lockfile state — and a comparison across differing environment keys is not a comparison. It is two measurements of two different systems, and the guardian says so rather than raising an alarm it cannot substantiate.

12.5 The baseline records more than a count and the additional fields are what make it un-gameable. It records the collected count, the executed count, the passed count, the skipped count, the file count, the invocation verbatim, the environment key, the commit, and — the field that costs the most and pays the most — the set of node identities. With identities, a baseline comparison can report which tests disappeared, and a report naming three specific tests is actionable in a way that a report naming a delta of three never is. Without identities, the guardian can detect that something was lost and can say nothing whatsoever about what, and a party investigating the alarm will spend an hour reconstructing what the guardian could have handed them.

12.6 A legitimate decrease exists and the guardian must accommodate it without becoming a formality. Tests are deleted for good reasons: a feature is removed, a module is retired, a suite is genuinely superseded. The protocol is an explicit baseline update carrying a reason, an author, and the identity set that was removed, and the update is a commit that a reviewer sees. The cost is a few minutes. The value is that the removal of protection becomes a visible, attributed, reviewable act rather than a side effect, and that is the entire mechanism: the guardian does not prevent deletion, it prevents deletion from being invisible.

12.7 The baseline update is subject to a firewall inherited directly from the constitution's treatment of gate mutation, and without it the entire guardian is decorative. The party whose change caused the decrease may not, in the same task, author the baseline update that permits it. The reason is not suspicion of that party; it is that when a gate is the only object standing between an agent and completion, editing the gate is one write and every honest path is work, and the gradient is followed by sincere parties as reliably as by hostile ones. A baseline lowered calmly, in its own commit, with a stated reason, is governance. The same baseline lowered inside the commit that made it necessary is an escape, and the two are indistinguishable in the artifact unless the firewall separates them in time.

12.8 Legitimate variance must be declared as a range or it will destroy the guardian by noise. A count that depends on the host, on the presence of an optional service, or on a parameterization that reads the environment is not a constant and cannot be baselined as one. The correct handling is a declared range with a stated cause, and — the load-bearing addendum — a range wider than a recorded threshold is itself a finding, because a suite whose executed count varies by hundreds across runs is a suite whose reach is not a property of the repository but a property of the weather. A guardian that quietly widens its tolerance until the alarms stop has been defeated by the thing it was built to catch.

12.9 The guardian must produce a greppable artifact and this requirement is not cosmetic. A done-gate consumes the guardian's verdict, a hook consumes it, a certification cites it, and every one of those consumers is a program. The output therefore carries a stable identifier per checked root, a verdict, the observed counts, the baseline counts, and the identity delta, and it exits non-zero on any unexplained decrease. This is the point at which the guardian becomes the evidence-producing stage of the chain in Part VI — the stage no repository in the audit instrumented — and it is why that stage is a stage rather than a formality: a pass that leaves no comparable artifact cannot be defended tomorrow against a decrease nobody will notice.

12.10 The guardian's final obligation is the one it is most likely to fail, and it is the law of this entire dataset applied to the dataset's own instrument. The guardian is a program. It lives in a file. That file can sit outside the canonical invocation exactly like the seventy files that provoked this corpus, and a guardian that is never invoked is a guardian that never refuses anything, and a control that has never refused anything is in exactly the epistemic position of a test that has never failed: it may be working perfectly, it may be entirely disconnected, and from the outside — where everything is green — the two are indistinguishable. The guardian must therefore appear in its own reach report, its own execution must be a gate in the canonical invocation, and a guardian that cannot demonstrate its own last firing is not evidence of protection. It is another inert node.

PART XII FINAL LAW

The state is frequently acceptable and the transition frequently is not, so the guardian measures the derivative: increases are free, and a silent decrease fails the build. Baselines are per-root, because a total permits redistribution and an entire root can die while a growing sibling absorbs it; keyed by environment, because the same repository yields sixteen hundred assertions under one toolchain and zero under another; and carry node identities, because a delta of three is an alarm and three names are an action. The party whose change caused the decrease may not author the baseline update that permits it — in the same task, that is not a correction, it is an escape.


PART XIII — THE PROTECTION SURFACE BASELINE: WHY COUNTING TESTS IS NOT ENOUGH

13.1 Every metric to this point has counted guards. Not one has counted the things being guarded, and that omission is the single largest blind spot in the instrument as specified so far, because a repository can achieve perfect reach over its authored tests while leaving its most abusable surface entirely unprotected — and the founding audit observed exactly that. KobiiCraft's reached suites pass one thousand five hundred and ninety-six assertions in one module and ten in another. Every reached test passes. And its economy service, the surface on which player money lives, carries zero test references anywhere in the entire plugin tree. Reach over tests is perfect. Protection over money is nil. No metric in Parts VII or VIII can see it.

13.2 The protection surface is the enumerated inventory of things that can break in a way somebody would care about, and constructing it is the work this Part specifies. Its elements are not lines of code and not functions, because those enumerations are too fine to be risk-weighted and too numerous to be owned. Its elements are the units at which a failure has a consequence somebody can name: modules with load-bearing logic, user-invocable commands, network routes, packet and message handlers, permission and authorization checks, persisted schemas, external contracts, scheduled jobs, recovery paths, and the guards themselves — the scanners, linters, and validators whose activation is Part XVII's subject.

13.3 The surface inventory is built by the same discipline as the topology map and from the same source, which is the disk. Commands are discovered from the manifest that declares them. Routes are discovered from the router. Permission checks are discovered by symbol search for the authorization primitives the stack uses. Persisted schemas are discovered from the migration set. Scheduled jobs are discovered from the scheduler configuration. Each discovery rule is cheap, each is stack-specific, each is committed as data rather than buried in code, and each carries the same self-reported uncertainty count as the test discovery rules, because a surface inventory that cannot state what it might have missed is asserting a completeness it has not earned.

13.4 The mapping from surface to protection is the operation that produces the metric and it admits two implementations at radically different costs. The cheap implementation is symbol reference: for each surface element, search the set of executed test files for a reference to its identifying symbol. It is shallow, it is fast enough to run on every commit, it produces false positives when a test merely imports a symbol without asserting on it, and it produces exactly zero false negatives of the kind that matter — because a surface element with no reference anywhere in any executed test is unambiguously unprotected, and that is precisely the finding the economy service represents. The expensive implementation is coverage arcs: instrument the executed suite and record which surface elements were actually entered. It is deep, it is slow, and it is the audit rather than the sentinel.

13.5 The two implementations are used at different cadences and confusing them is a design error. The symbol-reference sentinel runs constantly and answers one question: is there any surface element with zero references from any executed test. That question has a binary answer, the answer is cheap, and a positive answer is a finding regardless of how good the rest of the suite is. The coverage-arc audit runs periodically and answers a richer question: of the surface elements that are referenced, how many are actually entered, and with what assertion strength. The sentinel catches the catastrophic case. The audit catches the decorative case. Running only the audit is unaffordable; running only the sentinel leaves the decorative case standing, which is why Part XV's weakening detectors exist.

13.6 Risk weighting is applied at the surface and never at the test, and this is the inversion that makes the metric useful. A test is not risky; the thing it guards is. The risk ladder assigns a level to each surface element by what its failure costs: formatting and text are the lowest, ordinary functional surfaces are in the middle, and money, permissions, credentials, persistence, deploy, and recovery are at the top. An unprotected element at the top is a blocking finding and is reported as one. An unprotected element at the bottom is a note. The economy service sits at the top of that ladder by every criterion the ladder has, and the sealed taxonomy classifies its gap as both a logic gap and an authorization gap at the same coordinate, which is what a double classification is for.

13.7 The F-class dimension turns the sealed taxonomy into a baseline rather than a report. Eight failure classes were sealed by the founding audit, and for any repository the question is not merely how many tests exist but whether each class its domain admits has at least one executed test. A repository with thousands of unit tests and no integration test has a hole at the second class regardless of its count. A repository with a permission check and no deny-case test has a hole at the eighth class, and the deny case is the one that matters, because the allow case works and therefore looks tested. The F-class coverage vector is a small, fixed-width, cross-stack comparable artifact, and it is the only summary in this dataset compact enough to be read at a glance without being a lie.

13.8 The protection surface baseline is recorded exactly as the test count baseline is recorded and is subject to the same guardian, and the two baselines fail in opposite directions. The test baseline fails when the guard count falls. The surface baseline fails when the guarded set grows without a matching guard, which is the drift condition of Part XIV. Together they close a loop: protection cannot be withdrawn silently, and the surface cannot be extended silently. Either baseline alone is defeated by the other's failure mode, and the fact that most estates carry neither is why code and its protection are permitted to move independently for years.

13.9 The baseline is a vector and is never averaged, and the prohibition is inherited verbatim from the constitution's refusal to average a confidence vector, for exactly the reason given there. A repository may be well protected functionally, adequately protected on its routes, thoroughly protected on its parsers, and completely unprotected on its money surface, and the average of those is a comfortable number that authorizes shipping a system whose most abusable surface has no guard. The minimum critical dimension bounds the verdict. A protection surface baseline reduced to a single percentage is not a weaker instrument; it is the instrument that would have reported KobiiCraft as healthy.

13.10 The Part's closing observation is that the surface metric is the only one in this dataset that a repository cannot improve by writing more tests, and that property is what makes it the one that gates. Every other metric responds to volume: more tests, higher counts, better ratios. This one responds only to reaching an element that was not reached, and reaching it requires that somebody first enumerate what exists to be reached. That enumeration — the list of things that can break — is the artifact almost no repository has, and its absence is the reason a repository can accumulate one thousand six hundred passing assertions without any of them touching the money.

PART XIII FINAL LAW

Every metric before this one counts guards; this one counts the guarded. One thousand five hundred and ninety-six passing assertions stood beside an economy service with zero test references anywhere in its tree, and no reach metric could see it, because reach over tests was perfect. The surface is enumerated from the disk — commands, routes, handlers, permission checks, schemas, jobs, recovery paths, guards — risk-weighted at the surface and never at the test, and reported as a vector whose minimum bounds the verdict. It is the only metric a repository cannot improve by writing more tests, which is why it is the one that gates.


PART XIV — COVERAGE DRIFT DETECTION: NEVER LET CODE GROW FASTER THAN ITS PROTECTION

14.1 The baseline guardian catches loss and the surface baseline catches absence, and neither catches the condition that produces most unprotected surface in most repositories, which is not loss and not absence but a rate mismatch. Code is added. Protection is not added at the same rate. No individual commit is delinquent, no test is deleted, no configuration line excludes anything, and every measurement at every instant is acceptable. The integral of a thousand acceptable instants is a repository in which the protected fraction has been falling continuously for two years, and the fall is invisible at every point because it is a derivative and every instrument in the estate reports a level.

14.2 Drift is measured between two topology maps and two surface inventories at two commits, and the arithmetic is a comparison of deltas rather than a comparison of states. The surface delta is the count of surface elements added, weighted by their risk level. The protection delta is the count of surface elements newly reached by at least one executed test. A positive surface delta with a zero protection delta is a drift event. The measurement costs a comparison against a stored map, which is why the topology map is anchored to a commit, and why the anchor was specified as load-bearing rather than administrative in Part III.

14.3 The commit-level drift event is the atom and it is deliberately not a blocking gate at the lower risk levels. A commit that adds a formatting helper with no test is a drift event of negligible weight, and blocking it would produce exactly the resentment that gets a governance layer routed around. A commit that adds a route, a command, a permission check, a persisted field, or a recovery path with no new executed test reaching it is a drift event at a risk level where the constitution's ladder already demands more, and there the event is surfaced as a finding rather than swallowed as a statistic. The gradient is what makes the instrument survivable, and its absence is why uniform quality gates always end up disabled.

14.4 The repository-level drift trend is the integral and it is the number that actually tells the truth. A single drift event is noise. Forty drift events over a quarter, weighted by risk, with a protection delta of near zero, is a repository whose protected fraction is in structural decline, and the fact that no single commit was blameworthy is not a mitigation — it is the mechanism. The trend is reported at a fixed cadence, against a fixed reference, and mechanically, because a question asked when somebody feels uneasy is asked in the quarters when nothing is wrong and skipped in the quarters when the answer would have mattered.

14.5 The most insidious drift is the one where the ratio holds constant, and an instrument that watches only the ratio will report health throughout. Suppose a repository adds surface and adds tests at exactly the rate that preserves its reach percentage. The instrument is satisfied. And suppose that every new test lands in the module that is already the best-tested one, because that module is the one with the fixtures, the harness, and the habits, which is precisely how testing concentrates in every real repository. The audit observed the shape directly: two hundred and thirty-two of two hundred and sixty-eight test files in a single plugin, and a mocking framework wired into one build descriptor out of ninety. The ratio was fine. The distribution was the finding.

14.6 Drift must therefore be measured per surface region and never globally, and this is the same prohibition on averaging that has now appeared in four consecutive Parts, which is itself informative: nearly every failure in this corpus is a failure of aggregation. A global drift figure of zero can be composed of a well-tested module growing its protection and a money surface growing without any, and the sum reports equilibrium over a repository that is becoming more dangerous. The engine partitions the surface by region — by module, by risk level, by F-class — and reports a drift vector, and the vector's worst component is the finding regardless of what the others say.

14.7 The false positive that will destroy this detector if left unhandled is the deletion refactor. A commit removes a module, the surface shrinks, the protected fraction rises mechanically, and the drift figure improves without a single test being written. An instrument that celebrates this has been gamed by an act that was probably virtuous, which is the worst kind of false signal, because it teaches the estate that the metric rewards something it does not intend to reward. The countermeasure is the same one that guards every ratio in this dataset: the absolute executed count and the absolute protected-element count are reported alongside the ratio, and a ratio that improved while both absolutes fell is reported as a shrinkage, not as an improvement.

14.8 Drift detection and the baseline guardian are distinct instruments that people persistently try to merge, and the merge destroys both. The guardian answers whether protection was withdrawn. The drift detector answers whether protection failed to keep pace. A repository can pass the guardian perfectly — never losing a single test — while drifting continuously, because adding surface without adding tests removes nothing. A repository can pass the drift detector while failing the guardian, because a mass deletion of tests over a static surface is a loss with no drift. They catch different events, they emit different records, they have different owners, and an estate that implements only one has covered half of the failure space and will believe it has covered all of it.

14.9 The wiring is cheap and belongs at the commit boundary rather than at the release boundary, because a drift event detected at release is a report and a drift event detected at commit is a decision. The topology map and the surface inventory are both derivable from the disk in seconds, both are anchored to a commit, and the delta between the current commit and its parent is therefore available before anything is merged. The cost is a comparison. The value is that the moment at which the surface grew is the only moment at which the author still understands what the new surface does, what can break in it, and what a test for it would have to assert — and that understanding is gone within days and irrecoverable within months.

14.10 The Part's closing law is a statement about time rather than about testing. Every other detector in this dataset finds a defect that exists now. This one finds a trajectory, and a trajectory is the only defect that is guaranteed to be worse tomorrow than it is today. A repository with seventy orphaned files has a bounded problem that a single decision can fix. A repository whose code grows faster than its protection has an unbounded one, and no single decision fixes it, because the next commit will reopen it. Drift is the only condition in this corpus whose cost is not paid once.

PART XIV FINAL LAW

Loss is an event and drift is a trajectory, and only the trajectory is guaranteed to be worse tomorrow. No commit is delinquent, no test is deleted, no configuration excludes anything, and the integral of a thousand acceptable instants is a protected fraction in structural decline — because every instrument reports a level and drift is a derivative. The ratio holding constant is the most insidious case: two hundred and thirty-two of two hundred and sixty-eight test files in one plugin is a healthy ratio and a fatal distribution. Measure per region, report the absolutes beside the ratio, and detect at the commit, because the moment the surface grew is the last moment anybody understands it.


PART XV — TEST DELETION AND WEAKENING DETECTION

15.1 Deletion is loud and weakening is silent, and this Part exists because the guardian of Part XII catches only the loud one. A deleted test lowers a count, and a lowered count fails a build. A weakened test lowers nothing: the file is present, the case is collected, the case is executed, the case passes, the count is identical, every metric in this dataset reports no change, and the protection is gone. Weakening is the perfect attack against a count-based instrument, it is almost never performed with intent, and it is the exact mechanism by which a repository's suite can become decorative without a single alarming event ever occurring.

15.2 The first weakening is the removed assertion. A test that made three assertions now makes two, or one, or none, and it still passes — more reliably than before, because an assertion is the only thing in a test that can fail. The detector is arithmetic: the count of assertions per test case is extracted at each commit and compared, and a case whose assertion count fell while it continued to pass is a weakening event. The detector is trivially cheap, it is stack-portable, and it does not exist in any pipeline in the estate, because assertions are counted by nobody and a test with zero assertions is not a test but an execution, and every runner will report it as passing.

15.3 The second weakening is the widened exception handler, and it is the one that most often ships inside a sincere repair. A test that asserted a specific failure now catches a broad class of failures, or the code under test acquires a broad handler that swallows the error the test was written to observe. In both directions the assertion has been decoupled from the behaviour: the test now passes when the system does the wrong thing, because the wrong thing is inside the class the handler tolerates. The detector is a diff-level pattern match against the stack's broad-catch constructs, applied to the test files and to the units they exercise, and its output is a candidate list for review rather than a verdict, because a broad handler is sometimes correct.

15.4 The third weakening is the unreal fixture, and it is the hardest to detect and the most common. A fixture that supplied a realistic payload now supplies a minimal one. A parser tested against truncated documents is now tested against well-formed ones. A handler tested against a concurrent scenario is now tested against a single actor. The test still passes, the assertion is still strong, and the input no longer resembles anything production produces. There is no counting detector for this and it would be dishonest to claim one. The available instrument is the fixture diff, surfaced as a first-class review artifact, on the principle that a change to a fixture is a change to what the test means and deserves the attention that a change to an assertion would receive.

15.5 The fourth and fifth weakenings are the skip and the marker exclusion, and they are counted here as weakening rather than as loss because their signature is different from a deletion's: the file is present, the case is present, the count of authored cases is unchanged, and only the executed count moves. They are already measured — the runner prints them, Part VIII counts them, and Part VI classifies them as a collected-to-executed loss — and they are listed again here because they belong to the same intent-space as the other weakenings and because their remediation is the same: an owner and an age, visible, standing, and growing in public until somebody is embarrassed into acting.

15.6 The sixth weakening is over-mocking, and it is the one that makes a test pass regardless of whether the system works. Each collaborator that is replaced by a stub removes a real interaction from the exercise, and past a threshold the unit under test is the only real object in the room and the test is asserting that a function calls the stubs it was told to call. The detector is a count of mocked collaborators per test, trended over time, with a rising count flagged. The standard the estate already carries is that four or more mocked collaborators means the unit under test is doing too much; the weakening variant of that standard is that a test whose mock count rose while its assertion count did not has moved away from reality without moving away from green.

15.7 The seventh weakening is the lowered threshold, and it is the one that is always defensible and always cumulative. A numeric assertion is relaxed by ten percent. A timeout is raised because the suite was flaky. A coverage floor is dropped by two points to unblock a merge. A performance budget is widened once. Every one of these is a small, reasoned, individually correct decision, and their sum, over a year, is a set of gates that no longer constrain anything. The detector is that every threshold in the repository is an inventoried, versioned value, and every change to one is a governance event with a reason attached — not because any single change is wrong, but because the aggregate is invisible without a ledger and lethal with one.

15.8 The eighth weakening is the tautological assertion, and it is the endpoint of every other weakening. An assertion that a result is not null replacing an assertion that a result equals an expected value. An assertion that a call did not raise. An assertion that a collection has a length. Each passes for any implementation, including a broken one, and each raises no count, lowers no count, and appears in a coverage report as a covered line. Only a mutation probe distinguishes it: perturb the unit's output and demand that the suite notice. A cheap approximation is available and is worth more than its cost — take the units the sentinel says are protected, break one return value, run the tests that reference it, and record which ones remain green. Every green one is asserting nothing about the value it claims to protect.

15.9 The ninth and tenth weakenings are relocation and rewrite, and they are the two that defeat a naively-implemented guardian. A test moved out of the collection root has been deleted without a deletion, and a repository-total baseline will miss it if a sibling root grew, which is precisely why Part XII insists on per-root baselines and on identity sets rather than counts. A test deleted and reintroduced under the same name with a weaker body has kept its identity, kept its count, and lost its content, and only the content hash of the test file — recorded in the topology map for exactly this reason — reveals it. Both attacks are invisible to any instrument that stores numbers, and both are trivial to detect for any instrument that stores identities and hashes.

15.10 The governance link is what makes the whole catalogue enforceable rather than merely descriptive. A weakening authored by the party whose change the test was blocking is the test-accommodation self-deception named in the constitution: the product is modified, or the test is modified, to make a red go green, and the direction of the repair is chosen without ever establishing which side was wrong. The audit's own hermeticity finding is the case that proves the danger — a test polluting the host drive root broke a sibling suite, the product logic was correct, and a careless repair would have damaged correct code to satisfy a broken fixture. The firewall is therefore identical in shape to the baseline firewall: the party blocked by a test may not, within the same task, weaken it. Fault must be attributed before repair is chosen, and a weakening whose fault attribution was never performed is not a repair. It is a surrender with a passing suite.

PART XV FINAL LAW

Deletion lowers a count and fails a build; weakening lowers nothing and is the perfect attack on a count-based instrument. Ten mechanisms — removed assertion, widened handler, unreal fixture, skip, marker exclusion, over-mocking, lowered threshold, tautological assertion, relocation, and same-name rewrite — and the file is present, the case is collected, the case passes, and the protection is gone. Identities and content hashes defeat relocation and rewrite; assertion and mock counts defeat the rest; only a mutation probe defeats the tautology. And the party blocked by a test may never, in the same task, be the party that weakens it.



PART XVI — GREEN SIGNAL INTEGRITY: THE FIVE VERDICTS

16.1 A green signal is not a fact about a repository. It is a fact about a run, and the distance between those two sentences is the distance this dataset has spent fifteen Parts measuring. This Part supplies the classifier: given a green result, which of five kinds of green is it, what evidence discriminates them, and which of the constitution's twelve verdict states does each map onto. The classifier exists because green is the only signal in software engineering that a correct system and an unexamined system produce identically, and no amount of care in reading the artifact will separate them, because the information required to separate them is not in the artifact.

16.2 TRUE GREEN is the strongest kind and its requirements are cumulative rather than alternative. The reconciliation was performed and the orphan set is empty or fully declared with owners and expiries. The baseline is intact, per-root, keyed by the environment the run occurred in. The suite was executed three times from a clean state and produced identical results, which is the hermeticity standard the founding audit adopted as its reference. The protection-surface sentinel reports no risk-weighted element with zero references from an executed test. And the result landed in a durable, hashed, citable artifact, which is the sixth stage of the chain and the one nobody instruments. TRUE GREEN maps to PROVEN, and it is rare, and its rarity is the honest measurement rather than a defect in the standard.

16.3 PARTIAL GREEN is the verdict that makes honest incremental progress expressible and it is the state most real repositories should be reporting most of the time. The executed subset passed, the unexecuted remainder is enumerated by identity, and the boundary between them travels with the verdict rather than being left for a reader to reconstruct. TUA-X, after the founding audit, is the model: four thousand three hundred and sixty-eight cases green in the default collection, three hundred and ninety cases green outside it and reaching nothing, and the second figure published beside the first. That is a PARTIAL GREEN and it maps to PARTIALLY-VERIFIED, and it is a strictly more useful artifact than the unqualified green that preceded it, even though it looks worse — and the fact that it looks worse while being more true is the entire cultural obstacle this dataset faces.

16.4 MISLEADING GREEN is the verdict this corpus was founded to name and it is the one that has no red anywhere in it. The run is green. The green is arithmetically true. It describes a minority of the authored protection and it presents, in every artifact anybody reads, as a description of the whole. Claude Power Pack is the canonical instance: forty-three of forty-three passing, six of seventy-six files reached, a suite that reports success and a repository that is running under ten percent of its own protection. The critical mapping is that MISLEADING GREEN maps to UNVERIFIED, not to PARTIALLY-VERIFIED, and the reason is that a partial verification knows its own boundary and a misleading one does not. The surface is not partly verified. Its verification status is unknown, and the green is not evidence about it.

16.5 UNVERIFIED GREEN is the vacuous case and it is the most humiliating one to discover, because the exit code was zero and nothing whatsoever happened. A collection that matched no files. A suite in which every case is skipped. A scanner pointed at a scope containing no source. A job step configured to continue on error, whose guard failed and whose build passed. In each case the pipeline is green, the run occurred, and the number of things examined is zero. The discriminating probe is the one Part XI's exit-code trap demands: assert that the run reports a non-zero count of things it actually examined. UNVERIFIED GREEN maps to UNVERIFIED, and the reason it deserves its own name despite sharing a mapping with MISLEADING GREEN is that its remediation is entirely different — one is a broken invocation and the other is an incomplete one.

16.6 ENVIRONMENT-DEPENDENT GREEN is the verdict that the attribution law forces into existence and it is the subtlest of the five. The suite passes here and would not pass there, or passes only because a prior run left the host in a state this run depends on. The founding audit produced both shapes. The Java suite yields one thousand six hundred and six passing assertions under the correct toolchain and loses the entire run under a runtime one major version behind — a green that is a statement about a toolchain and not about a repository. And the GEO-audit hermeticity break, in which a test wrote outside its sandbox to the host drive root and thereby defeated a sibling suite, is a green whose truth depends on the order of execution and the residue of prior runs. ENVIRONMENT-DEPENDENT GREEN maps to CONDITIONALLY-PROVEN when the condition is stated and to ENVIRONMENT-INVALID when the environment is not qualified, and it maps to PROVEN never.

16.7 The classifier is deterministic, ordered, and short, and its ordering is what makes it usable by an agent under pressure. Probe one: did the run examine a non-zero count of things — if not, UNVERIFIED GREEN, stop. Probe two: is the environment qualified and is the result stable across three hermetic runs — if not, ENVIRONMENT-DEPENDENT GREEN, stop. Probe three: has a reconciliation been performed against an independently produced authored census — if not, MISLEADING GREEN, stop, and this is the default. Probe four: is the orphan set empty or fully declared, and is the protection-surface sentinel clean — if not, PARTIAL GREEN. Otherwise TRUE GREEN. Four probes, evaluated in order, and the third is the one that fires in every repository that has never run this engine.

16.8 The default verdict, in the absence of a reconciliation, is MISLEADING GREEN and not TRUE GREEN, and this inversion of the burden of proof is the single most consequential rule in this Part. It is not an accusation. It is the recognition that an unreconciled green carries exactly zero information about the fraction of authored protection it describes, and that a system which defaults such a green to healthy has adopted the presumption that produced every finding in this corpus. The burden falls on the party claiming protection to demonstrate reach, and it falls there because the alternative — a presumption of reach, rebutted by evidence — is a presumption that nothing in any pipeline is positioned to rebut.

16.9 The five verdicts are reported per root and per invocation, exactly as the metrics are, and the aggregation prohibition applies here with even more force than it does to a number. A repository is not green. A run of an invocation over a root in an environment is green, and it is one of five kinds of green, and rolling five verdicts across three roots into a single repository-level badge is the construction of precisely the artifact this dataset exists to discredit. The badge is the summary that replaced the output. It retains the pass and destroys the counts, the skips, and the reached-suite identities, and it is accurate about everything it kept.

16.10 The Part's closing observation belongs to the constitution and is restated here in operational form. A red signal is self-announcing: it arrives with a location, a trace, and a failing assertion, and no discipline is required to notice it. A green signal is silent by construction, and silence is indistinguishable from every other silence. The five verdicts are the instrument that makes a green signal interrogable, and the four probes are the interrogation, and the total cost of running them is minutes. The cost of not running them, measured once in the founding audit across eight repositories, was three hundred and ninety orphaned tests, seventy unreached files, one inert scanner, one hundred and forty-nine unverifiable ExUnit files, an unprotected money surface, and a host-polluting suite — every one of them sitting behind a green signal that nobody had any reason to doubt.

PART XVI FINAL LAW

Green is the only signal a correct system and an unexamined system produce identically, and the information that separates them is not in the artifact. Five kinds: TRUE GREEN, which is reconciled, baselined, hermetic, surface-clean, and durable; PARTIAL GREEN, which knows its own boundary and publishes it; MISLEADING GREEN, which is arithmetically true about a minority and presents as the whole; UNVERIFIED GREEN, which examined nothing and exited zero; and ENVIRONMENT-DEPENDENT GREEN, which is a statement about a toolchain. In the absence of a reconciliation the default verdict is MISLEADING GREEN — the burden of proof falls on the party claiming protection.


PART XVII — SECURITY TOOL ACTIVATION: INSTALLED, CONFIGURED, INVOKED, ENFORCED, PASSING

17.1 CostaLuz Lawyers carries a static security analyser in its dependency set and a configuration for it in its repository, and no job has ever invoked it. That single observation is what generalized this dataset beyond testing, because it demonstrates that the law under examination is not a property of test runners at all. This Part specifies the five-state model that the observation forces, applies it to every non-test guard in the estate, and names the two states in which a guard produces protection and the three in which it produces only the appearance of it.

17.2 The first state is installed. The tool is declared in a dependency manifest and is resolvable. Its protective value is exactly zero, and this is uncontroversial and universally understood, and it is nonetheless the state in which a great many security tools spend their entire lives. Installation is what happens when somebody reads a recommendation, adds a line, and is interrupted. It leaves a trace in the manifest that a future reader will interpret as evidence of a security practice, and the interpretation will be wrong, and nothing in the repository will contradict it.

17.3 The second state is configured. A configuration file exists, thresholds are set, exclusions are declared, and the tool would run correctly if anything ran it. Its protective value is still exactly zero, and it is the most dangerous of the five states, and the reason is that configuration is the strongest available evidence of deliberation. An installed-only tool might be an accident. A configured tool looks like a decision — somebody sat down, considered the surface, tuned the exclusions, and committed the result — and a reader encountering that artifact will conclude that the surface is scanned. The artifact is a false witness of exceptional persuasive power, and it was produced by somebody acting in complete good faith.

17.4 The third state is invoked. A job, a hook, a pre-commit gate, or a done-gate executes the tool and the tool produces a signal. This is the first state with a non-zero protective value and it is a small one, because a signal that nothing consumes is a number that nobody reads, and Part VIII's central observation applies without modification: quality signals do not decay by concealment, they decay by non-consumption. An invoked-but-unconsumed scanner produces a report that accumulates findings, the findings age, the report becomes furniture, and within two iterations the team has learned to scroll past it.

17.5 The fourth state is enforced. The tool's non-zero exit fails the build, or its finding blocks the merge, or its verdict is a required input to a done-gate that refuses to issue without it. This is the first state with a substantial protective value, and the distance between invoked and enforced is the distance between a report and a control. The signature of the gap is small and specific: a job step marked to continue on error, a command with its failure suppressed, a gate whose verdict is logged and discarded. Each of those is one token in one line, each was added for a reason that was good at the time, and each disarms the guard completely while leaving every other artifact intact.

17.6 The fifth state is passing, and it is the only state that permits a claim, and it carries a qualification that the previous four do not. A passing enforced guard is either clean or its findings are waived with an owner, an expiry, and a compensating control — and a waiver, per the constitution, does not propagate to a sibling component, does not survive a material change, and reverts on expiry rather than being renewed by inertia. A guard whose findings have been suppressed by a permanently widened exclusion list is not passing. It is configured, at best, and the exclusion list has become the mechanism by which the tool was disarmed without anybody disabling it.

17.7 The vacuous-pass trap defeats the entire ladder and must be probed for explicitly, because a tool can be installed, configured, invoked, enforced, and passing while examining nothing at all. A scanner whose scope pattern matches no files exits successfully. A type checker pointed at a directory that was renamed reports no errors. A linter whose include list was narrowed to a single file passes trivially. In every case, all five states are satisfied by the artifacts and the protection is zero, and the only discriminating probe is the one this dataset has now demanded three times in three different Parts: the run must report a non-zero count of the things it actually examined. A guard that cannot state what it looked at has not looked.

17.8 The detection procedure is a subtraction and it is the same subtraction that finds an orphaned test, which is the proof that the model is not a special case. Enumerate every declared guard from the dependency manifests, the configuration files, and the hook registry. Enumerate every command executed by every job, hook, and gate. Subtract. Each declared guard with no inbound invocation edge is at installed or configured. For each guard that does have an edge, interrogate the edge: does its failure fail the build, and does its run report a non-zero examined count. The procedure costs a few minutes, it requires no stack knowledge, and it had never been performed on the repository that carried the inert analyser.

17.9 The model generalizes past security tools and this generalization is where it earns its place in a verification corpus. Linters, type checkers, schema validators, secret scanners, license checks, and this estate's own governance hooks are all guards, and every one of them lives somewhere on the five-state ladder, and the estate's own experience supplies the sharpest instance: a hook that exists in the repository and is not the one the harness actually loads is a hook in the configured state, indistinguishable in every artifact from an enforced one, and observed to have shipped a whole class of governance that blocked nothing. The lesson is not about hooks. It is that a guard's presence in a repository and a guard's presence in an execution path are separate facts, recorded in separate places, and only the second one protects.

17.10 The ActivationRecord is the durable output, and its fields are chosen so that a state cannot be claimed without the evidence for it. Each record carries the guard's identity, its declared location, its state on the five-state ladder, the invocation edge if one exists, the evidence that the edge is load-bearing, the count of artifacts the last run examined, the timestamp of that run, and — the field that makes the record honest — the last observed refusal. A guard that has never refused anything is in exactly the epistemic position of a test that has never failed and a rollback that has never been executed: it may be working perfectly, it may be entirely disconnected, and from the outside, where everything is green and nothing is complaining, those two states are indistinguishable.

PART XVII FINAL LAW

Five states: installed, configured, invoked, enforced, passing — and only the last two protect anything. Configured is the most dangerous, because a tuned configuration is the strongest available evidence of deliberation and therefore the most persuasive false witness, and it was produced in complete good faith. The gap between invoked and enforced is one suppressed exit code. And all five can be satisfied while examining nothing, so every guard must report a non-zero count of what it looked at. A guard that has never refused anything is indistinguishable from a guard that is not connected.


PART XVIII — FAILURE MODES AND ADVERSARIAL CASES OF THE RECONCILIATION ENGINE ITSELF

18.1 Every measurement creates an incentive to improve the measurement, and the improvement of a measurement is not the improvement of the thing measured. That is a structural property rather than a hypothesis about bad actors, and it operates most strongly on sincere participants, because a sincere participant genuinely wants the number to rise and will find the cheapest honest-seeming route. This Part is the adversarial audit of this dataset's own instrument: eleven attacks that make reach look good without improving protection, each with the countermeasure that defeats it, and one attack that has no clean countermeasure and is named anyway.

18.2 The first attack is deletion. The orphan count is seventy; delete the seventy files; the orphan count is zero and the reach is one hundred percent. Every metric in Parts VII and VIII improves, the repository is measurably worse, and no rule anywhere in the ratio-based instrument was violated. The countermeasure is threefold and none of the three is optional: the absolute executed count must not fall, the Loss Ledger is append-only so the deletion appears as a line rather than as an absence, and every deletion is itself a weakening event under Part XV requiring an explicit, attributed, reviewed baseline update. The attack is defeated not by forbidding deletion but by making it impossible to perform silently.

18.3 The second attack is collection without execution. Widen the invocation until it collects every orphan, then mark the ones that fail as skipped or expected failures. Collected reach hits one hundred percent, executed reach is unchanged, and the repository has learned exactly nothing. The countermeasure is that reach is defined at the executed stage and never at the collected stage, which is why Part VI insists that collected-to-executed is a distinct transition with a distinct loss metric, and why the skip count carries an age and an owner. A one-hundred-percent collected reach beside a rising skip count is not an improvement. It is the same orphan set with a new label.

18.4 The third attack is denominator shrinkage, and it is the only attack that can be aimed at the instrument itself. Widen the discovery exclusion list — add a directory pattern, exclude a naming convention — and authored artifacts vanish from the census, and every ratio improves without a single test being connected. The countermeasures are three, and they need to be three: the exclusion list is a committed, diffed, governed artifact whose changes are reviewed as changes to a measuring instrument; the surprise set detects an over-broad exclusion the moment the runner executes something the census did not find; and the authored absolute count is reported beside every ratio, so a ratio that improved while the denominator fell is legible as what it is.

18.5 The fourth attack is test splitting. Take a hundred tests and make them three hundred, and the executed case count rises by two hundred and the baseline is satisfied and the reach ratios all hold. This attack is defeated by a metric rather than by a rule, and the metric is the Executed Protection Ratio of Part VIII, whose denominator is the risk surface and not the test population. Splitting a test does not touch a new surface element. The count moves, the surface metric does not, and the divergence between them is the signature. This is the clearest demonstration in the corpus of why a surface-based denominator is not a refinement but a necessity.

18.6 The fifth attack is decorative reach, and it is the most elegant, because it defeats the surface metric using the surface metric's own cheap implementation. The symbol-reference sentinel says the economy service has zero references from executed tests. Write one executed test that imports the economy service and asserts that it is not null. The sentinel is satisfied. The surface element is now referenced. Nothing is protected. The countermeasure is the mutation probe of Part XV: perturb the unit's return value and demand that some executed test notice. A reference without an assertion that can fail is a decoration, and the mutation probe is the only instrument that distinguishes an assertion from a decoration, which is why the sentinel is explicitly specified as a sentinel and never as a certificate.

18.7 The sixth attack is environment substitution. The baseline was recorded under a toolchain where the count is inconveniently high; record a new baseline under one where it is lower, or where a subproject fails to build and is therefore excluded from the run entirely. The countermeasure is that the baseline is keyed by the environment record, and a baseline update that changes the environment key is not an update. It is a new baseline for a different system, it does not discharge the old one, and the old one remains unsatisfied and blocking. This is the same rule that prevents a false alarm when the toolchain legitimately changes, and the fact that one rule serves both purposes is a sign that it is the correct rule rather than a patch.

18.8 The seventh attack is the one aimed at the auditor and it is the attack this dataset is most vulnerable to, because it requires no cleverness. The reconciliation engine is a file. Orphan it. Move it outside the canonical invocation, or let it sit there having never been wired in, and it will never run, and it will never refuse anything, and every artifact in the repository will be exactly as green as it was before. The engine is subject to the law it enforces, without exception and without irony. The countermeasure is that every reach report contains a mandatory self-reach field, that a report lacking a positive self-reach assertion is inadmissible, and that the engine's own execution is a gate within the canonical invocation. An auditor exempt from its own audit is not an auditor.

18.9 The eighth attack is aggregation. Report reach as a single repository-wide scalar and a healthy root will mask a dead one, and the scalar will be true, and it will be the summary that replaced the output. The countermeasure is the vector rule, which has now appeared in five Parts, and its repetition is not redundancy — it is the observation that nearly every failure in this corpus is a failure of aggregation, and that the pressure to collapse a vector into a scalar is the single most reliable pressure in any quality system, because a scalar is what fits on a dashboard and a vector is what tells the truth.

18.10 The ninth attack is fail-open. Make the engine tolerant: if the configuration cannot be parsed, assume everything is reached; if the runner cannot be invoked, assume the manifest is complete; if the join key normalization fails, assume the intersection is total. Every one of these is defensible as robustness, and their sum is an engine that reports perfect reach over any repository it cannot understand. The countermeasure is that unknown is a first-class output and that unknown is treated as unreached for the purposes of the verdict while being reported distinctly for triage. An engine that fails open is not robust. It is an engine whose failure mode is a favourable answer, and a favourable answer produced by a broken instrument is the exact artifact this corpus exists to prevent.

18.11 The tenth attack is noise, and it is the attack that requires no attacker. Report the hardware suites as orphans every day forever, report every deliberately manual test as a finding, report the same seventy files in every run, and within three weeks every reader has learned to skip the report. Credibility is the detector's only power — it has no authority except the willingness of somebody to read it — and an instrument that spends its credibility on findings nobody can act on has disarmed itself more thoroughly than any adversary could. The countermeasure is the declared exclusion with an owner and an expiry, which moves a genuinely unreachable artifact out of the orphan class and into an aging ledger, and the aging is what preserves the pressure without spending the credibility.

18.12 The eleventh attack has no clean countermeasure and is named here because naming it is the only defence available. It is selection bias in remediation. Confronted with three hundred and ninety orphaned tests, an agent connects them and they pass, and reach improves, and the estate celebrates — and the reason they passed is that they were passing all along, and connecting a passing orphan is free. The orphans that would go red on connection are the ones that stay orphaned, and every incentive in the system, including the reach metric itself, rewards connecting the safe ones first. The partial countermeasure is procedural: the orphan set is connected wholesale in a single change, the resulting reds are the finding rather than an obstacle, and connecting a subset requires naming the excluded subset and its reason. The residual risk is real and is recorded: an agent optimizing this metric will, on the margin, prefer the orphan that will not embarrass it, and no metric in this dataset can distinguish that preference from good judgement.

PART XVIII FINAL LAW

Eleven attacks on this instrument, and the sincere participant executes them as reliably as a hostile one. Delete the orphans; collect without executing; shrink the census denominator; split the tests; reference the surface without asserting on it; re-baseline under a friendlier toolchain; orphan the engine itself; aggregate a vector into a scalar; fail open on any confusion; and spend the detector's credibility on noise nobody can act on. Ten have countermeasures. The eleventh — connecting the orphans that will pass and leaving the ones that would go red — has only a procedure and an honest record of the residual risk.


PART XIX — METRICS, ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS, AND THE MATURITY LADDER

19.1 No metric in this dataset ships alone, and the pairing is a structural property of each metric's definition rather than a reporting convention. A metric defined without its countermetric is not partially specified; it is an attack surface, and Part XVIII is the demonstration. This Part fixes the pairings, states what each countermetric does when the first is gamed, and then supplies the maturity ladder — the observable, never-declared assessment of what a repository's verification capability can actually do on this axis.

19.2 Test File Reach is paired with the absolute executed file count. The pairing exists because reach is a ratio and a ratio improves when the denominator falls, and the cheapest way to raise reach is to delete the unreached files. Under that attack the ratio rises and the absolute falls, and the divergence is the finding. The pair is read together always: a reach of one hundred percent over six files is not the same object as a reach of one hundred percent over seventy-six, and no single number can express the difference.

19.3 Test Case Reach is paired with the Executed Protection Ratio. The pairing exists because case counts are trivially inflatable by splitting, and splitting moves the case count while leaving the surface untouched. A repository whose case count doubled and whose surface coverage is unchanged has performed an accounting operation, and the pair makes the operation legible. This is the same pairing the constitution fixes between test count and risk-weighted coverage, applied at the reach axis, and it is inherited rather than invented.

19.4 Suite Activation Ratio is paired with the per-root executed count. The pairing exists because activation is a count of roots and roots can be merged: fold three roots into one, and the activation ratio becomes one over one, and two thirds of the tests still do not run. The per-root executed count, retained across the merge, exposes it. Every structural metric in this dataset has a corresponding structural attack, and the attack is always to change the structure rather than the substance.

19.5 The Orphan Count is paired with the authored absolute, the Silent Collection Loss is paired with the age distribution of its skips, and the Executed Protection Ratio is paired with mutation adequacy on the reached surface. The first defeats deletion. The second defeats relabelling — a skip that is renamed to an expected failure has moved between categories and aged not at all, and the age is what makes the inventory uncomfortable. The third defeats decorative reach, and it is the only countermetric in the set that costs real compute, and it is worth it, because it is the only instrument in existence that distinguishes an assertion that could fail from one that cannot.

19.6 The guard metrics carry their own pairings. Scanner Activation Ratio — the fraction of declared guards in the enforced or passing state — is paired with the count of artifacts each guard last examined, which defeats the vacuous pass. Baseline integrity is paired with the frequency and the reasoning quality of baseline updates, which defeats baseline laundering: a repository whose baselines are always satisfied because they are rewritten every week has a guardian in name only. And the drift figure is paired with the absolute surface size, which defeats the shrink-to-win refactor that improves a protected fraction by removing code.

19.7 Above all of these sit the two supreme measures of the family, and this dataset does not redefine them; it contributes to them. The first is the ratio of failures institutionalized to failures observed, and this dataset's contribution is that every orphan class, every trap, and every weakening mechanism yields a record, an invariant, a detector, and a cross-repository scan — which is what institutionalization means operationally on this axis. The second is the ratio of claims supported by admissible evidence to total claims, and this dataset's contribution is that no reach claim may be quoted without its invocation and its environment, which is the mechanism that makes a reach claim scoped rather than absolute.

19.8 The maturity ladder for this axis is observed and never declared, and its levels are defined by what the capability can do rather than by what it has documented. At the first level the authored census exists — the repository knows what it has. At the second the executed manifest exists and is harvested as identities from the runner rather than as a summary. At the third the reconciliation runs and the diff is reported. At the fourth the diff is baselined, a silent decrease fails the build, and a protection-surface inventory exists. At the fifth the drift and weakening detectors run on every commit and orphans receive proposed remediations automatically. At the sixth the model is cross-repository: an orphan class found in one repository triggers a scan of every repository sharing the runner. At the seventh the engine proposes new trap classes from its own observed misses and audits its own reach.

19.9 The ladder's application to the estate that produced it is the honest test of whether the ladder means anything, and the answer is uncomfortable and correct. At the moment of the founding audit, Claude Power Pack — the repository that carries this corpus, that audits everything else, and that is the source of the doctrine — was at the second level on this axis. It had a green suite. It had never reconciled that suite against its authored surface. The ontology's rule is explicit that a repository with a green suite that has never been reconciled is at the second level at most regardless of its test count, and applying that rule to the auditor rather than to the audited is the only way it acquires any force.

19.10 The cost model determines which metric runs where, and getting it wrong is how a correct instrument becomes an unused one. The collection-time metrics — file reach, activation ratio, orphan count, the configuration probes — cost seconds and therefore run on every commit, in a hook, before anything merges, because a finding delivered at the commit is a decision and the same finding delivered at the release is a report. The execution-time metrics — case reach, silent collection loss, skip ages — cost a suite run and therefore run wherever the suite already runs, harvesting the runner's structured output rather than paying for a second invocation. The surface sentinel costs a symbol search and runs on the commit. The mutation probe and the coverage-arc audit cost real compute and run on a schedule, not on a commit, and pretending otherwise produces an engine that is correct, expensive, and disabled within a month. Each metric also emits a stable, greppable identifier and a verdict, because every consumer downstream of it — the done-gate, the certification, the hook — is a program and not a reader.

19.11 The cadence is fixed and the reference is external, and both properties are load-bearing for the same reason. The reach question asked whenever somebody feels uneasy is asked in the quarters when nothing is wrong and skipped in the quarters when the answer would have mattered, because unease is uncorrelated with drift and frequently anticorrelated with it — the periods of greatest confidence are the periods of least examination. The engine therefore runs on the commit, on the schedule, and at the done-gate, mechanically, whether or not anybody is worried, and it compares against a fixed written standard rather than against the estate's current practice, because drift is invisible from inside itself and trivially visible from a fixed point.

PART XIX FINAL LAW

Every metric here is paired at definition with the countermetric that moves adversarially when it is gamed: reach with the absolute, case count with surface coverage, activation with per-root counts, orphan count with the authored absolute, skips with their age, surface reach with mutation adequacy, scanner activation with the examined count, and drift with the absolute surface. The maturity ladder is observed, never declared — and the repository that authored this corpus stood at level two on its own axis, with a green suite it had never reconciled. A rule acquires force only when it is first applied to the auditor.


PART XX — INTERFACES TO SIBLING DATASETS, THE COMPOUND-INTELLIGENCE PIPELINE, AND DONE CRITERIA

20.1 This dataset composes a stack it does not own, and the discipline of composition is the discipline of not rebuilding. The evidence and confidence ladder belongs to ACIS, and every reach claim this engine produces is stamped with an ACIS level and never with a locally-invented scale: a reach report authored by the engine is a hypothesis at the second level; a reach report whose collection manifest a second, independent substrate has re-derived is at the third; a reach law written into the estate's enforced rules is higher still, and the engine does not promote its own claims, because the producer of a claim never certifies it. The decision kernel owns verdicts and blast radius: this engine supplies the evidence that a Q3 surface is unreached, and the kernel decides what evidence burden that raises and whether the change proceeds. FD-03 owns routing: an orphan class becomes a hard rule, a trap, a dataset Part, or a benchmark case by that router's judgement, and this engine produces the record and hands it over. Building a second router, a second graph, a second ladder, or a second recurrence counter is forbidden by name, and the temptation to do so is strongest at exactly the moment when the composed system is one field away from what this axis needs.

20.2 The knowledge graph belongs to graphify, and the topology map is the natural thing to fork it with, and forking it is the error this Part exists partly to prevent. The topology map is a coordinate set: artifacts, project nodes, invocations, and the edges between them. Those are nodes and edges, the graph already stores nodes and edges, and the correct behaviour is to publish into it — the artifact as a node, the invocation edge as an edge, the orphan as a node with no inbound edge from the canonical root — so that any agent navigating the graph to ask which tests protect a module receives an answer that already knows which of them run. A second graph would answer the same question with a different answer, and the estate would then have two truths and a reconciliation problem one level up.

20.3 The bug-to-invariant compiler belongs to the hard-rules module and is the destination of this dataset's most valuable output. The invariant that falls out of the founding audit is short and mechanical: every authored protective artifact is either reachable from a canonical invocation or carries a declared exclusion with an owner and an expiry. That sentence is a hard rule in waiting; it has a trigger, it has a stop action, it has an exception phrase, and it has an origin — an empirical incident, which is the only thing that licenses a hard rule at all. This engine derives it and the compiler seals it, and the seam between them is exactly where a fork would be tempting and exactly where composition is cheapest.

20.4 The done-gate scorer belongs to the output-contracts module and this dataset extends it rather than shadowing it. The extension is one check with three clauses: a code output claiming done must cite a reach report whose invocation and environment are named, whose orphan set is empty or fully declared, and whose self-reach field is positive. The scorer already refuses a done claim whose tests were not observed to pass; the extension refuses a done claim whose tests were observed to pass without anybody establishing which tests those were. That is the smallest possible change to the existing gate that would have caught every finding in the founding audit, and its smallness is the argument for it.

20.5 The sealed F1-through-F8 taxonomy is this dataset's spine and the relationship is precise: F5 is this dataset's home class, and this dataset is F5's instrumentation. The taxonomy named the class — behaviour that runs in production and is not covered by any test that actually executes in the normal invocation — and illustrated it with two independent instances, and stopped there, because a taxonomy's job is to name and not to measure. Everything in the preceding nineteen Parts is the measurement apparatus that the naming demanded, and the taxonomy remains the authority on the class boundaries. A new trap discovered by this engine is proposed as a subclass of F5 and is never a ninth F-class, because forking a sealed taxonomy is the same defect as forking a graph.

20.6 The compound-intelligence pipeline is the obligation that makes this dataset more than an instrument, and its contract is that a single reach failure must yield at least four permanent assets. The first is the causal asset: a FailureRecord carrying the failure class F5, the origin axis of config or ci or environment, the detectability axis of misleading-green, the root cause demonstrated rather than hypothesized, the exact configuration line or missing edge that severed the invocation, and the cross-repository exposure list naming every repository sharing that runner. The record is the atom, and every subsequent asset is derived from it.

20.7 The second asset is the preventive one: a RegressionRecord whose preventing test is a reach assertion — a test that asserts the canonical invocation collects the previously orphaned root — and whose red-before-green is observed by running that assertion against the unremediated configuration and watching it fail. This condition is not a formality and it is the one most often skipped: a reach assertion written after the collection path was widened, and observed only to pass, may be asserting a tautology and is a guard in name only. The record carries a field for whether the canonical invocation actually reaches the regression test itself, and a regression whose own reach is false protects nothing, which is the law of this dataset closing on its own output.

20.8 The third asset is the learning one: a paired training example, negative and positive. The negative is the artifact as it stood — the collection path scoped to a subtree, the seventy files outside the root, the configured analyser with no job — presented without commentary, because the whole point is that it looks entirely reasonable. The positive is the remediation with the reasoning attached: the widened invocation, the per-root baseline, the activation record. The pair is what teaches an agent to recognize the shape before it produces it, and a single-sided example teaches nothing, because the failure mode is precisely that the negative case looks correct.

20.9 The fourth asset is the evaluative one: a BenchmarkScenario derived from the real incident, never invented. Its seeded defect is a scope line that excludes a populated test root. Its expected detection is the orphan set, named by identity. And its false-positive trap — the field that makes the scenario worth more than a smoke test — is a legitimately excluded hardware suite that a naive detector will flag as an orphan, because a detector that cannot distinguish an orphan from a declared exclusion will drown its readers in noise and will be ignored within a month, and being ignored is the only way this instrument can actually die. Beyond the four, the incident also yields a gate candidate, a policy candidate, an invariant with a falsifier, and a cross-repository scan — and the scan is what the founding audit actually did, taking one configuration line in one repository and examining every repository in the estate for the same class of exclusion, which is how one incident becomes an estate-wide defence.

20.10 Done, for this dataset's implementation, is six conditions, and none of them is a document. A topology map exists for the repository, anchored to a commit, with its exclusion list committed and its uncertainty count reported. An executed manifest is harvested as node identities from the runner's own structured output, never from a summary. The reconciliation diff is produced, and its orphan set is empty or every member carries a declared exclusion with an owner and an expiry. Baselines exist per suite root, keyed by environment, carrying identities, and a silent decrease fails the build. Every declared guard carries an ActivationRecord in the invoked-and-enforced state or a waiver, and every guard reports a non-zero count of what it examined. And the engine's own file sits inside the canonical invocation, with its self-reach field positive in every report it emits. The sixth condition is the one that will be dropped first, and dropping it turns the entire apparatus into the artifact it was built to detect: present, configured, trusted, and inert.

PART XX FINAL LAW

This dataset composes and never forks: ACIS owns the evidence ladder, DRK owns verdicts and blast radius, FD-03 owns routing, graphify owns the graph, the hard-rules module owns bug-to-invariant, the output-contracts module owns the done-gate, and F5 owns the class this instrument measures. One reach failure yields four permanent assets — a causal record, a regression whose red was observed against the unremediated configuration, a paired training example whose negative case looks entirely reasonable, and a benchmark whose false-positive trap is a declared exclusion. And the last done-condition is the one that will be dropped first: an engine outside its own canonical invocation is present, configured, trusted, and inert.
