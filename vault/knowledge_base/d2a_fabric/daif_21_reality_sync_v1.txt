DAIF-21 — REALITY SYNCHRONIZATION AND SEMANTIC CHANGE

PART I — THESIS: EVERY COMPILATION IS A PHOTOGRAPH

1.1 Mission. This dataset states the law that makes the whole corpus survivable, and the law is one sentence: every compiled artifact is a photograph of a world that has since moved on, and an estate that does not detect the movement will act with total confidence on a picture of a reality that no longer exists. DAIF-02 compiles reality into representations. DAIF-03 proves the compilation was faithful — at the moment it was made, against the source as the source then stood. Neither dataset claims, and neither can claim, that the source held still afterward. It does not. The file is edited, the decision is reversed, the constraint is lifted, the gate is rewritten, the dependency is upgraded, the architecture moves under the module that named it, the environment shifts beneath the run that measured it, the evidence that grounded a claim is withdrawn by the very measurement that was supposed to confirm it. Each of those movements silently falsifies some set of compiled units, cached answers, assembled packs, open obligations, and standing contracts — and falsifies them without a single byte of the derived artifact changing, because a derived artifact has no way of noticing that its source moved. DAIF-21 is the mechanism that notices. It detects when reality has diverged from its representation, decides exactly what that divergence invalidates, and drives the recompilation that repairs it. Without this dataset the corpus is a beautifully typed, rigorously gated, provably faithful record of a moment that has passed.

1.2 The asymmetry that makes staleness uniquely dangerous. A wrong artifact and a stale artifact are not two grades of the same defect; they are opposites in the only dimension that governs damage, which is trust. A wrong artifact is distrusted. It failed once, it was caught, it is checked. It carries a scar, and the scar is a defense: the next consumer reads it warily, verifies before acting, and treats its claims as claims. A stale artifact carries no scar. It was right — genuinely, verifiably right — and it has a track record of being right, and every consumer that ever acted on it was rewarded for doing so. It has accumulated exactly the credit that suppresses verification, and it spends that credit at the precise moment it has stopped deserving it. This is the asymmetry: the artifact's trustworthiness is derived from its history, its danger is derived from the present, and the two have silently decoupled. The most damaging artifact in any estate is therefore never the one that was always wrong — it is the one that was right for a year and became wrong last Tuesday, because nothing about it looks different and everything about it invites belief. Staleness is a failure that hides inside a success, and it is the only failure whose probability rises with the artifact's reputation.

1.3 The cardinal principle, composing SQI. DAIF-21 does not invent its epistemic backbone; it composes SQI's reality-supremacy hierarchy whole and forks none of it. That hierarchy already ranks observed reality above the plan that predicted it, above the documentation that describes it, above the agent reasoning that reconstructed it, and above the green signal that reported it — and the ordering is exactly the ordering DAIF-21 needs, because a compiled artifact is a representation and every rank beneath observed reality is a representation of a representation. From that hierarchy the cardinal principle of this dataset follows directly and is stated once here for the whole corpus: observed reality outranks every representation of it, and the burden of proof sits on the representation to demonstrate that it still matches — never on reality to announce that it moved. Reality does not announce. It has no obligation, no channel, and no interest in telling a cached answer that its premise was withdrawn. A system built on the assumption that change will announce itself has inverted the burden, and an inverted burden is the whole failure: the representation goes on asserting, unchallenged, until the assertion causes a loss large enough to force an investigation, and the investigation discovers that nothing was ever wrong except the passage of time. The burden sits with the representation. It must earn its currency continuously, not once.

1.4 The absorption of D2A-14, stated explicitly. Semantic change detection was proposed as its own system and it is not one, and this dataset says so in its own voice rather than letting the fold pass silently. Change detection and reality synchronization are the same mechanism, decomposed the same way, with the same three moves: detect that something moved, decide what that movement invalidates, act on the invalidation. A detector that detects and does not decide produces a stream of notifications nobody actions. A decider that decides and does not act produces verdicts nobody honors. And an actor that acts without a detector acts on a schedule rather than on reality, which is the museum-keeping this dataset exists to abolish. The three moves are one pipeline and one pipeline is one dataset, so D2A-14's semantic-change candidate is absorbed here in full — its taxonomy in Part II, its hardest technique in Part IV, its verdicts in Part V — and no parallel change-detection system is built, because a second detector with its own invalidation logic would produce two verdicts on the same artifact's currency and the corpus's constitution convicts exactly that authority collision.

1.5 What DAIF-21 does not own. The dataset is thin by construction and its boundaries are declared before its first mechanism, because a synchronization layer that starts absorbing its neighbors becomes the estate's second compiler and its damage compounds. DAIF-21 does not compile. When it determines that an artifact must be rebuilt, it hands the rebuild to DAIF-02 and consumes the result; it owns no lowering pass, no representation, and no boundary heuristic. DAIF-21 does not judge fidelity. When a rebuilt artifact must prove that it faithfully carries its new source, DAIF-03's fidelity gate makes that judgment and DAIF-21 reads its verdict; a synchronization engine that decided for itself whether a recompilation was faithful would be marking its own homework at exactly the moment it is under the most pressure to declare the problem solved. DAIF-21 does not hold the graph. The typed edges that say which unit derived from which are GK-04's, the coordinates are graphify's, and DAIF-21 traverses them as a reader — it adds no node type and no edge type of its own. And DAIF-21 does not run the obligation lifecycle: it creates obligations and hands them to DAIF-07, which owns their aging, their escalation, and their closure. What remains after those four subtractions is DAIF-21's whole and only mandate: detect, decide, and drive.

1.6 The actors and the object model in outline. Four actors move through this dataset. The change source is whatever moved — a file, a decision, an environment, an authority, a measured result — and it is never a party to the synchronization; it changes and says nothing. The detector observes, through the push signals and pull probes Part III specifies, and emits a typed change event carrying what moved, when, in which of Part II's classes, and with what confidence. The invalidation engine of Part V consumes that event, traverses the derivation closure, and assigns each reachable artifact one of four currency verdicts — VALID, SUSPECT, INVALID, UNKNOWN — which are the dataset's central objects and are themselves typed cognitive objects in DAIF-01's discipline, carrying authority, scope, validity, provenance, and confidence like every other unit in the corpus. The driver of Part VI converts those verdicts into tracked work: a selective recompilation, a re-evaluation, a contract re-verification, a pack invalidation, and above all an obligation, because an invalidation that generates no tracked obligation is a message in a bottle and this corpus already knows precisely what becomes of work that nobody tracked.

1.7 The burden this dataset carries. The corpus's spine runs type → representation → fidelity → obligation, and every link in it produces artifacts whose correctness is indexed to an instant. DAIF-21 is the only dataset that carries the index forward in time, which makes its failure mode uniquely total: a gap anywhere else in the corpus produces one bad artifact, while a gap here produces a whole estate of artifacts that were once excellent and are now confidently, invisibly, historically wrong — and each of them will be trusted in proportion to how long it has been right. The burden is therefore completeness of detection over the change taxonomy, honesty about the classes it cannot detect, and a bias, declared and defended in Part IV, toward re-verifying too often rather than too rarely. The dataset earns its place only if a consumer, holding any compiled unit in the corpus, can ask one question — is this still true of the world — and get an answer that is grounded in an observation rather than in the artifact's reputation. Everything that follows is the mechanism that makes that question answerable.

PART I FINAL LAW. Every compiled artifact is a photograph of a world that has since moved on, and staleness is the uniquely dangerous defect because a wrong artifact is distrusted and checked while a stale one was right, has a history of being right, and is therefore trusted precisely when it has stopped deserving it; composing SQI's reality-supremacy hierarchy, observed reality outranks every representation of it and the burden falls on the representation to prove it still matches — reality never announces that it moved — and DAIF-21 absorbs D2A-14 entirely because change detection and reality synchronization are one mechanism with three moves, detect what changed, decide what it invalidates, act on the invalidation, while compiling nothing (DAIF-02 does), judging no fidelity (DAIF-03 does), and holding no graph (GK does).

PART II — THE CHANGE TAXONOMY

2.1 Mission. Not all change matters, and a synchronization layer that treats all change alike fails in both directions at once. Treat every byte movement as consequential and the estate drowns in invalidations it cannot service, until the actors that receive them learn to dismiss them wholesale — alert fatigue, which is not a nuisance but a total detection failure wearing the costume of a working detector. Treat change coarsely and the movements that actually falsify artifacts pass unnoticed while trivial reformattings consume the whole budget. The taxonomy in this Part is what makes discrimination possible: twelve classes, each with a detection surface, an invalidation reach, and a characteristic false negative — the specific way that class routinely escapes the instrument that is supposed to catch it. The classes are ordered from cheapest to most consequential, and the ordering is itself load-bearing, because it is the priority under which a detector with a bounded budget spends its attention.

2.2 The cheap classes: textual and formatting. A textual change is bytes moved with meaning intact — a rename that carries no semantics, a rewrapped line, a reordered set whose order was never significant, whitespace, a comment repaired. It is detected trivially by content hash against the CIR-0 fingerprint, and its invalidation reach is, correctly, near zero: nothing that derived from the unit's meaning is falsified, because the meaning did not move. Its characteristic false negative is the meaningful rename — an identifier or a term whose change looks purely lexical and in fact re-points a reference, so a unit that reads as textual is semantic in disguise. Formatting change is the adjacent class: structure re-expressed without content moving, a section resequenced, a table turned into prose. Its reach is likewise near zero and its characteristic false negative is the structural re-expression that silently drops a clause during the reshaping — the reformatting that lost the exception. Both classes exist in the taxonomy not because they matter but because they must be cheaply and confidently dismissed; a detector that cannot discharge them cheaply will spend its entire budget on them and have nothing left for the classes that count.

2.3 The class that matters most and hides best: semantic and behavioral change. A semantic change is a movement of meaning — a constraint's strength weakened from prohibition to preference, a definition's boundary redrawn, a scope narrowed, a claim's polarity reversed. It is detected by structural comparison of CIR units rather than by any hash, for the reason Part IV develops at length, and its invalidation reach is the full derivation closure of the changed unit. Its characteristic false negative is the semantic change expressed in a byte-identical form — the same sentence meaning something else because a term it uses was redefined elsewhere. Behavioral change is worse and is the most under-detected class in the entire taxonomy: what the system actually does moved, while nothing in the source looks different. A dependency was upgraded beneath an unchanged call, a gate's threshold was tuned in an environment that no artifact indexes, a default flipped, a probabilistic component drifted. The source is unchanged, so every source-watching detector reports stability with perfect sincerity, and the artifact that describes the system's behavior is now false while every freshness instrument in the estate says it is current. Behavioral change is detectable only by observation — by re-running the thing and comparing what it does, never by reading what it says — which is why Part III's pull probes exist and why a detection layer built exclusively on repository signals is structurally blind to the class that hurts it most.

2.4 The governance classes: authority and scope. An authority change moves who may decide. A rule that was the Owner's becomes an agent's to apply within bounds, or the reverse; a system that held final say over a domain is demoted; a precedence order between two rule families is re-ranked. It is detected in the authority dimension of DAIF-01's typed objects and in the standing rule registers, and its invalidation reach is unusually wide and unusually easy to under-estimate, because it does not falsify any single unit's content — it falsifies the right of a whole class of units to bind. Its characteristic false negative is precisely that width: the detector, finding no content change, records a low-consequence event, when in fact every decision that was made under the old authority is now of questionable standing. A scope change moves where a rule applies — a constraint that governed one project becomes universal, or a universal law is demoted to a single feature. Its detection surface is the scope dimension, and its reach is the set of units that were, or now are, inside the boundary. Its characteristic false negative is the silent widening: a scope enlarged, so units that never consulted the rule are now bound by it, and no artifact anywhere points at them to say so — the change is invisible from the changed unit's own closure, because the newly-governed units never derived from it in the first place.

2.5 The epistemic classes: evidence, lifecycle, and decision change. An evidence change is a movement in the support for a claim: a measurement strengthens it, a contrary result collapses it, an experiment that grounded it fails to reproduce. It is detected in the evaluation surface and its invalidation reach runs along the epistemic support chain — every unit whose status depends on the evidence must be re-typed, and a claim whose only support has collapsed does not merely weaken, it falls to the weakest link that remains. Its characteristic false negative is the quiet collapse: evidence is rarely withdrawn with a declaration, it is simply not reproduced, and an absence produces no signal. A lifecycle change is a movement in a unit's standing — superseded, expired, revoked — detected in DAIF-01's validity dimension, with a reach covering every consumer that is still citing the unit as live; its characteristic false negative is the expiry nobody read, a validity window that lapsed while every derived artifact went on treating the unit as current because nothing re-checked the window. A decision change is the reversal of a prior choice, and it is the class this dataset most explicitly serves: DAIF-02's decision representation deliberately carries revision conditions and an expiry so that a reversal has a place to land, and a reversed decision invalidates not only itself but everything justified by it, including the artifacts that never mentioned the decision and merely inherited its consequence.

2.6 The commitment classes: obligation, contract, and architectural change. An obligation change moves the work that is owed — a closure condition is redefined, an owner is reassigned, an obligation is discharged or abandoned — and its reach runs into DAIF-07's lifecycle, where a closure condition that moved after the obligation was opened is the specific poison that produces an obligation nobody can ever close. A contract change moves an interface between two systems: a schema field, an event shape, a guarantee one side made to the other. Its reach is both parties plus every artifact that assumed the old shape, it is detected against DAIF-04's typed contract objects, and its characteristic false negative is the compatible-looking break — a field added, a default changed — that passes every structural check and violates a guarantee the structure never expressed. An architectural change moves the shape of the system itself: a module is split, a boundary is redrawn, a layer is inverted. It is the most consequential class and the most expensive to service, because its reach is not a closure but a region — every artifact that named a location, a boundary, or a responsibility that has now moved — and its characteristic false negative is the slow architecture change, accumulated across dozens of individually-innocent commits, no one of which crosses any threshold while their sum has relocated the system out from under every artifact that described it.

2.7 The cardinal invariant: a hash tells you bytes moved, never that meaning moved. The twelve classes above are held apart by one invariant that this dataset states as its second law after reality supremacy: byte-identity and meaning-identity are independent properties, and a system that equates them will both cry wolf and sleep through the fire. It cries wolf on every reformatting, every whitespace repair, every re-serialization, flooding its consumers with invalidations that carry no meaning and training them to ignore the channel. And it sleeps through the fire on every behavioral change, every authority re-ranking, every quiet evidence collapse, every meaning that moved without a byte moving, reporting perfect currency while the estate acts on falsehoods. The hash is not thereby useless — it is the cheapest and most reliable instrument the detector owns, and Part III keeps it — but it answers exactly one question, whether this content is the content we last saw, and it must never be asked a second. The metric for this Part is class-resolved detection coverage, measured per class against a set of known real changes, and its anti-Goodhart countermetric is the behavioral-class detection rate held out separately: a taxonomy tuned to maximize aggregate coverage will optimize the classes a hash can see, score superbly, and remain totally blind to the one class that costs the most, so the aggregate is never permitted to speak for the hard class.

PART II FINAL LAW. Change is twelve classes, not one — textual and formatting (cheap, near-zero reach, dismissed cheaply so the budget survives), semantic and behavioral (the meaning moved, or what the system does moved while nothing in the source looks different, the most under-detected class in the estate and observable only by re-running rather than by reading), authority and scope (who may decide, where a rule binds), evidence, lifecycle, and decision (support strengthened or collapsed, a unit superseded or expired, a choice reversed), and obligation, contract, and architectural — each with its own detection surface, invalidation reach, and characteristic false negative; and over them all stands the second law of this dataset, that a hash tells you bytes moved and never that meaning moved, because a system which equates the two will cry wolf on every reformatting and sleep through every behavioral fire.

PART III — DETECTION: HOW REALITY ANNOUNCES ITSELF

3.1 Mission. Detection is the first of the three moves, and it is bounded by a fact the previous Part established: reality does not announce. Nothing in a changed world sends a message to the artifacts it falsified. What the estate has instead is a set of signal sources it already runs for other purposes, each of which incidentally carries evidence that something moved, and the whole of this Part is the discipline of composing those sources — never forking them, never building a parallel watcher beside a system that already watches — into a detection surface wide enough to cover the taxonomy. The mission is therefore not to construct an observer but to fuse the observers that exist, to classify what they emit into the twelve classes, and to be explicit and unashamed about the classes that no existing signal covers, because a detection layer that quietly omits a class it cannot see is worse than one that declares the blindness: the first produces false confidence, the second produces a probe.

3.2 The push signal sources. A push signal is one where reality, or a system watching reality, tells the detector without being asked. The estate has seven. Filesystem and repository movement — commits, diffs, content hashes against the CIR-0 fingerprints — is the cheapest and highest-volume source, and it covers textual, formatting, and much semantic change, while covering behavioral change not at all. Test and gate results are the second: a gate that flipped from pass to fail, or from fail to pass, is a behavioral observation the estate already produced for another reason, and it is the single most valuable push signal for the class the repository cannot see. Production evidence is the third — what the system actually did when it ran. Agent corrections are the fourth, and they are the highest-value push signal per unit volume, because a correction is the exact moment the estate's belief changed: an actor asserted something, reality or the Owner refuted it, and that refutation invalidates every artifact that carried the refuted belief. Owner directives are the fifth and carry the highest authority: a directive can move a decision, an authority, a scope, or a constraint in one sentence, and it is the only source that changes reality by declaring it. Event streams are the sixth — PM-03's bus and CEPS's event record are transports the estate already runs, and DAIF-21 subscribes rather than building a channel. And `liveness` findings are the seventh, reporting when a thing that was supposed to be running is not.

3.3 The pull probes. A pull check is one where the detector goes and asks reality, and it exists because the push sources are structurally incomplete. Behavioral change emits no push signal by construction: nothing changed in the source, so no source-watcher fires, and the only way to learn that the system's behavior moved is to make it behave and observe. A probe is therefore an executed question — re-run the gate the artifact claimed passes, re-resolve the dependency the artifact pinned, re-read the environment the artifact assumed, re-measure the value the artifact reported, re-check that the file the artifact cites still contains the span the provenance locator points at. Probes are expensive relative to push signals, which is the entire design tension of this Part: a probe costs an execution, and an estate cannot probe everything continuously, so probe budget must be allocated — and it is allocated by the decay model of Part VII, which is precisely the mechanism that says which artifacts have gone long enough without an observation to deserve one. The provenance-resolution probe deserves separate mention because it is the cheapest of them and catches an entire failure family: an artifact whose citation no longer resolves to the span it was compiled from has had its ground moved beneath it, and the check costs one read.

3.4 The invariant that forces the probe to exist. The distinction between push and pull is not an engineering preference; it is forced by an invariant that DAIF-21 composes directly from SQI: the absence of a change signal is not evidence of stability. SQI already holds that the absence of a failure signal is UNVERIFIED and never PROVEN — a gate that did not fire may have been satisfied, or may not have run, or may not have been wired, and the three are indistinguishable from the outside. DAIF-21 inherits that reasoning intact and applies it to currency: silence from the detection surface means no signal arrived, which is consistent with a world that did not move and equally consistent with a world that moved in a class the surface cannot see. The two readings are indistinguishable from the silence alone, and a system that reads silence as stability has converted an unverified state into a proven one — the exact epistemic collapse the corpus's type system was built to prevent. It follows that an artifact which has never been re-checked is not fresh. It is unexamined, which is a different verdict with a different consequence, and it is why time-triggered re-verification exists at all: the passage of time without a signal raises the probability that a silent class moved, and at some point that probability must be discharged by an observation rather than by an assumption.

3.5 Time as a first-class change source. The passage of time is itself a signal, and it enters the detector as one. An artifact carries an expiry — the validity window DAIF-01's type system already defines — and the lapse of that window is a change event with no external cause, emitted by the clock, requiring no push and no probe to detect. It is the cheapest detection in the taxonomy and it covers the lifecycle class outright. Time also drives the probe scheduler: an artifact whose decay model says it half-lives in days and which has not been observed in weeks is overdue by construction, and its overdue-ness is a first-class input to the probe budget. This is where DAIF-21 composes GK-07's freshness and integrity verdicts rather than computing its own — GK-07 already answers whether a node's knowledge is fresh, and the synchronization layer reads that verdict as an input to its currency decision instead of maintaining a rival notion of freshness that would inevitably drift from it. Two freshness authorities disagreeing about the same artifact is the authority collision the constitution convicts; there is one, and it is GK-07's.

3.6 Failure modes and the adversarial cases. The detector has three signature failures. The first is the silent source: a change arrives through a channel nobody subscribed to — a configuration edited outside the repository, an authority moved in conversation and never recorded, a dependency upgraded by a transitive resolution — so the surface reports stability with complete sincerity and the estate's confidence in that stability is exactly as high as its blindness is total. The second is the drowned signal: the surface fires correctly, at a volume that exceeds what any consumer can service, and the correct signal is lost inside the noise it arrived with; this is the alert-fatigue failure and it is a detection failure, not a presentation problem, because a signal that reaches a consumer who has learned to dismiss the channel has not been detected in any sense that matters. The third is the misclassified signal: a real semantic change classified as textual, dismissed cheaply, and never propagated — the most dangerous outcome available to this Part, because the detector fired, correctly logged an event, and confidently assigned it a reach of zero. The adversarial case against the detector is the change designed to look cheap: a semantic reversal expressed as a rename, an authority moved by editing one word, a constraint's strength weakened by deleting a single qualifier. The defense is that classification uncertainty resolves upward — a change that cannot be confidently discharged as textual is treated as semantic, paying the recompilation cost rather than the wrong-action cost, for the reasons Part IV makes explicit.

3.7 Metrics, countermetrics, and what consumes the detector. The detector is measured on time-to-detection per class — the interval between the world moving and the estate emitting the corresponding change event — and on class-resolved recall against a held-out set of real changes with known ground truth. Its anti-Goodhart countermetric is essential and is the actioned-signal rate: a detector optimizing for recall will maximize signal volume, score perfectly on recall, and destroy its own effectiveness by training every downstream consumer to ignore it, so recall is never permitted to be read without the fraction of emitted signals that produced a real invalidation. A third measure guards the silent-source failure: coverage of the taxonomy by source, computed as the set of classes for which at least one live signal exists, with the explicitly empty cells named rather than averaged away. The detector's outputs are consumed by exactly one system — the invalidation engine of Part V — and it emits nothing else, holds no verdict, and invalidates nothing on its own authority. The `learning-sentinel` and the UKDL register are its most valuable long-run consumers in the other direction: a change class that repeatedly escapes detection is not an incident, it is a law waiting to be sealed, and a detection blindness observed twice belongs in the rule register rather than in a report nobody re-reads.

PART III FINAL LAW. Reality never announces, so detection is the fusion of signals the estate already runs and never a parallel watcher — repository movement, gate and test results, production evidence, agent corrections, Owner directives, the PM-03 and CEPS event streams, and `liveness` findings as push signals; executed probes as pull checks for the behavioral class that emits no push signal by construction; and the clock itself as a first-class source through the expiry window — all governed by the invariant DAIF-21 composes from SQI, that the absence of a change signal is UNVERIFIED and never PROVEN, from which it follows that an artifact never re-checked is not fresh but merely unexamined, and that a detector optimizing recall without its actioned-signal countermetric will drown its own consumers and fail totally while scoring perfectly.

PART IV — SEMANTIC CHANGE: THE HARD PROBLEM

4.1 Mission. This Part is the technical core of the D2A-14 absorption and the hardest thing DAIF-21 attempts: determining that meaning moved, when the bytes may or may not have. The two directions of the problem are both real and neither is reducible to the other. Bytes may move without meaning moving — the reformatting, the rename, the re-serialization — and a detector that reads every byte movement as a meaning movement destroys itself by volume. And meaning may move without a single byte moving in the artifact that carries it — a term redefined elsewhere, an authority re-ranked, a dependency upgraded beneath an unchanged call, an evidence base quietly not reproduced — and a detector that requires a byte movement to fire is structurally blind to all of it. The problem is hard because it is not a comparison problem in the space where the comparison is cheap. It is a comparison problem in the space of meaning, and the estate's only access to that space is the compiled representation itself.

4.2 Structural comparison of CIR units. The first technique, and the one that carries most of the load, is to stop comparing text and start comparing the compiled units, because DAIF-02's representations were built precisely to expose the dimensions that a text comparison flattens. The comparison runs field by field over the typed objects and asks structural questions that have determinate answers. Has a constraint's strength changed — did an absolute prohibition become a preference, did a preference harden into a rule? Has a constraint's condition moved — is the case that bounded it now wider or narrower, which is the exact movement that turns a conditional prohibition into a universal one? Has a decision's justification been withdrawn — do the evidence pointers that grounded it still resolve, and do they still say what they said? Has a decision's authority or scope changed? Has an obligation's closure condition moved, which is the single most dangerous field movement in the corpus because it produces an obligation that can never be discharged? Has a unit's epistemic status weakened — did a claim that was verified fall back to inferred because the measurement that verified it no longer reproduces? Each of these is a comparison over a typed field, each has a defined answer, and each maps to a class in Part II's taxonomy. This is the payoff of compiling knowledge instead of re-sending it: a compiled corpus is comparable in the dimensions that matter, and a prose corpus is comparable only in bytes.

4.3 Dependency-aware propagation. The second technique is to recognize that a change to a unit is not confined to that unit. Meaning propagates along derivation, so a movement in a unit moves everything that derived from it, and the derived artifact does not need to change a byte to become false — its falsity is entirely inherited. The propagation is computed over the typed derivation edges that GK-04 already holds, which DAIF-21 traverses as a reader and never rebuilds, and it is the mechanism by which a single constraint's weakening invalidates the decision that assumed the constraint, the compiled artifact that encoded the decision, the assembled pack that carried the artifact, and the cached answer a consumer is about to act on. The propagation is also what makes the second-order semantic change detectable at all — the case where a unit's own bytes are identical but a term it depends on was redefined. That unit did not change; its dependency did; and the only instrument that sees this is one that walks the derivation edges rather than the file tree. Part V governs how far the walk goes and where it must stop.

4.4 The honest admission of incompleteness. Semantic change detection is fundamentally incomplete and this dataset says so plainly, because a mechanism that conceals its incompleteness converts a known limitation into an unknown one, which is strictly worse. Some meaning movements are invisible to any automated comparison the estate can run. Meaning that lives in the interaction of two units, neither of which changed, but whose relationship did. Meaning that was never compiled because the source expressed it implicitly and no representation captured it. Meaning that moved in the world with no representation at all — the assumption everyone shared, which stopped being true, and which no artifact ever wrote down because it was too obvious to state. Against this class, structural comparison is silent and will remain silent, and the correct response is not a better comparison but a different posture: because the detector's coverage is incomplete and its gaps are unknowable from the inside, the system must fail toward re-verification rather than toward assumed validity. That is the whole design consequence of the incompleteness admission, and it is why the default for an unexamined artifact is SUSPECT rather than VALID — not as a pessimism, but as the only verdict consistent with a detector that cannot enumerate what it missed.

4.5 The two error classes and their asymmetric cost. A semantic-change detector makes two kinds of error and they are not equally expensive. A false positive is a change reported that did not really move the meaning, and its cost is a recompilation: DAIF-02 runs over the affected closure, DAIF-03 re-gates the result, an obligation is opened and closed, and the estate spends compute and attention it did not strictly need to spend. The cost is real, it is bounded, and it is paid in a currency the estate has. A false negative is a change that moved the meaning and was not reported, and its cost is a confident wrong action: a consumer reads an artifact marked VALID, acts on it without verification precisely because it is marked VALID, and the action is wrong in a way that no gate downstream will catch, because every gate downstream is reading the same stale artifact. The cost is unbounded, it is paid at the point of consequence rather than at the point of error, and it is paid in the currency of trust — which, per Part I, is the currency that makes the next stale artifact more dangerous than this one. The asymmetry is not close. A false positive costs a recompilation; a false negative costs a wrong action taken with full confidence and a reputation that survives it.

4.6 The deliberate bias and the countermetric that keeps it honest. Because the cost asymmetry is that steep, DAIF-21 is deliberately tuned toward false positives. When a comparison is ambiguous, when a classification cannot confidently discharge a change as textual, when a derivation edge is uncertain, when an artifact's decay model is absent — in every one of these, the resolution is upward, toward suspicion and re-verification, and the recompilation cost is paid. This bias is stated as a design commitment rather than left as an emergent tendency, so that a future actor tuning the system for cost cannot quietly invert it. But a bias toward false positives is exactly the bias that produces alert fatigue, and alert fatigue is a total detection failure, so the bias cannot stand alone: it requires a countermetric that measures the thing the bias endangers. That countermetric is the actioned-invalidation rate — of the invalidations DAIF-21 emitted, what fraction produced a recompilation whose output differed materially from its predecessor. A high emission rate with a collapsing actioned rate means the system is invalidating things that did not need it, and the correct response is never to weaken the bias but to sharpen the comparison, because the bias protects against the unbounded error and the comparison quality is what makes the bias affordable. The failure to distinguish those two responses is how a synchronization layer talks itself into trusting its own photographs again.

4.7 Adversarial cases and what consumes this Part. The adversarial input against semantic comparison is the change engineered to look cheap — a qualifier deleted, a term redefined at a distance, a strength weakened by one word — and the defense is the upward resolution of ambiguity, which converts an adversarial cheap-looking change into an expensive-but-safe recompilation. The second adversarial input is the change that is genuinely invisible: a meaning that moved with no representation, which no defense catches and which the incompleteness admission of 4.4 exists to name rather than to conceal. The third is the flood — many small changes arriving together such that the propagation closure of their union covers the estate, which Part V's bounding must survive without either invalidating the world or silently truncating. This Part's output is consumed entirely by Part V's invalidation engine, which converts a detected semantic movement into a currency verdict over a closure; it is consumed indirectly by DAIF-03, whose fidelity gate reads the same compiled representations and asks a sibling question — not did this move, but does this still faithfully carry its source — and the two questions are the same question asked at two different times, which is exactly why DAIF-21 hands its recompilations back to DAIF-03 rather than answering for it.

PART IV FINAL LAW. Semantic change detection compares compiled units and not bytes — has a constraint's strength or condition moved, has a decision's justification been withdrawn, has an obligation's closure condition shifted, has an epistemic status weakened — and propagates the answer along GK-04's derivation edges so that a unit whose own bytes never moved is still invalidated by the movement of what it derived from; it is fundamentally incomplete, some meaning movements being invisible to any automated comparison, and that incompleteness forces the system to fail toward re-verification rather than toward assumed validity, because the two error classes are steeply asymmetric — a false positive costs a recompilation while a false negative costs a confident wrong action — so the bias toward false positives is a declared commitment, kept honest by the actioned-invalidation countermetric, and the correct response to a falling actioned rate is a sharper comparison, never a weaker bias.

PART V — THE INVALIDATION ENGINE

5.1 Mission. Detection tells the estate that something moved. It does not tell it what is now untrue, and the gap between those two facts is where a synchronization layer lives or dies. The invalidation engine is the second of the three moves: it consumes a typed change event and produces a currency verdict for every artifact the change can reach. Its whole difficulty is reach — computing it neither too widely, which invalidates the world and makes the mechanism unaffordable and therefore ignored, nor too narrowly, which leaves a stale artifact marked VALID and hands the estate exactly the confident falsehood Part I named as the corpus's most dangerous object. The engine is a graph traversal with a verdict function, and both halves are constrained: the graph is GK-04's and it does not rebuild it, and the verdict vocabulary is four values, closed, with a defined behavior for the one that means the engine could not answer.

5.2 The derivation chain of derived artifacts. The chain the engine walks is the corpus's own production line, and every arrow in it is a typed edge that already exists in the graph: source -> CIR units -> compiled artifacts -> Mission Packs -> cached answers -> obligations -> contracts. A source fragment produces CIR units through DAIF-02's compilation. Those units are assembled into compiled artifacts. Artifacts are selected and assembled into the Mission Packs that DAIF-08 hands to a reasoning session. Packs produce cached answers — the conclusions an actor reached and recorded rather than re-derived. Answers produce obligations, the tracked work DAIF-07 owns. And obligations, when they concern an interface between systems, produce or depend on the typed contracts DAIF-04 holds. Each arrow is a derivation, each derivation is an edge, and the engine's traversal is the transitive closure of those edges from the changed unit outward. The critical property of the chain is that falsity flows forward along it without any byte moving downstream: a compiled artifact three hops from a reversed decision becomes wrong the instant the decision reverses, and nothing about the artifact changes to reflect that. The chain is the only instrument that connects the two facts.

5.3 Propagation and its bounding. The naive engine invalidates the world on every commit, because the transitive closure of a sufficiently connected graph is the graph. The correct engine invalidates exactly the closure of what actually derived from the changed unit — not what merely mentions it, not what lives in the same file, not what shares a directory, but what has a derivation edge whose provenance chain terminates in the changed span. Three disciplines bound the walk. The first is edge typing: only derivation-class edges propagate invalidation, so a reference edge, a similarity edge, or a co-location edge is not a channel for falsity and the traversal does not cross it. The second is class-scoped reach: Part II assigned each change class an invalidation reach, and a formatting change with a near-zero reach does not begin a traversal at all, which is what keeps the cheap classes cheap. The third is dimension-scoped propagation: a change to a decision's authority invalidates the standing of what derived from it without invalidating its content, and a change to an evidence base invalidates the epistemic status of dependents without invalidating their structure — so the verdict a dependent receives is scoped to the dimension that moved rather than being total by default. Bounding is not an optimization. It is the difference between a mechanism the estate runs and a mechanism the estate turns off.

5.4 The four verdicts. The engine's output vocabulary is closed and has exactly four values. VALID means the artifact has been affirmatively determined to still match reality — not that no signal arrived, which is a different thing entirely, but that the derivation closure was walked and no change reached it, or that a probe observed it and confirmed it. SUSPECT means the artifact may have been falsified and must be re-verified before it is reused; it is not forbidden, it is not trusted, and any consumer that reaches for it must first pay the re-verification cost or decline to use it. INVALID means the artifact has been affirmatively determined to no longer match reality and may not be reused at all; it does not degrade to SUSPECT and it is not available under caution, because a known-false artifact used carefully is still a known-false artifact. And UNKNOWN means the derivation graph could not answer — an edge is missing, a provenance chain does not resolve, a decay model is absent, the closure could not be computed within budget. UNKNOWN is the verdict that decides whether this engine is honest.

5.5 UNKNOWN is treated as SUSPECT, never as VALID. The single rule that carries the most weight in this Part is that UNKNOWN degrades to SUSPECT and never to VALID, and it follows directly from the invariant Part III composed from SQI: the absence of an answer is not an affirmative answer. An engine that cannot determine whether an artifact was reached by a change has learned exactly nothing about that artifact's currency, and the two available defaults are not symmetric. Defaulting UNKNOWN to VALID converts every gap in the graph — every missing edge, every unresolved provenance pointer, every artifact whose lineage was never recorded — into a silent assertion of freshness, which means the engine's confidence is highest precisely where its knowledge is weakest, and the artifacts most likely to be stale (the old ones, the ones with lost lineage, the ones nobody has touched) are the ones it most confidently marks current. Defaulting UNKNOWN to SUSPECT costs a re-verification and preserves the honesty of the VALID verdict, which is the only thing that makes VALID worth anything at all. A VALID that includes the un-answerable is not a verdict, it is a shrug wearing a verdict's name.

5.6 The two failure modes, and which one is worse. The engine's first failure is the unbounded cascade: a traversal that crosses non-derivation edges, or that applies a total reach to a dimension-scoped change, and invalidates a substantial fraction of the estate on a routine commit. Its damage is economic and then behavioral — the recompilation cost exceeds what the estate will pay, the invalidations are dismissed, and within a few cycles the actors have learned to override the engine as a matter of routine, at which point the mechanism is decorative. This failure is loud, it is visible, and it is survivable. The second failure is the bounded cascade that bounds itself wrongly: a traversal that stops one edge short of a real dependent and leaves a genuinely stale artifact marked VALID. This failure is silent. It produces no cost signal, no complaint, and no visible symptom, and it delivers to a consumer an artifact bearing an affirmative freshness verdict that the engine had no right to issue. It is strictly worse than the unbounded cascade and it is worse than having no engine at all, because an estate with no engine knows it must verify, while an estate with a wrongly-bounding engine has been told, in the system's own authoritative voice, that verification is unnecessary. Every bounding discipline in 5.3 must therefore be conservative at its edge: when the traversal cannot determine whether an edge is a derivation edge, it crosses it and pays the cost, because the alternative is a false VALID.

5.7 Metrics, adversarial cases, and consumers. The engine is measured on invalidation precision — the fraction of invalidated artifacts whose recompilation produced a materially different output — and on the metric that matters far more and is far harder to obtain, the false-VALID rate, estimated by sampling artifacts the engine marked VALID and probing them directly. The countermetric discipline is unavoidable here: precision alone is maximized by invalidating less, and an engine tuned for precision will bound ever more tightly, score ever better, and manufacture the exact silent failure of 5.6, so precision is never permitted to be read without the sampled false-VALID rate beside it. The adversarial case is the change whose real dependents are connected by an edge the graph never recorded — a dependency that lives only in an actor's head, a coupling that was never compiled — and the defense is that an artifact with an incomplete lineage is UNKNOWN, and UNKNOWN is SUSPECT, so an unrecorded edge costs a re-verification rather than a false assurance. The engine's verdicts are consumed by Part VI, which converts them into tracked work; by DAIF-08, which must not assemble a Mission Pack out of INVALID units; and by every consumer in the estate that reads an artifact, because a currency verdict that is not checked at the point of use is a verdict that was computed for nobody.

PART V FINAL LAW. The invalidation engine walks the derivation chain — source -> CIR units -> compiled artifacts -> Mission Packs -> cached answers -> obligations -> contracts — over GK-04's typed edges which it reads and never rebuilds, bounding the traversal by edge type, by the change class's reach, and by the dimension that moved, so that it invalidates exactly the closure of what actually derived from the changed unit rather than the world; it emits four verdicts, VALID (affirmatively still matching), SUSPECT (re-verify before reuse), INVALID (may not be reused), and UNKNOWN (the graph could not answer), and it degrades UNKNOWN to SUSPECT and never to VALID, because the failure mode of the unbounded cascade is loud and survivable while the failure mode of the cascade that bounds itself wrongly is silent, marks a stale artifact VALID in the system's own authoritative voice, and is therefore worse than having no engine at all.

PART VI — WHAT CHANGE CREATES: RECOMPILATION, OBLIGATIONS, AND RE-EVALUATION

6.1 Mission. A verdict is not a repair. The invalidation engine of Part V produces an accurate account of what is no longer true, and if the pipeline ends there the estate has achieved nothing except a more precise description of its own decay. The third move — act — is what converts a currency verdict into a world where the artifact is once again faithful, and it is the move most often omitted, because it is the only one that costs real work rather than real compute. This Part specifies the five outputs a detected change generates, the sibling dataset each is handed to, and the invariant that governs all of them: DAIF-21 generates work, it does not perform work. It recompiles nothing itself, gates nothing itself, and closes nothing itself. Its entire contribution at this stage is to convert a verdict into a tracked commitment that some other system owns, and its entire failure mode is to convert a verdict into a notification that nobody owns.

6.2 Selective recompilation, handed to DAIF-02. The first output is the recompilation itself, and the operative word is selective. The naive repair recompiles the corpus, which is the same failure as the unbounded cascade transposed into the action phase — it is correct, it is expensive beyond what the estate will pay, and it will therefore be skipped, which converts a working repair mechanism into a decorative one. The correct repair recompiles exactly the closure the engine computed and nothing beyond it: the CIR units cut from the changed source span, the artifacts derived from those units, the packs assembled from those artifacts. DAIF-02's incremental ingest discipline is what makes this affordable, and DAIF-21 composes it rather than duplicating it — a source fragment whose content hash is unchanged is not re-acquired, so the recompilation touches only what genuinely moved and re-derives nothing that did not. The selectivity is also a correctness property and not merely an economy: recompiling an unchanged unit re-runs the boundary heuristics over content that did not move, and a boundary that lands differently on the second pass silently breaks every edge that pointed at the old unit, which means a gratuitous recompilation can manufacture the exact invalidation it was run to repair.

6.3 Re-evaluation, handed to DAIF-03. The second output is a fidelity re-evaluation, and it rests on a rule stated here without qualification: a recompiled artifact does not inherit its predecessor's PASS. The predecessor's fidelity verdict was a claim about a specific compilation of a specific source at a specific moment, and every one of those three has now changed — the source moved, which is why we are here; the compilation ran again, which means new boundaries, new units, and new opportunities to lose an exception; and the moment is a different moment. To carry the old PASS forward would be to assert fidelity on the authority of an artifact that no longer exists, which is the photograph fallacy of Part I applied to the fidelity gate itself. So the recompiled artifact re-enters DAIF-03 as a new artifact, spends its loss budget again, proves behavioral equivalence against the new source again, and earns a verdict of its own. This is the most-skipped step in any synchronization mechanism, because a recompilation that ran to completion feels finished, and the temptation to mark it current on the strength of its predecessor's record is exactly the trust-from-history mechanism this dataset was built to break.

6.4 The obligation, handed to DAIF-07, and the invariant that makes the whole thing real. The third output is the one that decides whether Parts I through V were engineering or theater. When the engine marks an artifact SUSPECT or INVALID, DAIF-21 opens a typed obligation — this artifact must be recompiled and re-verified — with an owner, an age, and a closure condition, and hands it to DAIF-07's lifecycle. The obligation is not a courtesy and it is not a log line. It is the mechanism by which the invalidation acquires a claim on the estate's future attention, and it is the difference between a system that noticed the decay and a system that repaired it. The invariant is stated bluntly: an invalidation that creates no tracked obligation is a message in a bottle, and this corpus already knows, in detail, what becomes of work that nobody tracked — the task named in a conversation that closed, the follow-up promised in a session that ended, the pending verification that attention moved past. Those are not hypothetical losses; they are the founding observation of DAIF-07 and the reason it exists. An invalidation is exactly that shape of work — named once, owed by nobody, forgotten by default — and it dies the same death unless it is typed, owned, aged, and closed. The obligation's closure condition is what forbids the comfortable ending: the obligation closes when the artifact has been recompiled and has re-cleared its fidelity gate, not when someone has acknowledged the notification.

6.5 Contract re-verification and pack invalidation, handed to DAIF-04 and DAIF-08. The fourth output concerns interfaces. When a change reaches a typed contract — a schema, an event shape, a guarantee one system made to another — the contract does not merely become SUSPECT; it must be re-verified against both parties, because a contract is the only artifact in the corpus whose falsity is immediately load-bearing on a system that did not change. DAIF-04 owns that verification and DAIF-21 hands it the verdict plus the changed unit that reached it. The fifth output concerns assembled context: a Mission Pack is a selection of artifacts frozen at assembly time, which makes it the most photograph-like object in the entire corpus, and when an artifact inside a pack is invalidated the pack itself inherits the verdict. DAIF-08 owns pack lifecycle and must not assemble, or continue to serve, a pack containing INVALID units. The subtle case is the pack in flight — assembled, handed to a reasoning session, and invalidated mid-session — and the rule is that the invalidation is surfaced into the session rather than silently swallowed, because an actor reasoning from a pack that has just been falsified is the precise situation in which a confident wrong action is about to be taken.

6.6 Failure modes of the action phase. Three failures recur. The first is the notification that nobody owns — the invalidation is emitted, correctly and precisely, into a channel with no consumer and no closure condition, and the estate's decay is now well-documented and entirely unrepaired; this is the failure the obligation invariant of 6.4 exists to make structurally impossible. The second is the inherited PASS — the recompiled artifact carried forward on its predecessor's fidelity verdict, which reintroduces at the repair site the exact staleness the repair was run to remove, and which is invisible because the artifact bears a PASS and PASSes are not questioned. The third is the partial repair: the closure was recompiled, the artifacts were re-gated, and the obligations, contracts, and packs that derived from them were not, so the estate is repaired at the layer that is easy to see and stale at the layers that are expensive to walk — and the layers that were skipped are precisely the ones closest to the point of action, which means the repair fixed the representation and left the consumption path untouched.

6.7 Metrics, countermetrics, and consumers. The action phase is measured on repair latency — the interval from change detection to a re-verified artifact — and on obligation closure rate for synchronization-generated obligations, which is the only number that distinguishes a mechanism from a report. The anti-Goodhart countermetric on repair latency is the re-gate pass rate: a system optimizing for fast repair will start skipping the DAIF-03 re-evaluation, achieve superb latency, and ship unverified recompilations at speed, so latency is never permitted to be read without the fraction of recompilations that actually re-cleared their fidelity gate. The countermetric on obligation closure rate is closure quality — the fraction of closed synchronization obligations whose artifact, when probed afterward, actually matches reality — because a closure rate is trivially maximized by closing obligations without doing the work, and the corpus has a standing rule about gates that are never observed to refuse. The outputs of this Part are consumed by DAIF-02, DAIF-03, DAIF-04, DAIF-07, and DAIF-08 respectively, which is the whole point: DAIF-21 is thin because every repair it commissions is performed by the dataset that owns the thing being repaired, and a synchronization layer that started performing those repairs itself would become a second compiler, a second fidelity gate, and a second obligation lifecycle, each of them drifting from the original it forked.

PART VI FINAL LAW. A detected change does not merely invalidate, it generates work, and the work must be tracked or the invalidation is theater: DAIF-21 hands selective recompilation to DAIF-02 (only the affected closure, never the corpus, because a gratuitous recompilation can manufacture the very invalidation it was run to repair), re-evaluation to DAIF-03 (a recompiled artifact does not inherit its predecessor's PASS — the source moved, the compilation re-ran, and the moment is different), contract re-verification to DAIF-04, pack invalidation to DAIF-08 (surfaced into any session holding a pack falsified in flight), and above all a typed obligation to DAIF-07 with an owner, an age, and a closure condition that closes only on a re-cleared fidelity gate — because an invalidation that creates no tracked obligation is a message in a bottle, and this corpus already knows exactly what becomes of work that nobody tracked.

PART VII — STALENESS, DECAY, AND THE HALF-LIFE OF KNOWLEDGE

7.1 Mission. Everything to this point has been reactive: something moved, the estate detected it, decided, and acted. This closing Part is the proactive half, and it exists because the reactive half is provably incomplete — Part IV admitted that some meaning movements are invisible to any comparison, and Part III established that silence from the detection surface is UNVERIFIED and never PROVEN. It follows that an artifact can be stale with no signal ever having been emitted about it, and the only instrument that reaches such an artifact is one that goes and asks. But the estate cannot ask about everything continuously, so the probe budget must be allocated, and allocating it requires knowing which artifacts are likely to have decayed. That is what a decay model is: a prediction of how fast a unit's currency erodes in the absence of any signal, and it is the mechanism that turns an unaffordable universal re-verification into an affordable targeted one. Knowledge does not decay at a uniform rate, and a system that treats it as though it does will either re-verify everything, which nobody will pay for, or re-verify on a flat schedule, which spends its budget uniformly across units whose decay rates differ by orders of magnitude.

7.2 The four decay classes. Model the decay explicitly and there are four kinds of unit. The near-permanent unit decays so slowly that its currency is effectively unconditioned: a physical constraint, a mathematical identity, a sealed law that the estate has promoted into its rule register precisely because it has stopped moving. Re-verifying it is nearly free of value and its probe budget is correspondingly near zero. The fast-decaying unit loses currency in hours or days: the state of a working tree, which process is alive, what a running session currently holds, the value of a measurement taken against a moving system. Its currency claim is almost always stale by the time it is read, and the correct treatment is often not to re-verify it but to refuse to compile it as a durable artifact at all — a unit that decays faster than the estate can observe it should be fetched live and never held. The scheduled-decay unit erodes on a clock the estate can predict: an environment description, a dependency version, a credential window, anything with a known refresh cadence, and it is the class the expiry field serves natively. And the event-decayed unit does not erode with time at all but falls the instant a specific event occurs: a decision stands until its revision condition fires, a constraint holds until its authority lifts it. Its half-life is meaningless and its probe schedule is a waste; what it needs is a watch on its triggering event, which is exactly the revision-condition slot DAIF-02's decision representation already carries.

7.3 The freshness verdict and the expiry field, composed and never forked. DAIF-21 does not compute freshness. GK-07 already issues freshness and integrity verdicts over the knowledge graph's nodes, and the synchronization layer reads those verdicts as an input to its currency decision rather than maintaining a rival notion that would inevitably drift from them — two freshness authorities disagreeing about the same node is the authority collision the constitution convicts, and the resolution is the one the corpus always applies: there is one authority, it is GK-07's, and DAIF-21 composes it. Nor does DAIF-21 define an expiry type. DAIF-01's Validity dimension already carries the window within which a typed cognitive object is current, and the decay model's output is written into that field rather than into a parallel structure, so a consumer that can read a DAIF-01 object can read its expiry without knowing DAIF-21 exists. The composition is what keeps this dataset thin: DAIF-21 contributes the decay model — the prediction of erosion rate — and contributes it into slots and verdicts that two sealed systems already own. It adds a rate to an existing field and an input to an existing verdict, and it adds nothing else.

7.4 The conservative default, and why permanence is the fatal assumption. A unit arrives with no decay model far more often than it arrives with one, and the rule for that unit is stated without exception: a unit with no decay model is treated as decaying. It is not treated as permanent, it is not treated as fresh, and its absence of a model is never read as a claim about its stability — it is read as what it is, an absence of information, which under the invariant Part III composed from SQI resolves to UNVERIFIED and therefore to SUSPECT. The reason the default runs this direction rather than the other is the whole argument of Part I compressed into a single design choice. Assuming permanence is comfortable, it is cheap, it is almost always locally correct, and it is the exact error that converts a knowledge base into a museum of confident falsehoods — a register full of beautifully typed, rigorously gated, fully provenanced units, every one of them describing a world that has moved, every one of them trusted in direct proportion to how long it has sat there untouched. The museum is not a hypothetical failure state. It is the default state of every knowledge system that does not model decay, and it is reached not by any single error but by the accumulation of thousands of individually-reasonable decisions to leave a currently-correct unit alone.

7.5 The half-life metric and its data source. The decay model is measurable and must be measured, because an unmeasured decay model is an assumption with a schedule attached. The metric is half-life per unit class: the elapsed time after which half the units of a given class, sampled and probed, are found to no longer match reality. Its data source is the estate's own repair history, which is a real and growing record — every synchronization obligation DAIF-07 closes carries the artifact's class, the interval since its last verification, and whether the recompilation produced a materially different output, and that last field is precisely a probe result. So the half-life of a class is not estimated from theory; it is computed from the observed rate at which units of that class were found stale, and the model improves as the estate repairs itself. Half-life then feeds directly back into the probe budget: a class with a short half-life gets frequent probes, a class with a long one gets rare ones, and a class whose half-life is unmeasured gets the conservative default of 7.4 until enough repairs have accumulated to measure it. The loop is closed and it is self-correcting, which is the property that distinguishes a decay model from a guess written into a configuration once and never revisited.

7.6 The anti-Goodhart countermetric, which is the sharpest one in this dataset. A decay model is a cost lever, and this makes it the most corruptible object in DAIF-21, because there is exactly one move that reduces recompilation cost to zero and it is available at any time: declare everything permanent. A model tuned to minimize recompilation cost will discover this move immediately. It will lengthen half-lives, extend expiry windows, promote units into the near-permanent class, and it will be rewarded on every cost metric the estate tracks while quietly rebuilding the museum. The countermetric that forbids this is the sampled staleness rate of units the model declared fresh: draw a sample from the units currently marked VALID with a live freshness verdict, probe them directly against reality, and measure what fraction is stale. That number is the model's honesty, it is orthogonal to cost by construction, and it must be read beside every cost improvement the model ever claims. A model that halved recompilation cost while its sampled staleness rate rose has not improved; it has moved the failure from a place where it was paid in compute to a place where it will be paid in a confident wrong action, which is the trade this entire dataset exists to refuse. Cost may never be read alone.

7.7 Failure modes, and the closing of the corpus. Three failures end this Part. The unmodeled unit, which is handled by the conservative default and would otherwise sit fresh forever. The optimistic model, which is the museum, guarded by the sampled staleness countermetric of 7.6. And the pessimistic model, which probes everything constantly, exhausts the budget on near-permanent units, and gets itself switched off — the same self-defeating over-firing that Part III named as alert fatigue and Part V named as the unbounded cascade, appearing here for the third and final time, because it is the recurring shape of every mechanism in this dataset that tries to be safe by being total. The corpus closes here for a reason. DAIF-01 typed the estate's knowledge, DAIF-02 compiled it, DAIF-03 proved the compilation faithful, DAIF-07 tracked the work it owed, DAIF-04 held the contracts between its systems, and DAIF-08 assembled it into missions — and every one of those artifacts is a photograph. DAIF-21 is the mechanism that keeps checking whether the world is still in the frame, and without it the corpus is a perfect, typed, gated, provenanced, and increasingly false account of a moment that has passed.

PART VII FINAL LAW. Knowledge decays at four rates — near-permanent, fast, scheduled, and event-triggered — and DAIF-21 contributes only the decay rate, writing it into DAIF-01's Validity window and feeding GK-07's freshness verdict rather than forking either; a unit with no decay model receives the conservative default and is treated as decaying, because assuming permanence is the single error that turns a knowledge base into a museum of confident falsehoods; and the half-life metric, computed from the estate's own repair history rather than from theory, is governed by the sharpest countermetric in this dataset — the sampled staleness rate of units the model declared fresh — because a decay model tuned to reduce recompilation cost will simply declare everything permanent, score perfectly on every cost measure, and quietly rebuild the museum it was created to prevent.
