DAIF-04 — INSTITUTIONAL CONTRACT FABRIC

PART I — THESIS: THE UNTYPED INTERACTION SURFACE

1.1 Mission. The Power Pack is not a collection of independent systems; it is an estate of systems that constantly act on one another. An agent dispatches another agent and consumes what comes back. A hook admits or refuses a write that some other component depends on having landed. A dataset cites a sibling dataset and reasons over guarantees that sibling is assumed to hold. A command invokes an engine and treats its output as authoritative. A gate declares a body of work admissible and everything downstream proceeds on that admission. Every one of those interactions is a promise: one party undertakes to deliver something, and another party proceeds as if the delivery is real. DAIF-04 exists because none of those promises is currently a typed object. They live in prose — a paragraph inside a dataset labelled as its integration contract, an assertion inside a code comment, an authority implied by which configuration section a gate happens to sit in — and prose is not checkable. The mission of this dataset is narrow and load-bearing: to make the estate's interaction surface typed, so that what a component promised and whether it delivered are questions a machine can ask and answer, rather than questions a human reconstructs after the damage has already been paid.

1.2 What a prose convention actually is. A prose convention is a promise recorded in a medium that cannot evaluate it. It has four properties, all fatal at scale. It cannot be checked: no runtime can read "this engine returns a well-formed decision record" and confirm the returned object satisfies it, because the sentence names no evaluable assertion and no evaluator. It cannot be versioned: the sentence has no version field, so when a consumer written against last quarter's understanding meets a provider that changed its output shape, nothing anywhere records that the understanding changed. It cannot be breached against: breach requires a stated postcondition to be found false, and a paragraph states no postcondition an evaluator can find false, so the provider can fail completely without any mechanism concluding that it failed. And it drifts silently: the prose is written once and the system it describes changes weekly, so the paragraph becomes a description of a system that no longer exists, still read and still trusted. The convention degrades from a promise into a fiction with no event marking the transition, which is precisely why nobody notices.

1.3 The cardinal consequence. From those four properties one consequence follows, and it is the founding argument of this dataset: without typed contracts, a breach is discovered by its damage rather than by its detection. Detection is a mechanism that evaluates a stated promise at the moment of delivery and concludes true or false. Damage is what happens when no such mechanism exists — the unmet promise propagates, a consumer builds on an output it believed satisfied a guarantee it never actually satisfied, a second consumer builds on the first, and the failure surfaces many hops away from its origin, disguised as a fault of the last component to touch it. The estate has paid this repeatedly: a gate believed to hold an authority it never held; an engine believed to write a field no producer ever populated; a component built, unit-green, and cited by name while nothing in the estate ever called it. Each was a promise that was never a contract, and each was discovered by damage. Worse, damage arrives on the schedule that suits it least: an unchecked promise holds fine while the parties happen to agree, and detonates the first time they diverge, which is exactly when the estate is under pressure and least able to absorb a surprise.

1.4 What a contract is. A contract, in this dataset, is a typed, authored, versioned promise between exactly two parties — a provider and a consumer — carrying evaluable preconditions the consumer must satisfy before the provider is obliged to perform, evaluable postconditions the provider guarantees afterward, an authority that entitles the promise, an owner who is accountable for it, a scope within which it binds, and a defined failure path that says what happens when the promise is not kept. Each of those seven elements is a DAIF-01 typed object rather than a sentence: the authority is a DAIF-01 authority value on the estate's one ladder, the scope is a DAIF-01 scope value on the estate's one nested reach, the validity is a DAIF-01 validity window, the confidence and provenance are DAIF-01 fields. DAIF-04 does not invent a parallel type system; it composes the sealed one and adds exactly one new object — the contract — whose fields are typed by DAIF-01 and whose evaluations are evidenced under the estate's existing reality supremacy. The contract is the smallest thing that turns an interaction from a habit into an obligation.

1.5 What DAIF-04 is not. Three exclusions bound this dataset and are enforced as hard limits on its own construction. It is not a message bus: the estate already has PM-03 for publication between components, and forking a second bus would create precisely the duplicate this fabric's constitution convicts. It is not an event log: CEPS already records events, and the hook chain already carries the synchronous admission signal that gates writes. It is not a scheduler: nothing in this dataset decides when a component runs. DAIF-04 absorbs the event candidate from the corpus map on exactly one axis — the typed event schema, which is a contract about what an emission must contain for a subscriber to consume it safely — and on no other. Transport belongs to PM-03, CEPS, and the hook chain, permanently. The typed schema without the transport is the whole absorption: a contract says what a well-formed emission holds and who may rely on which of its fields; the carriage of that emission from publisher to subscriber is a solved problem and stays solved where it is.

1.6 Why typing the surface is the highest-leverage move available. The estate has already typed its knowledge (DAIF-01), and is typing its representations, its fidelity budget, and its obligations. Those are all typings of things a system holds. The interaction surface is the one place where two systems meet, and it is therefore the one place where a defect crosses a boundary. A defect inside a component is contained by that component's own gates; a defect that crosses a boundary is a defect the receiving component's gates were never designed to catch, because the receiving component trusted the promise. Typing the boundary is thus worth more per unit of effort than typing anything on either side of it: it converts the estate's most common failure — a component behaving reasonably on an input the provider never promised to give it — from an unattributable mystery into a breach with a fault class, a responsible party, and a recorded event. The thin-dataset discipline follows directly: DAIF-04's value is the contract object and its lifecycle, not a re-narration of the systems it binds, and any Part that re-describes done_gate or spec_gate rather than typing the promise those gates make is padding and is rejected.

1.7 The two failure directions this dataset closes. Prose conventions fail in two directions and both are load-bearing. In the optimistic direction, a consumer trusts a promise that was never made — it reads an integration paragraph, infers a guarantee the provider never intended, and builds on it; the provider then changes freely, correctly, and within its own rights, and the consumer breaks without either party having done anything wrong, because there was no contract to constrain the change. In the pessimistic direction, a consumer defensively re-validates everything it receives, duplicating the provider's own checks, which is the redundant-verification tax that makes an estate slow and its components mutually distrustful. A typed contract closes both: the consumer knows exactly what it may rely on and validates nothing more, and the provider knows exactly what it may change without breaking anyone. Trust becomes bounded rather than blind or absent, and bounded trust is the only kind that scales past a handful of components.

1.8 The dataset's own contract. This corpus is bound by what it argues. It promises a typed contract object with an evaluable lifecycle (Part II), a taxonomy that names every interaction class the estate actually runs (Part III), a checkable pre/postcondition semantics with an evidence requirement (Part IV), an authority and scope model composed from DAIF-01 rather than forked (Part V), a breach model with fault classification preceding response (Part VI), and a failure semantics covering timeout, retry, rollback, and compensation (Part VII). It promises to reference done_gate, spec_gate, PM-03, CEPS, the hook chain, SQI, DRK, ACIS, CO-12, UKDL, GK, and owner_queue by name and never to re-narrate them. And it declares its own blind spot, in the manner Part III will make mandatory for every evaluator: DAIF-04 types the promise and detects the breach, but it cannot make a badly-designed component good, and a perfectly-typed contract over a wrong guarantee is a wrong guarantee that is now precisely stated.

PART I FINAL LAW. The estate's systems already promise one another everything and type none of it, so its interactions are governed by prose that cannot be checked, cannot be versioned, cannot be breached against, and drifts silently as the systems beneath it change — which guarantees that every unkept promise is discovered by its damage, arriving at the worst possible moment and disguised as someone else's fault; DAIF-04 closes that surface by making the promise a typed, authored, versioned object with evaluable preconditions, evaluable postconditions, an accountable owner, and a defined failure path, and it closes it without building a second transport, because the bus, the event log, and the hook chain already exist and forking them would be the duplication this fabric exists to convict.

PART II — THE CONTRACT OBJECT

2.1 Mission. This Part defines the object. A contract is a single typed record, addressable by identity, holding every field required to state a promise, evaluate it, version it, and fail from it safely. The object's design principle is that every field exists because its absence causes a specific, nameable breakage — a field that breaks nothing when omitted is not a field, it is decoration, and the thin-dataset discipline removes it. What follows names each field, what it holds, its invariant, and the failure that its absence produces. The fields divide into five groups: identity and parties, the promise itself, governance, evaluation, and failure semantics. Every typed value in the object draws from DAIF-01's type system — authority from the authority ladder, scope from the nested scope ladder, validity from the temporal types, confidence and provenance from the epistemic fields — and DAIF-04 defines no parallel vocabulary for any of them.

2.2 Identity and parties. A contract carries an identity that is stable across versions, so that a promise can be tracked as it evolves rather than appearing as a sequence of unrelated records, and it carries exactly two parties: a provider, who undertakes to perform, and a consumer, who is entitled to rely. The invariant is that both parties are named, resolvable references to real components in the estate's capability graph — GK coordinates, not descriptions — because a party that cannot be resolved cannot be held to anything. The two-party restriction is deliberate: a promise made to everyone in general is enforced by no one in particular, and a contract with three parties is two contracts that have not yet been separated. When a provider genuinely serves many consumers, the correct model is one contract with a consumer class typed as a scope rather than a list, so that the reach of the promise is a DAIF-01 scope value and admission of a new consumer is a scope check rather than an amendment. Absent identity, versioning is impossible; absent resolvable parties, breach has no defendant and no plaintiff, and a breach with no defendant is an incident report, not an enforcement.

2.3 The promise: preconditions, inputs, postconditions, outputs. The promise proper is four fields. Preconditions state what must be true before the provider is obliged to perform — the consumer's side of the bargain. Inputs type what the consumer supplies. Postconditions state what the provider guarantees to be true afterward. Outputs type what the provider returns. The distinction between inputs and preconditions is not pedantry: inputs are the shape of what arrives, preconditions are the state of the world that must hold for the arrival to be actionable, and a component that validates shape but not state is the component that accepts a well-formed request to act on a thing that does not exist. Likewise, outputs are the shape of what returns and postconditions are the state of the world the return implies. The invariant binding all four is that each is an evaluable assertion — a typed predicate an evaluator can decide — and never a sentence. Part IV develops the evaluation semantics; the object model's requirement is only that the four fields be populated with assertions rather than descriptions, because an assertion can be false and a description cannot.

2.4 The rule that a contract without a postcondition is not a contract. This is the object's central invariant and it governs the whole dataset. A contract whose postcondition field is empty, or filled with an unfalsifiable sentence, promises nothing: there is no proposition that could be found false, so nothing can ever be said to have been delivered, and nothing can ever be said to have been breached. Such a record is not a contract but a hope — a statement of intent with the enforcement surface of a wish. The estate has held many of these without noticing, because a hope reads exactly like a contract when both are written in prose. The invariant is enforced at creation, not at execution: a contract object with no evaluable postcondition is refused admission to the register, in the same way DAIF-01 refuses a unit whose type demands a field it does not carry. The refusal is the point. A promise that cannot fail cannot be relied on either, because reliance is precisely the expectation that a specific thing will be true, and if no specific thing is stated, the consumer's reliance is on its own imagination.

2.5 Governance fields: authority, ownership, scope, version, deprecation. Five fields make a contract governable. Authority is a DAIF-01 authority value naming the entitlement under which the promise is made, and it caps what the contract may bind — Part V develops this. Ownership names exactly one accountable party, because a contract owned by everyone is enforced by no one and a contract with no owner has no address to which a breach can be delivered. Scope is a DAIF-01 scope value bounding where the contract binds — project, repo, feature, power-pack, universal — and its invariant is that a contract cannot bind outside its declared scope, so a promise made for one project cannot silently govern another. Version records the contract's revision, and its invariant is that any change to a precondition, postcondition, or output type is a new version, never an in-place edit, because an in-place edit is exactly the silent drift the whole dataset exists to prevent. Deprecation policy names how the contract ends: the notice a consumer receives, the window in which both versions hold, and the terminal date. Absent version, the drift is invisible; absent deprecation policy, the drift is visible but the consumer discovers it as a sudden removal, which is a breach dressed as a release.

2.6 Evaluation fields: evidence requirements, done-gates, compatibility guarantees. Three fields make the promise verifiable rather than merely stated. Evidence requirements name what counts as proof that a postcondition holds — an observed exit code, a file that exists on disk, a query that returns a row, a recorded gate result — and the invariant is the estate's reality supremacy: a postcondition claimed without its required evidence is UNVERIFIED, which is a distinct state from satisfied and from breached, and it is never silently promoted to satisfied. The done-gate field names the evaluator that decides satisfaction, composing done_gate where the estate's existing gate already covers the surface rather than defining a rival evaluator. Compatibility guarantees state what the provider promises not to change without a version bump: which output fields are load-bearing, which are advisory, which may be added freely. Absent evidence requirements, a postcondition is satisfied by assertion and the contract becomes a self-certification, which is the failure Part IV convicts by separation of powers. Absent compatibility guarantees, every provider change is a potential breach and no provider can evolve without fear, so the estate freezes.

2.7 Failure-semantics fields: failure mode, timeout, retry policy, escalation, rollback, compensation. Six fields state what happens when the promise is not kept, and Part VII develops each. Failure semantics names how the provider fails — whether a failure is total or partial, whether partial effects persist, and whether the consumer may assume nothing happened. Timeout bounds the promise in time, and its invariant is absolute: an unbounded promise is not a promise but a hang, and the estate's own hard-won doctrine forbids ending a turn in an unbounded wait. Retry policy states under what conditions a retry is permitted and — critically — the pivot conditions under which retry is forbidden. Escalation names the path when neither performance nor retry resolves the matter, terminating at owner_queue when the estate cannot decide alone. Rollback names how the effect is undone. Compensation names the offsetting action when undo is impossible. The binding invariant: a contract whose effects are irreversible and which declares no compensation path is refused execution, because an irreversible effect with no offsetting action is a decision the estate cannot walk back and must not take by accident.

2.8 Lifecycle state. The contract holds a lifecycle state, and the states are: DRAFTED (authored, not yet admitted), ACTIVE (admitted, binding), SUSPENDED (temporarily not binding, by an authority entitled to suspend), BREACHED (a breach has been recorded against the current version and not yet resolved), SUPERSEDED (a later version binds and this one is retained only for reading), and VOID (the contract was invalid at creation and never bound at all — Part V shows how an authority overreach produces this at creation rather than at execution). The invariants are that transitions are one-way except SUSPENDED, that only the accountable owner or a higher authority may suspend or supersede, and that a BREACHED contract is not automatically VOID — a breached promise is still a promise, and voiding it on breach would let a provider escape its obligation by failing it. Absent lifecycle state, a retired contract is indistinguishable from a live one and consumers keep relying on promises that no party still intends to keep, which is drift with extra steps.

2.9 The register and the object's invariants as a whole. Contracts are held in a register keyed by identity and version, with the estate's freshness and identity guarantees applying to it as they apply to any register. Three whole-object invariants hold across every field. First, no contract is ACTIVE without an evaluable postcondition, an accountable owner, a scope, an authority, and a timeout — the five without which it is unenforceable. Second, no contract may be edited in place once ACTIVE; a change is a version. Third, every evaluation of a contract, satisfied or breached or unverified, is recorded, because an evaluation that leaves no record teaches the estate nothing and lets a chronic breacher present as reliable. The register is a producer as well as a holder: it publishes contract events on PM-03 under the typed event schema this dataset defines, so subscribers learn of a new version, a suspension, or a breach through the transport that already exists.

PART II FINAL LAW. A contract is one typed record holding two resolvable parties, evaluable preconditions and postconditions, typed inputs and outputs, an authority, exactly one accountable owner, a bounded scope, a version and a deprecation policy, evidence requirements bound to a named evaluator, compatibility guarantees, and a full failure semantics of timeout, retry, escalation, rollback, and compensation — and its central invariant is that a contract with no evaluable postcondition is not a contract but a hope, refused at creation rather than discovered at breach, because a promise that can never be found false can never be relied upon, only imagined.

PART III — THE CONTRACT TAXONOMY

3.1 Mission. The contract object of Part II is one shape; the estate's interactions are many kinds. This Part names the kinds, and for each one names its parties, its characteristic promise, and its characteristic breach — because a taxonomy that does not name the breach is a list of nouns, and the breach is what tells a designer which fields of the object carry the weight for that kind. The taxonomy is closed at nine kinds, and closure is deliberate: an open taxonomy invites a tenth kind for every awkward case, and the tenth kind is where untyped prose re-enters through the back door. An interaction that does not fit one of the nine is either two interactions that have not been separated, or a sign the taxonomy is genuinely wrong and must be amended by an authority entitled to amend it — never accommodated by an unclassified exception.

3.2 Agent-to-agent. Parties: a dispatching agent and a dispatched agent. The characteristic promise is what the dispatched agent undertakes to return — a typed result of a stated shape, within a stated budget, having touched only a stated surface, and having verified rather than assumed the claims it reports. The characteristic breach is the confident-but-unverified return: the dispatched agent reports completion of work it did not observe, and the dispatcher, having no postcondition to evaluate, relays the claim upward as fact. This is the estate's most expensive breach class because it launders an unverified claim through a boundary that looks like verification. The contract's load-bearing fields here are the postcondition (what the return must assert and evidence), the evidence requirement (the dispatched agent's claim of a passing gate must carry the observed result, not the intent to run it), and the scope (an agent that touched files outside the promised surface has breached even if its stated result is correct, because the consumer relied on the containment).

3.3 Dataset-to-dataset. Parties: a citing corpus and a cited corpus. The characteristic promise is what one corpus guarantees another may rely on — that a named law will not be silently reversed, that a taxonomy declared closed will not gain a member without a version, that a term defined in one dataset means the same thing when a sibling composes it. The characteristic breach is silent semantic drift: the cited corpus refines a definition for good reasons, the citing corpus continues to reason over the old meaning, and both remain internally coherent while the composition between them is now false. The load-bearing fields are the version (a definitional change is a new version, never an edit), the compatibility guarantee (which definitions are load-bearing for citers and which are illustrative), and the deprecation policy (a retired law gets a notice window, not a deletion). This is the contract that makes composition safe, and it is why DAIF-04 composes DAIF-01's types rather than restating them: the citation is the contract.

3.4 Command-to-engine. Parties: a command surface a human or agent invokes, and the engine it drives. The characteristic promise runs in both directions: the command promises to supply well-formed, authorized inputs, and the engine promises a typed result, a bounded runtime, and a defined behavior on partial failure. The characteristic breach is the silent no-op — the engine runs, exits zero, and does nothing, because the input it received was well-formed but semantically empty, and no postcondition asserted that the run must have produced an effect. The load-bearing fields are the postcondition stated as an effect rather than an exit code (a gate that runs is not a gate that acted), the timeout, and the failure semantics, because a command whose engine half-completed and returned zero has left the estate in a state the caller believes is clean.

3.5 Hook-to-runtime. Parties: a hook and the runtime whose actions it gates. The characteristic promise is a blocking guarantee: this hook, on this event, for this class of action, will refuse the action when its condition fires, and the refusal will actually prevent the action rather than merely record disapproval. The characteristic breach is the swallowed block — the hook fires, forms a refusal, and the refusal is discarded somewhere in the chain, so a gate that is believed to hold nothing through has been holding nothing at all. The estate has paid this exact breach. The load-bearing fields are the authority (a hook's entitlement to refuse is a DAIF-01 authority value, not an implication of where it sits in a configuration file), the postcondition stated as an observed refusal rather than an emitted verdict, and the evidence requirement — a hook's blocking guarantee is UNVERIFIED until a refusal has been empirically observed to prevent the action, which makes an occasional deliberate breach attempt part of the contract rather than a violation of it.

3.6 Engine-to-engine. Parties: two engines composed in a chain. The characteristic promise is the handoff: the upstream engine guarantees the shape, completeness, and freshness of what it passes, and the downstream engine guarantees it will not silently accept a degraded input. The characteristic breach is the starved field — a field the downstream engine reads and no upstream engine ever writes, so the consumer is coded, tested, live, and dead, reading null forever without a single error. The load-bearing fields are the output type (every field a downstream engine reads must appear in an upstream engine's postcondition as a field it undertakes to populate) and the evidence requirement (a producer's promise to populate a field is UNVERIFIED until the field has been observed non-null across live records). This is the contract that converts an orphan field from an invisible dead path into a breach with a defendant.

3.7 Mission-to-runtime. Parties: an assembled mission — a unit of work with a stated goal and a bounded surface — and the runtime that executes it. The characteristic promise is that the runtime will execute the mission within its declared scope, budget, and authority, and will halt rather than exceed any of the three. The characteristic breach is the scope creep that succeeds: the runtime completes the mission and more, touching surfaces the mission never authorized, and because the mission's stated goal was achieved, no evaluator flags the unauthorized reach. The load-bearing fields are scope (bounding the surface the runtime may touch) and the postcondition stated as goal-achieved-and-nothing-else, because a postcondition that only asserts the goal cannot detect the excess. This contract composes DAIF-08's assembly and consumes DAIF-07's obligations; it does not re-derive either.

3.8 Memory and register contracts. Parties: a register — any component that holds state for later reading — and its readers. The characteristic promise is about freshness and identity: this register asserts that a record it returns is current as of a stated window, and that a record's identity is stable, so that two readings of the same key mean the same thing. The characteristic breach is the stale-read-as-current: the register returns a record that no longer reflects the world, the reader has no way to distinguish it from a fresh one, and it acts on a truth that has expired. The load-bearing fields are the validity window (a DAIF-01 temporal type on the record, not on the register), the postcondition stated as a freshness assertion, and the failure semantics for the unmeasurable case — a register that cannot determine a record's freshness must return UNKNOWN, never the record, because the fail-open direction here is silence and the fail-closed direction is a lie. Freshness composes the estate's existing GK freshness signals and DAIF-21's synchronization triggers rather than forking a rival staleness model.

3.9 Evaluator contracts and the negative clause. Parties: a gate — any evaluator that admits or refuses work — and everything downstream that relies on its admission. The characteristic promise is a coverage claim: this gate catches this class of defect. The characteristic breach is the false clean bill — the gate passes work that carries exactly the defect the estate believed it caught. And here the taxonomy imposes its sharpest requirement: an evaluator contract MUST declare its negative clause, an explicit statement of what the gate does NOT promise to catch. A gate that declares only its coverage will be trusted for coverage it never had, because a consumer reading a green result cannot distinguish "checked and clean" from "not checked at all" — both present as silence. The estate has been burned by exactly this shape: gates whose vocabulary could not recognize an idiom and therefore counted zero occurrences of it, returning a clean signal that was structurally incapable of being dirty. The negative clause converts that from an invisible blind spot into a declared one, and a declared blind spot is a backlog item; an undeclared one is a future incident. The load-bearing fields are the postcondition (what a pass asserts, stated narrowly), the negative clause (what a pass does not assert), and the evidence requirement that the gate has been observed to REFUSE at least once — a gate never observed to refuse is indistinguishable from an inert one, and its coverage claim is UNVERIFIED regardless of how many passes it has produced.

3.10 Governance contracts. Parties: a governing instrument — a hard rule, a doctrine, a sealed law — and every actor bound by it. The characteristic promise is a constraint the estate undertakes to honor: this action will not be taken under these conditions, this claim will not be made without this evidence, this bypass requires this authority. The characteristic breach is the quiet exception — an actor takes the constrained action under a rationale the rule never admitted, and because the rule was prose, the rationale is indistinguishable from a legitimate exception. The load-bearing fields are the authority (governance contracts sit at the top of DAIF-01's ladder and cannot be overridden by a lower rung), the override policy (naming the exact authority and the exact recorded phrase that permits a bypass, for exactly one occasion), and the breach record — a governance breach is always recorded, never absorbed, because a rule that is silently bypassed twice is not a rule. Governance contracts compose UKDL and the estate's hard-rule archive; DAIF-04 types the promise, it does not re-legislate the rules.

PART III FINAL LAW. The estate runs nine kinds of contract — agent-to-agent, dataset-to-dataset, command-to-engine, hook-to-runtime, engine-to-engine, mission-to-runtime, memory-and-register, evaluator, and governance — each with a characteristic promise and a characteristic breach that tells the designer which fields carry the weight, and the taxonomy's hardest clause is the evaluator's negative one: a gate that declares only what it catches will be trusted for coverage it never had, because a green result from a blind gate and a green result from a clean surface are the same silence, so every evaluator contract must state what it does NOT promise to catch and must have been observed at least once to refuse.

PART IV — PRECONDITIONS, POSTCONDITIONS, AND EVIDENCE

4.1 Mission. This Part builds the checkable core. Everything else in the contract object is metadata about a promise; the preconditions and postconditions are the promise. Their design requirement is singular: each must be a typed, evaluable assertion — a proposition an evaluator can decide as true, false, or undecidable — and never a sentence a human interprets. The difference is not stylistic. A sentence admits interpretation, and interpretation under pressure always resolves in favor of whoever needs the answer to be convenient. An assertion admits only evaluation, and evaluation under pressure resolves the same way it resolves at rest. This Part defines the assertion form, the obligation semantics that preconditions carry, the guarantee semantics that postconditions carry, the evidence requirement that separates a claimed postcondition from a satisfied one, the separation of powers that keeps a provider from judging itself, and the unfalsifiable postcondition that is the whole model's signature failure.

4.2 The assertion form. A precondition or postcondition is a triple: a subject (a resolvable thing — a file, a field, a register record, a gate result, a process state), a predicate (a decidable relation — exists, is non-empty, matches a type, equals a value, falls within a window, was observed to refuse), and an evaluator (the named component that decides it). The evaluator is part of the assertion, not an implementation detail, because an assertion with no named evaluator is undecidable in practice regardless of how decidable it looks on paper. The triple's invariant is that all three resolve: a subject that names nothing, a predicate that no evaluator implements, or an evaluator that does not exist are each a malformed assertion, and a contract carrying one is refused at creation. This is the mechanical descendant of the estate's premise-verification doctrine: a plan that names a function which does not exist is a plan against an imagined system, and an assertion that names an evaluator which does not exist is a promise against an imagined checker.

4.3 Preconditions as the obligation trigger. A precondition states what must be true before the provider is obliged to perform. Its semantics are exact and often misread: a precondition is not a validation the provider performs as a courtesy, it is the boundary of the provider's obligation. If a precondition is false, the provider is not obliged, and behavior outside an obligation is not a breach by the provider — it is a breach by the caller. This is why precondition-checking must happen at the boundary and must be recorded: a provider that silently proceeds on a false precondition has accepted an obligation it never made, and it will be blamed for the outcome. The invariant is that the provider evaluates every precondition before performing, refuses when one is false, and records the refusal with the identity of the caller and the failed assertion. A refusal is not a failure of the contract; it is the contract working. The estate's own gates behave this way — spec_gate refuses to admit an oversized task with no spec, and that refusal is the gate keeping its promise, not breaking it.

4.4 Postconditions as the guarantee. A postcondition states what the provider guarantees to be true after performing, and it is the only field against which delivery can be judged. Its semantics are that the provider bears the burden: if the postcondition is false after performance, the provider has breached, without regard to why, and the why belongs to the fault classification of Part VI rather than to the question of whether a breach occurred. This burden allocation is deliberate and sometimes uncomfortable — an environment fault still produces a false postcondition, and the contract still records that the promise was not kept — because a model in which a provider can dispute whether a breach occurred, rather than only its fault class, is a model in which no breach is ever recorded. Separate the two questions: did the promise hold (an objective evaluation of an assertion) and who is at fault (a classification). Merging them lets fault-avoidance suppress detection, which is the exact dynamic that lets a chronic breacher present as reliable.

4.5 The evidence requirement. A postcondition claimed but not evidenced is UNVERIFIED, and UNVERIFIED is a distinct terminal state — never a synonym for satisfied, never a synonym for breached. This composes the estate's reality supremacy directly: the disk outranks the claim, an observed result outranks an intended one, and a component's own report of its success is a hypothesis until an instrument independent of it confirms the effect. The contract's evidence-requirement field names what would constitute confirmation: the exit code observed, the file present with the expected content hash, the register record queried and found non-null, the gate result recorded rather than asserted. The three-state model matters because two-state thinking collapses UNVERIFIED into whichever pole is cheaper, and the cheaper pole is always satisfied. An estate that treats unverified as satisfied is an estate whose contracts are self-certifying, and self-certification is not verification, it is paperwork. CO-12's telemetry-before-claims is the same law on the metrics surface, and DAIF-04 composes it rather than restating it: no claim about a delivery is admissible ahead of the measurement that grounds it.

4.6 Separation of powers: the provider may not be the sole judge. The provider may evaluate its own postconditions, and usually must — it is closest to the effect and cheapest to run. But it may not be the sole judge of its own delivery, because a provider that both performs and adjudicates has a structural incentive to define satisfaction as whatever it produced. The rule, composing DAIF-07's completion-authority separation, is that a postcondition's evaluator must be resolvable by a party other than the provider, and that at least one class of postcondition per contract — the ones whose falsity would be most expensive — must be evaluated by an evaluator the provider does not control. The independent evaluator need not be exotic: the disk is an independent evaluator, a downstream gate is an independent evaluator, and a consumer-side type check on the returned object is an independent evaluator. What is forbidden is the closed loop in which the provider asserts satisfaction, the record holds the assertion, and no instrument outside the provider ever touched the world the assertion describes. That loop produces green forever, including on the day everything is broken.

4.7 The unfalsifiable postcondition. The signature failure of this Part is the postcondition that cannot be false. "The output is high quality." "The agent performed correctly." "The context is sufficient." Each reads like a guarantee and each is a null: there is no state of the world an evaluator could observe that would render it false, so it can never be breached, and a promise that can never be breached promises nothing. The unfalsifiable postcondition is more dangerous than an absent one, because an absent postcondition is visibly absent and is refused at creation by Part II's central invariant, while an unfalsifiable one fills the field and passes the shape check. Detection is mechanical and it is the same falsifiability test ACIS applies to a theorem: for every postcondition, name the observation that would falsify it. If no such observation can be named, the postcondition is refused. This forces the author to convert a quality aspiration into a set of decidable assertions — the output parses, every field a consumer reads is non-null, the named gate returned a pass with its evidence attached, the runtime stayed under the declared bound — which is harder to write and is the only kind that can be relied on.

4.8 Undecidability and the honest UNKNOWN. Some assertions cannot be decided in a given run: the evaluator is unavailable, the subject cannot be reached, the measurement is not instrumented. The contract's discipline here is that an undecidable assertion returns UNKNOWN, and UNKNOWN never defaults to satisfied. This is the estate's own hard-won lesson on gates that could not recognize what they were counting and therefore counted zero: a quantity that cannot be measured must be reported as unmeasurable, not as zero, because zero is a value and unmeasurable is an absence, and treating the absence as a value produces a gate that passes precisely because it is blind. Every contract must therefore declare the behavior of each assertion under undecidability — whether an UNKNOWN blocks performance, permits it with a recorded caveat, or escalates — and the default, when the contract is silent, is to block for any postcondition whose falsity is expensive and to record for all others. A contract that lets its own unmeasurability pass silently has quietly become a hope again.

4.9 Composition, not duplication. Nothing in this Part builds a new evaluator. The assertion's evaluator field points at what the estate already runs: done_gate for admissibility, spec_gate for the spec precondition on large work, the hook chain for write-time refusals, SQI's benchmarks for behavioral evidence, ACIS's ladder for epistemic standing, CO-12 for measurement-before-claim. DAIF-04's contribution is the typed slot that binds an assertion to an evaluator and the three-state result semantics that keeps an unverified claim out of the satisfied bucket. The value is in the binding, and the binding is what converts a scattered set of good gates into a promise a consumer can rely on: not "there are gates somewhere upstream" but "this specific assertion is decided by this specific evaluator, whose own contract declares what it does not catch."

PART IV FINAL LAW. A precondition is the boundary of the provider's obligation and a postcondition is the whole of its guarantee, each a typed triple of resolvable subject, decidable predicate, and named evaluator rather than a sentence a reader interprets; a postcondition claimed without its required evidence is UNVERIFIED and never satisfied, an assertion that cannot be decided is UNKNOWN and never zero, the provider may evaluate its own delivery but may never be its sole judge, and the postcondition that no observation could falsify — the output is high quality, the agent performed correctly — is refused at creation, because it fills the field, passes the shape check, can never be breached, and therefore promises exactly nothing.

PART V — AUTHORITY, OWNERSHIP, AND SCOPE IN CONTRACTS

5.1 Mission. A contract is a promise, and a promise made by a party with no entitlement to make it binds nothing. This Part types the three fields that decide entitlement and reach: authority (who may create, amend, waive, or void a contract, and what a contract may bind), ownership (who is accountable for it), and scope (over what surface it binds). All three compose DAIF-01 directly — the authority ladder, the nested scope ladder — and DAIF-04 defines no rival vocabulary for either. What DAIF-04 adds is the application of those types to the contract object: the rules by which a contract's authority is checked at creation rather than at execution, the rule that exactly one party is accountable, and the isolation rule that stops a project-scoped promise from silently binding the estate.

5.2 The four authority-bearing acts. Four acts on a contract require authority, and each requires a different amount. Creation requires authority over the surface the contract binds: to promise something on behalf of a project, the creating party must hold at least project authority. Amendment — any change to a precondition, postcondition, or output type — requires the authority that created it or higher, and produces a new version rather than an edit, because an amendment made silently in place is the drift the fabric convicts. Waiver — permitting a single non-performance without recording a breach — requires strictly higher authority than creation, and is bounded to one occasion with a recorded rationale, because a waiver that can be self-issued by the provider is not a waiver but an escape. Voiding — declaring the contract never bound at all — requires the highest authority of the four and is reserved for a contract that was invalid at creation. The invariant across all four: the authority for an act is checked against DAIF-01's ladder before the act takes effect, and an unauthorized act does not fail loudly at execution — it never occurs.

5.3 The cardinal invariant: a party cannot grant authority it does not hold. This is the load-bearing rule of the Part. A contract in which a low-authority agent promises to override a hard constraint is VOID at creation, not breached at execution. The distinction is the entire mechanism. If such a contract were merely breachable, it would be admitted to the register, consumers would rely on it, the agent would attempt the override, and the estate would discover the problem at the moment of the attempted override — which is to say, at the moment of maximum damage, with a consumer already committed. Because it is void at creation, the register refuses it, the consumer never relies on it, and the failure is a refusal notice rather than an incident. The check is mechanical: the contract's promised effects are compared against the creating party's authority on DAIF-01's ladder, and any effect requiring an authority above the creator's rung voids the contract. An agent cannot promise what a project rule forbids; a project rule cannot promise what the constitution forbids; nothing in the estate can promise to set aside a hard rule, because a hard rule's override policy names an owner phrase and no contract can forge it.

5.4 Authority forgery and the provenance check. The adversarial case is the forged rung: a contract whose creator records an authority higher than its provenance entitles, so that the void-at-creation check passes on a lie. The defense is DAIF-01's own forged-authority detection, applied to the contract's creator field: an authority claim is valid only if its provenance names a source the ladder actually places at that rung, so a contract claiming constitutional authority whose provenance points at a single agent's session is convicted at intake. The generalization matters more than the instance: a low-authority party cannot promote itself by declaration, and a contract is a declaration. Without this check, the contract object would become the estate's cleanest self-elevation path — an agent authors a contract asserting its own high authority, the register admits it, and every subsequent check consults the contract rather than the ladder. The register consults the ladder, always, and the contract's authority field is a claim the ladder either entitles or refuses.

5.5 Ownership: exactly one accountable party. Every contract has exactly one owner, and the field is mandatory for the ACTIVE state. The owner is not the provider and not the consumer by necessity — often it is the provider, sometimes a governance authority, occasionally the Owner — but it is always a single resolvable party who is accountable for the promise: who answers when it is breached, who authorizes an amendment, who receives the escalation when the failure path terminates. The invariant that exactly one party is accountable exists because a contract owned by everyone is enforced by no one: with two owners, each defers to the other on breach, and the breach record accumulates against a party that does not exist. With no owner, a breach has no address, so the escalation path has no terminus and the estate's owner_queue receives an item with no assignee, which is an item that will not be worked. Ownership also carries the deprecation duty: a contract's owner is the party obliged to notice that the contract has outlived its purpose and to supersede it rather than leaving it ACTIVE and stale, because a stale ACTIVE contract is a promise nobody intends to keep and everyone still relies on.

5.6 Scope isolation between contracts. A contract's scope is a DAIF-01 scope value, and it bounds the surface over which the promise binds. The invariant is directional and strict: a contract binding at project scope may not silently bind at universal scope, and the resolution of an ambiguous scope is inward, to the narrowest defensible reach, exactly as DAIF-01 resolves an ambiguous unit. The reason is the same asymmetry: under-reach at worst omits a contract from a context it should have governed, an error whose correction is a deliberate widening; over-reach binds every unrelated context in the estate to a promise authored for one, an error discovered only after the leak has propagated and paid by a party who never saw the contract. Widening a contract's scope is itself an authority-bearing act, requiring authority proportional to the new reach: promoting a project contract to universal is a claim on the whole estate and must be made by a party entitled to make it. A contract may not acquire universal reach through an unfilled scope field, which is the silent path an untyped convention takes by default.

5.7 Scope and the two-party rule interacting. Part II restricted a contract to two parties while permitting a consumer class typed as a scope. The interaction with this Part is where that pays: a provider serving many consumers declares its consumer class as a scope value — every component within the power-pack scope, every agent within a project — and admission of a new consumer becomes a scope check rather than an amendment, so the provider's promise does not require re-authoring each time the estate grows. The invariant guarding this is that the consumer scope may never exceed the contract's binding scope: a contract scoped to a project cannot declare a universal consumer class, because it would then be promising to parties over whom its authority does not reach, and a promise to a party you cannot bind is a promise you cannot keep consistently. Scope thus governs both directions — the surface the promise touches and the population entitled to rely on it — and the two must be consistent or the contract is refused.

5.8 The overreach failure and its detonation profile. The characteristic failure of this Part is the overreaching contract that works for a long time. A contract authored under project authority, scoped by omission to universal, promising an effect the project genuinely delivers. Nothing breaks: the promise is kept, within the project, for months. Then a second project's component reads the register, finds a universal-scoped contract, relies on it, and receives nothing — because the provider never served that scope and never intended to. The breach record names the provider, who did nothing wrong; the fault is the contract's own (Part VI's contract-fault class), and the contract was defective at creation, not at execution. This is the deferred detonation the fabric convicts throughout: a defect that lies dormant precisely as long as the environments happen to agree, and fires the moment they diverge. The inward default of scope resolution and the authority-proportional widening rule are the two mechanisms that prevent it, and both operate at creation, because there is no cheap moment to catch it later.

5.9 Composition with the estate's existing authority mechanisms. Nothing in this Part is a new power structure. The ladder is DAIF-01's, which itself composes SQI-00's authorization levels, the hard-rule precedence, and DRK's limits on autonomous decision. The contract's contribution is that a promise now carries the rung under which it was made, so that a consumer reading it can rank it, a register admitting it can refuse an overreach, and a breach recorded against it can be delivered to a party who exists. The estate's one authority law governs its contracts as it governs its knowledge and its decisions, and the alternative — a contract layer with its own private notion of who may promise what — would be exactly the second source of truth that drifts from the first and collides with it under pressure.

PART V FINAL LAW. Authority decides who may create, amend, waive, or void a contract and caps what any contract may bind, so that a promise to override a hard constraint made by a party without the rung to override it is VOID at creation rather than breached at execution — refused at the register instead of discovered at the moment of maximum damage; ownership names exactly one accountable party, because a contract owned by everyone is enforced by no one and a breach with no address is an incident report rather than an enforcement; and scope binds the promise inward by default, since an under-reaching contract merely omits while an over-reaching one silently binds every unrelated context in the estate to a promise authored for exactly one.

PART VI — BREACH: DETECTION, CLASSIFICATION, AND RESPONSE

6.1 Mission. A promise that cannot be breached is not a promise, and a breach that cannot be detected, classified, and recorded teaches the estate nothing. This Part defines the breach lifecycle: the four ways a breach is detected, the four fault classes into which every breach resolves, the rule that classification always precedes response, the five-rung response ladder, and the recording obligation that makes a chronic breacher visible. The Part's governing insight is that response without classification is worse than no response at all, because a response applied to the wrong party trains the wrong system to change, and a system that has been trained to change something that was never broken now carries a defect introduced by its own remediation.

6.2 The four detections. A breach is detected in exactly four ways, and a contract that cannot be breached by any of the four is a contract whose fields are underspecified. First, a postcondition evaluates false: the provider performed, the assertion was decided, and the decision was negative. This is the canonical detection and it requires nothing but the machinery of Part IV. Second, a timeout expires: the provider did not deliver within the bound, and the absence of a result is itself a breach, because Part VII holds that an unbounded promise is not a promise. Third, a precondition is violated by the caller: the consumer supplied a state the provider never undertook to handle, which is a breach by the consumer and is detected at the provider's boundary before performance. Fourth, an output fails its type contract: the returned object does not satisfy the declared output type, which is detected by the consumer at the boundary of receipt and is the one detection the provider cannot suppress. The four are complementary, not alternatives, and a well-typed contract is checked by all four on every interaction it survives.

6.3 The four fault classes. Every detected breach resolves into exactly one of four classes, and the classification is a typed decision with its own provenance. Caller-fault: a precondition was violated, so the provider was never obliged and the consumer proceeded outside the bargain. Provider-fault: the preconditions held, the provider performed, and a postcondition is false — the promise was made, triggered, and not kept. Environment-fault: neither party is at fault; the substrate failed, the network dropped, the host refused, the transport dropped a frame. Contract-fault: the contract itself was wrong — an unsatisfiable postcondition, an overreaching scope, a precondition no caller could ever satisfy, a guarantee the provider was never capable of making. The classes are exhaustive and mutually exclusive by construction, and the resolution rule when two seem to apply is that contract-fault outranks the others: if the contract was defective, the parties' behavior is being judged against a broken standard and no conclusion about them is admissible.

6.4 Environment-fault and the canonical misattribution. Environment-fault deserves its own treatment because misattributing it is one of the estate's named errors. When a failure originates in the substrate — the bridge dropped, the host was saturated, the remote channel reset — and the estate attributes it to the product, two harms follow. The immediate harm is a false defect: an engineer changes code that was correct, and the change is a fresh defect introduced under the belief that it is a fix. The systemic harm is worse: the environment's real defect is never addressed, because the incident is closed against the wrong party, so it recurs and each recurrence produces another false fix. This is the environment qualification SQI already enforces on its measurements, composed here: before a breach is classified as provider-fault, the environment must be qualified, and a failure on an unqualified environment is classified environment-fault or, when the environment cannot be assessed at all, UNKNOWN — never provider-fault by default. The default direction matters because the cheap default is always to blame the last component to touch the work, and the last component to touch the work is almost never the substrate.

6.5 Why classification precedes response. The response ladder is potent and each rung acts on a party. Retry acts on the provider. Compensation acts on the effect. Escalation acts on the owner. Rollback acts on the world. Voiding acts on the contract. Apply any of them to the wrong fault class and the effect is not merely wasted — it is corrupting. Respond to a provider-fault as if it were a caller-fault and the consumer is hardened against an input it was entitled to supply, so the estate accumulates defensive code protecting against legitimate behavior while the real defect persists. Respond to a caller-fault as if it were a provider-fault and the provider widens its preconditions to accept states it cannot actually handle, converting a clean refusal into a silent misbehavior. Respond to an environment-fault as a provider-fault and both the false fix and the unaddressed substrate defect follow. Respond to a contract-fault as anything else and both parties are punished for a standard that was never satisfiable. Classification is therefore not a bookkeeping step before the real work; it is the step that decides which system is going to change, and changing the wrong system is how an estate accrues defects while believing it is fixing them.

6.6 The response ladder. Five rungs, applied in order of increasing cost and irreversibility, each gated by fault class. Retry: permitted only for environment-fault, and only under the pivot conditions of Part VII — an identical retry against a provider-fault is a loop, not a fix. Compensate: an offsetting action that neutralizes a partial effect, applicable when the provider produced something the consumer must not be left holding, and the primary rung for provider-fault with irreversible partial effects. Escalate: hand the breach to the accountable owner, terminating at owner_queue when the estate cannot resolve it, and the mandatory rung for any contract-fault, because neither party can repair a defective standard by acting under it. Roll back: undo the effect and return the world to its prior state, available only where the effect is reversible and the contract declared the path. Void: declare the contract unfit and refuse further reliance on it, reserved for contract-fault and requiring the authority of Part V. The ladder's discipline is that a party may not skip to a higher rung to avoid a recorded breach at a lower one: a provider that voids its own contract after failing it has escaped, not resolved, and voiding requires an authority the provider does not hold over its own promise.

6.7 The recording obligation. Every breach is recorded, without exception, including breaches that were immediately resolved, breaches classified as environment-fault, and breaches that a waiver excused. The record holds the contract identity and version, the detection (which of the four), the fault class and its provenance, the response applied, and the outcome. The obligation is absolute for one reason: an unrecorded breach teaches the system nothing and lets a chronic breacher look reliable. A provider that fails one interaction in twenty, each failure absorbed by a helpful retry and never recorded, presents to the estate as a provider that has never failed — and the estate builds on it accordingly, until the day the retry does not help. The record is what converts a scatter of individual incidents into a rate, and a rate is the only thing that can be reasoned about. Breach records publish on PM-03 under the typed event schema, are held for pattern analysis by CEPS, and feed the estate's existing recurrence machinery: a breach class that recurs is a candidate for a UKDL rule, and a recurrence with real damage is a candidate for a hard rule, which is the estate's existing bug-to-rule ladder consumed rather than forked.

6.8 Adversarial cases. Three deserve naming. The self-absolving classifier: the provider classifies its own breaches and discovers, over time, that most of them are environment-faults. The defense is the separation-of-powers rule of Part IV applied to classification — a provider may propose a fault class, but a class that absolves the provider requires evidence from an evaluator the provider does not control, and an environment-fault claim with no environment evidence is UNKNOWN, not environment-fault. The breach-by-waiver: a provider whose contract is repeatedly waived by a sympathetic authority never accumulates a breach record. The defense is that a waiver is itself recorded as an event against the contract, and a waiver rate is a metric, so a contract waived five times has a visible history even if its breach count is zero. The definition-shrinking provider: a provider that responds to breaches by narrowing its postconditions until nothing it does can be false. The defense is that an amendment is an authority-bearing act producing a new version, and a version history showing monotonically weakening guarantees is itself the signal — a promise that has been narrowed until it cannot be breached has become a hope again, and Part II refuses hopes at the register.

6.9 Metrics and anti-Goodhart. Four metrics with four countermetrics. Breach detection latency (time from unmet promise to recorded breach) is countered by the blind-gate check: a latency of zero because no detection exists is not excellence, so latency is only meaningful against a contract whose evaluators have been observed to refuse. Breach rate per contract is countered by the waiver rate and the amendment-weakening rate, because both can drive breach rate to zero without any improvement in delivery. Fault-class distribution is countered by the evidence rate on absolving classifications, because a distribution dominated by environment-fault is either a genuinely bad substrate or a self-absolving classifier, and only the evidence rate distinguishes them. And contract coverage — the fraction of the estate's real interactions that have a typed contract at all — is countered by the postcondition falsifiability rate, because coverage is trivially maximized by admitting contracts whose postconditions cannot be false, which is coverage of nothing.

PART VI FINAL LAW. A breach is detected four ways — a postcondition false, a timeout expired, a precondition violated by the caller, an output failing its type — and resolves into exactly one of four faults: caller, provider, environment, or contract; classification always precedes response, because retry, compensation, escalation, rollback, and voiding each act on a party, and a response applied to the wrong fault class trains the wrong system to change, which is how an estate accrues defects while believing it is fixing them — most canonically by attributing a substrate failure to the product and shipping a false fix over a defect that was never there; and every breach is recorded without exception, because an unrecorded breach teaches the estate nothing and lets a chronic breacher present as perfectly reliable right up until the day the helpful retry stops helping.

PART VII — FAILURE SEMANTICS: TIMEOUT, RETRY, ROLLBACK, COMPENSATION

7.1 Mission. Anyone can specify a contract's happy path; the hard part of a contract is what happens when things go wrong, and the fields that state it are the ones authors are most tempted to leave thin. This Part gives the four failure-semantics fields their full mechanism: timeout as a first-class term of the promise, retry policy with its mandatory pivot conditions, idempotency as the precondition of safe retry, and the rollback-versus-compensation distinction that culminates in the Part's hardest invariant — a contract with irreversible effects that declares no compensation path may not be executed at all. The Part's stance is that a contract is judged by its failure path, because the happy path is what the author imagined and the failure path is what the estate will actually live through.

7.2 Timeout as a term of the promise. Every contract carries a timeout, and the field is mandatory for the ACTIVE state. The reasoning is direct: an unbounded promise is not a promise, it is a hang. A consumer waiting on a provider that never answers has received neither a delivery nor a breach; it has received silence, which it cannot act on, and the estate's own doctrine — hard-won across many dead sessions where a turn closed on a passive wait for something that would never arrive — forbids exactly that state. The timeout converts silence into a typed event: at the bound, the absence of a result becomes a detected breach with a fault class, and the consumer's failure path activates. The invariant is that the bound is stated as a wall-clock quantity on the contract rather than as a runtime configuration on the provider, because a bound the consumer cannot read is a bound the consumer cannot plan around. And the bound must be finite: a contract stating an infinite or unspecified timeout is refused at the register, because it has re-created the hang under the appearance of a field.

7.3 Retry policy and the trap it exists to close. Retry is the most-reached-for response and the most misapplied. The trap is precise: retrying an identical failing call is a loop, not a fix. If the provider failed because a postcondition was unsatisfiable given the input, the same input will fail again, and again, and the retry budget converts one failure into N failures plus a wasted budget and a delayed detection. The estate's two-consecutive-failures law names the same shape at the tool level, and the contract layer inherits it as a hard requirement on the field: a retry policy must specify PIVOT conditions, not just retry counts. A pivot condition names what must be DIFFERENT for a retry to be legitimate — a re-qualified environment, a corrected input, a different code path, a different provider — and a retry attempted with nothing different is refused by the policy itself. The default policy, when a contract states only a count, is a count of zero: absent stated pivot conditions, no retry is authorized, because an unconditioned retry is the loop the law forbids.

7.4 Fault class governs retry eligibility. The retry policy is gated by Part VI's classification, and the gating is asymmetric. Environment-fault is the only class for which an identical retry is ever legitimate, and even then only after the environment has been re-qualified — a retry against a substrate that is still failing is the same loop wearing a different hat. Caller-fault permits a retry only with a corrected precondition, which is by definition not an identical call. Provider-fault permits no identical retry at all: the provider performed and did not keep its promise, and nothing about repeating the request changes the provider's capability. Contract-fault permits no retry under any condition, because the standard is defective and every attempt is being judged against it. This gating is what makes retry a mechanism rather than a reflex, and it is why the response ladder of Part VI places retry at its lowest rung and behind the classification step: retry is cheap only when it is correct, and when it is incorrect it is the most expensive rung on the ladder, because it delays detection while consuming budget.

7.5 Idempotency as the precondition of safe retry. A retry is safe only if the operation is idempotent — if performing it twice leaves the world in the same state as performing it once. This is not an implementation nicety; it is a contract term, and the contract must declare it. A non-idempotent operation retried after a timeout is the estate's most dangerous ambiguity: the consumer does not know whether the first attempt performed and the answer was lost, or never performed at all, and the two cases demand opposite responses. Declaring idempotency resolves the ambiguity by removing it — if the operation is idempotent, the consumer may retry without knowing which case obtains. If it is not, the contract must instead declare a status-check assertion the consumer can evaluate to determine whether the first attempt landed, and a non-idempotent contract that declares neither idempotency nor a status check may not be retried at all, only escalated. The invariant closes the double-effect breach: the same effect applied twice because a timeout hid a success.

7.6 Rollback versus compensation. Rollback undoes the effect: the world is returned to its prior state, and the interaction is as if it had not occurred. Compensation offsets the effect: the world cannot be returned, so a second, distinct action is taken whose purpose is to neutralize the first's consequences. The distinction is not stylistic — it is a fact about the effect, and it is the contract's job to state which applies. An effect on a private working state is usually reversible and takes a rollback path. An effect that crossed a boundary — a message published to the bus and consumed, a write that landed on a live substrate, a session that was ended, a decision another actor already acted upon — is typically irreversible, and the honest term is compensation. The failure the distinction prevents is the false rollback: a contract that declares a rollback path for an effect that cannot actually be undone, so the failure path executes, reports success, and leaves the world in a state the consumer believes has been reverted. A false rollback is worse than no rollback, because no rollback at least leaves the consumer knowing the effect persists.

7.7 The compensation invariant. Here is the Part's hardest rule and the one that binds hardest at creation: a contract with irreversible effects MUST declare a compensation path before it may be executed at all. Not before it fails — before it runs. The reasoning is that the compensation path cannot be authored in the moment of failure: at that moment the effect is already in the world, the estate is under pressure, the accountable owner is not necessarily present, and whatever offsetting action is invented under those conditions is an improvisation with no review. Authoring it at creation, in calm, with the authority that created the contract, is the only time it can be done well. The register enforces the rule mechanically: a contract whose declared effects include any irreversible one and whose compensation field is empty is refused ACTIVE status. The refusal is the point — an irreversible effect with no offsetting action is a decision the estate cannot walk back and must not take by accident, and the estate's own deploy and destructive-command doctrine already applies exactly this shape at the command surface, requiring a recorded backup or an explicit authorization before an unrecoverable action is dispatched.

7.8 Partial failure and the honest halting state. The hardest real case is neither clean success nor clean failure but the partial: the provider performed some of its effects and then failed. The contract must declare, for every effect, whether it persists on failure. A contract that says nothing here leaves the consumer facing a world in an unknown state, and an unknown state is one the consumer cannot safely act on in either direction — it can neither proceed as if the work happened nor as if it did not. Two disciplines address it. The first is atomicity where it is available: state the effects as a group that lands entirely or not at all, so partial failure is impossible by construction, and prefer this whenever the substrate permits it. The second, where atomicity is not available, is the declared halting state: the contract names, for each stage, exactly what persists if the provider fails at that stage, so a consumer facing a failure can evaluate a stage assertion, learn which halting state obtains, and take the compensation appropriate to it. What is forbidden is the undeclared partial, which is a promise that produces an unknowable world.

7.9 Escalation as the terminus and the anti-hang closer. Every failure path terminates. When retry is not eligible, compensation is not sufficient, and rollback is not available, the path ends at escalation to the accountable owner, and when the estate cannot resolve it internally, at owner_queue. The invariant is that the terminus is always reached in bounded time — the sum of the timeout, the authorized retries, and the compensation window is itself bounded, and the contract states the bound. This composes the estate's own anti-hang doctrine at the contract layer: no path may end in a wait, no failure may end in silence, and a consumer whose provider has exhausted every rung receives an escalation event rather than an absence. An escalation is an actionable outcome; an absence is not. The whole failure semantics exists to guarantee that a consumer, at every moment, holds either a delivery, a breach, or an escalation — and never the fourth thing, which is nothing at all.

7.10 Metrics and countermetrics for the failure path. Three pairs. Mean time to terminus (from first failure to a delivery, breach, or escalation) is countered by the premature-escalation rate, because a path that escalates instantly minimizes the metric while dumping every failure on the accountable owner. Retry success rate is countered by the identical-retry rate: a high retry success achieved by retrying identical calls against a flaky substrate is hiding a substrate defect the estate must see. And compensation coverage — the fraction of irreversible effects with a declared compensation path — is countered by the compensation-exercise rate, because a compensation path that has never once been executed is UNVERIFIED in exactly the sense Part IV means, and a failure path that has never run is a hypothesis about what will happen, not a guarantee.

PART VII FINAL LAW. A contract is judged by its failure path, so timeout is a first-class term because an unbounded promise is not a promise but a hang; retry is authorized only by fault class and only with stated PIVOT conditions, because an identical retry against a failing call is a loop rather than a fix and an unconditioned retry policy defaults to a retry count of zero; idempotency or a status-check assertion is the precondition of any safe retry, since a timeout that hides a success turns a retry into a double effect; rollback undoes and compensation offsets, and declaring a rollback for an effect that cannot be undone is worse than declaring nothing; and a contract whose effects are irreversible and whose compensation path is empty is refused execution outright — not refused at failure, refused at creation — because the offsetting action for an unrecoverable act can only be authored in calm, and never invented in the moment the estate discovers it needed one.
