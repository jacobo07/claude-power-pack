DAIF-04 — INSTITUTIONAL CONTRACT FABRIC

PART I — THESIS: THE UNTYPED INTERACTION SURFACE

1.1 Mission. The Power Pack is not a collection of independent systems; it is an estate of systems that constantly act on one another. An agent dispatches another agent and consumes what comes back. A hook admits or refuses a write that some other component depends on having landed. A dataset cites a sibling dataset and reasons over guarantees that sibling is assumed to hold. A command invokes an engine and treats its output as authoritative. A gate declares a body of work admissible and everything downstream proceeds on that admission. Every one of those interactions is a promise: one party undertakes to deliver something, and another party proceeds as if the delivery is real. DAIF-04 exists because none of those promises is currently a typed object. They live in prose — a paragraph inside a dataset labelled as its integration contract, an assertion inside a code comment, an authority implied by which configuration section a gate happens to sit in — and prose is not checkable. The mission of this dataset is narrow and load-bearing: to make the estate's interaction surface typed, so that what a component promised and whether it delivered are questions a machine can ask and answer, rather than questions a human reconstructs after the damage has already been paid.

1.2 What a prose convention actually is. A prose convention is a promise recorded in a medium that cannot evaluate it. It has four properties, all fatal at scale. It cannot be checked: no runtime can read "this engine returns a well-formed decision record" and confirm the returned object satisfies it, because the sentence names no evaluable assertion and no evaluator. It cannot be versioned: the sentence has no version field, so when a consumer written against last quarter's understanding meets a provider that changed its output shape, nothing anywhere records that the understanding changed. It cannot be breached against: breach requires a stated postcondition to be found false, and a paragraph states no postcondition an evaluator can find false, so the provider can fail completely without any mechanism concluding that it failed. And it drifts silently: the prose is written once and the system it describes changes weekly, so the paragraph becomes a description of a system that no longer exists, still read and still trusted. The convention degrades from a promise into a fiction with no event marking the transition, which is precisely why nobody notices.

1.3 The cardinal consequence. From those four properties one consequence follows, and it is the founding argument of this dataset: without typed contracts, a breach is discovered by its damage rather than by its detection. Detection is a mechanism that evaluates a stated promise at the moment of delivery and concludes true or false. Damage is what happens when no such mechanism exists — the unmet promise propagates, a consumer builds on an output it believed satisfied a guarantee it never actually satisfied, a second consumer builds on the first, and the failure surfaces many hops away from its origin, disguised as a fault of the last component to touch it. The estate has paid this repeatedly: a gate believed to hold an authority it never held; an engine believed to write a field no producer ever populated; a component built, unit-green, and cited by name while nothing in the estate ever called it. Each was a promise that was never a contract, and each was discovered by damage. Worse, damage arrives on the schedule that suits it least: an unchecked promise holds fine while the parties happen to agree, and detonates the first time they diverge, which is exactly when the estate is under pressure and least able to absorb a surprise.

1.4 What a contract is. A contract, in this dataset, is a typed, authored, versioned promise between exactly two parties — a provider and a consumer — carrying evaluable preconditions the consumer must satisfy before the provider is obliged to perform, evaluable postconditions the provider guarantees afterward, an authority that entitles the promise, an owner who is accountable for it, a scope within which it binds, and a defined failure path that says what happens when the promise is not kept. Each of those seven elements is a DAIF-01 typed object rather than a sentence: the authority is a DAIF-01 authority value on the estate's one ladder, the scope is a DAIF-01 scope value on the estate's one nested reach, the validity is a DAIF-01 validity window, the confidence and provenance are DAIF-01 fields. DAIF-04 does not invent a parallel type system; it composes the sealed one and adds exactly one new object — the contract — whose fields are typed by DAIF-01 and whose evaluations are evidenced under the estate's existing reality supremacy. The contract is the smallest thing that turns an interaction from a habit into an obligation.

1.5 What DAIF-04 is not. Three exclusions bound this dataset and are enforced as hard limits on its own construction. It is not a message bus: the estate already has PM-03 for publication between components, and forking a second bus would create precisely the duplicate this fabric's constitution convicts. It is not an event log: CEPS already records events, and the hook chain already carries the synchronous admission signal that gates writes. It is not a scheduler: nothing in this dataset decides when a component runs. DAIF-04 absorbs the event candidate from the corpus map on exactly one axis — the typed event schema, which is a contract about what an emission must contain for a subscriber to consume it safely — and on no other. Transport belongs to PM-03, CEPS, and the hook chain, permanently. The typed schema without the transport is the whole absorption: a contract says what a well-formed emission holds and who may rely on which of its fields; the carriage of that emission from publisher to subscriber is a solved problem and stays solved where it is.

1.6 Why typing the surface is the highest-leverage move available. The estate has already typed its knowledge (DAIF-01), and is typing its representations, its fidelity budget, and its obligations. Those are all typings of things a system holds. The interaction surface is the one place where two systems meet, and it is therefore the one place where a defect crosses a boundary. A defect inside a component is contained by that component's own gates; a defect that crosses a boundary is a defect the receiving component's gates were never designed to catch, because the receiving component trusted the promise. Typing the boundary is thus worth more per unit of effort than typing anything on either side of it: it converts the estate's most common failure — a component behaving reasonably on an input the provider never promised to give it — from an unattributable mystery into a breach with a fault class, a responsible party, and a recorded event. The thin-dataset discipline follows directly: DAIF-04's value is the contract object and its lifecycle, not a re-narration of the systems it binds, and any Part that re-describes done_gate or spec_gate rather than typing the promise those gates make is padding and is rejected.

1.7 The two failure directions this dataset closes. Prose conventions fail in two directions and both are load-bearing. In the optimistic direction, a consumer trusts a promise that was never made — it reads an integration paragraph, infers a guarantee the provider never intended, and builds on it; the provider then changes freely, correctly, and within its own rights, and the consumer breaks without either party having done anything wrong, because there was no contract to constrain the change. In the pessimistic direction, a consumer defensively re-validates everything it receives, duplicating the provider's own checks, which is the redundant-verification tax that makes an estate slow and its components mutually distrustful. A typed contract closes both: the consumer knows exactly what it may rely on and validates nothing more, and the provider knows exactly what it may change without breaking anyone. Trust becomes bounded rather than blind or absent, and bounded trust is the only kind that scales past a handful of components.

1.8 The dataset's own contract. This corpus is bound by what it argues. It promises a typed contract object with an evaluable lifecycle (Part II), a taxonomy that names every interaction class the estate actually runs (Part III), a checkable pre/postcondition semantics with an evidence requirement (Part IV), an authority and scope model composed from DAIF-01 rather than forked (Part V), a breach model with fault classification preceding response (Part VI), and a failure semantics covering timeout, retry, rollback, and compensation (Part VII). It promises to reference done_gate, spec_gate, PM-03, CEPS, the hook chain, SQI, DRK, ACIS, CO-12, UKDL, GK, and owner_queue by name and never to re-narrate them. And it declares its own blind spot, in the manner Part III will make mandatory for every evaluator: DAIF-04 types the promise and detects the breach, but it cannot make a badly-designed component good, and a perfectly-typed contract over a wrong guarantee is a wrong guarantee that is now precisely stated.

PART I FINAL LAW. The estate's systems already promise one another everything and type none of it, so its interactions are governed by prose that cannot be checked, cannot be versioned, cannot be breached against, and drifts silently as the systems beneath it change — which guarantees that every unkept promise is discovered by its damage, arriving at the worst possible moment and disguised as someone else's fault; DAIF-04 closes that surface by making the promise a typed, authored, versioned object with evaluable preconditions, evaluable postconditions, an accountable owner, and a defined failure path, and it closes it without building a second transport, because the bus, the event log, and the hook chain already exist and forking them would be the duplication this fabric exists to convict.

PART II — THE CONTRACT OBJECT

2.1 Mission. This Part defines the object. A contract is a single typed record, addressable by identity, holding every field required to state a promise, evaluate it, version it, and fail from it safely. The object's design principle is that every field exists because its absence causes a specific, nameable breakage — a field that breaks nothing when omitted is not a field, it is decoration, and the thin-dataset discipline removes it. What follows names each field, what it holds, its invariant, and the failure that its absence produces. The fields divide into five groups: identity and parties, the promise itself, governance, evaluation, and failure semantics. Every typed value in the object draws from DAIF-01's type system — authority from the authority ladder, scope from the nested scope ladder, validity from the temporal types, confidence and provenance from the epistemic fields — and DAIF-04 defines no parallel vocabulary for any of them.

2.2 Identity and parties. A contract carries an identity that is stable across versions, so that a promise can be tracked as it evolves rather than appearing as a sequence of unrelated records, and it carries exactly two parties: a provider, who undertakes to perform, and a consumer, who is entitled to rely. The invariant is that both parties are named, resolvable references to real components in the estate's capability graph — GK coordinates, not descriptions — because a party that cannot be resolved cannot be held to anything. The two-party restriction is deliberate: a promise made to everyone in general is enforced by no one in particular, and a contract with three parties is two contracts that have not yet been separated. When a provider genuinely serves many consumers, the correct model is one contract with a consumer class typed as a scope rather than a list, so that the reach of the promise is a DAIF-01 scope value and admission of a new consumer is a scope check rather than an amendment. Absent identity, versioning is impossible; absent resolvable parties, breach has no defendant and no plaintiff, and a breach with no defendant is an incident report, not an enforcement.

2.3 The promise: preconditions, inputs, postconditions, outputs. The promise proper is four fields. Preconditions state what must be true before the provider is obliged to perform — the consumer's side of the bargain. Inputs type what the consumer supplies. Postconditions state what the provider guarantees to be true afterward. Outputs type what the provider returns. The distinction between inputs and preconditions is not pedantry: inputs are the shape of what arrives, preconditions are the state of the world that must hold for the arrival to be actionable, and a component that validates shape but not state is the component that accepts a well-formed request to act on a thing that does not exist. Likewise, outputs are the shape of what returns and postconditions are the state of the world the return implies. The invariant binding all four is that each is an evaluable assertion — a typed predicate an evaluator can decide — and never a sentence. Part IV develops the evaluation semantics; the object model's requirement is only that the four fields be populated with assertions rather than descriptions, because an assertion can be false and a description cannot.

2.4 The rule that a contract without a postcondition is not a contract. This is the object's central invariant and it governs the whole dataset. A contract whose postcondition field is empty, or filled with an unfalsifiable sentence, promises nothing: there is no proposition that could be found false, so nothing can ever be said to have been delivered, and nothing can ever be said to have been breached. Such a record is not a contract but a hope — a statement of intent with the enforcement surface of a wish. The estate has held many of these without noticing, because a hope reads exactly like a contract when both are written in prose. The invariant is enforced at creation, not at execution: a contract object with no evaluable postcondition is refused admission to the register, in the same way DAIF-01 refuses a unit whose type demands a field it does not carry. The refusal is the point. A promise that cannot fail cannot be relied on either, because reliance is precisely the expectation that a specific thing will be true, and if no specific thing is stated, the consumer's reliance is on its own imagination.

2.5 Governance fields: authority, ownership, scope, version, deprecation. Five fields make a contract governable. Authority is a DAIF-01 authority value naming the entitlement under which the promise is made, and it caps what the contract may bind — Part V develops this. Ownership names exactly one accountable party, because a contract owned by everyone is enforced by no one and a contract with no owner has no address to which a breach can be delivered. Scope is a DAIF-01 scope value bounding where the contract binds — project, repo, feature, power-pack, universal — and its invariant is that a contract cannot bind outside its declared scope, so a promise made for one project cannot silently govern another. Version records the contract's revision, and its invariant is that any change to a precondition, postcondition, or output type is a new version, never an in-place edit, because an in-place edit is exactly the silent drift the whole dataset exists to prevent. Deprecation policy names how the contract ends: the notice a consumer receives, the window in which both versions hold, and the terminal date. Absent version, the drift is invisible; absent deprecation policy, the drift is visible but the consumer discovers it as a sudden removal, which is a breach dressed as a release.

2.6 Evaluation fields: evidence requirements, done-gates, compatibility guarantees. Three fields make the promise verifiable rather than merely stated. Evidence requirements name what counts as proof that a postcondition holds — an observed exit code, a file that exists on disk, a query that returns a row, a recorded gate result — and the invariant is the estate's reality supremacy: a postcondition claimed without its required evidence is UNVERIFIED, which is a distinct state from satisfied and from breached, and it is never silently promoted to satisfied. The done-gate field names the evaluator that decides satisfaction, composing done_gate where the estate's existing gate already covers the surface rather than defining a rival evaluator. Compatibility guarantees state what the provider promises not to change without a version bump: which output fields are load-bearing, which are advisory, which may be added freely. Absent evidence requirements, a postcondition is satisfied by assertion and the contract becomes a self-certification, which is the failure Part IV convicts by separation of powers. Absent compatibility guarantees, every provider change is a potential breach and no provider can evolve without fear, so the estate freezes.

2.7 Failure-semantics fields: failure mode, timeout, retry policy, escalation, rollback, compensation. Six fields state what happens when the promise is not kept, and Part VII develops each. Failure semantics names how the provider fails — whether a failure is total or partial, whether partial effects persist, and whether the consumer may assume nothing happened. Timeout bounds the promise in time, and its invariant is absolute: an unbounded promise is not a promise but a hang, and the estate's own hard-won doctrine forbids ending a turn in an unbounded wait. Retry policy states under what conditions a retry is permitted and — critically — the pivot conditions under which retry is forbidden. Escalation names the path when neither performance nor retry resolves the matter, terminating at owner_queue when the estate cannot decide alone. Rollback names how the effect is undone. Compensation names the offsetting action when undo is impossible. The binding invariant: a contract whose effects are irreversible and which declares no compensation path is refused execution, because an irreversible effect with no offsetting action is a decision the estate cannot walk back and must not take by accident.

2.8 Lifecycle state. The contract holds a lifecycle state, and the states are: DRAFTED (authored, not yet admitted), ACTIVE (admitted, binding), SUSPENDED (temporarily not binding, by an authority entitled to suspend), BREACHED (a breach has been recorded against the current version and not yet resolved), SUPERSEDED (a later version binds and this one is retained only for reading), and VOID (the contract was invalid at creation and never bound at all — Part V shows how an authority overreach produces this at creation rather than at execution). The invariants are that transitions are one-way except SUSPENDED, that only the accountable owner or a higher authority may suspend or supersede, and that a BREACHED contract is not automatically VOID — a breached promise is still a promise, and voiding it on breach would let a provider escape its obligation by failing it. Absent lifecycle state, a retired contract is indistinguishable from a live one and consumers keep relying on promises that no party still intends to keep, which is drift with extra steps.

2.9 The register and the object's invariants as a whole. Contracts are held in a register keyed by identity and version, with the estate's freshness and identity guarantees applying to it as they apply to any register. Three whole-object invariants hold across every field. First, no contract is ACTIVE without an evaluable postcondition, an accountable owner, a scope, an authority, and a timeout — the five without which it is unenforceable. Second, no contract may be edited in place once ACTIVE; a change is a version. Third, every evaluation of a contract, satisfied or breached or unverified, is recorded, because an evaluation that leaves no record teaches the estate nothing and lets a chronic breacher present as reliable. The register is a producer as well as a holder: it publishes contract events on PM-03 under the typed event schema this dataset defines, so subscribers learn of a new version, a suspension, or a breach through the transport that already exists.

PART II FINAL LAW. A contract is one typed record holding two resolvable parties, evaluable preconditions and postconditions, typed inputs and outputs, an authority, exactly one accountable owner, a bounded scope, a version and a deprecation policy, evidence requirements bound to a named evaluator, compatibility guarantees, and a full failure semantics of timeout, retry, escalation, rollback, and compensation — and its central invariant is that a contract with no evaluable postcondition is not a contract but a hope, refused at creation rather than discovered at breach, because a promise that can never be found false can never be relied upon, only imagined.

PART III — THE CONTRACT TAXONOMY

3.1 Mission. The contract object of Part II is one shape; the estate's interactions are many kinds. This Part names the kinds, and for each one names its parties, its characteristic promise, and its characteristic breach — because a taxonomy that does not name the breach is a list of nouns, and the breach is what tells a designer which fields of the object carry the weight for that kind. The taxonomy is closed at nine kinds, and closure is deliberate: an open taxonomy invites a tenth kind for every awkward case, and the tenth kind is where untyped prose re-enters through the back door. An interaction that does not fit one of the nine is either two interactions that have not been separated, or a sign the taxonomy is genuinely wrong and must be amended by an authority entitled to amend it — never accommodated by an unclassified exception.

3.2 Agent-to-agent. Parties: a dispatching agent and a dispatched agent. The characteristic promise is what the dispatched agent undertakes to return — a typed result of a stated shape, within a stated budget, having touched only a stated surface, and having verified rather than assumed the claims it reports. The characteristic breach is the confident-but-unverified return: the dispatched agent reports completion of work it did not observe, and the dispatcher, having no postcondition to evaluate, relays the claim upward as fact. This is the estate's most expensive breach class because it launders an unverified claim through a boundary that looks like verification. The contract's load-bearing fields here are the postcondition (what the return must assert and evidence), the evidence requirement (the dispatched agent's claim of a passing gate must carry the observed result, not the intent to run it), and the scope (an agent that touched files outside the promised surface has breached even if its stated result is correct, because the consumer relied on the containment).

3.3 Dataset-to-dataset. Parties: a citing corpus and a cited corpus. The characteristic promise is what one corpus guarantees another may rely on — that a named law will not be silently reversed, that a taxonomy declared closed will not gain a member without a version, that a term defined in one dataset means the same thing when a sibling composes it. The characteristic breach is silent semantic drift: the cited corpus refines a definition for good reasons, the citing corpus continues to reason over the old meaning, and both remain internally coherent while the composition between them is now false. The load-bearing fields are the version (a definitional change is a new version, never an edit), the compatibility guarantee (which definitions are load-bearing for citers and which are illustrative), and the deprecation policy (a retired law gets a notice window, not a deletion). This is the contract that makes composition safe, and it is why DAIF-04 composes DAIF-01's types rather than restating them: the citation is the contract.

3.4 Command-to-engine. Parties: a command surface a human or agent invokes, and the engine it drives. The characteristic promise runs in both directions: the command promises to supply well-formed, authorized inputs, and the engine promises a typed result, a bounded runtime, and a defined behavior on partial failure. The characteristic breach is the silent no-op — the engine runs, exits zero, and does nothing, because the input it received was well-formed but semantically empty, and no postcondition asserted that the run must have produced an effect. The load-bearing fields are the postcondition stated as an effect rather than an exit code (a gate that runs is not a gate that acted), the timeout, and the failure semantics, because a command whose engine half-completed and returned zero has left the estate in a state the caller believes is clean.

3.5 Hook-to-runtime. Parties: a hook and the runtime whose actions it gates. The characteristic promise is a blocking guarantee: this hook, on this event, for this class of action, will refuse the action when its condition fires, and the refusal will actually prevent the action rather than merely record disapproval. The characteristic breach is the swallowed block — the hook fires, forms a refusal, and the refusal is discarded somewhere in the chain, so a gate that is believed to hold nothing through has been holding nothing at all. The estate has paid this exact breach. The load-bearing fields are the authority (a hook's entitlement to refuse is a DAIF-01 authority value, not an implication of where it sits in a configuration file), the postcondition stated as an observed refusal rather than an emitted verdict, and the evidence requirement — a hook's blocking guarantee is UNVERIFIED until a refusal has been empirically observed to prevent the action, which makes an occasional deliberate breach attempt part of the contract rather than a violation of it.

3.6 Engine-to-engine. Parties: two engines composed in a chain. The characteristic promise is the handoff: the upstream engine guarantees the shape, completeness, and freshness of what it passes, and the downstream engine guarantees it will not silently accept a degraded input. The characteristic breach is the starved field — a field the downstream engine reads and no upstream engine ever writes, so the consumer is coded, tested, live, and dead, reading null forever without a single error. The load-bearing fields are the output type (every field a downstream engine reads must appear in an upstream engine's postcondition as a field it undertakes to populate) and the evidence requirement (a producer's promise to populate a field is UNVERIFIED until the field has been observed non-null across live records). This is the contract that converts an orphan field from an invisible dead path into a breach with a defendant.

3.7 Mission-to-runtime. Parties: an assembled mission — a unit of work with a stated goal and a bounded surface — and the runtime that executes it. The characteristic promise is that the runtime will execute the mission within its declared scope, budget, and authority, and will halt rather than exceed any of the three. The characteristic breach is the scope creep that succeeds: the runtime completes the mission and more, touching surfaces the mission never authorized, and because the mission's stated goal was achieved, no evaluator flags the unauthorized reach. The load-bearing fields are scope (bounding the surface the runtime may touch) and the postcondition stated as goal-achieved-and-nothing-else, because a postcondition that only asserts the goal cannot detect the excess. This contract composes DAIF-08's assembly and consumes DAIF-07's obligations; it does not re-derive either.

3.8 Memory and register contracts. Parties: a register — any component that holds state for later reading — and its readers. The characteristic promise is about freshness and identity: this register asserts that a record it returns is current as of a stated window, and that a record's identity is stable, so that two readings of the same key mean the same thing. The characteristic breach is the stale-read-as-current: the register returns a record that no longer reflects the world, the reader has no way to distinguish it from a fresh one, and it acts on a truth that has expired. The load-bearing fields are the validity window (a DAIF-01 temporal type on the record, not on the register), the postcondition stated as a freshness assertion, and the failure semantics for the unmeasurable case — a register that cannot determine a record's freshness must return UNKNOWN, never the record, because the fail-open direction here is silence and the fail-closed direction is a lie. Freshness composes the estate's existing GK freshness signals and DAIF-21's synchronization triggers rather than forking a rival staleness model.

3.9 Evaluator contracts and the negative clause. Parties: a gate — any evaluator that admits or refuses work — and everything downstream that relies on its admission. The characteristic promise is a coverage claim: this gate catches this class of defect. The characteristic breach is the false clean bill — the gate passes work that carries exactly the defect the estate believed it caught. And here the taxonomy imposes its sharpest requirement: an evaluator contract MUST declare its negative clause, an explicit statement of what the gate does NOT promise to catch. A gate that declares only its coverage will be trusted for coverage it never had, because a consumer reading a green result cannot distinguish "checked and clean" from "not checked at all" — both present as silence. The estate has been burned by exactly this shape: gates whose vocabulary could not recognize an idiom and therefore counted zero occurrences of it, returning a clean signal that was structurally incapable of being dirty. The negative clause converts that from an invisible blind spot into a declared one, and a declared blind spot is a backlog item; an undeclared one is a future incident. The load-bearing fields are the postcondition (what a pass asserts, stated narrowly), the negative clause (what a pass does not assert), and the evidence requirement that the gate has been observed to REFUSE at least once — a gate never observed to refuse is indistinguishable from an inert one, and its coverage claim is UNVERIFIED regardless of how many passes it has produced.

3.10 Governance contracts. Parties: a governing instrument — a hard rule, a doctrine, a sealed law — and every actor bound by it. The characteristic promise is a constraint the estate undertakes to honor: this action will not be taken under these conditions, this claim will not be made without this evidence, this bypass requires this authority. The characteristic breach is the quiet exception — an actor takes the constrained action under a rationale the rule never admitted, and because the rule was prose, the rationale is indistinguishable from a legitimate exception. The load-bearing fields are the authority (governance contracts sit at the top of DAIF-01's ladder and cannot be overridden by a lower rung), the override policy (naming the exact authority and the exact recorded phrase that permits a bypass, for exactly one occasion), and the breach record — a governance breach is always recorded, never absorbed, because a rule that is silently bypassed twice is not a rule. Governance contracts compose UKDL and the estate's hard-rule archive; DAIF-04 types the promise, it does not re-legislate the rules.

PART III FINAL LAW. The estate runs nine kinds of contract — agent-to-agent, dataset-to-dataset, command-to-engine, hook-to-runtime, engine-to-engine, mission-to-runtime, memory-and-register, evaluator, and governance — each with a characteristic promise and a characteristic breach that tells the designer which fields carry the weight, and the taxonomy's hardest clause is the evaluator's negative one: a gate that declares only what it catches will be trusted for coverage it never had, because a green result from a blind gate and a green result from a clean surface are the same silence, so every evaluator contract must state what it does NOT promise to catch and must have been observed at least once to refuse.

PART IV — PRECONDITIONS, POSTCONDITIONS, AND EVIDENCE

4.1 Mission. This Part builds the checkable core. Everything else in the contract object is metadata about a promise; the preconditions and postconditions are the promise. Their design requirement is singular: each must be a typed, evaluable assertion — a proposition an evaluator can decide as true, false, or undecidable — and never a sentence a human interprets. The difference is not stylistic. A sentence admits interpretation, and interpretation under pressure always resolves in favor of whoever needs the answer to be convenient. An assertion admits only evaluation, and evaluation under pressure resolves the same way it resolves at rest. This Part defines the assertion form, the obligation semantics that preconditions carry, the guarantee semantics that postconditions carry, the evidence requirement that separates a claimed postcondition from a satisfied one, the separation of powers that keeps a provider from judging itself, and the unfalsifiable postcondition that is the whole model's signature failure.

4.2 The assertion form. A precondition or postcondition is a triple: a subject (a resolvable thing — a file, a field, a register record, a gate result, a process state), a predicate (a decidable relation — exists, is non-empty, matches a type, equals a value, falls within a window, was observed to refuse), and an evaluator (the named component that decides it). The evaluator is part of the assertion, not an implementation detail, because an assertion with no named evaluator is undecidable in practice regardless of how decidable it looks on paper. The triple's invariant is that all three resolve: a subject that names nothing, a predicate that no evaluator implements, or an evaluator that does not exist are each a malformed assertion, and a contract carrying one is refused at creation. This is the mechanical descendant of the estate's premise-verification doctrine: a plan that names a function which does not exist is a plan against an imagined system, and an assertion that names an evaluator which does not exist is a promise against an imagined checker.

4.3 Preconditions as the obligation trigger. A precondition states what must be true before the provider is obliged to perform. Its semantics are exact and often misread: a precondition is not a validation the provider performs as a courtesy, it is the boundary of the provider's obligation. If a precondition is false, the provider is not obliged, and behavior outside an obligation is not a breach by the provider — it is a breach by the caller. This is why precondition-checking must happen at the boundary and must be recorded: a provider that silently proceeds on a false precondition has accepted an obligation it never made, and it will be blamed for the outcome. The invariant is that the provider evaluates every precondition before performing, refuses when one is false, and records the refusal with the identity of the caller and the failed assertion. A refusal is not a failure of the contract; it is the contract working. The estate's own gates behave this way — spec_gate refuses to admit an oversized task with no spec, and that refusal is the gate keeping its promise, not breaking it.

4.4 Postconditions as the guarantee. A postcondition states what the provider guarantees to be true after performing, and it is the only field against which delivery can be judged. Its semantics are that the provider bears the burden: if the postcondition is false after performance, the provider has breached, without regard to why, and the why belongs to the fault classification of Part VI rather than to the question of whether a breach occurred. This burden allocation is deliberate and sometimes uncomfortable — an environment fault still produces a false postcondition, and the contract still records that the promise was not kept — because a model in which a provider can dispute whether a breach occurred, rather than only its fault class, is a model in which no breach is ever recorded. Separate the two questions: did the promise hold (an objective evaluation of an assertion) and who is at fault (a classification). Merging them lets fault-avoidance suppress detection, which is the exact dynamic that lets a chronic breacher present as reliable.

4.5 The evidence requirement. A postcondition claimed but not evidenced is UNVERIFIED, and UNVERIFIED is a distinct terminal state — never a synonym for satisfied, never a synonym for breached. This composes the estate's reality supremacy directly: the disk outranks the claim, an observed result outranks an intended one, and a component's own report of its success is a hypothesis until an instrument independent of it confirms the effect. The contract's evidence-requirement field names what would constitute confirmation: the exit code observed, the file present with the expected content hash, the register record queried and found non-null, the gate result recorded rather than asserted. The three-state model matters because two-state thinking collapses UNVERIFIED into whichever pole is cheaper, and the cheaper pole is always satisfied. An estate that treats unverified as satisfied is an estate whose contracts are self-certifying, and self-certification is not verification, it is paperwork. CO-12's telemetry-before-claims is the same law on the metrics surface, and DAIF-04 composes it rather than restating it: no claim about a delivery is admissible ahead of the measurement that grounds it.

4.6 Separation of powers: the provider may not be the sole judge. The provider may evaluate its own postconditions, and usually must — it is closest to the effect and cheapest to run. But it may not be the sole judge of its own delivery, because a provider that both performs and adjudicates has a structural incentive to define satisfaction as whatever it produced. The rule, composing DAIF-07's completion-authority separation, is that a postcondition's evaluator must be resolvable by a party other than the provider, and that at least one class of postcondition per contract — the ones whose falsity would be most expensive — must be evaluated by an evaluator the provider does not control. The independent evaluator need not be exotic: the disk is an independent evaluator, a downstream gate is an independent evaluator, and a consumer-side type check on the returned object is an independent evaluator. What is forbidden is the closed loop in which the provider asserts satisfaction, the record holds the assertion, and no instrument outside the provider ever touched the world the assertion describes. That loop produces green forever, including on the day everything is broken.

4.7 The unfalsifiable postcondition. The signature failure of this Part is the postcondition that cannot be false. "The output is high quality." "The agent performed correctly." "The context is sufficient." Each reads like a guarantee and each is a null: there is no state of the world an evaluator could observe that would render it false, so it can never be breached, and a promise that can never be breached promises nothing. The unfalsifiable postcondition is more dangerous than an absent one, because an absent postcondition is visibly absent and is refused at creation by Part II's central invariant, while an unfalsifiable one fills the field and passes the shape check. Detection is mechanical and it is the same falsifiability test ACIS applies to a theorem: for every postcondition, name the observation that would falsify it. If no such observation can be named, the postcondition is refused. This forces the author to convert a quality aspiration into a set of decidable assertions — the output parses, every field a consumer reads is non-null, the named gate returned a pass with its evidence attached, the runtime stayed under the declared bound — which is harder to write and is the only kind that can be relied on.

4.8 Undecidability and the honest UNKNOWN. Some assertions cannot be decided in a given run: the evaluator is unavailable, the subject cannot be reached, the measurement is not instrumented. The contract's discipline here is that an undecidable assertion returns UNKNOWN, and UNKNOWN never defaults to satisfied. This is the estate's own hard-won lesson on gates that could not recognize what they were counting and therefore counted zero: a quantity that cannot be measured must be reported as unmeasurable, not as zero, because zero is a value and unmeasurable is an absence, and treating the absence as a value produces a gate that passes precisely because it is blind. Every contract must therefore declare the behavior of each assertion under undecidability — whether an UNKNOWN blocks performance, permits it with a recorded caveat, or escalates — and the default, when the contract is silent, is to block for any postcondition whose falsity is expensive and to record for all others. A contract that lets its own unmeasurability pass silently has quietly become a hope again.

4.9 Composition, not duplication. Nothing in this Part builds a new evaluator. The assertion's evaluator field points at what the estate already runs: done_gate for admissibility, spec_gate for the spec precondition on large work, the hook chain for write-time refusals, SQI's benchmarks for behavioral evidence, ACIS's ladder for epistemic standing, CO-12 for measurement-before-claim. DAIF-04's contribution is the typed slot that binds an assertion to an evaluator and the three-state result semantics that keeps an unverified claim out of the satisfied bucket. The value is in the binding, and the binding is what converts a scattered set of good gates into a promise a consumer can rely on: not "there are gates somewhere upstream" but "this specific assertion is decided by this specific evaluator, whose own contract declares what it does not catch."

PART IV FINAL LAW. A precondition is the boundary of the provider's obligation and a postcondition is the whole of its guarantee, each a typed triple of resolvable subject, decidable predicate, and named evaluator rather than a sentence a reader interprets; a postcondition claimed without its required evidence is UNVERIFIED and never satisfied, an assertion that cannot be decided is UNKNOWN and never zero, the provider may evaluate its own delivery but may never be its sole judge, and the postcondition that no observation could falsify — the output is high quality, the agent performed correctly — is refused at creation, because it fills the field, passes the shape check, can never be breached, and therefore promises exactly nothing.

PART V — AUTHORITY, OWNERSHIP, AND SCOPE IN CONTRACTS

5.1 Mission. A contract is a promise, and a promise made by a party with no entitlement to make it binds nothing. This Part types the three fields that decide entitlement and reach: authority (who may create, amend, waive, or void a contract, and what a contract may bind), ownership (who is accountable for it), and scope (over what surface it binds). All three compose DAIF-01 directly — the authority ladder, the nested scope ladder — and DAIF-04 defines no rival vocabulary for either. What DAIF-04 adds is the application of those types to the contract object: the rules by which a contract's authority is checked at creation rather than at execution, the rule that exactly one party is accountable, and the isolation rule that stops a project-scoped promise from silently binding the estate.

5.2 The four authority-bearing acts. Four acts on a contract require authority, and each requires a different amount. Creation requires authority over the surface the contract binds: to promise something on behalf of a project, the creating party must hold at least project authority. Amendment — any change to a precondition, postcondition, or output type — requires the authority that created it or higher, and produces a new version rather than an edit, because an amendment made silently in place is the drift the fabric convicts. Waiver — permitting a single non-performance without recording a breach — requires strictly higher authority than creation, and is bounded to one occasion with a recorded rationale, because a waiver that can be self-issued by the provider is not a waiver but an escape. Voiding — declaring the contract never bound at all — requires the highest authority of the four and is reserved for a contract that was invalid at creation. The invariant across all four: the authority for an act is checked against DAIF-01's ladder before the act takes effect, and an unauthorized act does not fail loudly at execution — it never occurs.

5.3 The cardinal invariant: a party cannot grant authority it does not hold. This is the load-bearing rule of the Part. A contract in which a low-authority agent promises to override a hard constraint is VOID at creation, not breached at execution. The distinction is the entire mechanism. If such a contract were merely breachable, it would be admitted to the register, consumers would rely on it, the agent would attempt the override, and the estate would discover the problem at the moment of the attempted override — which is to say, at the moment of maximum damage, with a consumer already committed. Because it is void at creation, the register refuses it, the consumer never relies on it, and the failure is a refusal notice rather than an incident. The check is mechanical: the contract's promised effects are compared against the creating party's authority on DAIF-01's ladder, and any effect requiring an authority above the creator's rung voids the contract. An agent cannot promise what a project rule forbids; a project rule cannot promise what the constitution forbids; nothing in the estate can promise to set aside a hard rule, because a hard rule's override policy names an owner phrase and no contract can forge it.

5.4 Authority forgery and the provenance check. The adversarial case is the forged rung: a contract whose creator records an authority higher than its provenance entitles, so that the void-at-creation check passes on a lie. The defense is DAIF-01's own forged-authority detection, applied to the contract's creator field: an authority claim is valid only if its provenance names a source the ladder actually places at that rung, so a contract claiming constitutional authority whose provenance points at a single agent's session is convicted at intake. The generalization matters more than the instance: a low-authority party cannot promote itself by declaration, and a contract is a declaration. Without this check, the contract object would become the estate's cleanest self-elevation path — an agent authors a contract asserting its own high authority, the register admits it, and every subsequent check consults the contract rather than the ladder. The register consults the ladder, always, and the contract's authority field is a claim the ladder either entitles or refuses.

5.5 Ownership: exactly one accountable party. Every contract has exactly one owner, and the field is mandatory for the ACTIVE state. The owner is not the provider and not the consumer by necessity — often it is the provider, sometimes a governance authority, occasionally the Owner — but it is always a single resolvable party who is accountable for the promise: who answers when it is breached, who authorizes an amendment, who receives the escalation when the failure path terminates. The invariant that exactly one party is accountable exists because a contract owned by everyone is enforced by no one: with two owners, each defers to the other on breach, and the breach record accumulates against a party that does not exist. With no owner, a breach has no address, so the escalation path has no terminus and the estate's owner_queue receives an item with no assignee, which is an item that will not be worked. Ownership also carries the deprecation duty: a contract's owner is the party obliged to notice that the contract has outlived its purpose and to supersede it rather than leaving it ACTIVE and stale, because a stale ACTIVE contract is a promise nobody intends to keep and everyone still relies on.

5.6 Scope isolation between contracts. A contract's scope is a DAIF-01 scope value, and it bounds the surface over which the promise binds. The invariant is directional and strict: a contract binding at project scope may not silently bind at universal scope, and the resolution of an ambiguous scope is inward, to the narrowest defensible reach, exactly as DAIF-01 resolves an ambiguous unit. The reason is the same asymmetry: under-reach at worst omits a contract from a context it should have governed, an error whose correction is a deliberate widening; over-reach binds every unrelated context in the estate to a promise authored for one, an error discovered only after the leak has propagated and paid by a party who never saw the contract. Widening a contract's scope is itself an authority-bearing act, requiring authority proportional to the new reach: promoting a project contract to universal is a claim on the whole estate and must be made by a party entitled to make it. A contract may not acquire universal reach through an unfilled scope field, which is the silent path an untyped convention takes by default.

5.7 Scope and the two-party rule interacting. Part II restricted a contract to two parties while permitting a consumer class typed as a scope. The interaction with this Part is where that pays: a provider serving many consumers declares its consumer class as a scope value — every component within the power-pack scope, every agent within a project — and admission of a new consumer becomes a scope check rather than an amendment, so the provider's promise does not require re-authoring each time the estate grows. The invariant guarding this is that the consumer scope may never exceed the contract's binding scope: a contract scoped to a project cannot declare a universal consumer class, because it would then be promising to parties over whom its authority does not reach, and a promise to a party you cannot bind is a promise you cannot keep consistently. Scope thus governs both directions — the surface the promise touches and the population entitled to rely on it — and the two must be consistent or the contract is refused.

5.8 The overreach failure and its detonation profile. The characteristic failure of this Part is the overreaching contract that works for a long time. A contract authored under project authority, scoped by omission to universal, promising an effect the project genuinely delivers. Nothing breaks: the promise is kept, within the project, for months. Then a second project's component reads the register, finds a universal-scoped contract, relies on it, and receives nothing — because the provider never served that scope and never intended to. The breach record names the provider, who did nothing wrong; the fault is the contract's own (Part VI's contract-fault class), and the contract was defective at creation, not at execution. This is the deferred detonation the fabric convicts throughout: a defect that lies dormant precisely as long as the environments happen to agree, and fires the moment they diverge. The inward default of scope resolution and the authority-proportional widening rule are the two mechanisms that prevent it, and both operate at creation, because there is no cheap moment to catch it later.

5.9 Composition with the estate's existing authority mechanisms. Nothing in this Part is a new power structure. The ladder is DAIF-01's, which itself composes SQI-00's authorization levels, the hard-rule precedence, and DRK's limits on autonomous decision. The contract's contribution is that a promise now carries the rung under which it was made, so that a consumer reading it can rank it, a register admitting it can refuse an overreach, and a breach recorded against it can be delivered to a party who exists. The estate's one authority law governs its contracts as it governs its knowledge and its decisions, and the alternative — a contract layer with its own private notion of who may promise what — would be exactly the second source of truth that drifts from the first and collides with it under pressure.

PART V FINAL LAW. Authority decides who may create, amend, waive, or void a contract and caps what any contract may bind, so that a promise to override a hard constraint made by a party without the rung to override it is VOID at creation rather than breached at execution — refused at the register instead of discovered at the moment of maximum damage; ownership names exactly one accountable party, because a contract owned by everyone is enforced by no one and a breach with no address is an incident report rather than an enforcement; and scope binds the promise inward by default, since an under-reaching contract merely omits while an over-reaching one silently binds every unrelated context in the estate to a promise authored for exactly one.

PART VI — BREACH: DETECTION, CLASSIFICATION, AND RESPONSE

6.1 Mission. A promise that cannot be breached is not a promise, and a breach that cannot be detected, classified, and recorded teaches the estate nothing. This Part defines the breach lifecycle: the four ways a breach is detected, the four fault classes into which every breach resolves, the rule that classification always precedes response, the five-rung response ladder, and the recording obligation that makes a chronic breacher visible. The Part's governing insight is that response without classification is worse than no response at all, because a response applied to the wrong party trains the wrong system to change, and a system that has been trained to change something that was never broken now carries a defect introduced by its own remediation.

6.2 The four detections. A breach is detected in exactly four ways, and a contract that cannot be breached by any of the four is a contract whose fields are underspecified. First, a postcondition evaluates false: the provider performed, the assertion was decided, and the decision was negative. This is the canonical detection and it requires nothing but the machinery of Part IV. Second, a timeout expires: the provider did not deliver within the bound, and the absence of a result is itself a breach, because Part VII holds that an unbounded promise is not a promise. Third, a precondition is violated by the caller: the consumer supplied a state the provider never undertook to handle, which is a breach by the consumer and is detected at the provider's boundary before performance. Fourth, an output fails its type contract: the returned object does not satisfy the declared output type, which is detected by the consumer at the boundary of receipt and is the one detection the provider cannot suppress. The four are complementary, not alternatives, and a well-typed contract is checked by all four on every interaction it survives.

6.3 The four fault classes. Every detected breach resolves into exactly one of four classes, and the classification is a typed decision with its own provenance. Caller-fault: a precondition was violated, so the provider was never obliged and the consumer proceeded outside the bargain. Provider-fault: the preconditions held, the provider performed, and a postcondition is false — the promise was made, triggered, and not kept. Environment-fault: neither party is at fault; the substrate failed, the network dropped, the host refused, the transport dropped a frame. Contract-fault: the contract itself was wrong — an unsatisfiable postcondition, an overreaching scope, a precondition no caller could ever satisfy, a guarantee the provider was never capable of making. The classes are exhaustive and mutually exclusive by construction, and the resolution rule when two seem to apply is that contract-fault outranks the others: if the contract was defective, the parties' behavior is being judged against a broken standard and no conclusion about them is admissible.

6.4 Environment-fault and the canonical misattribution. Environment-fault deserves its own treatment because misattributing it is one of the estate's named errors. When a failure originates in the substrate — the bridge dropped, the host was saturated, the remote channel reset — and the estate attributes it to the product, two harms follow. The immediate harm is a false defect: an engineer changes code that was correct, and the change is a fresh defect introduced under the belief that it is a fix. The systemic harm is worse: the environment's real defect is never addressed, because the incident is closed against the wrong party, so it recurs and each recurrence produces another false fix. This is the environment qualification SQI already enforces on its measurements, composed here: before a breach is classified as provider-fault, the environment must be qualified, and a failure on an unqualified environment is classified environment-fault or, when the environment cannot be assessed at all, UNKNOWN — never provider-fault by default. The default direction matters because the cheap default is always to blame the last component to touch the work, and the last component to touch the work is almost never the substrate.

6.5 Why classification precedes response. The response ladder is potent and each rung acts on a party. Retry acts on the provider. Compensation acts on the effect. Escalation acts on the owner. Rollback acts on the world. Voiding acts on the contract. Apply any of them to the wrong fault class and the effect is not merely wasted — it is corrupting. Respond to a provider-fault as if it were a caller-fault and the consumer is hardened against an input it was entitled to supply, so the estate accumulates defensive code protecting against legitimate behavior while the real defect persists. Respond to a caller-fault as if it were a provider-fault and the provider widens its preconditions to accept states it cannot actually handle, converting a clean refusal into a silent misbehavior. Respond to an environment-fault as a provider-fault and both the false fix and the unaddressed substrate defect follow. Respond to a contract-fault as anything else and both parties are punished for a standard that was never satisfiable. Classification is therefore not a bookkeeping step before the real work; it is the step that decides which system is going to change, and changing the wrong system is how an estate accrues defects while believing it is fixing them.

6.6 The response ladder. Five rungs, applied in order of increasing cost and irreversibility, each gated by fault class. Retry: permitted only for environment-fault, and only under the pivot conditions of Part VII — an identical retry against a provider-fault is a loop, not a fix. Compensate: an offsetting action that neutralizes a partial effect, applicable when the provider produced something the consumer must not be left holding, and the primary rung for provider-fault with irreversible partial effects. Escalate: hand the breach to the accountable owner, terminating at owner_queue when the estate cannot resolve it, and the mandatory rung for any contract-fault, because neither party can repair a defective standard by acting under it. Roll back: undo the effect and return the world to its prior state, available only where the effect is reversible and the contract declared the path. Void: declare the contract unfit and refuse further reliance on it, reserved for contract-fault and requiring the authority of Part V. The ladder's discipline is that a party may not skip to a higher rung to avoid a recorded breach at a lower one: a provider that voids its own contract after failing it has escaped, not resolved, and voiding requires an authority the provider does not hold over its own promise.

6.7 The recording obligation. Every breach is recorded, without exception, including breaches that were immediately resolved, breaches classified as environment-fault, and breaches that a waiver excused. The record holds the contract identity and version, the detection (which of the four), the fault class and its provenance, the response applied, and the outcome. The obligation is absolute for one reason: an unrecorded breach teaches the system nothing and lets a chronic breacher look reliable. A provider that fails one interaction in twenty, each failure absorbed by a helpful retry and never recorded, presents to the estate as a provider that has never failed — and the estate builds on it accordingly, until the day the retry does not help. The record is what converts a scatter of individual incidents into a rate, and a rate is the only thing that can be reasoned about. Breach records publish on PM-03 under the typed event schema, are held for pattern analysis by CEPS, and feed the estate's existing recurrence machinery: a breach class that recurs is a candidate for a UKDL rule, and a recurrence with real damage is a candidate for a hard rule, which is the estate's existing bug-to-rule ladder consumed rather than forked.

6.8 Adversarial cases. Three deserve naming. The self-absolving classifier: the provider classifies its own breaches and discovers, over time, that most of them are environment-faults. The defense is the separation-of-powers rule of Part IV applied to classification — a provider may propose a fault class, but a class that absolves the provider requires evidence from an evaluator the provider does not control, and an environment-fault claim with no environment evidence is UNKNOWN, not environment-fault. The breach-by-waiver: a provider whose contract is repeatedly waived by a sympathetic authority never accumulates a breach record. The defense is that a waiver is itself recorded as an event against the contract, and a waiver rate is a metric, so a contract waived five times has a visible history even if its breach count is zero. The definition-shrinking provider: a provider that responds to breaches by narrowing its postconditions until nothing it does can be false. The defense is that an amendment is an authority-bearing act producing a new version, and a version history showing monotonically weakening guarantees is itself the signal — a promise that has been narrowed until it cannot be breached has become a hope again, and Part II refuses hopes at the register.

6.9 Metrics and anti-Goodhart. Four metrics with four countermetrics. Breach detection latency (time from unmet promise to recorded breach) is countered by the blind-gate check: a latency of zero because no detection exists is not excellence, so latency is only meaningful against a contract whose evaluators have been observed to refuse. Breach rate per contract is countered by the waiver rate and the amendment-weakening rate, because both can drive breach rate to zero without any improvement in delivery. Fault-class distribution is countered by the evidence rate on absolving classifications, because a distribution dominated by environment-fault is either a genuinely bad substrate or a self-absolving classifier, and only the evidence rate distinguishes them. And contract coverage — the fraction of the estate's real interactions that have a typed contract at all — is countered by the postcondition falsifiability rate, because coverage is trivially maximized by admitting contracts whose postconditions cannot be false, which is coverage of nothing.

PART VI FINAL LAW. A breach is detected four ways — a postcondition false, a timeout expired, a precondition violated by the caller, an output failing its type — and resolves into exactly one of four faults: caller, provider, environment, or contract; classification always precedes response, because retry, compensation, escalation, rollback, and voiding each act on a party, and a response applied to the wrong fault class trains the wrong system to change, which is how an estate accrues defects while believing it is fixing them — most canonically by attributing a substrate failure to the product and shipping a false fix over a defect that was never there; and every breach is recorded without exception, because an unrecorded breach teaches the estate nothing and lets a chronic breacher present as perfectly reliable right up until the day the helpful retry stops helping.

PART VII — FAILURE SEMANTICS: TIMEOUT, RETRY, ROLLBACK, COMPENSATION

7.1 Mission. Anyone can specify a contract's happy path; the hard part of a contract is what happens when things go wrong, and the fields that state it are the ones authors are most tempted to leave thin. This Part gives the four failure-semantics fields their full mechanism: timeout as a first-class term of the promise, retry policy with its mandatory pivot conditions, idempotency as the precondition of safe retry, and the rollback-versus-compensation distinction that culminates in the Part's hardest invariant — a contract with irreversible effects that declares no compensation path may not be executed at all. The Part's stance is that a contract is judged by its failure path, because the happy path is what the author imagined and the failure path is what the estate will actually live through.

7.2 Timeout as a term of the promise. Every contract carries a timeout, and the field is mandatory for the ACTIVE state. The reasoning is direct: an unbounded promise is not a promise, it is a hang. A consumer waiting on a provider that never answers has received neither a delivery nor a breach; it has received silence, which it cannot act on, and the estate's own doctrine — hard-won across many dead sessions where a turn closed on a passive wait for something that would never arrive — forbids exactly that state. The timeout converts silence into a typed event: at the bound, the absence of a result becomes a detected breach with a fault class, and the consumer's failure path activates. The invariant is that the bound is stated as a wall-clock quantity on the contract rather than as a runtime configuration on the provider, because a bound the consumer cannot read is a bound the consumer cannot plan around. And the bound must be finite: a contract stating an infinite or unspecified timeout is refused at the register, because it has re-created the hang under the appearance of a field.

7.3 Retry policy and the trap it exists to close. Retry is the most-reached-for response and the most misapplied. The trap is precise: retrying an identical failing call is a loop, not a fix. If the provider failed because a postcondition was unsatisfiable given the input, the same input will fail again, and again, and the retry budget converts one failure into N failures plus a wasted budget and a delayed detection. The estate's two-consecutive-failures law names the same shape at the tool level, and the contract layer inherits it as a hard requirement on the field: a retry policy must specify PIVOT conditions, not just retry counts. A pivot condition names what must be DIFFERENT for a retry to be legitimate — a re-qualified environment, a corrected input, a different code path, a different provider — and a retry attempted with nothing different is refused by the policy itself. The default policy, when a contract states only a count, is a count of zero: absent stated pivot conditions, no retry is authorized, because an unconditioned retry is the loop the law forbids.

7.4 Fault class governs retry eligibility. The retry policy is gated by Part VI's classification, and the gating is asymmetric. Environment-fault is the only class for which an identical retry is ever legitimate, and even then only after the environment has been re-qualified — a retry against a substrate that is still failing is the same loop wearing a different hat. Caller-fault permits a retry only with a corrected precondition, which is by definition not an identical call. Provider-fault permits no identical retry at all: the provider performed and did not keep its promise, and nothing about repeating the request changes the provider's capability. Contract-fault permits no retry under any condition, because the standard is defective and every attempt is being judged against it. This gating is what makes retry a mechanism rather than a reflex, and it is why the response ladder of Part VI places retry at its lowest rung and behind the classification step: retry is cheap only when it is correct, and when it is incorrect it is the most expensive rung on the ladder, because it delays detection while consuming budget.

7.5 Idempotency as the precondition of safe retry. A retry is safe only if the operation is idempotent — if performing it twice leaves the world in the same state as performing it once. This is not an implementation nicety; it is a contract term, and the contract must declare it. A non-idempotent operation retried after a timeout is the estate's most dangerous ambiguity: the consumer does not know whether the first attempt performed and the answer was lost, or never performed at all, and the two cases demand opposite responses. Declaring idempotency resolves the ambiguity by removing it — if the operation is idempotent, the consumer may retry without knowing which case obtains. If it is not, the contract must instead declare a status-check assertion the consumer can evaluate to determine whether the first attempt landed, and a non-idempotent contract that declares neither idempotency nor a status check may not be retried at all, only escalated. The invariant closes the double-effect breach: the same effect applied twice because a timeout hid a success.

7.6 Rollback versus compensation. Rollback undoes the effect: the world is returned to its prior state, and the interaction is as if it had not occurred. Compensation offsets the effect: the world cannot be returned, so a second, distinct action is taken whose purpose is to neutralize the first's consequences. The distinction is not stylistic — it is a fact about the effect, and it is the contract's job to state which applies. An effect on a private working state is usually reversible and takes a rollback path. An effect that crossed a boundary — a message published to the bus and consumed, a write that landed on a live substrate, a session that was ended, a decision another actor already acted upon — is typically irreversible, and the honest term is compensation. The failure the distinction prevents is the false rollback: a contract that declares a rollback path for an effect that cannot actually be undone, so the failure path executes, reports success, and leaves the world in a state the consumer believes has been reverted. A false rollback is worse than no rollback, because no rollback at least leaves the consumer knowing the effect persists.

7.7 The compensation invariant. Here is the Part's hardest rule and the one that binds hardest at creation: a contract with irreversible effects MUST declare a compensation path before it may be executed at all. Not before it fails — before it runs. The reasoning is that the compensation path cannot be authored in the moment of failure: at that moment the effect is already in the world, the estate is under pressure, the accountable owner is not necessarily present, and whatever offsetting action is invented under those conditions is an improvisation with no review. Authoring it at creation, in calm, with the authority that created the contract, is the only time it can be done well. The register enforces the rule mechanically: a contract whose declared effects include any irreversible one and whose compensation field is empty is refused ACTIVE status. The refusal is the point — an irreversible effect with no offsetting action is a decision the estate cannot walk back and must not take by accident, and the estate's own deploy and destructive-command doctrine already applies exactly this shape at the command surface, requiring a recorded backup or an explicit authorization before an unrecoverable action is dispatched.

7.8 Partial failure and the honest halting state. The hardest real case is neither clean success nor clean failure but the partial: the provider performed some of its effects and then failed. The contract must declare, for every effect, whether it persists on failure. A contract that says nothing here leaves the consumer facing a world in an unknown state, and an unknown state is one the consumer cannot safely act on in either direction — it can neither proceed as if the work happened nor as if it did not. Two disciplines address it. The first is atomicity where it is available: state the effects as a group that lands entirely or not at all, so partial failure is impossible by construction, and prefer this whenever the substrate permits it. The second, where atomicity is not available, is the declared halting state: the contract names, for each stage, exactly what persists if the provider fails at that stage, so a consumer facing a failure can evaluate a stage assertion, learn which halting state obtains, and take the compensation appropriate to it. What is forbidden is the undeclared partial, which is a promise that produces an unknowable world.

7.9 Escalation as the terminus and the anti-hang closer. Every failure path terminates. When retry is not eligible, compensation is not sufficient, and rollback is not available, the path ends at escalation to the accountable owner, and when the estate cannot resolve it internally, at owner_queue. The invariant is that the terminus is always reached in bounded time — the sum of the timeout, the authorized retries, and the compensation window is itself bounded, and the contract states the bound. This composes the estate's own anti-hang doctrine at the contract layer: no path may end in a wait, no failure may end in silence, and a consumer whose provider has exhausted every rung receives an escalation event rather than an absence. An escalation is an actionable outcome; an absence is not. The whole failure semantics exists to guarantee that a consumer, at every moment, holds either a delivery, a breach, or an escalation — and never the fourth thing, which is nothing at all.

7.10 Metrics and countermetrics for the failure path. Three pairs. Mean time to terminus (from first failure to a delivery, breach, or escalation) is countered by the premature-escalation rate, because a path that escalates instantly minimizes the metric while dumping every failure on the accountable owner. Retry success rate is countered by the identical-retry rate: a high retry success achieved by retrying identical calls against a flaky substrate is hiding a substrate defect the estate must see. And compensation coverage — the fraction of irreversible effects with a declared compensation path — is countered by the compensation-exercise rate, because a compensation path that has never once been executed is UNVERIFIED in exactly the sense Part IV means, and a failure path that has never run is a hypothesis about what will happen, not a guarantee.

PART VII FINAL LAW. A contract is judged by its failure path, so timeout is a first-class term because an unbounded promise is not a promise but a hang; retry is authorized only by fault class and only with stated PIVOT conditions, because an identical retry against a failing call is a loop rather than a fix and an unconditioned retry policy defaults to a retry count of zero; idempotency or a status-check assertion is the precondition of any safe retry, since a timeout that hides a success turns a retry into a double effect; rollback undoes and compensation offsets, and declaring a rollback for an effect that cannot be undone is worse than declaring nothing; and a contract whose effects are irreversible and whose compensation path is empty is refused execution outright — not refused at failure, refused at creation — because the offsetting action for an unrecoverable act can only be authored in calm, and never invented in the moment the estate discovers it needed one.

PART VIII — THE TYPED EVENT SCHEMA

8.1 Mission, and the exact boundary of the absorption. The corpus map convicted a standalone event fabric as a duplicate, and the conviction was correct: the estate already carries events, and it carries them well. PM-03 publishes between components. CEPS records what happened, for later pattern analysis. The hook chain carries the synchronous admission signal that decides whether a write lands at all. Three carriages, each sealed, each with an owner, each already load-bearing. DAIF-04 absorbed exactly one thing from that convicted candidate and nothing else: the typed event SCHEMA — the contract that states what an emission must contain for a subscriber to consume it safely. The schema is not transport and does not imply transport. This Part defines no queue, no delivery mechanism, no retention policy, no subscription registry, no ordering engine, and no acknowledgement protocol. Each of those belongs to a carriage that already exists, and any subsection of this dataset that began to specify one would be building the second bus the fabric's constitution exists to convict. What DAIF-04 owns is the shape of the fact and the promise attached to it. What it composes, permanently and without amendment, is the carriage. The distinction is load-bearing enough to state as an invariant: a schema without a transport is a contract, and a schema with a transport is a fork.

8.2 What an event is. An event is a typed, authored fact that something happened. Three words in that sentence each carry weight. Typed: the emission has a declared kind and a declared payload shape, so a subscriber can decide, mechanically, whether what arrived is what it expected — an untyped emission is a blob that every subscriber interprets privately, which is nine private interpretations of one fact and eight opportunities for them to diverge. Authored: the emission names its producer as a resolvable party in the capability graph, because an anonymous fact has no one to hold to its shape and no address for the breach when the shape changes. And fact-that-happened: an event is in the past tense and is immutable — it is not a request, not an intention, and not a plan. A component that publishes "I will write the register" has published a hope; a component that publishes "the register was written, here is the identity of what landed" has published a fact. The tense matters because subscribers act, and an actor that acts on an intention is acting on a promise nobody has yet kept. The event's contract is therefore a contract about the past, and its postcondition is not "this will be true" but "this was true at the stated instant, and the evidence is attached."

8.3 The schema fields. Eleven fields, each present because its absence produces a nameable breakage. Kind: the event's type, drawn from a closed vocabulary in the DAIF-01 type system, never a free string, because a free string means a typo silently invents a kind that nobody subscribes to. Producer: the resolvable party that emitted it. Subject: the thing the fact is about — the contract identity, the obligation identity, the compiled artifact, the register key — because an event with no subject cannot be joined to anything. Payload type: the declared shape of the accompanying data, versioned independently of the kind. Authority: the DAIF-01 authority rung under which the fact is asserted, so a subscriber can rank a claim made by a session-scoped agent against one made by a governing instrument. Scope: the DAIF-01 reach over which the fact is relevant, so a project-scoped event does not silently instruct the whole estate. Ordering guarantee: the declared relation this emission bears to its siblings — total order within a subject, causal order behind a named predecessor, or none at all — stated so the subscriber knows what it may infer from arrival sequence rather than assuming a guarantee it was never given. Idempotency key: a value that identifies the FACT rather than the DELIVERY, so a fact received twice is recognized as one fact. Provenance: what produced the belief, composing DAIF-01's epistemic fields directly. Confidence: how strongly the producer holds it. And timestamp semantics: not merely a timestamp, but a declaration of WHICH instant it names.

8.4 Timestamp semantics and ordering, stated precisely because they are silently assumed. There are at least three instants in the life of an event — when the fact occurred in the world, when the producer observed it, and when the emission was carried — and a schema with a single unlabelled time field lets every subscriber pick a different one. The rule is that the schema names occurrence time as mandatory and observation time as mandatory whenever the two can differ by more than the smallest interval a consumer reasons over; carriage time belongs to the carriage and DAIF-04 does not define it. The invariant that follows is the ordering rule: a subscriber may infer nothing from arrival sequence unless the event's ordering-guarantee field grants it. This is the honest inversion of the usual convention, in which a subscriber assumes order because order happened to hold during development and then breaks the first time the substrate reorders under load. Declaring "no order" is not a weakness of the contract; it is the contract telling the truth, and a subscriber that needs order and receives a no-order guarantee must obtain order from the subject's own state, not from the sequence of arrivals. A fact's clock is a term of the promise, not an implementation artifact of the pipe.

8.5 Why events need contracts too. A producer promises a shape and a consumer relies on it, which is precisely the two-party structure of Part II — the event is a contract whose provider is the producer, whose consumer is the subscriber class typed as a scope, and whose postcondition is the payload's type together with the truth of the asserted fact. This is not a metaphor and it is not a stretch of the object; it is the object, applied to an emission. It follows immediately that an unversioned schema change is a breach — and the worst-shaped breach in the estate, because it is a breach against every consumer at once. A provider that adds a field breaks nobody. A provider that renames one, narrows one, changes a unit, or begins to emit null where it previously emitted a value has silently falsified the postcondition every subscriber was written against, and it has done so in a single edit, with no failing test on the producer's side, because the producer's own tests were rewritten in the same commit that broke everyone else. The producer's suite goes green precisely as the estate goes dark. This is why Part IX's versioning discipline is not administrative overhead but the only mechanism standing between a well-intentioned refinement and a simultaneous multi-consumer outage.

8.6 Event-to-obligation binding. An event may CREATE an obligation. A breach recorded against a contract creates an obligation on the accountable owner to respond. A gate refusal creates an obligation to remediate. A deprecation notice creates an obligation on every consumer within the notice's scope to migrate before sunset. The binding between the event kind and the obligation it creates is itself a contract — a typed, registered, versioned promise — and never an implicit convention buried in whichever subscriber happened to notice. The distinction is the difference between an estate in which "a breach event creates an obligation" is a fact a machine can check and one in which it is a habit that holds until the subscriber that implemented the habit is refactored away. DAIF-04 types the binding; DAIF-07 owns the obligation's lifecycle from that point forward and this dataset does not re-derive a single stage of it. The invariant on the binding is completeness in one direction only: every obligation-creating event kind must name the obligation it creates, but not every event kind creates one, and inventing an obligation for a fact that requires no response is how an estate manufactures a backlog nobody can drain.

8.7 Event-to-recompilation binding. An event may INVALIDATE a compilation. A change to a source of truth means every artifact derived from it is a hypothesis about a world that has moved, and DAIF-21 owns the synchronization and invalidation machinery that resolves that. What DAIF-04 supplies is the typed binding: this event kind, on this subject, invalidates that class of derived artifact, and the binding is registered rather than assumed. The failure this closes is the silently stale derivative — a compiled context pack, an assembled mission, a distilled index, still being consumed with confidence long after the fact it was derived from stopped being true, with nothing anywhere recording that the derivation had expired. The event is the trigger, the binding is the contract, and the recompilation is DAIF-21's. Once again the discipline is that the binding is the entire contribution: a Part that began to describe how invalidation propagates, in what order, with what fan-out control, would be re-narrating a sibling and would be cut.

8.8 The four failure modes. Schema drift: the payload's shape changes without a version, so every consumer's assumption is falsified simultaneously and the first symptom appears in whichever consumer happens to be least tolerant, which is almost never the one nearest the cause. The unconsumed event: a producer emitting into a void — a contract with no counterparty, which is not a contract at all but an expensive habit, and which presents as healthy because emission succeeds and nothing complains. It is the event-surface twin of the orphan module and it is detected the same way: by asking who reads it, not by asking whether it runs. The event storm: a fact that is true continuously and is emitted continuously, so a subscriber that must act on each one is overwhelmed, and the correct contract term is a state-change event rather than a state event — emit when the value CHANGES, never on every observation of a value that has not. And duplicate delivery: any carriage that guarantees at-least-once will eventually deliver twice, so a missing idempotency key turns one fact into two effects, which on a non-idempotent consumer is the double-effect breach of Part VII arriving through the event surface instead of through a retry.

8.9 Metrics and countermetrics. Four pairs. Event schema coverage — the count of emitted kinds that have a registered, versioned schema — is countered by the unconsumed-kind count, because a producer can drive coverage upward by registering schemas for facts nobody reads. Consumer breakage per schema change is countered by the change-frequency itself, since a producer can hold breakage at zero by never evolving, which is a frozen surface rather than a safe one. Duplicate-effect incidents are countered by idempotency-key coverage, because zero incidents on a substrate that has not yet reordered or redelivered is a measurement of luck rather than of design. And event volume per subject per unit time is countered by the action rate on those events: a high volume with a near-zero action rate is a storm being politely absorbed, and a near-zero volume on a subject the estate is actively changing is a producer that has gone quiet without anyone noticing. Each metric names its data source, and none is admissible ahead of the measurement that grounds it, which is CO-12's law composed rather than restated.

PART VIII FINAL LAW. An event is a typed, authored, immutable fact that something happened — carrying kind, producer, subject, payload type, authority, scope, ordering guarantee, idempotency key, provenance, confidence, and an explicitly-named clock — and it is a contract in the full sense of Part II, because a producer promises a shape and a consumer relies on it; therefore an unversioned schema change is not a refinement but a single breach against every consumer at once, committed by an edit whose own tests go green as the estate goes dark, and a fact emitted into a void is a contract with no counterparty, an event that fires on every observation rather than on every change is a storm, and a missing idempotency key converts an at-least-once carriage into a double effect — while transport itself remains, permanently and without amendment, the property of PM-03, CEPS, and the hook chain, which DAIF-04 composes and never forks, because a schema without a transport is a contract and a schema with one is a fork.

PART IX — VERSIONING, COMPATIBILITY, AND NEGOTIATION

9.1 Mission. Contracts change. That is not a defect to be engineered away but the ordinary condition of a live estate: a provider learns that its guarantee was too narrow, a consumer discovers it needs a field, a governing instrument tightens what may be promised, a substrate makes an old assurance impossible. The question a fabric must answer is not how to prevent change but how to make change SAFE — how a promise can evolve without falsifying, in one silent edit, every reliance that was placed on it. This Part supplies the mechanism: a compatibility taxonomy that classifies every proposed change before it lands, a versioning discipline that makes a breaking change a governance event rather than a commit, a negotiation model that lets two parties agree on which version binds them, and a deprecation lifecycle whose central invariant is that a contract may not be removed while a live consumer depends on it. The Part's stance is that a contract's version history is a first-class artifact of the estate — it is the record of how a promise's meaning moved, and an estate that cannot read that record is an estate that cannot tell a refinement from a betrayal.

9.2 The compatibility taxonomy. Every proposed change to an ACTIVE contract classifies into exactly one of three kinds, and the classification is made BEFORE the change lands, by an evaluator, not after it by an incident. Backward-compatible: consumers written against the old version continue to work unchanged against the new one. The canonical shapes are adding an optional output field, widening a precondition so that inputs previously refused are now accepted, and strengthening a postcondition so that the provider now guarantees more than it did. Forward-compatible: consumers written against the NEW version tolerate a provider still speaking the old one — which is the direction almost nobody designs for and the direction that decides whether an estate can deploy its consumers and providers independently. Breaking: neither holds. The canonical shapes are removing or renaming an output field, narrowing a precondition so that previously-valid callers are now refused, weakening a postcondition so that the provider now guarantees less, changing a unit or an encoding without changing a name, and — the subtlest — changing the SEMANTICS of a field whose type is unchanged, which passes every shape check ever written while falsifying every reliance ever placed on it.

9.3 The asymmetry that authors get backwards. The taxonomy's two directions are not symmetric in cost, and the intuition most authors carry is inverted. Strengthening a postcondition — promising MORE — is backward-compatible with respect to consumers, because a consumer relying on a weaker guarantee is still satisfied by a stronger one. Weakening it is breaking, always, without exception, no matter how reasonable the reason. Widening a precondition — demanding LESS of callers — is backward-compatible, because every caller that satisfied the old, stricter condition satisfies the new, looser one. Narrowing it is breaking, because callers who were entitled to be served are now refused. Authors reliably get this backwards under pressure, reasoning that a smaller promise is a smaller change and therefore a safer one, when the truth is exactly reversed: a smaller promise is the change that falsifies reliance, and reliance is the only thing a contract exists to make possible. The mechanical statement is that compatibility runs with the direction of obligation, not with the direction of the diff's size — a one-character edit that removes a guarantee is a breaking change, and a two-hundred-line addition that adds an optional field is not.

9.4 Versioning discipline. A version is not a label attached to a release; it is the identity of a particular meaning of a promise. The discipline has three clauses. First, any change to a precondition, a postcondition, an output type, or a payload schema produces a NEW version and never an in-place edit — this is Part II's invariant, and it exists because an in-place edit is precisely the drift the fabric convicts, a change to what was promised with no record that the promise moved. Second, the version is visible to the consumer: a consumer can read which version it is bound to, and a provider can report which versions it currently serves, because a version nobody can read is a version that cannot be negotiated over. Third, and hardest: a BREAKING change to a published contract is a GOVERNANCE EVENT, not a commit. It requires the authority of Part V, it emits a typed event under Part VIII, it opens the deprecation lifecycle of 9.6, and it creates obligations on every consumer within the contract's scope, which DAIF-07 then owns. What it may never be is a silent edit that ships on an ordinary afternoon inside an ordinary change to the provider, which is exactly the shape it takes in every estate that has not made this rule mechanical.

9.5 Negotiation between parties. When a provider serves several versions and a consumer can speak several, some mechanism must decide which version binds a given interaction, and the mechanism must be explicit. The model is a declared intersection: the provider publishes the version set it currently honors, the consumer declares the set it can consume, and the binding version is the highest member of the intersection — with the invariant that an EMPTY intersection is a refusal, not a fallback. This is where most estates quietly fail: faced with no common version, a tolerant implementation falls back to "best effort", which means the consumer proceeds against a provider whose promise it cannot actually interpret, and the resulting behavior is undefined in the precise sense that nobody has defined it. The refusal is the correct outcome and it is cheap: a refusal at negotiation is a clean error with two named parties and an obvious remedy, whereas a best-effort binding is a silent misinterpretation that surfaces, days later, as corrupt state with no attributable cause. Negotiation is also where a consumer's honest declaration matters — a consumer that claims to speak a version it has not been verified against has forged its half of the handshake, and the reliance the provider places on that claim is misplaced in exactly the way Part V's forged-rung defense addresses on the authority axis.

9.6 The deprecation lifecycle. Four states, one direction, no shortcuts. ANNOUNCED: a successor version exists, the current version still binds fully, and every consumer within scope has received the typed event saying a migration is coming. DEPRECATED: the version still binds and is still served, but new consumers may not bind to it — admission to the register is refused for fresh reliance — and existing consumers accrue an obligation to migrate. SUNSET: the version is served only for a declared window, migration is now overdue, and each remaining consumer is a named, tracked item rather than a statistic. REMOVED: the version no longer binds and no longer answers. The invariant that governs the whole ladder is the one clause that costs real discipline to hold: a contract may not be REMOVED while a live consumer depends on it. Not "should not." May not. The register refuses the removal, and it refuses it on evidence — the estate's own liveness signals and DAIF-07's orphan-blocker rule composed directly, because the question "does anyone still depend on this" is exactly the question the obligation fabric already answers, and forking a second dependency check here would be the duplication this corpus exists to convict.

9.7 Why the removal block is not negotiable. The counter-argument is always the same and always sounds reasonable: the remaining consumer is legacy, the migration is stalled, the version is a maintenance burden, and removing it will FORCE the migration to happen. It will not. It will force an outage, and the outage will be attributed to the consumer that broke rather than to the provider that removed the ground beneath it. Worse, it teaches the estate the lesson that a published promise has an unannounced expiry, and once a consumer believes that, it defends itself the only way it can — by re-validating everything it receives and trusting nothing, which is the redundant-verification tax that makes an estate slow and its components mutually suspicious. The correct escalation for a stalled migration is not removal; it is an obligation on the consumer's accountable owner, aged, visible, and terminating at owner_queue when it will not move. An unfinished migration is a governance problem with a named party. A removal that breaks a live consumer is an engineering incident with no defensible cause, and the difference between the two is entirely the estate's willingness to hold a rule that is occasionally inconvenient.

9.8 The signature failure: the silent breaking change. It deserves its own naming because it is the failure this whole Part exists to prevent, and because it is uniquely deceptive. A provider makes a change that is breaking under 9.2 but does not classify it as such — most often because the change looks small, or because the provider's own tests were updated in the same act and are therefore green, or because the semantics moved while the type did not and no shape check could see it. The version does not bump. No event is emitted. No deprecation opens. And in that instant, every consumer's passing test becomes a lie SIMULTANEOUSLY: the tests still pass, because they were written against a mock or a fixture that encodes the old meaning, and the mock did not change when the provider did. This is the specific reason contract tests and consumer-driven tests exist and why Part XIII treats a green signal as a hypothesis: an estate whose consumers verify against their own recorded assumptions rather than against the live provider has built a mutual hallucination that goes green forever, including on the day everything is broken. The versioning discipline is the mechanical defense; the contract test is the empirical one; neither alone is sufficient.

9.9 Metrics and countermetrics. Breaking-change frequency per contract is countered by the change frequency overall, because a provider can drive breakages to zero by freezing, and a frozen contract in a moving estate is drift wearing a clean shirt. Migration completion time — from ANNOUNCED to the last consumer migrated — is countered by the count of consumers who migrated by simply breaking and being fixed afterward, which completes the migration on paper while paying its cost in incidents. Deprecation-window adherence is countered by the removal-block trigger rate: a register that never has to refuse a removal is either perfectly disciplined or is not checking. And the count of unversioned semantic changes detected after the fact is the honest one, since it is a direct measurement of the failure mode, and its countermetric is the detection method's own coverage — a count of zero from an instrument that cannot see semantic drift is a report about the instrument, never about the estate.

PART IX FINAL LAW. A change to a promise classifies before it lands, into backward-compatible, forward-compatible, or breaking, and the asymmetry authors reliably invert is that promising MORE and demanding LESS are safe while promising LESS and demanding MORE are breaking regardless of how small the edit looks — because compatibility runs with the direction of obligation and never with the size of the diff; a breaking change to a published contract is therefore a governance event carrying an authority check, a typed announcement, an opened deprecation lifecycle, and obligations on every consumer in scope, never a silent edit that ships on an ordinary afternoon; a version may not be removed while a live consumer depends on it, no matter how stalled the migration, because a removal that breaks a live consumer teaches the estate that published promises have unannounced expiries and drives every component into defensive re-validation of everything it receives; and the signature failure is the unversioned semantic change, which converts every consumer's passing test into a lie at the same instant, since the fixtures encoding the old meaning did not move when the provider did.

PART X — CONTRACT COMPOSITION AND INHERITANCE

10.1 Mission. Real interactions are rarely atomic. A mission is executed by dispatching agents, each of which promises something, and the mission as a whole promises something to whoever assembled it. A corpus inherits guarantees from the family it belongs to and adds its own. A command drives an engine that drives two more. Each of these is a COMPOSITE contract — a promise whose terms are derived from the promises of its parts — and the whole question of this Part is how the derivation runs. The answer must be mechanical, because the alternative is that a composite's terms are authored by hand, from optimism, and the composite ends up promising things none of its constituents ever undertook to deliver. That failure has a name in this Part and it is the one the estate pays for most often: the over-promising composite, a mission that guarantees an outcome no agent within it actually contracted to provide, discovered only when the outcome fails to appear and every constituent can truthfully say it kept its own promise.

10.2 The composite object. A composite contract is a contract in the full sense of Part II — it has two parties, evaluable preconditions and postconditions, an authority, one accountable owner, a scope, a timeout, and a failure semantics — and it additionally carries a constituent set: the identities and versions of the contracts it composes. The constituent set is versioned reference, never copy: a composite that copies its parts' terms into itself has forked them, and the fork drifts the moment a part is amended, which reproduces at the composition layer the exact drift the fabric exists to convict. The composite therefore holds pointers, and its own terms are DERIVED from what those pointers resolve to at the version they name. This has an immediate and useful consequence: when a constituent publishes a new version, the composite's derived terms are stale until recomputed, which is precisely an invalidation, precisely an event under Part VIII, and precisely DAIF-21's business. The composite does not implement its own staleness detection; it declares its constituents and inherits the estate's synchronization.

10.3 The four derivation rules. Authority: the composite's authority is the LOWEST of its parts. A chain is only as authorized as its weakest link, because the composite can only cause what its constituents can cause, and a mission composed of agents that hold session authority cannot promise an effect requiring governance authority merely because the mission's author wrote a higher rung into its authority field. Scope: the composite's scope is the INTERSECTION of its parts' scopes. A promise is binding only where every part that must act is entitled to act, and a composite claiming a scope wider than the intersection is claiming reach it cannot deliver in the region where a part falls silent. Strength: the composite's strength is the STRICTEST of its parts. If any part demands a stronger precondition, the composite demands it, because the composite cannot be triggered into a state where one of its parts refuses. And failure semantics: the composite's failure semantics is the UNION of every failure path any part can take. If any part can fail irreversibly, the composite can fail irreversibly, and Part VII's compensation invariant therefore binds the composite even if the composite's author never contemplated an unrecoverable effect.

10.4 Why the four rules run in those directions. Each rule takes the pessimistic direction, and the pessimism is not conservatism for its own sake — it is the only direction in which the composite's terms are guaranteed satisfiable by its parts. Authority takes the minimum because the composite acts only through its parts, so an authority above any part's is an authority that cannot be exercised where that part is the actor. Scope takes the intersection because a region covered by only some parts is a region where the composite is partial, and a partial composite is not the composite. Strength takes the maximum on preconditions because a precondition that any part requires must hold before the composite may begin, and taking the minimum would admit the composite into a state where a part immediately refuses — an admitted interaction that cannot proceed, which is a self-inflicted breach. Failure semantics takes the union because a failure mode that exists anywhere in the constituent set exists in the composite, and a composite that declares only the failure modes its author thought about has declared its author's imagination rather than its actual behavior. In each case, the pessimistic direction is the one under which the derived term is a THEOREM about the parts rather than a hope about them.

10.5 Postconditions: the hardest derivation. Postconditions do not compose by a simple lattice operation, and pretending they do is where most composition models go wrong. The composite's postcondition is not the conjunction of its parts' postconditions, because the parts run in an order, and a later part may depend on, weaken, or overwrite what an earlier one guaranteed. Nor is it the last part's postcondition, because effects from earlier parts persist. The correct derivation is a chain: the composite's postcondition is what remains true after every part has performed IN THE DECLARED ORDER, with each part's preconditions checked against the state its predecessors left. This makes the composite's postcondition an entailment obligation on its author, and it makes the invariant checkable: a composite's postcondition may not promise more than its parts, chained in order, can deliver. Anything a composite asserts that no chain of constituent postconditions entails is an over-promise, and it is refused at creation in exactly the way Part IV refuses an unfalsifiable postcondition — not discovered at execution, refused at the register, because the moment of execution is the moment of maximum damage and the moment of authoring is free.

10.6 The no-silent-weakening invariant. The second central rule of this Part, and the one that guards the composite from being used as a laundering device: composition may never SILENTLY WEAKEN a constituent's guarantee. A composite may not present to its own consumer a promise weaker than one of its parts made, without that weakening being explicit, versioned, and attributable. The failure this prevents is subtle and expensive. A constituent guarantees that a write is durable. The composite that wraps it, for reasons of latency, returns before the write is confirmed and presents a promise of "accepted" — which is weaker, is nowhere stated to be weaker, and is consumed by a caller who reads the composite's name, recalls the constituent's reputation, and reasons about durability that the composite quietly declined to deliver. The constituent's guarantee has been laundered into something less through a boundary that looks like a pass-through. The invariant makes the weakening visible: if the composite promises less than a part, the composite's postcondition must say so in terms the consumer evaluates, and the difference is a declared property of the composite, not a footnote nobody reads.

10.7 Inheritance: family guarantees. A corpus inherits its family's contract — a fabrication discipline, a depth floor, a contamination gate, a citation obligation — and adds its own. Inheritance is a constrained form of composition with one additional rule: an inheriting contract may STRENGTHEN an inherited term and may never weaken one. A dataset may hold itself to a stricter depth floor than its family requires; it may not exempt itself from the family's contamination gate because the gate is inconvenient for its subject matter. The rule exists because the family's guarantee is what a citing sibling relies upon: a corpus that composes another corpus is relying on the family's contract as much as on the individual dataset's, and an inheritor that quietly weakens a family term has falsified every citation ever made to the family without touching a single one of them. Where a genuine exemption is warranted — and occasionally it is — it is an amendment to the FAMILY contract, made at the family's authority, versioned, announced, and inherited by every member, which is expensive precisely because it should be.

10.8 The over-promising composite, and its adversarial siblings. The signature failure: a mission contract that guarantees an outcome — the work is complete and verified — while every agent it dispatched contracted only to attempt, to report, or to touch a bounded surface. The mission's postcondition is not entailed by any chain of its constituents' postconditions, and nothing detected this because nobody performed the entailment check. When the outcome fails to appear, every constituent truthfully says it kept its promise, and the composite's owner is left holding a breach with no provider to attribute it to — which is Part VI's contract-fault, arriving through the composition surface. Three adversarial siblings deserve naming. The phantom constituent: a composite that names a constituent contract which does not exist in the register, so its derived terms are computed against an imagined part — the premise-verification failure, at the composition layer. The version-floating constituent: a composite that names a constituent without a version, so its derived terms silently change meaning whenever the part is amended, which is exactly the drift versioning exists to kill. And the authority-laundering composite: a low-authority composite whose author writes a high rung into its own field, hoping the register checks the composite and not the parts. The register checks the parts, always, and the derivation gives the composite the minimum.

10.9 Metrics and countermetrics. Entailment-check coverage — the fraction of composite contracts whose postcondition has been verified as entailed by its constituents' chained postconditions — is countered by the constituent-set completeness rate, because entailment is trivially satisfied by a composite that under-declares its parts. Composite depth is countered by the derived-authority distribution: a deep composite whose derived authority has collapsed to the lowest rung of a long chain may be structurally incapable of the effects it was assembled to produce, and that is a design signal worth surfacing before it is a runtime one. Silent-weakening incidents detected is countered by the detection method's own coverage, since a zero from an instrument that only compares field names and never semantics is a report about the instrument. And version-pinning rate on constituent references is the cheapest honest metric in the Part, because a floating reference is a defect by construction and needs no judgment to count.

PART X FINAL LAW. A composite contract derives its terms from its parts rather than authoring them from optimism — its authority the LOWEST of its constituents because a chain is only as authorized as its weakest link, its scope their INTERSECTION, its preconditions the STRICTEST any part demands, and its failure semantics the UNION of every path any part can take, so that a composite whose parts can fail irreversibly is itself an irreversible contract bound by the compensation invariant whether or not its author contemplated one; its postcondition is not a conjunction but a chain, evaluated in declared order with each part's preconditions checked against the state its predecessors left, and it may not promise anything that chain does not entail; composition may never silently weaken a constituent's guarantee, because a weakening laundered through a boundary that looks like a pass-through is consumed by a caller who reasons about a durability the composite quietly declined to deliver; and the signature failure is the over-promising composite — a mission guaranteeing an outcome no agent within it ever contracted to provide, which detonates with every constituent truthfully declaring that it kept its promise and no provider anywhere to attribute the breach to.

PART XI — THE CONTRACT REGISTER AND DISCOVERY

11.1 Mission. A typed promise that nobody can find is a typed promise that nobody can rely on, so the fabric needs a place where contracts live and a mechanism by which a party finds the one it needs. This Part defines the register — its fields, its admission gate, its guarantees — and the discovery path a consumer walks before it depends on a provider. The Part's central invariant is stated up front because everything else derives from it: a contract not in the register does not exist. Not "is discouraged", not "is informal" — does not exist, in the specific sense that no party may rely on it and no breach may be recorded against it. An unregistered promise is an ASSUMPTION, and assumptions are precisely the failure class that DAIF-00's premise discipline exists to kill: a plan written against a function that was never verified to exist, a reliance placed on a guarantee that was never verified to have been made. The register converts the estate's promises from things components believe about one another into things the estate can enumerate.

11.2 The register's fields. Identity: stable across versions, so a promise can be tracked as it evolves rather than appearing as a sequence of unrelated records. Version: which meaning of the promise this record holds, with the full version set the provider currently honors resolvable from the identity. Parties: the provider, and the consumer or consumer-class-as-scope, both as resolvable capability-graph references and never as descriptions. Status: the lifecycle state of Part II — DRAFTED, ACTIVE, SUSPENDED, BREACHED, SUPERSEDED, VOID — because a consumer that cannot see status will rely on a promise nobody still intends to keep. Owner: exactly one accountable party, per Part V. And evidence of last verification: when this contract's postconditions were last observed to hold, by which evaluator, with what result. That last field is the one most registers omit and the one that decides whether the register is a source of truth or a filing cabinet. A record with no verification evidence tells a consumer what was PROMISED. A record with it tells the consumer whether the promise has been KEPT lately, and those are different questions with different answers.

11.3 Discovery: how a consumer finds what it may rely on. The discovery path has three steps and each is mandatory. Locate: the consumer resolves the provider in the capability graph — GK's coordinates, not a search of the source tree — and asks the register what contracts that provider offers within the consumer's scope. Read: the consumer reads the contract's postconditions, its negative clauses where it is an evaluator contract, its compatibility guarantees, and its failure semantics, and it derives from those exactly what it may assume and exactly what it must handle. Bind: the consumer declares the version it will consume, and the binding is recorded, which is what makes the consumer VISIBLE — and consumer visibility is what makes Part IX's removal block enforceable, because a register that does not know who depends on a contract cannot refuse to remove it. The three steps are one motion in practice and take a subscriber less effort than the defensive re-validation it would otherwise write, which is the argument that carries the discipline: discovery is cheaper than distrust.

11.4 The invariant restated as a mechanism. A contract not in the register does not exist, and the mechanism that gives that clause teeth is the reliance rule: a consumer that acts on an unregistered promise has no claim. If the provider changes and the consumer breaks, there is no breach — the provider broke nothing, because it promised nothing that any register recorded, and the consumer's reliance was on its own inference. This is uncomfortable and it is correct. The alternative, in which an inferred promise carries the same weight as a registered one, makes every provider hostage to every consumer's imagination and freezes the estate: no provider can change anything, because someone somewhere may have inferred a dependency on the current behavior. Registration is what converts an inference into an obligation, and the price of the provider's freedom to evolve is that the consumer must ask rather than assume. The estate has already paid the other side of this bill — components built, tested, cited by name, and never called by anything, because the citation was an inference nobody registered and nothing enforced.

11.5 Registration as an admission gate. Registration is not a filing step that happens after a contract is authored; it is the gate at which a contract becomes real, and the gate ENFORCES the invariants of the earlier Parts rather than merely recording them. It refuses a contract with no evaluable postcondition (Part II). It refuses an unfalsifiable postcondition, one no observation could render false (Part IV). It refuses an assertion naming an evaluator that does not exist (Part IV's premise check, applied mechanically). It refuses an authority above the creator's rung, and it refuses a forged rung whose provenance the ladder does not entitle (Part V). It refuses an irreversible effect with no compensation path (Part VII). It refuses an infinite timeout. And it refuses a composite whose postcondition its constituents cannot entail (Part X). This composes spec_gate and done_gate rather than rivaling them: spec_gate already refuses large work with no spec, and a contract IS the spec of an interaction, so a registration refusal and a spec refusal are the same species of admission control applied to different surfaces. What DAIF-04 adds is the surface, not a second gate philosophy.

11.6 The shadow contract. The signature failure of this Part: two systems with a working understanding nobody wrote down. It is not a hypothetical and it is not rare — it is the default state of every estate that has not made registration mechanical, and it is invisible precisely because it WORKS. The provider behaves a certain way. The consumer relies on that behavior. Both are correct, both are tested, both are green, for months. Then either changes hands — a refactor, a fresh author, an amendment made in good faith by someone who read the source and saw no promise there because no promise was written — and the understanding evaporates without any party having done anything wrong. The shadow contract's defining property is that its breach has no defendant: the provider changed behavior it never promised to preserve, so it did not breach; the consumer relied on a promise never made, so it has no claim; and the damage is real and belongs to nobody. It is the purest form of the estate's founding failure — a promise discovered by its damage — and it is the reason registration cannot be optional.

11.7 Detecting shadow contracts. A rule nobody can enforce is a rule nobody keeps, so the register must be able to FIND the promises that were never registered, which is harder than refusing the ones that are offered. Three detection paths, none complete alone. Behavioral: the estate observes actual interactions — which component calls which, which field is read by whom — and any interaction crossing a boundary with no registered contract governing it is a shadow candidate. This composes the capability graph's edges rather than deriving a fresh call analysis. Textual: an integration paragraph, a code comment asserting what another component returns, a dataset's prose declaration of what it relies on a sibling to hold — each is a promise recorded in a medium that cannot evaluate it, which is Part I's definition of a prose convention, and each is a shadow contract with its own confession attached. Incidental: a breach with no registered contract is a shadow contract announcing itself the expensive way, and every such incident must produce a registration, not merely a fix. The three together give coverage; each alone gives a number that flatters.

11.8 Register invariants and adversarial cases. Four invariants. The register is authoritative — where the register and a component's own belief disagree about what was promised, the register wins, because the alternative is that every component holds a private truth and the estate has none. The register is append-and-version — an ACTIVE record is never edited in place, so a promise's history is legible. The register is queryable by both parties in both directions — a provider can enumerate who depends on it, which is what makes the removal block and the deprecation lifecycle enforceable, and a consumer can enumerate what it depends on, which is what makes an impact assessment possible before a change lands. And the register publishes: every admission, suspension, supersession, and breach is a typed event under Part VIII, carried by the transport that already exists. Two adversarial cases: the self-registered promise, in which a component registers a contract on behalf of a provider it does not own — refused, because registration requires the authority of the surface the contract binds; and the register-as-aspiration, in which contracts are registered for interactions that do not exist, inflating coverage with promises nobody has ever invoked. The countermetric for the second is invocation evidence, and Part XII owns it.

11.9 Metrics and countermetrics. Registered-contract count is the metric everyone reaches for and it is nearly worthless alone; its countermetric is the invoked-contract count, because a register full of promises nobody exercises is a filing cabinet with good posture. Shadow-contract discovery rate is countered by the detection method's own coverage — a falling discovery rate means either that the estate is registering its promises or that the detector has gone blind, and only the detector's own verification distinguishes them. Time-to-discovery, from a consumer's need to a bound contract, is countered by the wrong-binding rate, because a fast discovery that binds a consumer to the wrong version is worse than a slow one. And the age of the oldest record with no verification evidence is the register's honesty metric: it measures how long the estate has been relying on a promise it has not checked, and it cannot be gamed by adding records, only by checking them.

PART XI FINAL LAW. A contract not in the register does not exist — a consumer that acts on an unregistered promise has no claim and a provider that changes an unregistered behavior has committed no breach — because an unregistered promise is an assumption, and an assumption is the exact failure class the estate's premise discipline exists to kill; the register therefore holds identity, version, parties, status, owner, and the evidence of last verification, and it is not a filing cabinet but an ADMISSION GATE that mechanically refuses the empty postcondition, the unfalsifiable one, the nonexistent evaluator, the forged authority, the irreversible effect with no compensation, the infinite timeout, and the composite its parts cannot entail; and the failure it exists to end is the shadow contract, the working understanding nobody wrote down, whose defining property is that it functions perfectly for months and then breaks the moment either side changes hands, leaving a breach with no defendant, no claimant, and real damage belonging to nobody.

PART XII — OBSERVABILITY AND CONTRACT TELEMETRY

12.1 Mission. A fabric that cannot see itself is a fabric that will be trusted exactly as much as it deserves for exactly as long as nothing has gone wrong, and no longer. This Part defines what the contract fabric MEASURES, where each measurement comes from, and — with equal weight, because the metric alone is an invitation to game it — what countermetric prevents each from being satisfied by a lie. The Part opens with the law it composes rather than restates: CO-12's Telemetry-Before-Claims, which holds that no claim is admissible ahead of the measurement that grounds it. A dataset that asserts the fabric is healthy without a measured triple — the value, its source, and the instrument that produced it — has committed the same offence as a provider asserting a postcondition with no evidence, and Part IV already convicts that. Every metric below therefore names its data source explicitly, and where the source does not yet exist, the honest report is that the metric is UNMEASURED, which is a distinct state from zero and is never promoted to it.

12.2 Contract coverage, and why it is gated as an absolute. The first and most important measurement: how much of the estate's real interaction surface is actually typed. The naive form is a ratio — typed interactions over total interactions — and the naive form is forbidden, because a ratio gate is satisfied by SHRINKING ITS DENOMINATOR, which is DAIF-03's ratio law and the estate's own hard-won lesson. A component that stops counting an awkward interaction class raises coverage without typing anything. A capability-graph pass that fails to see a whole family of edges reports a denominator that flatters. The rule is therefore that coverage is gated as an ABSOLUTE on both terms: the count of typed interactions and the count of KNOWN untyped ones are each reported, each gated, and neither is permitted to move the other by arithmetic. The gate is on the untyped count falling and the typed count rising, independently. A build that types six interactions and discovers eleven new untyped ones has made the ratio worse and the estate better, and any instrument that reports that as a regression is measuring the wrong thing.

12.3 Coverage weighted by risk, and the naming of the remainder. Coverage has a second and subtler gaming path, and it is the central risk of the whole telemetry surface: coverage is trivially maximized by typing only the EASY interactions. A team under a coverage gate types the small, well-understood, low-consequence surfaces first — they are cheap, they are quick, they move the number — and leaves untyped exactly the interactions where a breach would cost the most, because those are hard, contested, and slow. The estate ends with high coverage and its most dangerous boundaries still governed by prose. The countermeasure has two clauses, both mandatory. First, coverage is WEIGHTED BY THE RISK of the surface it covers, where risk is a function of the blast radius of a breach — how many consumers rely, how irreversible the effects, how high the authority — so that typing one governance boundary counts for more than typing twelve advisory ones. Second, and non-negotiable, the untyped remainder is NAMED, not merely subtracted: every known untyped interaction is a registered item with an identity, an owner, and an estimated risk, held as institutional debt in DAIF-07, and the remainder is enumerable rather than a residue. A subtracted remainder is invisible. A named one is a backlog.

12.4 Breach rate by fault class. The metric is a rate, not a count, and it is decomposed by Part VI's four classes, because an undecomposed breach rate is a number that cannot be acted on. Data source: the breach records themselves, which Part VI makes mandatory without exception and which publish as typed events under Part VIII. The decomposition is what makes it useful — a provider-fault-dominated distribution says the providers are failing; a caller-fault-dominated distribution says the preconditions are misunderstood, badly documented, or wrong; an environment-fault-dominated distribution says the substrate is bad OR the classifier is self-absolving; and a contract-fault-dominated distribution says the estate is authoring bad standards, which is the most valuable signal of the four and the one nobody instruments. Countermetrics, one per gaming path: the waiver rate, because a contract that is waived rather than breached shows a clean rate; the amendment-weakening rate, because a provider that narrows its postconditions until nothing it does can be false has driven its breach rate to zero by ceasing to promise; and, on environment-fault specifically, the evidence rate on absolving classifications, because an environment-fault claim with no environment evidence is UNKNOWN and must be counted as such.

12.5 Time-to-detect. From the instant a promise was not kept to the instant a breach was recorded. This is the fabric's founding metric, because Part I's whole argument is that a breach discovered by its damage arrives late, far from its origin, and disguised as someone else's fault — and time-to-detect is the direct measurement of that distance. Data source: the breach record's detection timestamp against the interaction's occurrence timestamp, which is why Part VIII made timestamp semantics explicit rather than leaving a single unlabelled clock field for every subscriber to interpret privately. The countermetric is the blind-gate check and it is essential: a time-to-detect of zero because NO DETECTION EXISTS is not excellence, it is silence, and a contract whose evaluators have never been observed to refuse contributes no meaningful latency at all. Time-to-detect is therefore only admissible for contracts whose evaluators have been empirically observed to fire — the negative-clause and observed-refusal disciplines of Part III, composed here as a precondition on the metric's own validity. A gate that has never refused is indistinguishable from an inert one, and an inert gate detects everything instantly by detecting nothing.

12.6 Unverified-postcondition rate, and the age of the oldest unverified contract. Part IV's three-state model makes UNVERIFIED a terminal state distinct from satisfied and from breached, and this metric is what stops the estate from quietly collapsing it into the cheaper pole. It measures what fraction of postcondition evaluations completed WITHOUT their required evidence — a claim recorded, an assertion asserted, and no instrument independent of the provider having touched the world it describes. Its companion is the honest one and it is the metric a mature fabric watches most closely: the AGE OF THE OLDEST UNVERIFIED CONTRACT — the promise the estate has relied on longest without checking. This age cannot be gamed by adding contracts, cannot be gamed by narrowing definitions, and cannot be gamed by shrinking a denominator; it can only be reduced by actually verifying something, which is exactly the property a good metric has. Its countermetric is the verification-depth check: a verification that merely re-asserts what the provider claims, rather than observing the effect through an instrument the provider does not control, resets the age while proving nothing, which is Part IV's separation-of-powers failure surfacing on the telemetry surface as a cosmetically healthy number.

12.7 Shadow-contract discovery rate and schema-drift incidents. Shadow-contract discovery rate: how many unregistered promises the estate is finding per unit of time, from Part XI's three detection paths. Its interpretation is genuinely two-sided and must be reported as such — a FALLING discovery rate means either that the estate has registered its promises or that the detector has gone blind, and the two are indistinguishable from the number alone. The countermetric is therefore the detector's own coverage, verified by deliberate seeding: an unregistered interaction is introduced, and a detector that does not find it has failed, regardless of how clean its recent output looks. This is the estate's law that a gate never observed to refuse is indistinguishable from an inert one, applied to a discovery instrument rather than an admission one. Schema-drift incidents: the count of payload or output shape changes that landed without a version bump, detected after the fact. Data source is the version history against the observed shapes. The countermetric is the drift detector's semantic coverage, because a detector that compares field NAMES and never MEANINGS will report zero drift through a change that moved a unit, an encoding, or a definition while leaving every name intact — which is Part IX's subtlest breaking change, invisible to exactly the instrument most estates deploy against it.

12.8 The anti-Goodhart posture as a whole. Each metric above carries a countermetric, and the pattern across all of them is one shape repeated: every contract-fabric metric can be improved by REDUCING WHAT IS MEASURED rather than improving what is delivered. Coverage improves by not counting hard interactions. Breach rate improves by promising less. Time-to-detect improves by not detecting. Unverified rate improves by verifying shallowly. Discovery rate improves by looking with a duller instrument. The countermetrics are not garnish; they are the metric's other half, and reporting a metric without its countermetric is a Telemetry-Before-Claims violation dressed as diligence. The structural rule that falls out of this is that no contract-fabric metric may be gated ALONE. Every gate is a conjunction of a metric and its countermetric, both measured, both sourced, both reported — and a gate on a single number is refused at authoring, because a single number in this domain is always reducible to an act of not looking, and the estate has already learned, expensively, that an unrecognized idiom reads as zero and a zero can never fall.

12.9 Instrumentation and the honest UNMEASURED. Some of what this Part specifies is instrumented today from breach records, register queries, and the capability graph's edges; some of it is not, and the honest report distinguishes them. A metric with no instrument is UNMEASURED, and UNMEASURED is reported as itself — never as zero, never as healthy, never quietly omitted from a dashboard so that the remaining numbers look complete. The estate's own scar here is precise: a quantity that could not be measured was reported as zero, the gate compared zero against a floor, the floor was satisfied, and the gate passed BECAUSE it was blind. Every metric in this Part therefore carries, alongside its data source and its countermetric, a declaration of what it CANNOT see — which is the evaluator's negative clause from Part III applied to the fabric's own instruments, on the principle that the fabric may not demand of its constituents a discipline it declines to practise on itself.

PART XII FINAL LAW. The contract fabric measures coverage, breach rate by fault class, time-to-detect, unverified-postcondition rate, the age of the oldest unverified contract, shadow-contract discovery rate, and schema-drift incidents — each with a named data source, each with a countermetric, and none gated alone, because every metric in this domain improves when the estate simply looks at less: coverage rises by not counting the hard interactions, breach rate falls by promising less, time-to-detect goes to zero when no detector exists, and a discovery rate falls just as convincingly for a blind instrument as for a clean estate; coverage is therefore gated as an ABSOLUTE on both terms and never as a ratio that a shrinking denominator satisfies, it is WEIGHTED by the risk of the surface it covers so that the easy interactions cannot buy the number, and the untyped remainder is NAMED as institutional debt with an identity, an owner, and a risk rather than silently subtracted — because a subtracted remainder is invisible and a named one is a backlog, and no claim about the fabric's health is admissible ahead of the measurement that grounds it.

PART XIII — TESTING, VERIFICATION, AND THE CONTRACT AS A TEST ORACLE

13.1 Mission. The claim of this Part is that a typed contract is EXECUTABLE KNOWLEDGE, and the claim is literal rather than rhetorical. Part IV defined a precondition and a postcondition as a triple of resolvable subject, decidable predicate, and named evaluator. That is not merely LIKE a test — it IS a test, missing nothing: the subject is the thing under examination, the predicate is the assertion, and the evaluator is the runner. The consequence is that a well-typed contract does not need a test to be written for it; it needs to be RUN. This inverts the usual relationship, in which tests are authored separately from the promise and drift from it silently, each expressing a different author's understanding of what the component was supposed to do. When the contract is the oracle, the test cannot drift from the promise, because they are the same object. What remains, and what this Part defines, is the discipline around execution: who runs it, how often, against what, and — the hardest clause — what it means that a verification EXPIRES.

13.2 Contract tests: does the provider satisfy what it promised. The first execution mode. Take the contract's preconditions, arrange a world in which they hold, invoke the provider, and evaluate the postconditions with the named evaluators. The arrangement is AAA in the estate's existing discipline and DAIF-04 does not restate it. What the contract adds is that the assertions are not authored by the test writer from an interpretation of the requirements; they are READ FROM THE REGISTER, which means a change to the promise changes the test mechanically and a test that passes against an old version fails to even compile against a new one. Two invariants govern. First, the evaluator must be one the provider does not control for at least the class of postconditions whose falsity is most expensive — Part IV's separation of powers, executed. Second, the test must exercise the REFUSAL path as well as the satisfaction path: a contract test that only ever arranges worlds in which the preconditions hold has never verified that the provider actually REFUSES when they do not, and an unverified refusal is exactly the inert-gate failure the estate has already paid for.

13.3 Consumer-driven tests: does the provider satisfy what its consumers actually rely on. The second execution mode, and it answers a different question, which is why both exist. A provider's contract states what it promises. Its consumers rely on some SUBSET of that — and, dangerously, sometimes on things OUTSIDE it. A consumer-driven test is authored from the consumer's actual reliance: for each field the consumer reads, each assertion it assumes, each ordering it depends on, an assertion is generated and run against the live provider. The output is a reliance profile, and comparing it to the registered contract is where the real information is. Two gaps appear and they mean opposite things. Reliance NARROWER than the promise: the provider is promising more than anyone uses, which is over-specification — not a defect, but a maintenance burden and a candidate for a deliberate, versioned narrowing that costs nothing because nobody relies on the part being removed. Reliance BROADER than the promise: the consumer is depending on behavior the provider never undertook to preserve, which is a shadow contract with a live consumer already committed to it, and it is a defect that will detonate on the provider's next entirely-legitimate change.

13.4 The drift between the two, and what it tells you. The gap between what is promised and what is relied upon is the fabric's single most diagnostic quantity, and neither test mode reveals it alone. Contract tests can be green while every consumer is broken, if the consumers rely on things outside the contract. Consumer-driven tests can be green while the contract is a fiction, if the consumers happen to rely only on the subset the provider still honors and the rest of the promise has quietly rotted. Running both and DIFFING them is what produces the actionable finding, and the diff resolves three ways. Promised-and-relied: the healthy core, and the part that must never be weakened without the full Part IX governance event. Promised-not-relied: dead promise, a candidate for a cheap deliberate narrowing, but only after checking that "not relied" is not an artifact of an incomplete consumer census — a consumer the register does not know about is a consumer whose reliance is invisible, which is exactly why Part XI makes binding visible. And relied-not-promised: the live shadow, the highest-priority finding the fabric produces, and the one that must become a registered contract or an eliminated dependency, never a note somebody meant to get to.

13.5 The green signal is a hypothesis. SQI composed, directly and without restatement: a contract test that is AUTHORED but never EXECUTED protects nothing, and a green result is a hypothesis until it is reconciled against the world it claims to describe. The estate's scars here are specific and they all apply to this surface. A gate whose vocabulary could not recognize the idiom it was counting returned zero and passed a floor. A test suite whose fixtures encoded the old meaning stayed green through a semantic change that broke every real consumer. A component that was unit-green and cited by name was never called by anything. Each is the same shape: a signal that was structurally incapable of being anything other than green. The discipline that follows is threefold. A contract test must have been observed to FAIL at least once, on a deliberately-broken provider, or its passing tells the estate nothing about the provider and only something about the test. A contract test that runs against a mock rather than the live provider is testing the mock, and the mock is the consumer's own recorded assumption, which is precisely the artifact that does NOT move when the provider does. And a suite whose failure has never been observed is UNVERIFIED in the exact sense of Part IV.

13.6 The expiry invariant. The hardest clause in this Part and the one that costs real discipline: a contract's verification has an EXPIRY. A promise verified once and never re-checked is a HISTORICAL FACT, not a current guarantee — it records that on some past day, in some past state of the world, a provider satisfied an assertion. It says nothing about today. The world moved: the substrate was upgraded, a dependency changed, a sibling contract was amended, the data the provider reads now has a shape it did not have. Every one of those can falsify a postcondition without a single line of the provider changing, which is why "the provider hasn't changed, so the verification still holds" is a fallacy and a common one. Each contract therefore declares a verification validity window — a DAIF-01 temporal type on the verification record, not on the contract — and a contract whose last verification has aged past its window returns to UNVERIFIED. It is not breached, it is not suspended, and it is emphatically not satisfied: it is a promise the estate no longer knows the truth of, which is why the age of the oldest unverified contract is the metric Part XII treats as the fabric's honesty gauge.

13.7 What the expiry does not license. Two misreadings must be closed. The expiry does not license continuous re-verification of everything, which is a cost with no ceiling and would make the fabric a tax rather than an asset. The window is set per contract, proportional to the RISK of the surface — a governance contract with an irreversible effect and a wide consumer scope has a short window; an advisory contract inside a single feature has a long one — and the setting is a term of the contract, authored at creation by the party that owns it, which is the only moment it can be reasoned about calmly. Nor does the expiry license treating an expired verification as a breach: a provider whose verification aged out has not failed anything, and recording a breach against it would corrupt the very breach rate Part XII relies on, converting a measurement of delivery into a measurement of how recently anyone bothered to look. Expired means UNKNOWN, and UNKNOWN is a call to measure, an obligation on the accountable owner, and an item DAIF-07 ages and escalates — never a verdict on the provider.

13.8 Adversarial cases. Four. The tautological contract test: a test whose assertions are generated from the provider's own output rather than from the registered postconditions, so it passes by construction and would pass against any behavior whatsoever — the self-certifying loop of Part IV, wearing a test suite. The mock-frozen consumer: a consumer whose tests run entirely against a recorded fixture, so it is green forever including through every breaking change the provider makes, and it is the mechanism by which a silent breaking change converts every consumer's passing test into a lie simultaneously. The verification-refresh-without-verification: a party that resets the verification timestamp on a contract by re-running a suite that never actually reached the provider — the environment was down, the invocation was skipped, the suite exited zero having asserted nothing — which resets the honesty gauge while proving nothing, and whose defense is that a verification record must carry the EVIDENCE, not merely the outcome. And the narrowing-under-test-pressure provider, which responds to a failing contract test by amending the postcondition down until it passes; the defense is Part IX, where that amendment is a breaking change requiring the full governance event, and Part VI, where a version history of monotonically weakening guarantees is itself the signal that a promise has been narrowed until it cannot be breached.

13.9 Metrics and countermetrics. Contract-test execution rate — the fraction of registered contracts whose tests actually RUN, not merely exist — is countered by the observed-failure rate, because a suite that has never failed is a suite that may be structurally incapable of failing. Reliance-diff coverage, the fraction of consumer bindings for which a reliance profile has been computed, is countered by the consumer-census completeness, since a diff over an incomplete consumer set will report "promised-not-relied" for a promise that a consumer the register never knew about depends on entirely. Live-provider ratio — the fraction of consumer tests exercising the real provider rather than a fixture — is one of the few metrics whose direction is unambiguous, and its countermetric is test flakiness, because an estate that mocks everything is buying its green from a fixture and an estate that mocks nothing may be buying its red from the substrate. And verification freshness is countered by verification depth: a fresh, shallow verification that re-asserts the provider's own claim is worse than an aged, deep one, because it looks current and proves nothing.

PART XIII FINAL LAW. A typed contract is executable knowledge — its preconditions and postconditions ARE the test, read from the register rather than re-authored from an interpretation, so a promise and its verification cannot drift because they are one object — and the fabric therefore runs two modes and DIFFS them: the contract test asks whether the provider satisfies what it promised, the consumer-driven test asks whether it satisfies what its consumers actually rely on, and the gap between the two is the fabric's most diagnostic quantity, with "relied-not-promised" naming a live shadow already committed to and "promised-not-relied" naming a dead promise safe to narrow only after the consumer census is proven complete; a green signal is a hypothesis until reconciled — a suite never observed to fail is structurally indistinguishable from a suite that cannot fail, and a consumer testing against its own recorded fixture is testing the artifact that does NOT move when the provider does; and a verification EXPIRES, because a promise checked once and never re-checked is a historical fact rather than a current guarantee, since the world can falsify a postcondition without the provider changing a single line.

PART XIV — MIGRATION AND EVOLUTION

14.1 Mission. Every rule in this dataset has been stated as though the estate were being built fresh, and it is not. The estate exists, it works, and its interactions are governed today by prose conventions, comments, integration paragraphs, and habits — thousands of them, most of them functioning, none of them typed. This Part answers the only question that decides whether DAIF-04 is a real system or an elegant one: how does an estate that already runs on untyped promises get to typed ones WITHOUT a rewrite? The answer has four parts — incremental adoption ordered by risk, the shim contract as an honest wrapper over a legacy interaction, the coexistence discipline that lets typed and untyped surfaces run side by side without either lying about the other, and the registration of the untyped remainder as institutional debt rather than a residue nobody counts. And it has one anti-pattern, stated as strongly as anything in this corpus, because it is the failure mode a fabric of this kind reliably produces when it is adopted with enthusiasm rather than discipline: the big-bang rollout.

14.2 Incremental adoption, ordered by risk. The order in which surfaces are typed is not a matter of convenience and it is where most adoptions go wrong, for exactly the reason Part XII named: the easy surfaces are the tempting ones, they move the coverage number, and they are almost never the ones where a breach costs anything. The ordering rule is therefore that the HIGHEST-RISK surfaces are typed first, where risk is the blast radius of a breach — how many consumers rely on the promise, how irreversible its effects, how high the authority under which it acts, and how far a breach would propagate before it surfaced. That ordering is uncomfortable by construction: the highest-risk surfaces are the contested ones, the ones with several stakeholders and an unclear owner and a semantics nobody wrote down, and they are slow. The discomfort is the point. An adoption that begins with the easy surfaces produces a rising number and a fabric that protects nothing, and it produces it fast enough to be declared a success before anyone notices that the boundaries where the estate actually bleeds are still governed by a paragraph. Type the surfaces where a breach would hurt, and accept a coverage curve that looks worse and is worth more.

14.3 The shim contract. A legacy interaction cannot always be typed properly on first contact: the provider's real behavior is not fully known, its preconditions were never stated, its failure semantics are whatever the code happens to do, and discovering all of that may cost more than the surface is worth right now. The shim contract is the honest intermediate. It is a typed wrapper over an untyped interaction that states EXACTLY what it can guarantee today and — this is the whole of its value — states EXPLICITLY what it cannot yet guarantee. It carries real postconditions for the properties that have been observed and verified, and for the properties that have not, it carries a declared UNKNOWN rather than an optimistic assertion. Its negative clause is long and it is supposed to be. A shim that says "this interaction returns an object of this shape, and we have verified that; whether it is idempotent is UNKNOWN; whether it is atomic under partial failure is UNKNOWN; its timeout is observed to be under this bound but is not guaranteed" is enormously more valuable than the paragraph it replaces, because every one of those UNKNOWNs is now a NAMED gap with an owner rather than an assumption a consumer will make silently and wrongly.

14.4 The shim's invariants. Three, and they are what separate a shim from a lie. First, a shim may never assert a guarantee it has not verified — the temptation is precisely the opposite, because a shim full of UNKNOWNs looks like a poor contract and a shim full of confident assertions looks like a good one, and the second is a fabrication that a consumer will rely on. Second, a shim is REGISTERED as a shim: its status is visible, so a consumer reading it knows it is binding to a partially-characterized promise and can decide whether its own risk tolerance permits that. A shim that presents as a full contract has laundered uncertainty into confidence across a boundary, which is Part X's silent-weakening failure arriving through the migration surface. Third, every UNKNOWN in a shim is an item of institutional debt, registered in DAIF-07 with an owner and an age, so the shim's incompleteness is a tracked obligation that ages and escalates rather than a permanent state that everyone stops seeing. A shim is a stage, not a destination, and the only mechanism that keeps it from becoming a destination is that its gaps are visibly aging in a queue somebody owns.

14.5 Coexistence. During a migration that will take a long time — and it will — typed and untyped surfaces run side by side, and the discipline is that NEITHER may lie about the other. A typed contract may not assume that an untyped provider it depends on behaves as its own postconditions require, because nothing binds that provider to anything; the correct model is that a typed contract depending on an untyped surface has an UNVERIFIED precondition at that boundary, and it must say so. This is uncomfortable and it is honest: it means a typed contract's guarantee is capped by the weakest untyped thing beneath it, which is Part X's derivation rules extended across the typed/untyped frontier — the composite's authority is the lowest, its failure semantics the union, and an untyped constituent contributes an UNKNOWN failure semantics, which unions to UNKNOWN. That cap is the truth of the estate's current state and the fabric's job is to make it visible, not to paper over it. The alternative — a typed contract that asserts a clean guarantee resting on an uncharacterized foundation — is worse than no contract at all, because it manufactures exactly the false confidence that the next subsection convicts.

14.6 The untyped remainder as registered debt. The most important clause of the Part, and the one that decides whether the migration converges or stalls indefinitely at a comfortable plateau. Every known untyped interaction is REGISTERED — given an identity, an owner, and an estimated risk — and held as institutional debt in DAIF-07, where it ages, escalates, and terminates at owner_queue if it will not move. It is not tolerated, not deferred, not left as a residue that the coverage arithmetic quietly subtracts. The reason is mechanical rather than moral: a subtracted remainder is invisible, and an invisible remainder never gets smaller, because nothing anywhere is obliged to reduce it and no signal ever fires about it. A NAMED remainder is a backlog, and a backlog with owners and ages is a thing the estate will eventually drain, because DAIF-07's obligation machinery is built for exactly that and DAIF-04 does not fork a single stage of it. The corollary is the discovery obligation: the remainder must be actively FOUND, through Part XI's shadow-contract detection paths, because a remainder consisting only of the untyped interactions somebody happened to remember is a remainder that flatters the estate about how much of its surface it has actually seen.

14.7 The big-bang anti-pattern. The failure this Part exists to prevent, and it is the failure a fabric like this one invites, because the fabric is convincing and the temptation to apply it everywhere at once is genuine. A large body of contracts is authored in a short period — generated from source, inferred from behavior, written by a task force — and registered. Coverage leaps. The dashboard is excellent. And nothing verifies them: their postconditions have never been evaluated against a live provider, their evaluators may not exist, their preconditions were inferred rather than observed, and their failure semantics were filled in from a template. What the estate now has is a large body of contracts that NOBODY VERIFIES and therefore, correctly, NOBODY TRUSTS — and this is strictly WORSE than having no contracts at all. Not equivalent. Worse. Because with no contracts, a consumer knows it must ask, must check, must be careful. With a large body of unverified ones, the consumer reads a confident postcondition and relies on it, and the estate has MANUFACTURED FALSE CONFIDENCE at scale — which is Part I's founding failure, industrialized, with better tooling and a nicer report.

14.8 Why the big-bang fails specifically, and what defeats it. It fails for a reason worth naming precisely: a contract's value comes from its VERIFICATION, not from its existence, and verification does not scale the way authoring does. Authoring a thousand contracts is a week of generation. Verifying a thousand contracts means arranging a thousand worlds, invoking a thousand providers, and observing a thousand postconditions through instruments the providers do not control — and that work is irreducible, because it is the work of actually finding out what is true. An adoption that authors faster than it verifies accumulates unverified contracts, and by Part XIII's expiry invariant those contracts are UNVERIFIED, which means the register is full of records in the state that means the estate does not know. The defense is a throughput rule and it is the whole discipline of a sane migration: a contract is not registered ACTIVE until it has been VERIFIED ONCE against a live provider, so the adoption's rate is bounded by its verification capacity rather than its authoring capacity. Contracts authored ahead of that capacity sit in DRAFTED, which is honest, visible, and binds nobody.

14.9 Evolution beyond migration, and the fabric's own honest limit. When the migration converges, the fabric does not become static — it becomes the estate's normal way of changing. New interactions are born typed, because registration is an admission gate and an unregistered promise cannot be relied upon. Existing contracts evolve through Part IX's governance events. Shadow contracts continue to be discovered, because an estate that stops finding them has almost certainly stopped looking. And the fabric measures itself under Part XII's countermetrics, which are built precisely to resist the flattering number a mature fabric will otherwise produce. The dataset closes on the blind spot it declared in Part I and has now earned the right to restate: DAIF-04 types the promise and detects the breach, but it cannot make a badly-designed component good, and it cannot make a wrong guarantee right. A perfectly-typed contract over a wrong guarantee is a wrong guarantee that is now precisely stated, verified on schedule, and breached by nobody — which is a real improvement, because a precisely-stated wrong guarantee is at last something the estate can SEE, argue about, and amend, and that is the entire distance between a promise governed by prose and a promise governed by a type.

PART XIV FINAL LAW. An estate that already runs on untyped promises reaches typed ones by typing its HIGHEST-RISK surfaces first — accepting a coverage curve that looks worse and is worth more, because the easy surfaces are the tempting ones and almost never the ones where a breach costs anything — by wrapping legacy interactions in shim contracts that assert only what has been verified and declare every remaining gap an explicit UNKNOWN registered as aging debt, by letting typed and untyped surfaces coexist under the rule that a typed contract resting on an uncharacterized foundation is capped by that foundation and must say so, and by NAMING the untyped remainder as institutional debt with an identity, an owner, and an age rather than subtracting it into invisibility, since an invisible remainder never shrinks and a named one is a backlog the obligation fabric will eventually drain; and it reaches none of that through a big-bang rollout, which authors a thousand confident contracts faster than anything can verify them and thereby manufactures false confidence at scale — strictly WORSE than no contracts at all, because with no contract a consumer knows it must check, while with an unverified one it reads a confident postcondition and relies on it, which is the founding failure of this entire dataset industrialized, with better tooling and a nicer report.
