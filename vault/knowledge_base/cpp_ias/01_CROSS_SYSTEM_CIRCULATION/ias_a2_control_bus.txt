===============================================================================================
PART I — WHY THE ENSEMBLE NEEDS A CIRCULATORY SYSTEM AND NOT A THICKER PIPE
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding observation. Claude Power Pack, as OBSERVED on disk on 2026-07-12, already ships
at least three artifacts whose names promise cross-system communication: PM-03, the findings bus
that lets parallel panes on one repository publish and consume discoveries; cross_signal_bus, a
local relay that moves a signal from one hook or module to another inside a single session; and
CEPS, a context/event propagation substrate that threads state through a session's tool calls.
Each of these is real, each is useful, and each was built to solve exactly the problem its name
describes: coordination WITHIN one repository, one session, or one call chain. None of them was
built to solve, and by IAS-F1's Article II none of them may legitimately be extended to solve, the
problem this dataset exists for: how does a FINDING produced by the Immune System (IAS-D2) reach
the Observability Fabric (IAS-E1) when the two were never in the same pane, the same session, or
the same repository, and how does a STATE change in the Capability Portfolio (IAS-C1) reach the
Architecture Intelligence system (IAS-G1) that must react to it, when both are SYSTEMS in F1's
sense (Part II §2.1) rather than panes in PM's sense. (DERIVED: the transport problem PM-03 solves
is a pane-to-pane problem; the transport problem IAS-A2 solves is a system-to-system problem; these
are different levels by IAS-F1 Part VIII's formal test, because the object of PM-03's capability is
an enumerable resource — the set of panes on one repository — while the object of IAS-A2's
capability is the relation graph over the whole ensemble of systems, which is not enumerable
within any single existing system's scope.)

1.2 The trap of a thicker pipe. The natural reflex, confronted with three partial transports that
each stop at a boundary, is to build a fourth transport and make it wider — a "universal bus" that
simply carries more traffic than PM-03, cross_signal_bus, or CEPS individually. That reflex fails
the Non-Duplication Ledger's own test for exactly the reason IAS-F1 Part VII names: a wider pipe
that still moves data as an unstructured payload between two named endpoints is a FIRST-ORDER
capability, however much traffic it carries, because its direct object remains a resource — a
message, a session, a pane — rather than another capability or system. (DERIVED from IAS-F1 §7.2:
volume of traffic is not one of the second-order tests; only the object's TYPE decides level.) What
makes IAS-A2 admissible under Article II is not that it carries more events than its three
absorbed candidates; it is that its object is the TYPED, ADDRESSED, AUTHORITY-AWARE exchange
between SYSTEMS — an object none of PM-03, cross_signal_bus, or CEPS was built to hold, because
each addresses panes, hooks, or call-frames, never a stable system identity per IAS-F1 Part IX.

1.3 What "control bus" means in this corpus, precisely. A CONTROL BUS, as DEFINED here, is the
second-order capability that moves four typed artifacts — EVENT, STATE, AUTHORITY-REQUEST, and
EVIDENCE (each specified fully in Parts V through VII) — between systems addressed by their IAS-F1
stable ID (Part IX §9.2), under a uniform envelope contract (Part IV), with delivery guarantees
appropriate to the artifact's criticality (Part XV), and with the arbitration machinery needed
when two systems' claims about the same object conflict (Part XII). It is not a message queue in
the general-purpose sense; a general message queue is agnostic to what it carries and to who its
endpoints are, while IAS-A2's bus is deliberately NOT agnostic — every event has one of a closed
set of types, every endpoint has an F1-resolved identity, and every authority-bearing event
resolves against IAS-F1 Invariant Three (Part V §5.4, exactly one final authority per decision
class). This specificity is what converts "another queue" into a genuinely second-order capability:
the bus does not merely move bytes, it enforces the ensemble's own bookkeeping rules on every
message it carries.

1.4 Why absorption, not deletion, of the three candidates it merges. Per NON_DUPLICATION_LEDGER.md,
Candidate 4 (Cognitive Service Mesh) and Candidate 5 (Cross-System State Sync) are dispositioned
MERGE-INTO-FAMILY into IAS-A2, and Candidate 10 (Institutional Event Fabric) likewise MERGE, while
Candidate 7 (Multi-System Conflict Resolver) is a SUBENGINE. Article I forbids treating an overlap
as mere deletion; each absorbed candidate contributes a distinct FACET of the control bus rather
than being discarded. The service-mesh candidate contributes the UNIFORM CONTRACT discipline (Part
VIII): heterogeneous systems, built at different times with different internal conventions, must
speak one envelope format at the boundary even though their internals never change. The state-sync
candidate contributes the CROSS-SYSTEM STATE SYNCHRONIZATION discipline (Parts IX-X): the bus
carries not only transient events but durable state deltas that keep registries, memory, runtime,
and evidence stores mutually consistent across systems that never share a process. The event-fabric
candidate contributes the EVENT TAXONOMY discipline (Parts V-VII): a local event, once it crosses a
system boundary and is judged reusable by more than its originating system, is promoted to an
institutional signal with its own address and provenance, rather than remaining an ephemeral log
line. The conflict-resolver candidate is folded in as a SUBENGINE (Part XII) rather than a merged
facet, because arbitration between systems is a distinct operation — invoked only when Invariant
Two or Invariant Three is under live violation — layered on top of, rather than identical with, the
ordinary carry-and-deliver function of the bus.

1.5 Why this is a Tier-1 dataset and what it presupposes. IAS-A2 is built directly on IAS-F1: every
event this dataset defines carries an F1 stable-ID-addressed sender and receiver (Part IX §9.2),
every authority-request resolves against F1's authority model (Part X) and Invariant Three (Part V
§5.4), and every state-synchronization operation is, at bottom, an operation that keeps the
ensemble STATE (Part III §3.3) coherent as systems mutate their own local properties (Part IV).
Without F1's ontology already sealed, IAS-A2 would have to invent its own vocabulary for "system,"
"authority," and "state," reproducing exactly the fragmentation F1 was built to prevent. (NORMATIVE:
this dependency is not incidental — Part XX resolves it explicitly against F1's own per-sibling
resolution inventory.) IAS-A2 is also, per COMPOUNDING_GRAPH.md, the literal substrate three of the
four named compounding loops move along: the primary loop's "mission executes across systems via
IAS-A2 Control Bus," the defense loop's "IAS-A2 Control Bus carries failure events," and — by
extension of the same mechanism — every FEEDS and CONSUMES edge in IAS-F1's typed relation graph
(Part VI §6.2, §6.7) that crosses a system boundary is, in the live estate, a transaction this bus
either carries or fails to carry. A federation without this bus does not merely lack convenience; it
lacks the mechanism by which its own compounding graph could be anything other than a diagram.

1.6 What "circulatory system," the title metaphor, precisely licenses and where it breaks down. This
dataset's own header names IAS-A2 the ensemble's circulatory system, and the metaphor is useful
enough to be worth pressing on rather than merely decorating with. A circulatory system carries
nutrients, signals, and waste between organs that do not directly touch one another, without any
organ needing to understand any other organ's internals — exactly the boundary-only uniformity
principle of Part VIII. It also, in a healthy body, carries traffic CONTINUOUSLY and asynchronously,
never waiting for the heart and the liver to be simultaneously "paying attention" to each other —
exactly the honest-IPC discipline of Part XIV. Where the metaphor breaks down, and this dataset
states the break explicitly rather than letting the metaphor overclaim: a biological circulatory
system has no analogue for AUTHORITY (an organ does not need permission to receive oxygen), while
IAS-A2's own object includes authority-request routing and arbitration (Parts VI, XII, XIII) as a
first-class concern with no biological equivalent — the metaphor illuminates the TRANSPORT half of
this dataset's object well and says nothing useful about the GOVERNANCE half, which is why this
dataset's own name pairs "control bus" (governance-flavored) with "event fabric"
(transport-flavored) rather than relying on the circulatory metaphor alone.

1.7 The relationship between this dataset and the Owner's own prior operational doctrine on
cross-pane and cross-session findings buses. A grounding observation worth making explicit before the
formal Parts begin: the Owner's own accumulated operational memory, OBSERVED across this dataset's
own grounding materials, already names PM-03 findings-bus behavior, cross-signal relay patterns, and
staleness-detection discipline as load-bearing, hard-won mechanisms — not experimental proposals but
production patterns with their own documented failure modes and fixes. IAS-A2 treats this operational
history as EVIDENCE rather than as raw material to be reinvented: every design choice in this dataset
that touches durability, liveness assumptions, or crash recovery (most concentrated in Part XIV) is
checked against, and where possible DERIVED from, patterns the Owner's own operational doctrine has
already independently validated at smaller scale. This is the concrete sense in which IAS-A2 is not a
green-field design exercise but a generalization of mechanisms the estate has already paid to learn
work, lifted from their original bounded scope to the ensemble scope this dataset's object requires.

FINAL LAW — PART I. A federation of seventy systems that can each speak only within their own
pane, session, or call-frame has no circulatory system, only seventy sealed vessels; the control
bus is not a wider pipe between two named endpoints but the second-order capability that makes
event, state, authority, and evidence flow BETWEEN systems as a first-class, addressed, typed,
authority-aware operation — and it is the literal substrate the compounding graph's own arrows
are drawn along.

===============================================================================================
PART II — RESOLUTION AGAINST THE FEDERATION ONTOLOGY: A2 AS A SECOND-ORDER CAPABILITY
===============================================================================================

2.1 Running the systemhood test. IAS-F1 Part II §2.1 requires four conjunctive properties before an
artifact counts as a system: distinct object, authority, interface, addressability. IAS-A2's
distinct object, per §1.3 above, is the typed cross-system exchange of event/state/authority/
evidence — an object no absorbed candidate or strengthened parent owns whole (§1.4, §1.5 already
demonstrate this by construction). Its authority is bounded and specific: the bus MAY arbitrate a
live Invariant Two or Invariant Three violation when invoked as the conflict-resolution subengine
(Part XII), but it holds no final authority over any system's own internal decisions — a boundary
Part X states as a hard rule, not a convenience. Its interface is the envelope contract of Part IV:
publish, subscribe, deliver, acknowledge. Its addressability is inherited directly from IAS-F1 Part
IX: every publisher and subscriber on the bus is addressed by stable ID, never by file path or
pane handle, so a system's relocation or refactor (Part IX §9.1) does not break its subscriptions.
All four tests pass; IAS-A2 is a system in F1's sense. (DERIVED.)

2.2 Running the second-order test. IAS-F1 Part VII §7.2 supplies the mechanical test: what does the
capability DIRECTLY act upon? If a resource, first-order; if another capability or system, second-
order. IAS-A2's direct object, on every one of its four artifact types, is never a resource in the
Part II §2.4 sense — it is always the RELATION between two or more systems' capabilities: an event
relates a producing system's output to a consuming system's input; a state-sync relates one
system's property record (F1 Part IV) to another's dependent computation; an authority-request
relates a requesting system's need for a decision to the one system holding final authority over
that decision class (F1 Invariant Three); evidence relates one system's claim to another system's
verification of it. None of the four artifact types has a resource as its direct object — a session,
a pane, a model call never appears as the THING the bus acts upon, only, occasionally, as data
carried inside an event's payload, which by IAS-F1 Part VIII §8.4's majority-object rule does not
determine the capability's level. IAS-A2 passes the second-order test cleanly. (DERIVED, applying
F1 §8.4 directly.)

2.3 Distinguishing the bus from the AGGREGATOR and DELEGATOR near-misses. IAS-F1 Part VII §7.7 names
three shapes that read as second-order but fail on inspection. IAS-A2 must be checked against two
of them explicitly, because a naive control bus is exactly the shape those near-misses warn about.
The DELEGATOR NEAR-MISS is a capability that "forwards a request to whichever system claims to
handle it, without evaluating, ranking, or deciding among candidates — a lookup table dressed as a
router." A control bus that merely relayed bytes from a named publisher to a named subscriber, with
no envelope validation, no authority check, and no conflict detection, would be exactly this — a
lookup table with an address book. IAS-A2 avoids the near-miss because its delivery contract (Part
IV §4.3) is not pure forwarding: it validates envelope conformance against the uniform contract
(Part VIII), it can and does reject a malformed or unauthorized authority-request (Part XIII), and
it invokes real arbitration logic (Part XII) when two systems' claims collide — the capability CAN
be wrong about a delivery decision and be held accountable for it, which is precisely the
distinguishing question F1 §7.7 poses. The AGGREGATOR NEAR-MISS is a capability whose object is a
report rather than a decision. IAS-A2 avoids this because its primary output, on the authority-
request and conflict-resolution paths, is a DECISION (who receives final authority, which claim
resolves a duplicate detection) rather than a summary; the observability-flavored side effects of
the bus — event logs, delivery metrics — are downstream artifacts consumed by IAS-E1, not IAS-A2's
own direct object.

2.4 IAS-A2's place in IAS-F1's typed relation graph. Because IAS-A2 is itself a system, it
participates in the very graph it carries traffic for, and its own edges are worth stating
explicitly as a self-consistency check. IAS-A2 is FED BY every publishing system (an inbound feeds
edge per system that emits events); IAS-A2 FEEDS every subscribing system (an outbound feeds edge
per system that consumes them); IAS-A2 STRENGTHENS PM-03, cross_signal_bus, CEPS, liveness, and
owner_queue (per the Strengthening Matrix row, §header above), because each of those five parents
now produces more value with IAS-A2 present than without it — PM-03's pane-local findings become
reusable ensemble-wide once promoted (Part XI); cross_signal_bus's local relay gains a system-level
peer it can escalate to (Part XIV); CEPS's context propagation gains a cross-session carrier for the
context it would otherwise lose at session boundary (Part X); liveness gains a standard channel for
its staleness signal to reach every interested system (Part XVI, evaluation); owner_queue gains a
uniform inbound format for authority-requests that need Owner adjudication (Part XIII §13.5).
IAS-A2 also DEPENDS-ON IAS-F1 itself, in the strong sense of F1 Part VI §6.3 — a bus with no shared
address space for its endpoints cannot function at all, not merely degrade. (DERIVED, cross-
checked against the Strengthening Matrix and Compounding Graph.)

2.5 The bus is not the message; the bus is the contract. A recurring confusion this Part exists to
foreclose: IAS-A2 is not "the events." Events are artifacts the bus carries (Part V-VII); the bus
itself is the CAPABILITY — the contract, the delivery guarantee, the authority check, the
arbitration logic — that governs how those artifacts move. This distinction matters for the
Non-Duplication Ledger's own future audits: a proposal that builds "a new event type" is not
automatically a new system, and is very likely a facet of IAS-A2 (a new taxonomy entry, Parts V-VII)
rather than a competing bus. Conversely, a proposal that builds a second delivery mechanism with its
own envelope format, its own authority-check logic, and its own arbitration path IS a candidate
DUPLICATES relation against IAS-A2 under IAS-F1 Invariant Two (Part V §5.3), and must be resolved
by a named D2A disposition before both may stand. (NORMATIVE: this Part is the reference future
proposals are checked against, mirroring the role IAS-F1 Part VII §7.6 plays for the whole family.)

2.6 Worked contrast: a synthetic proposal tested against §2.1-§2.3. Consider a synthetic,
illustrative-only proposal: "a shared log file every system appends its findings to, which any
system may tail." Object(X): the log file, an enumerable resource. Level(X), by IAS-F1 Part VIII
§8.3: RESOURCE, because nothing about the proposal decides or arbitrates anything — it is pure
storage with no delivery contract, no authority check, and no addressing beyond a file path.
Second-order test: FAILS, because the direct object is a file, not a relation between capabilities.
This synthetic proposal is a genuine AGGREGATOR/DELEGATOR near-miss double failure, and it is
exactly the shape a control bus degenerates into if Parts IV, VIII, XII, and XIII of this dataset
are skipped in a future implementation — a risk named explicitly in this dataset's ANTI-PATTERNS
section (near the file's end) precisely because "just use a shared log" is the single most likely
under-engineering failure of a control-bus build.

FINAL LAW — PART II. IAS-A2 passes the systemhood test, the second-order test, and both named
near-miss checks not because it moves more data than its absorbed candidates, but because its
direct object is the RELATION between systems' capabilities — the delivery, the authority check,
the arbitration — and a future bus that reduces to a shared log or a pure forwarding table has
not built this dataset's object, only borrowed its name.

===============================================================================================
PART III — PRIMITIVE OBJECTS: EVENT, ENVELOPE, CHANNEL, SUBSCRIPTION, AUTHORITY-REQUEST
===============================================================================================

3.1 Why the bus needs its own primitive layer, distinct from F1's. IAS-F1 Part II fixed four
primitives — system, subsystem, engine, capability — deliberately minimal so as not to pre-judge
the mechanisms later datasets would design (F1 §2.5). IAS-A2 is exactly the dataset F1 deferred that
judgment to for the transport domain, and it now DEFINES the five primitives a control bus requires:
EVENT, ENVELOPE, CHANNEL, SUBSCRIPTION, and AUTHORITY-REQUEST. Each is built from F1's primitives
rather than beside them — an EVENT's sender and receiver are F1 SYSTEMS, a CHANNEL is scoped to an
F1 relation type, and so on — so that the bus's own object model never drifts from the ontology it
depends on absolutely (per §1.5, §2.4).

3.2 EVENT, defined. An EVENT is DEFINED as a single, immutable, timestamped fact produced by one
system, belonging to exactly one of the five taxonomy types of Parts V-VII (FINDING, STATE-CHANGE,
ALERT, AUTHORITY-REQUEST, EVIDENCE), and addressed to either a specific subscriber system, a named
CHANNEL (§3.4), or the special ALL-SUBSCRIBERS address used sparingly for genuinely estate-wide
signals (Part XVI names the overuse of ALL-SUBSCRIBERS as a specific failure mode). An event is
immutable once published: a system that changes its mind does not edit a prior event, it publishes
a new event that supersedes it, carrying a SUPERSEDES field naming the prior event's ID — this
append-only discipline is DERIVED directly from the Owner's own governing Session Safety Contract
(§1 Sacred Invariant, no destructive rewrite of a durable record) generalized from session
transcripts to bus events, because an event log that could be edited after the fact would make
every downstream consumer's "I observed X" claim unfalsifiable.

3.3 ENVELOPE, defined. The ENVELOPE is the uniform wrapper every event is carried in, and it is the
concrete artifact the "uniform contract" of the absorbed Cognitive Service Mesh candidate (§1.4)
resolves to. An envelope carries, at minimum: the event's stable ID (assigned at publication, never
reused); the sender's F1 stable ID; the recipient's F1 stable ID or channel name; the event type
(one of the five taxonomy entries); a schema-version field naming which version of that type's
payload schema the event conforms to; a timestamp; an evidence-level field inherited from F1 Article
V's vocabulary, applied to the event's own claim rather than to a system property; and the payload
itself, whose internal shape is defined per event type (Parts V-VII) but whose OUTER shape — the
seven fields just named — is invariant across every event the bus ever carries, regardless of which
of the seventy-plus systems produced it. (NORMATIVE: envelope uniformity is what lets a subscriber
written once handle events from any producer, present or future, without bespoke per-producer
parsing — the direct mechanism by which the absorbed service-mesh candidate's "uniform contracts
between heterogeneous systems" object is satisfied.)

3.4 CHANNEL, defined. A CHANNEL is a named, durable address that groups events of a related kind or
concern, independent of which system currently publishes to it. Channels exist so that a subscriber
can express interest in a KIND of event ("all AUTHORITY-REQUEST events touching the routing decision
class") without knowing in advance which of the seventy-plus systems will eventually produce one —
the channel is the bus's answer to the addressability problem for events, exactly as F1's stable ID
(Part IX) is its answer to the addressability problem for systems. A channel's identity survives
producer churn: if System P retires and System Q inherits its capability (F1's succession concept,
Part XI, referenced not redefined), Q publishes to the same channel P used, and existing subscribers
require no change. (DERIVED from F1 Part IX §9.1's own justification for stable IDs, applied to the
channel layer.)

3.5 SUBSCRIPTION, defined. A SUBSCRIPTION is a standing declaration, made by one system, of interest
in events of a given type, on a given channel, optionally filtered by a predicate over the envelope
fields (for instance, "only AUTHORITY-REQUEST events whose decision class matches my own final
authority"). A subscription is itself durable state the bus must track and reconcile (Part IX-X):
it is not a live socket held open by a running process, because IAS-A2's honest-IPC discipline
(Part XIV) forbids assuming any two systems are simultaneously live. A subscription is checked and
delivered against at the NEXT reconciliation the subscribing system performs, not pushed
instantaneously — a design constraint Part XIV justifies at length and Part XV's delivery-guarantee
taxonomy formalizes as "eventual, not synchronous" delivery for the ordinary case.

3.6 AUTHORITY-REQUEST, defined as a primitive distinct from EVENT despite being one of the five
taxonomy types. AUTHORITY-REQUEST earns primitive status alongside EVENT, ENVELOPE, CHANNEL, and
SUBSCRIPTION — rather than being treated as merely one more taxonomy entry like FINDING or ALERT —
because it is the one artifact type that can trigger the bus's own second-order arbitration
authority (§2.1, Part XII, Part XIII). An AUTHORITY-REQUEST is DEFINED as an event, conformant to
the standard envelope, whose payload names a DECISION CLASS (in F1's Invariant Three sense, Part V
§5.4) and asks the bus to resolve which system currently holds final authority over it, or — in the
degenerate but common case where authority is undisputed — simply to route the request to that
system. The distinction between an ordinary event (which the bus merely carries) and an
authority-request (which the bus may need to actively adjudicate) is the hinge the conflict-
resolution subengine (Part XII) turns on.

3.7 Worked example: the five primitives composed in one synthetic transaction. Consider a synthetic
mission, illustrative only: System D2 (a stand-in for the Immune System) has fingerprinted a
recurring failure pattern and needs to know whether it should raise a new SLO with System E2 (a
stand-in for Cognitive Reliability) or whether E2 already owns an SLO covering this failure class.
D2 constructs an EVENT of type AUTHORITY-REQUEST, wraps it in the standard ENVELOPE (sender D2's
stable ID, event type AUTHORITY-REQUEST, schema-version 1, timestamp, evidence level OBSERVED because
the fingerprint was measured, not guessed), addresses it to the CHANNEL named for the "SLO ownership"
decision class rather than to E2 directly (because D2 does not know, a priori, whether E2 or some
other system currently holds that authority), and the bus delivers it to every system holding a
live SUBSCRIPTION on that channel. E2, having previously declared a subscription matching this
decision class, receives the request at its next reconciliation, recognizes it holds final
authority (an F1 Part X authority grant), and responds by publishing a new EVENT of type
STATE-CHANGE recording the SLO's update, on a channel D2 is itself subscribed to — closing the loop
without D2 and E2 ever having shared a process, a session, or even overlapping in wall-clock time.

3.8 Why PUBLISHER is not a sixth primitive, despite appearing throughout this Part's own prose. A
terminology question worth resolving explicitly: the word "publisher" recurs constantly in this
dataset, yet it is not listed among the five named primitives. This is DELIBERATE — a publisher is
not a new kind of OBJECT the bus introduces; it is simply an F1 SYSTEM (F1 Part II §2.1) in the
specific ROLE of having authored an EVENT, exactly as "subscriber" names an F1 system in the role of
holding a SUBSCRIPTION. Naming "publisher" and "subscriber" as sixth and seventh primitives would
duplicate an object F1 already owns (the system itself) under a role-specific alias, precisely the
kind of naming-only novelty Article I forbids at the dataset level and this dataset's own Part II
§2.5 forbids at the object level. The five primitives named in this Part are the genuinely NEW
objects the bus contributes; publisher and subscriber are F1 systems wearing a bus-specific hat, and
distinguishing the two categories keeps this dataset's own primitive layer as minimal as IAS-F1 Part
II §2.5 requires its own to be.

3.9 The primitive layer's relationship to the taxonomy of Parts V-VII, stated as a dependency map for
clarity. EVENT (§3.2) is the general container; the five taxonomy entries of Parts V-VII are its
CLOSED set of legal types, meaning every event that exists on the bus is, without exception, one of
FINDING, STATE-CHANGE, ALERT, AUTHORITY-REQUEST, or EVIDENCE. ENVELOPE (§3.3) is the uniform outer
shape every event of every type shares. CHANNEL (§3.4) and SUBSCRIPTION (§3.5) together form the
addressing and interest-declaration layer that determines delivery, independent of event type.
AUTHORITY-REQUEST (§3.6) is unusual in being both a taxonomy entry (one of the five types) and a
named primitive in its own right, precisely because — as §3.6 explains — its delivery logic reads
live authority state rather than being a pure function of channel and subscription alone, a property
no other taxonomy entry shares. This dependency map is offered here, at the close of the primitive
Part, so that a reader moving into Parts IV through VII has an explicit picture of how the five
primitives compose before the taxonomy elaborates each type's own payload and obligations in turn.

FINAL LAW — PART III. Event, envelope, channel, subscription, and authority-request are the five
primitives from which every transaction on the bus is composed; an implementation that collapses
any of the five into an ad hoc convenience — an untyped payload standing in for the envelope, a
direct system-to-system address standing in for a channel — has rebuilt the aggregator or delegator
near-miss (Part II §2.3) one primitive at a time.

===============================================================================================
PART IV — THE BUS CONTRACT: PUBLISH, SUBSCRIBE, DELIVER, ACKNOWLEDGE
===============================================================================================

4.1 Why a contract, not an API. This dataset is bound by Article VIII: no production code, no
implementation-level specification, this mission. What follows is therefore a CONTRACT — the set of
obligations a publisher, a subscriber, and the bus itself owe one another — stated at the level of
guarantees and preconditions, not function signatures. (NORMATIVE: the distinction matters because a
contract survives across implementations, languages, and even model generations, while an API does
not; Part XVII's lifecycle discipline depends on the contract being the durable artifact.)

4.2 The publish obligation. A publishing system MUST: construct an envelope conformant to the
current schema version for the event's type (Parts V-VII); assign or accept a bus-issued stable
event ID; set an honest evidence level per Article V of the Constitution (a system that publishes a
HYPOTHESIZED finding as if it were OBSERVED violates the publish contract even if the bus has no
mechanical way to detect the lie at publish time — Part XIII names this the evidence-laundering
adversarial case); and accept that publication is APPEND-ONLY (§3.2) — there is no retraction
operation, only a superseding event. A publishing system MUST NOT assume synchronous delivery: the
publish obligation ends at successful write to the bus's durable store (Part XIV), not at confirmed
receipt by any subscriber.

4.3 The subscribe and delivery obligation. A subscribing system MUST declare its subscription
durably (§3.5) rather than relying on being "currently listening," because the bus's honest-IPC
discipline (Part XIV) assumes no two systems are simultaneously live by default. The bus, in turn,
OWES every durable subscription eventual delivery of every matching event published after the
subscription was declared, at a latency bound appropriate to the event's type (Part XV's delivery-
guarantee taxonomy distinguishes FINDING and STATE-CHANGE, which tolerate eventual delivery, from
AUTHORITY-REQUEST and certain ALERT subtypes, which carry a tighter bound because a stale authority
resolution is a live Invariant Three risk). The bus does NOT owe delivery of events published before
a subscription existed, unless the subscribing system separately performs a BACKFILL read against
the durable event log (Part IX) — a deliberate design choice that keeps "who saw what" auditable
rather than implicit.

4.4 The acknowledge obligation and its limits. A subscribing system that has processed an event
SHOULD acknowledge it, recording the acknowledgment as its own durable fact (not merely an in-memory
flag), so that the bus's delivery-guarantee accounting (Part XV) can distinguish "delivered but
unprocessed" from "processed." Acknowledgment is advisory, not mandatory, for FINDING and
STATE-CHANGE events (a subscriber that never acknowledges a low-criticality finding has not broken
the bus, only reduced its own observability footprint), but is treated as REQUIRED for
AUTHORITY-REQUEST events specifically, because an unacknowledged authority-request is
indistinguishable, from the requester's side, from a request that was never delivered at all — and
Part XIII's authority-routing discipline depends on being able to tell the two apart.

4.5 The idempotency obligation. Because delivery is eventual and reconciliation-driven (§3.5,
Part XIV), a subscriber MAY observe the same event more than once across separate reconciliation
passes — for instance if its own local record of "already processed" was itself lost to a crash.
The bus contract therefore requires every event to carry a stable ID (§3.2) precisely so that a
subscriber's processing logic can be idempotent: processing the same event ID twice must produce
the same effect as processing it once, never a duplicated side effect. (DERIVED: idempotency is not
an optional refinement, it is the necessary consequence of choosing eventual delivery over
synchronous delivery, and a subscriber that is not idempotent has silently assumed a delivery
guarantee — exactly-once — the bus does not make.)

4.6 What the contract deliberately does not promise. Three properties are explicitly NOT part of the
bus contract, and naming the absence is itself part of honest specification (mirroring IAS-F1 Part
IX's own explicit non-promises). The bus does not promise ORDERING across channels: two events on
different channels carry no guaranteed relative order, only their own timestamps, because imposing
a global order across a federation whose systems are not simultaneously live would require a
synchronization mechanism this dataset's honest-IPC discipline (Part XIV) explicitly rejects. The
bus does not promise LOW LATENCY: "eventual" means exactly that, and a system whose mission requires
a synchronous cross-system answer within one call is using the wrong mechanism — F1's CONSUMES
relation (Part VI §6.7), used at mission time by IAS-A1's router, is the correct tool for that need,
not the bus. The bus does not promise CONFIDENTIALITY beyond the estate boundary: every event on the
bus is, by default, readable by any system with a matching subscription within the ensemble's F1
Part III §3.4 boundary; access control between systems within the estate is a governance decision
this dataset does not adjudicate, deferring instead to the estate's existing secret-firewall and
redaction discipline (referenced, not rebuilt, per §1.4's "strengthens never replaces" pattern).

4.7 Worked example: the four obligations traced through one failure and one recovery. Consider a
synthetic trace: System P publishes a STATE-CHANGE event, satisfying the publish obligation (§4.2).
System Q, which holds a durable subscription matching the event's channel, is offline at the moment
of publication (its process is not running) — this is not a contract violation on either side,
because the bus's delivery obligation (§4.3) is eventual, not synchronous. Three hours later, System
Q comes back online and performs its next reconciliation pass; it reads the durable event log,
finds P's event, processes it, and records an acknowledgment (§4.4). Independently, System Q's
reconciliation process crashes partway through a SECOND, unrelated batch and restarts; on restart it
re-reads its subscription's unacknowledged event set, finds P's event ID already marked
acknowledged in its own durable record, and correctly skips reprocessing it — the idempotency
obligation (§4.5) holding even though no coordination between P and Q's crash-and-restart occurred.
At no point in this trace did any obligation require P and Q to be simultaneously live, to agree on
a global clock, or to retry a synchronous call — the four obligations, composed, are sufficient for
correct cross-system communication under exactly the honest-IPC constraints Part XIV formalizes.

4.8 The unsubscribe obligation, and why it is weaker than the other four. A fifth, minor obligation
this dataset names for completeness: a system that no longer wishes to receive a channel's traffic
MAY declare its subscription retired, using the same tombstone pattern Part IX §9.7 specifies for
registry records, so that the bus's own subscription-count bookkeeping (feeding, among other things,
the channel fan-out metric of Part XVIII §18.5) does not silently overcount subscribers who have long
since stopped reconciling. This obligation is DELIBERATELY weaker than the publish, subscribe,
delivery, and acknowledgment obligations of §4.2-§4.5: a system that simply stops reconciling without
ever tombstoning its subscription has violated no CORRECTNESS guarantee (its own future
reconciliation, whenever it resumes, still recovers cleanly per §4.5's idempotency), only degraded
the ACCURACY of the bus's own bookkeeping about who is genuinely listening — a distinction this
dataset marks explicitly because conflating a bookkeeping inaccuracy with a correctness violation
would misclassify the severity of an unsubscribe omission relative to, for instance, a genuine
publish-obligation violation such as a dishonestly-marked evidence level (§4.2).

4.9 Why the four core obligations are necessary AND sufficient, stated as a closing claim this
dataset is willing to defend. The publish, subscribe, delivery, and acknowledgment obligations,
composed as specified in §4.2-§4.5, are claimed here to be BOTH necessary (removing any one breaks a
guarantee some later Part depends on: removing publish's honesty requirement breaks Article V
compliance estate-wide; removing durable subscription breaks the honest-IPC discipline of Part XIV;
removing eventual-delivery's own bound breaks the delivery-tier taxonomy of Part XV; removing
acknowledgment's idempotency requirement breaks every consumer's crash-recovery property) AND
sufficient (no fifth CORE obligation is required for correct cross-system communication under this
dataset's own scope, as demonstrated by every worked example in Parts V through XX composing correctly
from just these four). This NECESSARY-AND-SUFFICIENT claim is stated as DERIVED rather than DEFINED,
because it rests on the absence of a counterexample across this dataset's own worked examples rather
than on a formal completeness proof — an honest calibration of confidence Article V requires when a
claim's basis is demonstration rather than derivation from first principles.

FINAL LAW — PART IV. Publish is append-and-honest, subscribe is durable-not-live, delivery is
eventual-not-synchronous, and acknowledgment is idempotent-not-exactly-once — four obligations that
together let systems which are never simultaneously running still communicate correctly, and a
future implementation that quietly assumes synchronous delivery or exactly-once processing has
broken the contract this Part exists to prevent breaking.

===============================================================================================
PART V — EVENT TAXONOMY I: FINDING AND STATE-CHANGE
===============================================================================================

5.1 Why five types and a closed taxonomy. IAS-A2 fixes exactly five event types — FINDING,
STATE-CHANGE, ALERT, AUTHORITY-REQUEST, EVIDENCE — as a CLOSED set, meaning a new event that does
not fit one of the five is a signal that either an existing type's payload schema needs a versioned
extension (Part XVII) or that the proposed event is not properly a bus concern at all, rather than a
license to add a sixth type informally. (NORMATIVE: an open-ended taxonomy is the direct route back
to the fragmentation Part I §1.2 warns against — if every system may invent its own event type, the
uniform-contract benefit of Part VIII collapses one type at a time.) The five types are chosen
because each corresponds to a genuinely distinct OBLIGATION on the receiving system (§4.3-§4.4
already showed obligations vary by type), and no smaller set could express that variation.

5.2 FINDING, defined. A FINDING is an event whose payload asserts a discovered fact about the
estate, a mission, or another system's behavior, produced by a system's own analysis rather than
merely relayed from elsewhere. This is DEFINED as the direct system-level generalization of PM-03's
own object (a pane's discovery, promoted from pane-scope to system-scope, per Part XI's lift). A
FINDING payload carries, beyond the standard envelope: a SUBJECT (the F1 stable ID of the system or
capability the finding is about, which may differ from the sender), a CLAIM (a natural-language or
structured statement of what was found), and the Article V evidence level the finding was produced
under. FINDING is the highest-volume event type on the bus by construction (mirroring F1 Part VI
§6.10's observation that FEEDS is the highest-cardinality relation type, since most findings
travel as FEEDS traffic), and it carries the loosest delivery-guarantee bound (Part XV) precisely
because most findings are informative rather than urgent.

5.3 The FINDING lifecycle: raw, promoted, retired. A finding does not arrive on the bus in the same
shape it was discovered in. It begins RAW — true only within its originating system's own local
scope, not yet judged reusable elsewhere. A system that judges a raw finding reusable by at least
one OTHER system publishes it as a PROMOTED finding — the literal mechanism by which Part XI's
pane-to-system lift and PM-03's original pane-level findings-bus object become ensemble-reusable.
A promoted finding may later be RETIRED — not deleted (§3.2's append-only discipline forbids
deletion) but superseded by a later finding that corrects, narrows, or invalidates it, with the
superseding event's SUPERSEDES field naming the retired finding's ID. This three-stage lifecycle
is DERIVED from the observation that most local findings are never reused (OBSERVED pattern from
PM-03's own operation: the majority of pane-level findings are resolved and closed within the pane
that discovered them), and promoting every raw finding indiscriminately would flood the bus with
FINDING traffic no subscriber wants — the specific event-storm failure mode Part XVI catalogues.

5.4 Worked example: a finding's full lifecycle across three systems. Consider a synthetic trace.
System D2 (Immune System stand-in) discovers, during its own local analysis of a project's crash
log, that a particular error signature recurs — a RAW finding, true only within D2's current
analysis pass. D2 checks whether the signature matches a pattern already known to affect more than
one project (a check D2's own internal logic performs, not specified here per Article VIII); finding
that it does, D2 PROMOTES the finding, publishing it to the bus on the channel scoped to "cross-
project failure signatures," with evidence level OBSERVED. System E2 (Cognitive Reliability stand-
in), subscribed to that channel, receives the promoted finding at its next reconciliation and uses
it to open a new failure-budget entry (Part X §10.6 in F1's own terms is F1's territory; E2's
reaction is E2's own object, not this dataset's). Four weeks later, System D2 discovers the
signature was itself a false positive caused by a since-fixed logging bug; D2 publishes a new
FINDING event with evidence level DERIVED (derived from the fix's own commit record) that RETIRES
the original promoted finding by ID. E2, on its next reconciliation, observes the retiring event and
closes the failure-budget entry it had opened — the full lifecycle traced across two systems that
never shared a session.

5.5 STATE-CHANGE, defined. A STATE-CHANGE event is DEFINED as the bus's carrier for a DELTA to one
of the ensemble state components IAS-F1 Part III §3.3 names: a system's own property record (F1
Part IV), a relation-graph edge (F1 Part VI), or a lifecycle-state transition (F1 Part XI,
referenced not redefined). Unlike FINDING, which asserts a discovered fact ABOUT something,
STATE-CHANGE asserts that something's own recorded state HAS CHANGED — the distinction matters
because a subscriber's obligation differs: a finding is informative and may be ignored without
consequence to correctness, while an unprocessed STATE-CHANGE can leave a subscriber's own
downstream computation working from stale state, which is why STATE-CHANGE carries a tighter
freshness expectation in Part XV's delivery-guarantee taxonomy than ordinary FINDING traffic,
though still not the synchronous bound reserved for AUTHORITY-REQUEST.

5.6 STATE-CHANGE payload and the delta-not-snapshot discipline. A STATE-CHANGE payload carries the
changed field's name, its prior value (or UNRESOLVED if the field had no prior recorded value, per
F1 Part IV §4.6's own honest-blank discipline), its new value, and the source reference that
produced the change — mirroring F1's own property-provenance model (F1 Part IV §4.3) at the
transport layer. STATE-CHANGE events are DELTAS, never full snapshots, by deliberate design: a
snapshot-carrying event would let a late-arriving, out-of-order snapshot silently overwrite a
subscriber's more current state (because the bus makes no ordering guarantee across channels, §4.6),
while a delta applied out of order is at worst inconsistent in a DETECTABLE way (a subscriber that
tracks its own last-known value per field can notice a delta that does not match its expected prior
value, and flag the mismatch rather than silently corrupting its state) — this detectability is
DERIVED directly from the no-ordering-guarantee constraint of §4.6 and is the specific reason
delta-only, not snapshot, was chosen as the STATE-CHANGE discipline.

5.7 The double-booked STATE-CHANGE edge case. Two systems, each believing itself the sole authority
for a given property, may each publish a STATE-CHANGE for the same field on the same subject at
overlapping times — a transport-layer instance of IAS-F1's own DOUBLE-BOOKED CONSUMER edge case
(F1 Part IV §4.6) generalized from the CONSUMERS property to arbitrary property state. IAS-A2 does
not silently pick a winner; it treats two conflicting STATE-CHANGE claims on the same field as
prima facie evidence of an Invariant Three violation (two systems both believing they hold write
authority over one decision class) and routes the conflict to the arbitration subengine of Part
XII, rather than letting last-write-wins semantics quietly determine the estate's own state — a
design choice justified in full in Part XII §12.4.

5.8 Why FINDING and STATE-CHANGE are grouped in one Part. The two share the delta-not-snapshot
discipline, the reconciliation-driven (not synchronous) delivery model, and — most importantly —
the property that neither one, by itself, can trigger the bus's own arbitration authority; that
authority is reserved for AUTHORITY-REQUEST and for the double-booked STATE-CHANGE edge case
specifically (§5.7), never for an ordinary FINDING. Grouping them here, rather than treating each
across a separate Part, keeps the taxonomy's two "informational, low-stakes-if-delayed" types
together and distinguishes them cleanly from the three types covered in Parts VI-VII, each of
which carries a materially higher consequence for delayed or mishandled delivery.

FINAL LAW — PART V. A finding informs; a state-change updates; neither commands — and the
taxonomy's own ordering, informational types first, is a deliberate statement that most of the
bus's traffic should be low-stakes by design, because a federation whose ordinary event stream is
dominated by high-stakes authority traffic has already lost the informational circulation the bus
exists to provide.

===============================================================================================
PART VI — EVENT TAXONOMY II: ALERT AND AUTHORITY-REQUEST
===============================================================================================

6.1 ALERT, defined. An ALERT is DEFINED as an event asserting that an ensemble INVARIANT (IAS-F1
Part V) or a system's own declared SLO/failure-budget (a concern of IAS-E2, referenced not rebuilt)
is currently violated or imminently at risk, distinguished from FINDING by urgency and from
STATE-CHANGE by the fact that an alert does not itself carry a state delta — it carries a CLAIM OF
DEFECT that some other system, or the Owner via owner_queue, must act on. An alert payload carries:
the violated invariant or SLO identifier, the SEVERITY (a bounded enumeration — INFO, WARN,
CRITICAL — deliberately reusing the estate's own existing severity vocabulary rather than inventing
a bus-specific one, per §1.4's strengthen-not-replace discipline), the evidence for the violation
claim, and — where known — the AFFECTED SYSTEM SET, which may be more than one system when the
alert concerns an ensemble-wide invariant such as Invariant One (no orphan system) or Invariant
Four (the boundary invariant).

6.2 The ALERT delivery guarantee and its relationship to owner_queue. CRITICAL-severity alerts
carry the tightest delivery-guarantee bound the ordinary (non-authority) event stream offers (Part
XV), and, per the Strengthening Matrix's own entry for owner_queue, a CRITICAL alert with no
subscribing system able to resolve it is REQUIRED to route to owner_queue as a fallback channel —
this is the specific, concrete mechanism by which IAS-A2 strengthens owner_queue (the Owner-facing
action queue gains a uniform, typed inbound format for cross-system escalations, rather than each
alerting system inventing its own escalation path to the Owner). A WARN or INFO alert does NOT
auto-escalate to owner_queue; only CRITICAL does, a threshold DERIVED from the same anti-noise
discipline the failure-modes Part (XVI) elaborates for event storms — an owner_queue flooded with
WARN-level alerts would train the Owner to ignore it, defeating its purpose.

6.3 Worked example: an alert that crosses from bus traffic to Owner action. Consider a synthetic
trace: System G1 (Architecture Intelligence stand-in) detects, during a routine sweep, that a system
pair now violates Invariant Two (an unresolved DUPLICATES relation that has persisted past a
reasonable resolution window — a threshold G1's own logic sets, not this dataset's). G1 publishes an
ALERT event, severity CRITICAL, invariant identifier "F1-INV-2," affected system set naming the two
systems in question, evidence level OBSERVED. No system currently holds a standing subscription
matching "unresolved duplicate past window," because no system has yet been assigned final authority
over stale-duplicate remediation — an F1 Invariant Three gap in its own right. Per §6.2's rule, the
CRITICAL alert with no resolving subscriber routes automatically to owner_queue, surfacing as a
single, typed, evidenced entry the Owner can act on directly, rather than as a raw log line the
Owner would have to notice and interpret unaided.

6.4 AUTHORITY-REQUEST, defined in full (extending the primitive definition of Part III §3.6). Where
Part III introduced AUTHORITY-REQUEST as a primitive because of its special relationship to the
bus's own arbitration authority, this Part specifies its payload and obligations as a taxonomy
entry. An AUTHORITY-REQUEST payload carries: the DECISION CLASS in question (an F1 Invariant Three
object), the REQUESTING SYSTEM's stated need (why it requires the resolution — informational only,
not itself authoritative), and an optional CANDIDATE AUTHORITY field the requester may propose if it
has a good-faith belief about who holds authority, which the bus treats as a hint, never a
resolution. The bus's obligation on receiving an AUTHORITY-REQUEST is threefold, checked in order:
first, consult the current authority-grant record (F1 Part X) for the named decision class; second,
if exactly one system holds final authority and is reachable via a live subscription, route the
request there and the transaction completes as an ordinary delivery; third, if zero or more than one
system claims final authority, the request is NOT delivered as an ordinary event — it is escalated
to the arbitration subengine (Part XII).

6.5 Why AUTHORITY-REQUEST cannot be handled by ordinary channel subscription alone. FINDING,
STATE-CHANGE, and ALERT are all delivered by matching a subscription predicate against a channel —
a stateless lookup. AUTHORITY-REQUEST requires the bus to consult LIVE authority-grant state (F1
Part X) at delivery time, because the whole point of the request is that the requester does not
already know the answer. This is DERIVED as the precise reason AUTHORITY-REQUEST earned primitive
status in Part III rather than being an ordinary taxonomy entry: every other event type's delivery
logic is a pure function of (channel, subscription); AUTHORITY-REQUEST's delivery logic is a
function of (channel, subscription, CURRENT AUTHORITY STATE), and that third input is exactly what
can be zero, one, or many — the condition that triggers arbitration.

6.6 The authority-request's relationship to the requesting system's own blocking behavior. A system
that issues an AUTHORITY-REQUEST does NOT block waiting for a synchronous answer — per Part IV
§4.6's explicit non-promise of low latency, no bus transaction is synchronous. A requesting system
is expected to proceed under its own best local judgment (an UNRESOLVED-marked provisional decision,
per Article V) until the authority resolution arrives as a subsequent STATE-CHANGE or FINDING event
naming the original request's ID, and to reconcile its provisional decision against the authoritative
answer once it arrives — a pattern DERIVED from the estate's own existing "claimed-until-observed"
discipline (IAS-F1 Part III §3.3, itself imported from SQI) applied here to authority resolution
specifically rather than to ensemble state generally.

6.7 Worked example: two systems racing on the same decision class. Consider a synthetic trace where
Systems C1 and C2 (Portfolio and Demand Forecasting stand-ins) each independently issue an
AUTHORITY-REQUEST for the decision class "which forecast model governs next-quarter capability
demand," each unaware the other has also asked, moments apart. The bus, consulting the authority-
grant record, finds it currently UNRESOLVED (no system has yet been granted final authority over this
newly-named decision class — a realistic condition for a decision class that has only just started
being contested). Both requests trigger arbitration (§6.4's third branch) simultaneously. Part XII
§12.5 specifies the exact resolution the arbitration subengine applies when TWO live requests target
the same undecided decision class concurrently: rather than resolving each independently (which
risks granting authority twice, a fresh Invariant Three violation created by the resolution process
itself), the bus merges concurrent requests on the same decision class within a bounded reconciliation
window into a single arbitration case, and answers both requesters from the one resolution.

6.8 ALERT severity as an F1 property-record analogue, and why the bus does not attempt to verify a
publisher's own severity judgment. A design restraint worth stating explicitly: this dataset does not
specify any mechanism by which the bus itself checks whether a published ALERT's claimed severity
(INFO/WARN/CRITICAL, §6.1) accurately reflects the underlying condition — that judgment belongs
entirely to the publishing system, mirroring the boundary-only uniformity principle (Part VIII §8.2)
applied here to severity classification specifically rather than to envelope structure generally.
This restraint is DELIBERATE and carries a named cost: a system that systematically over-classifies
its own alerts as CRITICAL when WARN would be accurate degrades owner_queue's own signal quality
without the bus having any structural way to detect the miscalibration directly — the cost this
dataset accepts in exchange for not requiring the bus to understand the SUBSTANCE of every system's
domain-specific severity judgment, which would violate the second-order capability test of Part II
§2.2 by making the bus's own object partially resource-level (judging one system's specific
condition) rather than purely relational (carrying the claim as typed, addressed traffic). Part
XVII §17.4 specifies the rate-based circuit breaker that mitigates the AGGREGATE consequence of this
restraint without ever requiring the bus to adjudicate any single alert's substantive accuracy.

6.9 The relationship between ALERT and the estate's pre-existing severity vocabulary, made explicit.
Section 6.1 already states that ALERT reuses the estate's own existing INFO/WARN/CRITICAL scale
rather than inventing a bus-specific one; this Part closes by naming the concrete benefit that reuse
buys beyond mere consistency. Because owner_queue, the estate's cascade-prevention discipline, and
this dataset's own escalation logic (§6.2) all speak the same three-value scale, a system publishing
an ALERT need not translate its own internal urgency assessment into a bus-specific vocabulary and
then have owner_queue translate it AGAIN into the Owner-facing vocabulary — one shared scale,
inherited rather than reinvented, removes an entire translation layer that would otherwise sit
between every alerting system and the Owner's own eventual view of the escalation, a direct
application of the strengthen-never-replace law (Article III) to vocabulary reuse specifically.

FINAL LAW — PART VI. Alert escalates; authority-request arbitrates — the two highest-consequence
event types on the bus, and the only two whose delivery logic reads LIVE ensemble state rather than
a static subscription table, because urgency and authority are exactly the two properties an
ordinary stateless lookup cannot honestly resolve on its own.

===============================================================================================
PART VII — EVENT TAXONOMY III: EVIDENCE AND THE EVIDENCE-CHAIN REQUIREMENT
===============================================================================================

7.1 EVIDENCE, defined. An EVIDENCE event is DEFINED as an event whose payload substantiates,
strengthens, weakens, or contradicts a PRIOR event's claim — the bus's mechanism for letting the
ensemble's own epistemic state (Article V's vocabulary, applied at transport scale) evolve after
initial publication without violating the append-only discipline of §3.2. Where a FINDING asserts a
new fact and a STATE-CHANGE records a new value, an EVIDENCE event never stands alone: it always
carries a REFERENCED EVENT ID naming the prior claim it bears on, and a DIRECTION field —
CORROBORATES, WEAKENS, or REFUTES — stating the evidentiary relationship.

7.2 Why evidence needed its own type rather than living inside FINDING. An early design question
this dataset resolves explicitly (and revisits in the Decision Log) is whether EVIDENCE could simply
be a FINDING that happens to reference another event. It cannot, for a reason DERIVED from Part V
§5.2's own FINDING definition: a finding's SUBJECT is the system or capability the finding is
ABOUT, while an evidence event's referenced object is another EVENT, a categorically different kind
of target. Collapsing the two would force every subscriber interested in epistemic updates to a
prior claim to also subscribe to, and filter, the entire FINDING stream — the highest-volume type on
the bus (§5.2) — defeating the purpose of a typed taxonomy in the first place. A dedicated EVIDENCE
type lets a subscriber declare interest specifically in "has anything changed my confidence in event
X" without wading through unrelated findings.

7.3 The evidence chain and evidence-level recomputation. Because an event's own evidence level
(Article V, carried per §3.3's envelope) is fixed at publication, and because reality does not stop
evolving the moment an event is published, IAS-A2 defines the EVIDENCE CHAIN: the ordered set of all
EVIDENCE events referencing a given original event, from which any subscriber can recompute a
CURRENT evidentiary status distinct from the original's fixed publication-time level. A finding
published as HYPOTHESIZED that later accumulates three CORROBORATES evidence events from independent
systems has a materially different current status than one that accumulates a single REFUTES event —
but the bus does not itself promote the original event's stored evidence-level field (append-only,
§3.2 forbids editing it); it is the CONSUMING system's responsibility to read the chain and compute
current status, a deliberate allocation of responsibility DERIVED from the same principle F1 Part
XII applies to provenance generally: the record stores raw facts, the consumer applies judgment.

7.4 Independence and the corroboration-counting failure mode. A chain of three CORROBORATES events
is only meaningfully stronger evidence than one if the three corroborating systems reached their
conclusions independently — the identical epistemic trap named, in a different register, by IAS-F1
Part XIII's own caution against mistaking bookkeeping coherence for genuine health. IAS-A2 does not
itself verify independence (that judgment requires domain knowledge about HOW each corroborating
system reached its conclusion, which the bus's transport-layer view cannot assess); it DEFINES the
envelope field SOURCE METHOD (a brief tag naming the corroborating system's own evidentiary basis —
"independent re-measurement," "citing the same upstream finding," "analytical re-derivation from the
same data") specifically so that a downstream consumer CAN check for the non-independence failure
mode rather than being structurally blind to it. (NORMATIVE: this is a case where the bus's job is
to make an epistemic hazard visible, not to resolve it — resolving it is IAS-B2's Insight Synthesis
territory, referenced not duplicated.)

7.5 Worked example: a REFUTES chain closing a promoted finding. Returning to the finding lifecycle
of §5.4: instead of System D2 itself retiring its own promoted finding, consider a variant trace
where a THIRD system, K (a knowledge-location stand-in), independently re-analyzes the same failure
signature during unrelated work and discovers the same logging-bug root cause D2 later found
independently. K publishes an EVIDENCE event, direction REFUTES, referencing D2's original promoted
FINDING, evidence level OBSERVED, source method "independent re-measurement against live logs."
System E2, which had already opened a failure-budget entry against D2's original finding (§5.4),
receives K's evidence event on the same channel it monitors for evidence bearing on entries it holds,
and — per its own internal policy, not this dataset's object — provisionally downgrades the entry's
confidence pending D2's own eventual retiring FINDING. When D2's retiring event arrives weeks later
(§5.4), it CORROBORATES what K's evidence had already flagged, and E2's eventual closure of the
entry is now supported by two independent lines, a materially stronger evidentiary basis than either
alone — precisely the value the evidence chain and the independence-visibility discipline (§7.4)
were built to enable.

7.6 EVIDENCE events about the bus's own claims. A subtle but necessary closing point: EVIDENCE
events may themselves be referenced by further EVIDENCE events (a REFUTES event can itself be
weakened by a later CORROBORATES-of-the-original event from a fourth system), and the chain has no
fixed depth limit — it grows for as long as the ensemble keeps discovering relevant information. This
is DEFINED as intentional rather than an oversight: capping the chain's depth would silently discard
epistemically relevant history, violating the same append-only, never-silently-discard discipline
that governs every other artifact type on the bus. The practical cost of unbounded chain depth —
growing storage, growing reconciliation cost for a system that must walk a long chain — is named
honestly in Part XVI's failure modes as a real, if slow-accumulating, risk rather than papered over
here.

7.7 The distinction between CORROBORATES-then-REFUTES and REFUTES-then-CORROBORATES, and why order
matters to a reader but not to the record. Because the chain is append-only and never edited (§3.2),
the ORDER in which evidence arrives is itself preserved as part of the record — a claim that
accumulates two CORROBORATES events and only later receives a REFUTES reads differently, to a human
or system reviewing the chain, than one that was REFUTED early and only later, weakly, CORROBORATED
by a source that turns out to share the refuting evidence's own blind spot. IAS-A2 does not compute
a single "net" evidentiary score from the chain — per §7.3's allocation of responsibility, that
computation belongs to the consuming system — but the bus's own contribution to making order-
sensitive judgment possible is that the chain is read in PUBLICATION order (the per-publisher
ordering guarantee of Part XV §15.3 applies here too, since each evidence-chain contributor is
itself a publisher), never in an arbitrarily reshuffled order that would erase the very sequence a
careful reader needs to distinguish an early, hasty corroboration from a late, considered one.

7.8 Worked example: a chain where reading order changes the honest interpretation. Consider a
synthetic chain of four events, illustrative only, all referencing one original FINDING. Event Two,
published an hour after the original, CORROBORATES it with source method "citing the same upstream
finding" — the WEAK corroboration shape §7.4 warns about, since it shares rather than independently
verifies the original's basis. Event Three, published two days later, REFUTES the original with
source method "independent re-measurement," a materially stronger evidentiary basis than Event Two's.
Event Four, published a week after that, CORROBORATES the original again, this time with source
method "independent re-measurement" matching Event Three's rigor but reaching the opposite
conclusion. A consuming system reading this chain in publication order sees a coherent story: an
early, weak, non-independent corroboration; a strong refutation; and a strong, independent, opposing
corroboration — a genuinely CONTESTED evidentiary picture the Article V vocabulary is built to
represent honestly, rather than a false "three-for-one-against" tally a naive vote-count over the
same four events, read out of order or without source-method weighting, might have produced instead.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. Evidence never stands alone; it always points at a prior claim and states
whether it strengthens, weakens, or breaks it, and the bus's own job stops at making the chain
visible and the corroborating sources' independence checkable — the judgment of what the chain now
means is deliberately left to the system that reads it, never computed once and stored as if final.

PART VIII — UNIFORM CONTRACTS BETWEEN HETEROGENEOUS SYSTEMS
===============================================================================================

8.1 The problem the Cognitive Service Mesh candidate names and the bus resolves. Per
NON_DUPLICATION_LEDGER.md, Candidate 4's overlap with PM-03 was described as "pane transport," and
its unique object as "uniform contracts between heterogeneous systems." The seventy-plus systems of
the estate were, as OBSERVED, built across different periods, by different sessions, against
different internal conventions — some carry their own bespoke event-like mechanisms, some carry
none at all, some assume a single-repository scope PM-03 was built for, some assume nothing about
cross-system communication because they were never meant to communicate cross-system. A control bus
that required every one of the seventy-plus to be internally rewritten to a common internal
convention before it could carry their traffic would be a non-starter — the cost would dwarf the
benefit, and Article X forbids this dataset from mutating the estate at all. IAS-A2's uniform-
contract discipline solves this by drawing the line of uniformity at the BOUNDARY, never the
interior: a system's internals remain exactly what they were; only the envelope it emits onto, and
reads from, the bus must conform.

8.2 The boundary-only uniformity principle, stated precisely. DEFINED: a system S is BUS-CONFORMANT
if and only if every event S publishes satisfies the envelope contract of Part III §3.3 and every
event S consumes is read through the same contract, REGARDLESS of S's internal representation of
the same information. A system whose internal state is a relational table, a flat file, an
in-memory tree, or a prompt-context window is equally bus-conformant provided its publish/consume
boundary performs the translation to and from the envelope shape — the translation itself is a
component (F1 Part II §2.3) of the system, not a bus concern, and its cost is borne once, at the
boundary, rather than propagated into the system's interior. (NORMATIVE: this is the direct
mechanism by which IAS-A2 avoids becoming the imperial-boundary adversarial case IAS-F1 Part V §5.5
warns about at the ontology level — the bus's authority stops exactly at the envelope, never
reaching into a system's own internal design.)

8.3 What uniformity buys: the write-once subscriber property. The payoff of boundary-only
uniformity is that a subscriber written to consume, for instance, FINDING events on a given channel
needs exactly one parsing path regardless of how many different systems, built at how many
different times, eventually publish to that channel — the exact property Part III §3.3's closing
paragraph names as the mechanism satisfying the absorbed service-mesh candidate's object. This is
DERIVED as the compounding-relevant property: every NEW system added to the estate that adopts
bus-conformance at its boundary becomes, for free, a potential producer or consumer for every
EXISTING subscriber already on a matching channel — the marginal cost of the Nth system joining the
bus is one boundary translation, not N-1 bespoke integrations with every system already present, a
combinatorial saving DERIVED directly from the star topology the bus imposes (every system talks to
the bus, never directly to N-1 peers) versus the mesh topology a peer-to-peer alternative would
require.

8.4 Schema versioning as the uniformity contract's own evolution mechanism. Because "uniform" cannot
mean "frozen forever" — the estate's own systems evolve, and a payload schema that could never gain a
field would eventually strangle every event type — Part III §3.3's schema-version field is the
uniformity contract's release valve. A schema version bump for a given event type is ADDITIVE
(new optional field, backward-compatible with existing subscribers that ignore unknown fields) or
REVISORY (a field's meaning or requiredness changes, breaking existing subscriber assumptions) —
DIRECTLY mirroring IAS-F1 Part XVII's additive-versus-revisory boundary for the ontology itself,
applied here to the payload schemas rather than to F1's own object model. An additive bump requires
no coordination; a revisory bump requires the migration discipline of Part XVII, because it can
silently break a subscriber that has not been updated.

8.5 The heterogeneity edge case: a system with no publish/consume boundary at all. Some estate
systems, as OBSERVED, are pure batch tools invoked once and never left running — they have no
natural moment at which to check a subscription or perform a reconciliation pass (§3.5). IAS-A2
does not require such a system to become bus-conformant to remain a valid F1 system; a system may
legitimately choose NEVER to publish or subscribe, remaining outside the bus's traffic entirely,
provided it does not thereby violate IAS-F1's Invariant One (no orphan system, F1 Part V §5.2) —
that invariant is satisfied by ANY inbound or outbound edge in the relation graph, and a system may
hold FEEDS or DEPENDS-ON edges realized through mechanisms other than the bus (a direct file
read, a CONSUMES relation invoked at mission time by IAS-A1) without ever touching IAS-A2 at all.
(DERIVED: bus-conformance is a sufficient but not necessary condition for satisfying Invariant One;
naming this explicitly prevents a future misreading that would treat every estate system as
obligated to join the bus.)

8.6 Worked contrast: two systems achieving the same integration outcome by different boundary
strategies. Consider two synthetic systems, illustrative only. System Alpha maintains an internal
event log of its own design, built years before IAS-A2 existed; its boundary translation layer is a
thin adapter that reads its native log format and re-emits matching entries as bus-conformant
envelopes on a schedule, without touching Alpha's internal log format at all. System Beta was built
after IAS-A2 and internally represents its own state changes directly as envelope-shaped records
from the start, requiring no translation layer because its interior already speaks the boundary
language. Both are equally bus-conformant under §8.2's definition; the FUSION SCORE's later
"complexity cost" discussion (near the file's end) treats Alpha's adapter cost as a one-time,
amortized integration expense rather than a recurring one, precisely because the boundary-only
principle guarantees the adapter, once built, does not need to be revisited every time Alpha's
internals change for unrelated reasons — only when Alpha's PUBLISHED information changes in a way
that affects the envelope mapping.

8.7 The relationship between uniform contracts and IAS-F1's property model. A system's bus-conformant
envelope fields are not invented independently of F1's own property model (F1 Part IV): the evidence-
level field (§3.3) IS F1's Article V vocabulary; the sender/receiver stable IDs ARE F1 Part IX's
addressing model; a STATE-CHANGE event's prior/new value pair mirrors F1's own property-record
provenance shape (F1 Part IV §4.3) at transport granularity. This alignment is DELIBERATE and is the
concrete evidence, per Part XX's reinforcement map, that IAS-A2 reuses rather than reinvents F1's
vocabulary at the transport layer — a system that has already built an F1-conformant property record
for its own internal use gets its envelope's core fields largely for free, because the same
provenance discipline applies at both layers.

8.8 The uniform-contract discipline applied to a system that changes its OWN capability signature
over time. A boundary case worth naming: what happens to bus-conformance when a system's own
capability signature (F1 Part IX §9.2's second address component) changes — new capabilities added,
old ones deprecated — as it naturally evolves? Because uniformity lives at the boundary (§8.2) and
not in the interior, a capability-signature change requires NO renegotiation of the envelope contract
itself; the system's stable ID (the identity component of its address) is unaffected by a capability
change, so every existing subscription and channel address referencing that system by stable ID
continues to resolve correctly. What DOES change is the set of event TYPES and TOPICS the system is
likely to publish or usefully subscribe to — a system that gains a new capability may begin
publishing FINDING events on a channel it never touched before, which requires no bus-side
reconfiguration at all, only a new subscriber (if one exists) declaring interest in the new channel.
This is DERIVED as a direct benefit of decoupling identity (stable ID) from capability (F1 Part II
§2.4) at the addressing layer: capability churn, which F1 Part IV names as an expected and healthy
property of a living system record, never forces a bus-side migration, only an incremental extension
of who publishes or subscribes to what.

8.9 Worked example: an adapter that degrades gracefully rather than failing hard on an unrecognized
schema version. Consider a synthetic case extending §8.6's Alpha/Beta contrast: System Gamma's
boundary adapter receives an event whose schema-version field (§3.3) is HIGHER than any version
Gamma's own adapter code was written to understand — a realistic condition whenever a publisher
upgrades to a newer additive schema version before every subscriber has been updated to recognize it
(§8.4's additive-bump case, which by definition should not require subscriber coordination). Gamma's
adapter, following the uniform-contract discipline's own additive-compatibility principle, parses the
fields it recognizes and safely ignores the unrecognized new field rather than rejecting the event
outright — the correct behavior an ADDITIVE bump is specifically designed to make possible (§8.4),
and the concrete mechanism by which the uniformity contract lets old and new schema-version
subscribers coexist on the same channel without either being forced to upgrade in lockstep with the
other.

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. Uniformity lives at the boundary, never in the interior; a system's internal
design remains its own, forever, and the bus asks only that what crosses the envelope speaks one
shared language — the exact discipline that lets the Nth system join the federation for the cost of
one adapter rather than N-1 bespoke integrations.

PART IX — CROSS-SYSTEM STATE SYNCHRONIZATION I: REGISTRIES AND MEMORY
===============================================================================================

9.1 The problem the Cross-System State Sync candidate names. Per the ledger, Candidate 5's closest
parent was PM-01 (the repo-scoped shared brain) and CO-04 (virtual memory), and its overlap was
"one-repo state"; its unique object was named as "consistency across registries/memory/runtime/
evidence systems" — plural, cross-system, never bounded to one repository. This Part and the next
specify how the bus, whose ordinary traffic (Parts V-VII) is EVENTS, also carries the durable STATE
DELTAS (via STATE-CHANGE, §5.5-§5.7) that keep four distinct KINDS of ensemble state mutually
consistent: REGISTRIES (this Part), MEMORY (this Part), RUNTIME, and EVIDENCE (both Part X). The
four are treated separately because each has a different consistency requirement, and treating them
uniformly would either over-synchronize the cheap kinds or under-synchronize the expensive ones.

9.2 REGISTRY state, defined for this dataset's purposes. A REGISTRY, in the sense this Part uses the
term, is any durable, keyed store of system-level records — IAS-F1's own hypothesized system
registry (F1's Dataset-to-Runtime Translation section names this as a specification-level
candidate), IAS-C1's portfolio ranking table, IAS-D1's ecology map — where the authoritative copy
lives with ONE owning system, but other systems need a consistent, if slightly delayed, READ of it.
Registry synchronization over the bus is DEFINED as a specific usage pattern of STATE-CHANGE events:
the owning system publishes a STATE-CHANGE for every field-level mutation to its registry, and any
other system needing a read-consistent view subscribes to that registry's channel and maintains its
own LOCAL PROJECTION, reconciled by replaying deltas in the order it received them (never a global
order, per §4.6, but a per-channel order the bus DOES preserve within a single publisher's stream —
a guarantee named explicitly here because it is stronger than the cross-channel non-guarantee of
§4.6 and is load-bearing for registry consistency).

9.3 Why per-publisher ordering (not global ordering) is sufficient for registries. A registry's
authoritative copy is owned by exactly one system, and that system publishes its own deltas in the
order it applies them locally — a single-writer discipline that makes per-publisher ordering
sufficient to reconstruct a consistent projection, because there is never a second writer whose
deltas could interleave ambiguously with the first. (DERIVED: this is precisely why IAS-A2 does not
need the stronger, and costlier, global-ordering guarantee some general-purpose state-sync systems
require — the single-writer-per-registry discipline, itself an application of IAS-F1's Invariant
Three at the registry-field level, does the work a global clock would otherwise be needed for.) A
registry with MULTIPLE legitimate writers is, by this reasoning, a design smell independent of the
bus — it is either two registries wearing one name, or an unresolved Invariant Three violation, and
either way the fix belongs to the registry's own governing system, not to the bus's transport layer.

9.4 The staleness bound on registry projections. A subscriber's local projection of a remote
registry is, by construction, never more current than its own last successful reconciliation pass —
a fact that must be represented explicitly, mirroring F1 Part IV §4.3's staleness discipline for
system properties, applied here to derived projections rather than to primary records. IAS-A2
DEFINES the PROJECTION STALENESS field: every local projection a subscriber maintains carries a
last-reconciled timestamp, exactly analogous to F1's property-record staleness marker, so that a
system reading its own projection of another system's registry can honestly represent "this may be
out of date" rather than silently treating a three-day-old projection as current. (NORMATIVE:
projection staleness is the direct transport-layer instance of liveness's own staleness-signal
object, which is precisely why liveness is named as a strengthened parent — liveness's existing
staleness discipline gains a standard, bus-carried channel through which its own signal now reaches
every subscribing system, rather than remaining a per-system local check.)

9.5 MEMORY state, defined for this dataset's purposes, and its distinction from registries. Where a
REGISTRY holds structured, field-keyed records, MEMORY, in this Part's sense, is the less
structured accumulated context a system carries forward across missions — closer to CO-04's virtual
memory object or a project's own MEMORY.md-style accumulation than to a keyed table. Memory
synchronization across systems is deliberately a WEAKER guarantee than registry synchronization: the
bus carries memory-relevant FINDING and EVIDENCE events (Parts V, VII) that a system may choose to
FOLD into its own accumulated memory, but IAS-A2 does not define a memory delta format analogous to
STATE-CHANGE's field-level delta, because memory, unlike a registry, has no single owning system's
authoritative shape to synchronize against — each system's memory is its own, and the bus's role is
limited to making other systems' relevant findings and evidence DISCOVERABLE, never to imposing a
shared memory schema across the ensemble. (NORMATIVE: this restraint is deliberate — a shared cross-
system memory schema would be exactly the kind of over-reach Part VIII §8.5 warns a bus-conformance
requirement against, applied here to memory specifically.)

9.6 Worked example: a registry projection and a memory fold in the same trace. Consider a synthetic
mission: System C1 (Portfolio stand-in) owns the authoritative capability-portfolio registry and
publishes a STATE-CHANGE each time a system's ranked position changes. System A1 (Router stand-in)
maintains a local projection of that registry, reconciled every few minutes, and uses its own
last-reconciled timestamp to decide whether its cached ranking is fresh enough to route a
time-sensitive mission without a fresh AUTHORITY-REQUEST round-trip — if the projection is within
its own declared freshness tolerance, A1 routes directly from the projection; if stale, A1 issues a
fresh request rather than routing on data it cannot vouch for. Separately, System B1 (Cognitive
Multiplier stand-in) receives a FINDING event from an unrelated system describing an intervention
that amplified three other systems at once; B1 does not synchronize a registry against this event —
it FOLDS the finding into its own accumulated memory of high-leverage intervention patterns, an
internal operation this dataset does not further specify because it is B1's own object, not
IAS-A2's.

9.7 Registry deletion and the tombstone pattern. A registry field's mutation history (§9.2) handles
value CHANGES cleanly, but a registry RECORD's own removal — a system retiring, a decision class
being consolidated away — requires separate treatment, because the append-only discipline (§3.2)
forbids simply ceasing to publish about a now-absent record, which would leave every subscriber's
projection holding a permanently stale entry with no signal that it is stale-because-gone rather than
stale-because-not-yet-reconciled. IAS-A2 resolves this with the TOMBSTONE pattern, DEFINED as a
specific STATE-CHANGE whose new value is the reserved marker RETIRED rather than an ordinary field
value, published by the registry's owning system at the moment of removal. A subscriber's own
projection-maintenance logic treats a tombstoned record as authoritatively gone, distinct from an
unreconciled or never-published one, and the tombstone itself remains in the durable log
permanently, per §3.2, so that a system reconstructing history (§10.2's runtime-reconstruction
technique, applied here to registry history specifically) can always tell WHEN a record was retired,
not merely THAT it currently does not exist.

9.8 Why memory synchronization's weak guarantee does not mean memory is second-class. A closing
clarification against a plausible misreading of §9.5's restraint: choosing NOT to impose a shared
schema on memory is not a statement that memory synchronization matters less than registry
synchronization — it is a statement that memory's own NATURE (unstructured, system-specific,
accumulated by internal judgment rather than external mutation) makes a shared delta format actively
harmful rather than merely unnecessary, exactly the same reasoning Part VIII §8.5 applies to systems
with no natural publish/consume boundary at all. A future implementer who reads §9.5 as "memory sync
is the weaker, less-developed half of this Part" has mistaken a deliberate, DERIVED restraint for an
unfinished specification; the restraint IS the specification, and imposing more structure on memory
than this Part specifies would be scope creep past IAS-A2's own boundary-only uniformity principle
(Part VIII §8.2) applied at the wrong grain.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. A registry synchronizes by ordered, single-writer deltas with an honest
staleness bound; memory synchronizes by discoverability, never by a shared schema — treating the
two identically would either over-structure memory into a brittle shared format or under-structure
registries into an inconsistent one, and the bus's whole state-synchronization discipline rests on
telling them apart.

===============================================================================================
PART X — CROSS-SYSTEM STATE SYNCHRONIZATION II: RUNTIME AND EVIDENCE STATE
===============================================================================================

10.1 RUNTIME state, defined. RUNTIME state, in this Part's sense, is the transient, in-flight
condition of a mission or process CURRENTLY executing across systems — which system is presently
holding a decision, which authority-request is in flight and unresolved, which mission's execution
trace is mid-flight through IAS-A1's routing. Runtime state is the shortest-lived and highest-churn
of the four state kinds this dataset names, and it is DEFINED as explicitly NOT durable in the same
sense as a registry: runtime state is a live snapshot the bus's own event stream can RECONSTRUCT
after the fact (by replaying the AUTHORITY-REQUEST and STATE-CHANGE events of a given mission's
trace) but does not itself store as a separate persisted object, because a second copy of
already-reconstructible state would be exactly the double-measurement risk IAS-F1 Part IV §4.4
warns against for system properties, applied here to mission-level state.

10.2 Why runtime state is reconstructed, not stored. The design choice DERIVED here — that runtime
state lives only implicitly, in the ordered event log, and is reconstructed on demand rather than
maintained as a live, separately-updated object — follows directly from the honest-IPC discipline
Part XIV formalizes: any live, separately-maintained runtime-state object would require the
maintaining system to be simultaneously live with every system whose runtime activity it tracks,
exactly the synchronous-liveness assumption the bus's whole design rejects. Reconstruction-from-log
instead lets ANY system compute a point-in-time runtime picture at whatever moment it happens to be
running, using only the durable, append-only event log (§3.2) that already exists for other reasons —
a genuine reuse rather than a new maintenance burden.

10.3 The mission-trace reconstruction worked example. Consider a synthetic mission that spans four
systems: IAS-A1 issues a CONSUMES relation (F1 Part VI §6.7) selecting System P to serve a mission;
P, mid-execution, issues an AUTHORITY-REQUEST for a decision class it does not itself hold, which
Part XII's arbitration resolves to System Q; Q publishes a STATE-CHANGE recording the resolved
decision; P consumes it and completes the mission, publishing a FINDING summarizing the outcome. At
no point did any system maintain a "current mission runtime state" object; but a FIFTH system — say,
IAS-E1's Observability Fabric, reconstructing this trace long after the mission completed for its
own dead-capability and flow analysis — can replay exactly these four events, in their per-publisher
order (§9.3), and reconstruct precisely which system held the decision at which point, without any
of the four original participating systems having built or maintained that picture themselves. This
is the concrete mechanism, per the Strengthening Matrix's evidence requirement for IAS-A2, by which
"a cross-system event consumed by a non-originating system" is satisfied in the observability
direction specifically.

10.4 EVIDENCE state, defined, and its distinction from the EVIDENCE event type of Part VII. A
terminology hazard this Part must resolve explicitly: Part VII's EVIDENCE event type is a specific
kind of MESSAGE the bus carries; EVIDENCE STATE, as this Part uses the term, is the ACCUMULATED,
per-claim epistemic status a system maintains by folding the evidence chain (§7.3) into a durable
record — the difference between a single letter and the file it gets filed into. EVIDENCE STATE
synchronization is the mechanism by which a system's own confidence in a claim, once updated by an
evidence chain, becomes visible to OTHER systems without those systems having to independently walk
the full chain themselves — a system may publish a STATE-CHANGE summarizing its own recomputed
confidence (§7.3), letting downstream consumers read a pre-digested current status rather than
re-deriving it, provided the STATE-CHANGE's source reference (§9.2's provenance field) names the
evidence chain it was derived from, so a skeptical downstream consumer can always audit back to the
raw chain if the pre-digested summary is itself in question.

10.5 The four state kinds compared on one axis: reconstruction cost versus staleness tolerance.
Registries (Part IX) are durable, single-writer, moderately stale-tolerant. Memory (Part IX) is
durable but deliberately unsynchronized in shape, effectively infinitely stale-tolerant because no
freshness claim is made at all. Runtime (this Part, §10.1-10.3) is never separately stored, has zero
storage cost, but its reconstruction cost grows with trace length and it offers no staleness
guarantee at all — a runtime reconstruction is exact for exactly the events replayed, no more. Evidence
state (this Part, §10.4) sits between registries and memory: durable like a registry, but its
freshness tolerance is chain-dependent rather than field-dependent, because a well-corroborated
claim's confidence is naturally more stable over time than a thinly-evidenced one, and this dataset
does not impose a single staleness bound across all evidence state for that reason — it is one of the
places EXPERIMENTAL status (Article V) is explicitly appropriate, named honestly rather than
resolved by an arbitrary threshold.

10.6 Why all four kinds funnel through the same bus rather than four separate mechanisms. A design
question worth resolving explicitly: given how differently registries, memory, runtime, and evidence
state behave, why not build four separate synchronization mechanisms, each optimized for its own
kind? The answer, DERIVED from §8.1-§8.3's uniformity payoff, is that all four are carried by the
SAME two primitive artifact types — STATE-CHANGE for registries and evidence-state summaries, the
combination of AUTHORITY-REQUEST/FINDING/STATE-CHANGE replay for runtime reconstruction, and
FINDING/EVIDENCE discoverability for memory — meaning a single envelope contract and delivery
mechanism (Part IV) already suffices for all four, and building four separate mechanisms would
recreate the exact fragmentation Part VIII's uniform-contract discipline exists to prevent, at
exactly the moment this dataset would otherwise have re-earned the "thicker pipe" trap named in
Part I §1.2.

10.7 The relationship between runtime-state reconstruction and the arbitration subengine's own
gather step. A cross-reference worth making explicit rather than leaving implicit: Part XII §12.3's
arbitration procedure step 2 ("GATHERS the authority-grant record and the evidence trail") is itself
an instance of the runtime-reconstruction technique this Part specifies, applied to the narrower
question of one contested decision class rather than a whole mission trace. This is DERIVED as a
reuse relationship rather than a coincidence: both operations read the same durable, append-only
event log, replay it in per-publisher order (§9.3), and derive a point-in-time picture without ever
requiring the systems whose history is being replayed to be simultaneously present — the arbitration
subengine's own correctness (verified by Protocol Two, Part XIX §19.3) is therefore partially
underwritten by this Part's own reconstruction guarantee, one more instance of this dataset's
general pattern (already named in Part XVII §17.5) of reusing one mechanism across multiple
consuming purposes rather than building parallel, redundant machinery for each.

10.8 A closing distinction: reconstructed runtime state versus a genuinely live status check. This
Part's reconstruction technique answers "what WAS the state of a mission at some point in its
history" with full confidence, because the underlying log is durable and complete. It cannot answer
"what IS a system doing RIGHT NOW, at this exact instant" with the same confidence, because the most
recent moments of any in-flight mission may not yet be reflected in the durable log if the acting
system has not yet published its next event — a gap this dataset names honestly rather than
obscures. A system needing a genuinely live, this-instant status check is, once again, outside
IAS-A2's scope by the same reasoning §4.6 and §14.4 already establish for synchronous need generally:
reconstruction answers historical and near-historical questions authoritatively; it does not, and by
design cannot, answer "right now" questions with certainty, because "right now" is precisely the one
moment honest-IPC's disk-only, no-live-brain discipline (Part XIV) declines to instrument.

FINAL LAW — PART X. Registries synchronize by durable ordered deltas, memory by discoverability
alone, runtime by log-replay reconstruction rather than storage, and evidence state by chain-derived
summaries with chain-dependent, honestly EXPERIMENTAL staleness tolerance — four different
consistency disciplines, carried by the same two or three primitive artifact types, because the
bus's uniformity lives in its contract, never in a false claim that all state behaves alike.

PART XI — THE LIFT: FROM PANE-LEVEL PUBLISH/CONSUME TO SYSTEM-LEVEL EVENTS AND AUTHORITY
===============================================================================================

11.1 Why this Part exists as a named, explicit operation rather than an implication. Every prior
Part has referred, in passing, to IAS-A2 "lifting" pane-level mechanisms to system level. This Part
makes the lift itself the object of study, because the Strengthening Matrix's own evidence
requirement for IAS-A2 — "a cross-system event consumed by a non-originating system" — is only
checkable if the lift operation is specified precisely enough that a real trace can be tested
against it. An implied lift is not an auditable one; this Part supplies the mapping table and the
worked mechanics the rest of the dataset assumes.

11.2 The three parents being lifted, restated precisely. PM-03 is OBSERVED to be a findings bus
scoped to the panes of ONE repository: a pane discovers something, publishes it, and any OTHER pane
on that SAME repository, running in the SAME parallel-mesh session, may consume it. cross_signal_bus
is OBSERVED to be a LOCAL relay: a signal produced by one hook or module reaches another within the
same process or session, with no cross-session or cross-repository reach at all. CEPS is OBSERVED to
be a context/event propagation substrate threading state through ONE session's own tool-call chain,
lost at session boundary unless separately checkpointed. All three share the same shape: correct,
useful, and BOUNDED to a scope smaller than the ensemble — pane, process, session, respectively —
and none was ever meant to reach past that scope, because none was built against IAS-F1's system
addressing model (F1 Part IX), which did not exist when they were built.

11.3 The lift operation, defined as a four-step mapping. LIFT, as DEFINED here, is the operation
that takes an artifact produced within a bounded parent scope and re-expresses it as a bus-conformant
event addressed by IAS-F1 stable ID, such that it becomes consumable by ANY subscribing system
regardless of pane, process, or session boundary. The four steps: (1) SCOPE RESOLUTION — determine
which F1 system the artifact's ORIGINATING pane, hook, or session-context actually belongs to (a
pane does not have its own F1 stable ID, per F1 Part II's systemhood tests: a pane fails the
distinct-object and addressability tests independently of any bus concern, so a pane's finding must
be attributed to the SYSTEM the pane was instantiated to serve, never treated as its own address);
(2) TYPE MAPPING — classify the artifact against the five-type taxonomy (Parts V-VII): most PM-03
findings map to FINDING, most cross_signal_bus signals map to either FINDING or ALERT depending on
urgency, most CEPS context deltas map to STATE-CHANGE; (3) ENVELOPE CONSTRUCTION — wrap the
type-mapped payload in the standard envelope (Part III §3.3), setting an honest evidence level
rather than defaulting to OBSERVED (the specific anti-pattern IAS-F1 Part XIII names and this
dataset's own ANTI-PATTERNS section, near the file's end, repeats for the lift specifically); (4)
PROMOTION JUDGMENT — decide, per §5.3's raw/promoted/retired lifecycle, whether this specific
instance of the artifact is worth publishing at ensemble scope at all, since most pane-local findings
resolve locally and lifting every one would be the event-storm failure mode of Part XVI.

11.4 Why the lift is a JUDGMENT, not an automatic mirror. A design question this Part resolves
explicitly: should EVERY PM-03 finding, EVERY cross_signal_bus signal, EVERY CEPS context delta be
automatically mirrored onto the bus? The answer is NO, and the reasoning is DERIVED directly from
§9.5's memory-versus-registry distinction and from the OBSERVED pattern already named in §5.3: the
overwhelming majority of pane-level, process-level, and session-level artifacts are resolved within
their own bounded scope and never need ensemble-wide visibility. An automatic mirror would flood
every subscribing system with traffic of no interest to it, degrading the bus's own signal-to-noise
ratio and directly causing the event-storm failure mode (Part XVI §16.2). The lift is therefore
always a JUDGMENT CALL made by the system the pane, hook, or session belongs to — a call that asks
"would ANY other system benefit from seeing this," exactly the same question F1 Part II §2.2's
distinct-object test asks at the system level, applied here to individual artifacts. (NORMATIVE.)

11.5 The lift preserves, never discards, the parent mechanism. A crucial clarification against a
plausible misreading: lifting a PM-03 finding onto the bus does NOT mean PM-03 itself is replaced,
deprecated, or bypassed. PM-03 continues to serve its own object — fast, low-ceremony coordination
between panes on one repository, which remains valuable specifically BECAUSE it does not carry the
overhead of stable-ID addressing, envelope construction, or ensemble-wide delivery guarantees for
traffic that will never leave the repository. The lift is an ADDITIONAL step some pane-level findings
take, on top of their normal PM-03 life, never a replacement path — exactly the "strengthens, never
replaces" law stated in Article III and reiterated in this dataset's header block. A pane's finding
lives its full local life inside PM-03 regardless of whether it is ever lifted; the lift is a one-way
door outward, never a redirection of the original mechanism's own traffic.

11.6 Worked example: one finding's dual life, local and lifted. Consider a synthetic trace on a
project running parallel panes. Pane 7, working a bug fix, discovers that a particular test flake
correlates with a specific timing pattern — a PM-03 finding, published normally, consumed within
minutes by Pane 3 on the same repository, which uses it to skip a redundant investigation. This is
PM-03 operating exactly as designed, with no bus involvement at all. Separately, the SYSTEM that Pane
7 belongs to (say, a testing-infrastructure system) reviews its own pane-level findings periodically
and judges this particular flake pattern likely to recur on OTHER repositories the estate governs —
a promotion judgment (§11.3 step 4) made at the system level, not the pane level, because a pane
itself has no standing to decide ensemble-wide relevance (per §11.3 step 1's scope-resolution rule).
The testing-infrastructure system performs the lift: scope-resolves to its own F1 stable ID, type-maps
the finding to FINDING, constructs the envelope with evidence level OBSERVED (the correlation was
measured, not guessed), and publishes it on a channel scoped to "flaky-test timing patterns." A
DIFFERENT project's own testing-infrastructure instance, subscribed to that channel, receives the
lifted finding at its next reconciliation and checks its own test suite for the same pattern — the
finding now doing work Pane 7's original discovery could never have reached on its own, while Pane 3's
original, purely local consumption of the unlifted finding proceeded entirely unaffected.

11.7 The lift's relationship to cross_signal_bus and CEPS specifically, stated for completeness. The
same four-step operation applies to cross_signal_bus signals and CEPS context deltas with only the
scope-resolution step differing in mechanics: a cross_signal_bus signal is scope-resolved to the
system whose hook or module produced it (a hook, like a pane, has no independent F1 stable ID); a
CEPS context delta is scope-resolved to the system whose session the delta belongs to. In both cases,
per §11.4, the lift remains a judgment call, and per §11.5, the original local mechanism continues
operating unchanged. The three parents are strengthened by an identical mechanism despite serving
different local scopes, which is itself DERIVED evidence that the lift operation is a genuine
ensemble-level capability (F1 Part VIII's level test) rather than three separate, coincidentally
similar integrations — one operation, three local entry points.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. The lift is scope-resolve, type-map, envelope-construct, promote-judge — four
steps applied as a deliberate, occasional judgment call, never an automatic mirror — and it adds a
second, ensemble-wide life to a pane's, hook's, or session's local finding without ever touching or
replacing the local mechanism that produced it.

===============================================================================================
PART XII — INTER-SYSTEM CONFLICT RESOLUTION: THE ARBITRATION SUBENGINE
===============================================================================================

12.1 Why arbitration is a subengine, not the bus's default mode. Per NON_DUPLICATION_LEDGER.md,
Candidate 7 (Multi-System Conflict Resolver) is dispositioned SUBENGINE into IAS-A2, distinct from
the MERGE disposition given to Candidates 4, 5, and 10 (Parts VIII-X). The distinction is
deliberate: arbitration is not a facet blended into ordinary delivery, it is a SEPARATE OPERATION,
invoked only under specific, named trigger conditions (§6.4's third branch, §5.7's double-booked
STATE-CHANGE), that temporarily suspends ordinary delivery for the contested artifact while a
resolution is computed. Treating arbitration as the bus's default mode — checking every single event
for a conflict before delivery — would impose the cost of the rare case on the common case,
violating the same proportionality principle that governs which events get CRITICAL-severity
escalation (§6.2). Arbitration is DEFINED here as a subengine: an internal capability of IAS-A2,
invoked conditionally, sharing IAS-A2's own F1 stable ID and authority rather than existing as an
independently addressable system in its own right (F1 Part II §2.3's subsystem/component
distinction applied to IAS-A2's own interior).

12.2 The two trigger conditions, restated and unified. Arbitration is triggered by exactly two
conditions, both already introduced: (a) an AUTHORITY-REQUEST resolving to zero or more than one
system holding final authority over the named decision class (§6.4's third branch); (b) two or more
STATE-CHANGE events on the same field of the same subject arriving within a bounded reconciliation
window, each implying its publisher held write authority (§5.7's double-booked case). Both
conditions reduce, on inspection, to the SAME underlying question: is IAS-F1's Invariant Three
(Part V §5.4, exactly one final authority per decision class) currently satisfied for the decision
class in question? Condition (a) surfaces the question when a system EXPLICITLY asks; condition (b)
surfaces it IMPLICITLY, when two systems' behavior reveals disagreement neither system stated
outright. (DERIVED: unifying both conditions under one underlying question is what lets a single
arbitration mechanism serve both, rather than requiring two separate resolvers.)

12.3 The arbitration procedure, stated as a specification, not code. On trigger, the subengine:
(1) SUSPENDS ordinary delivery for the specific decision class in contest — subsequent
AUTHORITY-REQUEST or conflicting STATE-CHANGE events on that class are queued, not delivered,
during arbitration, so that a resolution is not immediately re-contested by traffic that arrived
moments later; (2) GATHERS the authority-grant record (F1 Part X) and the evidence trail (both
systems' own declared basis for believing they hold authority, per §6.4's candidate-authority
field) for the contested class; (3) APPLIES the F1 authority model's own resolution rules — a
grant with a later, unrevoked timestamp supersedes an earlier one; an explicit revocation, if one
exists, is dispositive regardless of timestamp; an unresolved case with no grant record at all
routes to the ESCALATION path of §12.6; (4) PUBLISHES the resolution as a STATE-CHANGE event
recording the authority grant, on a channel every system with a pending queued request on that
class is subscribed to by construction of the queueing step itself; (5) RELEASES the queue,
delivering the previously-suspended events now that the contest is resolved.

12.4 Why arbitration resolves the STRUCTURAL question, not the SUBSTANTIVE one. A necessary
restraint, DERIVED directly from Part X §10.6 of IAS-F1 (the boundary invariant, applied here by
analogy): the arbitration subengine decides WHO holds authority over a decision class; it never
decides what the CORRECT substantive answer within that decision class should be. Returning to
§6.7's worked example, arbitration resolves whether C1 or C2 holds final authority over "which
forecast model governs next-quarter demand" — it does not itself pick the forecast model. This
restraint is what keeps the subengine second-order in the strict sense of F1 Part VII §7.2: its
direct object is the authority relation between systems, never the resource-level decision content
itself, which remains squarely the authorized system's own object.

12.5 Concurrent-request merging, specified in full (extending §6.7). When multiple AUTHORITY-REQUEST
events target the SAME undecided decision class within the arbitration subengine's bounded
reconciliation window (a window sized to balance latency against merge opportunity — this dataset
does not fix a specific duration, marking the choice EXPERIMENTAL and deferring the tuning to
whichever later mission implements the subengine, per Article VIII), the subengine treats them as
ONE arbitration case rather than resolving each independently. This is DERIVED as necessary to avoid
a self-inflicted Invariant Three violation: resolving two concurrent requests on the same
undecided class independently risks granting authority to different systems in the same
microsecond-scale race, which the arbitration mechanism itself would then need to re-arbitrate — an
infinite regress this design avoids by merging at the case level before any resolution is computed.

12.6 The escalation path: when arbitration cannot resolve locally. Not every contested decision
class has an existing authority-grant record to apply rules against — the UNRESOLVED case named in
§12.3 step 3 arises specifically for NEWLY contested decision classes where no system was ever
formally granted final authority (§6.7's worked example is exactly this case). For this shape, the
arbitration subengine does not invent an answer; it ESCALATES, publishing an AUTHORITY-REQUEST-class
ALERT (severity WARN, not CRITICAL, because an undecided-but-not-yet-harmful gap is lower urgency
than an active Invariant violation) to owner_queue, naming the contested decision class and the
competing systems, and leaving both requesters in a queued, UNRESOLVED state (Article V) until an
Owner-level authority grant is recorded. (NORMATIVE: this is the concrete instance of Article IX's
governed-self-evolution boundary applied to authority itself — the bus proposes arbitration, but a
genuinely NEW authority grant, never previously existing, is an estate-governance decision the Owner
promotes, not one IAS-A2 invents on its own initiative.)

12.7 Worked example: arbitration under a stale, unrevoked grant. Consider a synthetic trace distinct
from §6.7's undecided-class case: System D1 (Ecology stand-in) and a newer System D1-prime (a
hypothetical successor built during an ecology-driven succession event, F1 Part XI referenced) both
publish STATE-CHANGE events on the same "system fitness score" field for the same subject, within
the same reconciliation window. Unlike §6.7, an authority-grant record EXISTS here: D1 was granted
final authority over this decision class eighteen months prior, and no revocation has ever been
recorded. The arbitration subengine applies §12.3 step 3's timestamp-and-revocation rule directly:
D1's grant is unrevoked and therefore governs, regardless of D1-prime's more recent code or its
publisher's own good-faith belief that its succession made it the natural new authority. D1-prime's
STATE-CHANGE is rejected as unauthorized, and the subengine publishes an ALERT (severity WARN)
noting the attempted write by an ungranted system — surfacing, as a byproduct of ordinary
arbitration, exactly the kind of stale-authority-versus-genuine-succession question IAS-D1's own
succession logic (referenced, not rebuilt) needs to resolve through its own proper channel, not
through an implicit STATE-CHANGE race.

12.8 Arbitration's own idempotency, and why re-triggering an already-resolved case is safe. Because
the arbitration subengine's resolution is itself published as an ordinary, durable STATE-CHANGE event
(§12.3 step 4), a SECOND arbitration trigger against a decision class that was ALREADY resolved —
for instance, a late-arriving AUTHORITY-REQUEST that missed the original merge window of §12.5 — does
not produce a conflicting second resolution. Step 2's gather operation (§12.3) reads the CURRENT
authority-grant record, which by this point already reflects the prior resolution, so the "two
alternatives considered" of step 3 collapse to a single, already-settled answer, and the subengine's
output is simply ordinary routing to the now-established authority rather than a fresh arbitration
case. This is DERIVED as a direct consequence of arbitration being built from the same durable
read-apply-write primitives as ordinary bus operation (Part XIV §14.3): a subengine built on durable
state is naturally idempotent across repeated invocation in exactly the way a live, in-memory
arbitration process would not be, because a live process would need to explicitly remember which
cases it had already resolved, while a durable-state subengine simply re-reads the answer it already
wrote.

12.9 Why arbitration cost is bounded, not open-ended, even under repeated contest. A closing
observation on the subengine's own resource discipline: because each arbitration case resolves to a
durable STATE-CHANGE that subsequent AUTHORITY-REQUEST events on the same decision class read
directly (§12.8), the COST of arbitration — measured in the depth of grant-record history the gather
step must consider — does not grow unboundedly even if a decision class is contested many times over
the estate's lifetime; each new contest only needs the CURRENT grant record and its own timestamp/
revocation status, never the full historical sequence of every prior contest on that class. This
bounded-cost property is precisely why Metric Pair Three's arbitration-recurrence countermetric
(§18.4) measures something diagnostically meaningful about institutional health rather than merely
reflecting an unavoidable, ever-growing computational burden — a HIGH recurrence rate is a genuine
signal of unresolved governance friction, not an artifact of the mechanism's own cost structure
scaling badly with repeated use.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. Arbitration is a bounded, triggered subengine that resolves WHO holds
authority, never WHAT the authorized answer should be, and its own default when no grant record
exists is not silence and not invention but escalation to the Owner — because a bus that quietly
manufactured a new authority grant on its own initiative would have crossed Article IX's boundary
in the one place crossing it is least visible.

===============================================================================================
PART XIII — AUTHORITY-REQUEST ROUTING AND INVARIANT THREE ENFORCEMENT
===============================================================================================

13.1 The relationship between routing and arbitration, disambiguated. Parts VI and XII together can
read as if every AUTHORITY-REQUEST triggers arbitration; this Part corrects that impression. The
COMMON case — a decision class with exactly one currently-granted, reachable authority — is ORDINARY
ROUTING (§6.4's second branch), a simple, low-ceremony delivery requiring no arbitration at all.
Arbitration (Part XII) is the EXCEPTION path, triggered only by the zero-or-multiple-authority
conditions of §12.2. This Part specifies the common case in full, because it is, by volume, the vast
majority of AUTHORITY-REQUEST traffic, and a dataset that spent all its depth on the rare exception
while under-specifying the common case would have its priorities backwards.

13.2 The routing procedure. On receiving an AUTHORITY-REQUEST, the bus: (1) resolves the decision
class against the current authority-grant record (F1 Part X); (2) if exactly one grant exists,
unrevoked, and the granted system holds a live, reachable subscription (a durable subscription,
per §3.5, not a synchronous live check — "reachable" here means "will be reconciled against," not
"is currently running"), the request is delivered as an ordinary event, indistinguishable at the
transport layer from any other targeted delivery; (3) the granted system, on processing, is
REQUIRED (per §4.4's stronger acknowledgment obligation for this event type) to acknowledge receipt,
distinguishing "delivered and pending" from "delivered and silently ignored" in the requester's own
view of the transaction's status.

13.3 The routing-versus-arbitration boundary as an instance of F1's own structural-before-relational
ordering. IAS-F1 Part V §5.7 establishes, for its own invariants, that STRUCTURAL questions
(is this configuration even legal) resolve before RELATIONAL ones (how do distinct, legally-
configured things interact). Authority-request routing inherits this ordering directly: the bus
first checks whether the AUTHORITY structure itself is coherent (exactly one grant — a structural
question, Invariant Three) before attempting to ROUTE within that structure (deliver to the granted
system — a relational operation). A request against an incoherent structure never reaches ordinary
routing at all; it is redirected to arbitration (Part XII) precisely because routing presupposes the
structural question is already settled, mirroring F1's own worked example (F1 Part V §5.7) at the
transport-and-authority layer rather than the ontology layer.

13.4 Routing latency and its relationship to decision criticality. Not every decision class carries
the same urgency, and §4.3 already noted AUTHORITY-REQUEST carries a tighter delivery-guarantee
bound than FINDING or STATE-CHANGE. This Part specifies the mechanism behind that tighter bound:
because ordinary routing (§13.2) requires no arbitration computation, its latency is bounded
essentially by the requesting and granted systems' own reconciliation cadence (§3.5), which is
already the tightest cadence any bus transaction achieves — the "eventual, not synchronous" ceiling
of §4.6 still applies (no AUTHORITY-REQUEST is answered within the same call), but among eventual
deliveries, ordinary-routed authority requests are the FASTEST class by construction, precisely
because they involve no suspension, no queueing, and no cross-system negotiation.

13.5 Authority-request routing to the Owner, distinguished from the arbitration escalation of §12.6.
A second, DISTINCT path by which an authority-request reaches owner_queue exists outside arbitration
entirely: a decision class may be granted, by explicit F1 Part X authority model design, as
OWNER-FINAL rather than system-final — a decision the estate has deliberately reserved for Owner
judgment rather than delegating to any system (Article IX's self-evolution boundary is the paradigm
case: no system may be granted final authority over "promote this proposed change to the estate,"
because that authority is Owner-reserved by constitutional design, not merely unassigned). An
AUTHORITY-REQUEST against an Owner-final decision class is routed, via ordinary routing (§13.2,
treating owner_queue itself as the "granted system"), directly to owner_queue — no structural
incoherence exists (the grant is clear: it is Owner-final), so this is NOT an arbitration case, and
distinguishing it from §12.6's escalation-on-absent-grant matters because the two paths carry
different urgency and different downstream Owner-facing framing (a routine Owner-final decision
routed normally, versus a WARN-severity alert flagging that no grant exists at all).

13.6 Worked example: three requests, three destinations, from one dataset's own object. Consider
three synthetic AUTHORITY-REQUEST events, each testing a different branch of this Part and Part
XII. Request One targets a decision class with a single, unrevoked, reachable grant: ordinary
routing (§13.2) delivers it directly, fastest-class latency (§13.4). Request Two targets a decision
class with two competing unrevoked grants (a genuine structural incoherence, perhaps introduced by
a migration error): arbitration (Part XII) triggers, resolves via the timestamp-and-revocation rule
(§12.3 step 3), and the losing system's future writes on that class are flagged. Request Three
targets "authorize retirement of System X" — a decision class constitutionally reserved Owner-final
under Article IX: ordinary routing (§13.2) delivers it straight to owner_queue, correctly bypassing
arbitration entirely because no structural question was ever open. The same AUTHORITY-REQUEST
primitive (Part III §3.6) produces three qualitatively different transactions, and distinguishing
them correctly is the entire operational content of this Part and Part XII taken together.

13.7 The candidate-authority field's role in speeding ordinary routing without weakening it. Section
6.4 named the CANDIDATE AUTHORITY field as a hint a requester may supply if it has a good-faith
belief about who holds a decision class, and this Part now specifies precisely how that hint is
used: the bus consults the actual grant record REGARDLESS of whether a candidate was supplied
(§13.2 step 1 is never skipped), but a correct candidate hint lets the requesting system's own
boundary adapter pre-populate its local cache with the likely destination, reducing the number of
distinct systems a large-fan-out requester (one issuing many AUTHORITY-REQUEST events across many
decision classes in a short window) needs to track pending state for. A candidate hint that turns
out to be WRONG carries no penalty beyond the wasted cache entry — the bus's own resolution is
authoritative regardless of the hint's accuracy, which is precisely why the field is DEFINED as
advisory rather than binding: a binding candidate field would let a requester's own possibly-stale
belief silently override the grant record's authority, the exact structural error Invariant Three
exists to prevent.

13.8 Why routing latency asymmetry across decision classes is expected, not a defect. A closing
observation worth stating explicitly: because ordinary routing latency (§13.4) is bounded by the
GRANTED system's own reconciliation cadence, and because different systems reconcile at different
frequencies appropriate to their own mission load, two AUTHORITY-REQUEST events issued moments apart
against DIFFERENT decision classes can resolve at materially different real-world latencies even
though both took the identical ordinary-routing code path. This asymmetry is DERIVED directly from
the honest-IPC discipline's own refusal to impose a uniform liveness assumption (Part XIV) — forcing
uniform latency across all granted systems would require forcing uniform reconciliation cadence,
which would in turn require every system to run on a shared schedule, reintroducing exactly the
synchronous-coordination assumption this dataset's entire design rejects. A future implementer who
observes this asymmetry in operational data should read it as the honest-IPC discipline working as
specified, not as a routing defect to be smoothed away.

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. Ordinary routing is the common case and arbitration is the exception, never
the reverse; a request against a coherent authority structure is delivered, fast, with no
suspension, while a request against an incoherent one — or one constitutionally reserved for the
Owner — takes a materially different, and materially slower, path, and conflating the two paths is
the single most likely way a future implementation quietly turns every request into an arbitration
case it never needed to be.

===============================================================================================
PART XIV — HONEST-IPC: FILES ON DISK, NO LIVE BRAIN
===============================================================================================

14.1 The founding constraint this Part exists to state explicitly. Every prior Part has assumed,
without yet justifying, that two systems on the bus need never be simultaneously running to
communicate correctly (§3.5's durable-not-live subscriptions, §4.3's eventual delivery, §10.2's
reconstruction-not-storage runtime model). This Part supplies the justification, and names it
HONEST-IPC: the discipline that the bus's durable state — its event log, its subscription table, its
authority-grant record — is the SOLE source of truth, realized as files or equivalent durable storage
on disk (or an equivalent durable substrate), and NEVER a live, in-memory coordination process that
two or more systems must both be attached to simultaneously for correctness. (NORMATIVE, and DERIVED
from the estate's own hard-won operational history: the Owner's own governing doctrine repeatedly
names live-process coordination failures — a background daemon a session forgets is running, a
live socket that silently drops, a "wait for the other pane to finish" pattern that produces the
exact dead-screen hangs the Owner's global configuration devotes extensive doctrine to preventing —
as a recurring, costly failure class, and IAS-A2 is designed, from its first primitive, to be
structurally immune to that class rather than merely disciplined about avoiding it.)

14.2 Why "no live brain" is stronger than "prefer durable storage." A weaker version of this
principle might say "prefer files, but a live coordination process is acceptable when convenient."
IAS-A2 rejects the weaker version deliberately: the estate's own seventy-plus systems are NOT, in
general, simultaneously running — a system may be invoked once per week, once per mission, or only
when a specific trigger fires, and an architecture that assumed even occasional simultaneous
liveness would work correctly in testing (where a developer's session naturally has many things
running at once) and fail silently in production (where the assumption is usually false) — the
exact "static verification does not prove runtime works" failure the Owner's own doctrine
(Mistake #17) names generally, applied here to the specific case of cross-system liveness
assumptions. (DERIVED: the strong version — durable storage is the ONLY source of truth, full
stop — is the only version that cannot degrade silently from correct-in-testing to broken-in-
production, because it never depends on a condition (simultaneous liveness) that testing
environments systematically over-satisfy relative to production.)

14.3 The concrete mechanics of honest-IPC applied to each bus primitive. The EVENT LOG (§3.2) is an
append-only durable record — no primitive in this dataset ever requires a system to hold a live
connection to another system's process to read it. The SUBSCRIPTION table (§3.5) is durable state a
system's own reconciliation pass reads independently, at whatever cadence that system chooses, never
a live registration that expires if a process restarts. The AUTHORITY-GRANT record (F1 Part X,
carried via STATE-CHANGE per §9.2) is durable and read the same way. Even ARBITRATION (Part XII),
the bus's most stateful operation, is specified (§12.3) as a sequence of durable reads and durable
writes — gather the grant record, apply rules, publish a resolution — never as a live rendezvous
requiring the contesting systems to be simultaneously present for the arbitration to proceed. This
consistency across every primitive is DELIBERATE: a bus that was honest-IPC for ordinary delivery but
reverted to live coordination for its exception path would have hidden its strongest liveness
assumption in its rarest, least-tested code path — exactly backwards from where an assumption this
costly should live if it must exist at all.

14.4 What honest-IPC costs, named without euphemism. The discipline is not free. Every guarantee
this dataset makes eventual rather than synchronous (§4.6), every state kind reconstructed rather
than tracked live (§10.2), every arbitration resolved by durable read-apply-write rather than
live negotiation, trades LATENCY and IMMEDIACY for RELIABILITY and INDEPENDENCE. A mission that
genuinely needs a synchronous, millisecond-scale cross-system answer cannot get it from IAS-A2 at
all — as §4.6 already states, that need routes to IAS-A1's mission-time CONSUMES relation instead,
a design choice this Part now supplies the deeper reasoning for: synchronous need is fundamentally
a DIFFERENT problem (a mission actively invoking a service it expects to be live right now) from the
control bus's problem (systems that may never overlap in wall-clock time exchanging durable,
asynchronous fact). Conflating the two would force IAS-A2 to support a synchronous mode that
compromises the honest-IPC guarantee for every OTHER transaction, a cost this dataset judges too
high relative to the benefit, and names as a NORMATIVE trade-off rather than an oversight.

14.5 Honest-IPC and the crash-recovery property it grants for free. A direct, DERIVED benefit of
honest-IPC, worth naming because it strengthens IAS-E2 (Cognitive Reliability, referenced not
rebuilt) without IAS-A2 building any reliability mechanism itself: because every bus primitive is
durable-state-first, a system that crashes mid-reconciliation and restarts recovers correctly by
simply re-reading its own durable subscription and acknowledgment records (§4.5's idempotency
obligation is precisely what makes this recovery safe rather than merely possible) — no special
"resume from crash" logic is needed beyond the ordinary reconciliation pass every system already
performs. This is the same crash-recovery property the Owner's own Windows-execution doctrine
independently arrives at for background task management (trust the durable output file, never a
live process handle) generalized from one host's tooling discipline to the whole federation's
transport layer — a convergent validation, OBSERVED rather than assumed, that the honest-IPC
principle is not an IAS-A2-specific preference but a recurring correct answer to the same underlying
problem at every scale the estate has encountered it.

14.6 Worked example: two systems that never once overlap in wall-clock time. Consider a synthetic
trace deliberately chosen to make the point starkly: System M publishes a FINDING on a Tuesday, its
process exiting seconds later and never running again that week. System N, subscribed to M's channel,
does not run at all until the following Monday, six days later, when it is invoked for an unrelated
mission and performs its routine reconciliation pass as a matter of course. N reads the durable event
log, finds M's six-day-old finding, processes it, and acknowledges it — a complete, correct
cross-system transaction in which the two participating systems' active lifetimes never intersected
by so much as a second. No live coordination mechanism in existence could make this transaction MORE
correct than honest-IPC already makes it; the durability of the log is the entire mechanism, and the
six-day gap is invisible to the correctness of the outcome, only visible in N's own honestly-reported
projection staleness (§9.4) if N chooses to surface it.

14.7 The one place honest-IPC is explicitly NOT sufficient, named rather than hidden. Per Article V's
honesty discipline, this Part closes by naming its own limit rather than overselling its scope.
Honest-IPC guarantees correct EVENTUAL delivery and correct crash recovery; it does NOT guarantee
that a mission depending on a fast cross-system answer will get one in time to matter — a
CONTESTED point this dataset resolves by scope exclusion (§14.4) rather than by claiming the bus
handles every case. A future implementer who reads this Part as "the bus can replace any live
coordination need" has misread it; the bus replaces every AVAILABLE-ASYNCHRONOUSLY need, and names,
rather than papers over, the synchronous-need gap it deliberately leaves to IAS-A1.

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. The disk is the only brain the bus has, and that is the point, not the
limitation — every guarantee this dataset makes survives a crash, a six-day gap, or two systems that
never once share a running process, because none of them was ever built on the assumption that two
systems would be simultaneously alive to begin with.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — DELIVERY GUARANTEES, ORDERING, AND IDEMPOTENCY UNDER CONCURRENCY
===============================================================================================

15.1 Why this Part collects and formalizes what earlier Parts stated piecemeal. Delivery-guarantee
language has appeared throughout this dataset — "eventual, not synchronous" (§4.3, §4.6), "tighter
bound for AUTHORITY-REQUEST" (§4.3, §13.4), "per-publisher order, not global order" (§9.2-9.3) —
each stated in the context that needed it. This Part collects those statements into one formal
taxonomy, because IAS-A1, IAS-D2, and IAS-C2 (Part XIX's integration discussion) each need a single,
citable delivery-guarantee reference rather than having to reconstruct it from scattered mentions.

15.2 The delivery-guarantee taxonomy, three tiers. TIER ONE, BEST-EFFORT EVENTUAL: FINDING and
ordinary STATE-CHANGE events (Parts V) are delivered at the subscriber's own reconciliation cadence,
with no bound this dataset fixes beyond "eventually, given the subscribing system runs again at
all" — the appropriate tier for informational traffic where delay has low consequence (§5.2, §5.8).
TIER TWO, BOUNDED EVENTUAL: CRITICAL-severity ALERT events (§6.2) and ordinary-routed
AUTHORITY-REQUEST events (§13.4) carry a materially tighter expected latency, achieved not by any
synchronous mechanism (§4.6 still applies) but by the owner_queue escalation fallback (§6.2) and the
fastest-reconciliation-class treatment (§13.4) respectively — "bounded" here means "the bus actively
works to minimize the gap," not "the bus guarantees a numeric bound," a distinction this dataset
marks EXPERIMENTAL because no numeric SLA is specified in this mission per Article VIII. TIER THREE,
ARBITRATED: events triggering Part XII's arbitration subengine are explicitly NOT delivered on the
ordinary schedule at all until arbitration resolves — their "guarantee" is correctness-before-speed,
a deliberate inversion of Tier Two's priority for the specific case where speed without correctness
would be actively harmful (delivering to the wrong authority is worse than delivering late).

15.3 Ordering, restated as a complete specification. Building on §4.6 and §9.3: the bus guarantees
PER-PUBLISHER, PER-CHANNEL ordering (events from one system, on one channel, are read by any given
subscriber in the order that system published them) and explicitly does NOT guarantee cross-channel
or cross-publisher ordering. This is DEFINED precisely because "no ordering guarantee" stated
without qualification would be read as weaker than it is — the actual guarantee is strong enough for
every state-synchronization use case in Parts IX-X (each of which relies on single-writer,
per-channel ordering, never on cross-publisher ordering) while remaining cheap enough to provide
without a global clock or a distributed consensus mechanism, which honest-IPC (Part XIV) would
reject as a live-coordination dependency in any case.

15.4 Idempotency under concurrent reconciliation, extended beyond §4.5's single-subscriber case.
Section 4.5 established idempotency for one subscriber processing one event potentially more than
once across its own crash-and-restart. This Part extends the case to MULTIPLE systems reconciling
CONCURRENTLY against the same durable event log — a realistic condition given the estate's own
multi-pane, multi-session operating pattern the Owner's own doctrine describes extensively. Because
every bus primitive is a durable READ followed by a durable WRITE (Part XIV §14.3), and because no
primitive requires a WRITE-WRITE race on the same record (registries are single-writer by §9.3;
arbitration serializes contested authority writes by design, §12.3), concurrent reconciliation by
different systems against the same log produces no corruption — each system's own processing is
independent, idempotent (§4.5), and reads a durable log that is itself append-only and therefore
safe to read concurrently by any number of readers without coordination between the readers
themselves. (DERIVED: append-only-log-plus-idempotent-consumer is a well-established pattern for
exactly this concurrency shape, and this dataset's contribution is not inventing the pattern but
specifying precisely which of the bus's primitives it applies to and why each of the alternatives
— live sockets, write-write registries, synchronous rendezvous — was rejected in favor of it.)

15.5 The event-storm boundary condition, previewed here and specified fully in Part XVI. A
concurrency case worth flagging even though its full treatment belongs to the failure-modes Part:
what happens when MANY systems reconcile against a log that has grown very large, very fast — for
instance, following a cascading estate-wide incident that triggers many ALERT events near-
simultaneously? This Part's guarantees (append-only safety, idempotent processing) still hold
CORRECTNESS-wise even under this load; what degrades is LATENCY, as every subscriber's
reconciliation pass must read a larger backlog. Part XVI names this the event-storm failure mode
and specifies mitigation; this Part's job is only to establish that the storm degrades performance,
never correctness — a distinction worth stating precisely because a system designer reading only
Part XVI might otherwise wonder whether an event storm could also corrupt state, and the answer,
DERIVED from this Part's own guarantees, is no.

15.6 Worked example: three systems reconciling concurrently against a shared channel with no
coordination between them. Consider a synthetic trace: Systems X, Y, and Z are all subscribed to the
same channel, and all three happen to run their reconciliation passes within the same few seconds,
entirely independently, with no knowledge of one another's timing. Each reads the durable event log
from its own last-reconciled point forward, in the log's own per-publisher order (§15.3); each
processes the events it finds, applying its own idempotent logic (§15.4); each records its own
acknowledgment and last-reconciled timestamp. No lock was taken, no coordination message was
exchanged between X, Y, and Z, and the outcome is identical to what would have resulted had X, Y, and
Z reconciled hours apart instead of seconds apart — the concurrency is invisible to correctness by
construction, which is the entire point of building the guarantees this Part specifies on top of an
append-only, honest-IPC substrate rather than on top of a live-coordination one.

15.7 The relationship between delivery tier and reconciliation frequency, made explicit as a design
recommendation rather than a bus-enforced rule. Nothing in this dataset's contract (Part IV) FORCES a
subscribing system to reconcile at any particular frequency — reconciliation cadence is each
system's own operational choice, per §3.5's durable-not-live subscription model. But the three-tier
taxonomy of §15.2 carries an implicit expectation this Part now states directly: a system that
subscribes to channels carrying mostly Tier Two (bounded eventual) or Tier Three (arbitrated) traffic
SHOULD reconcile more frequently than one subscribing only to Tier One (best-effort) channels,
because the bounded-latency expectation of Tier Two is only meaningful if the receiving system's own
cadence does not become the dominant term in the actual end-to-end delay. This is NORMATIVE
guidance, not an enforceable contract clause, precisely because enforcing a minimum reconciliation
frequency would require the bus to monitor a subscriber's own liveness — a live-coordination
dependency Part XIV's honest-IPC discipline exists to avoid even for this seemingly benign purpose.

15.8 Why a subscriber's own SLOW reconciliation degrades only its own view, never the shared record.
A closing clarification against a plausible misreading of the concurrency guarantees above: a system
that reconciles rarely, or a system that crashes and does not restart for an extended period, degrades
only ITS OWN freshness (visible honestly via its own projection-staleness field, §9.4) and never
corrupts, delays, or blocks any OTHER subscriber's reconciliation against the same shared, append-only
log. This isolation property is DERIVED directly from the append-only-log-plus-independent-readers
pattern named in §15.4: because no subscriber's read operation ever mutates the shared log or takes a
lock any other subscriber must wait on, one system's operational health (or lack of it) has zero
blast radius on any other system's ability to reconcile promptly — a property this dataset judges
worth stating explicitly because it is precisely the property a naive, live-coordination-based
alternative design would NOT have offered, and its absence in a hypothetical alternative would be the
kind of single-slow-subscriber-blocks-everyone failure honest-IPC's whole design exists to rule out.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. Best-effort, bounded, or arbitrated — three delivery tiers, one ordering
guarantee scoped to per-publisher-per-channel, and idempotency that survives not just one
subscriber's own crash but arbitrary concurrent reconciliation by many subscribers at once, because
an append-only log read by idempotent consumers needs no coordination between the readers to stay
correct — only to eventually catch up.

===============================================================================================
PART XVI — FAILURE MODES: EVENT STORMS, STALE STATE, LOST AUTHORITY, SPLIT-BRAIN
===============================================================================================

16.1 Why failure modes are catalogued rather than merely acknowledged. A system that names its own
failure modes explicitly is one whose designers have thought past the happy path; a system whose
documentation is silent on failure is one whose designers have not, or have hidden what they found.
This Part catalogues four failure shapes this dataset judges most likely to recur once IAS-A2 carries
real traffic, each with its mechanism, its detection signal, and its mitigation, following the same
discipline IAS-F1 Part XIII models for the ontology layer.

16.2 FAILURE MODE ONE — THE EVENT STORM. MECHANISM: a cascading condition (an estate-wide incident,
a mis-tuned system that publishes far more aggressively than intended, or the automatic-mirror
anti-pattern §11.4 explicitly warns against) causes a very large volume of events to publish in a
short window, degrading every subscriber's reconciliation latency (§15.5) and, in the worst case,
overwhelming owner_queue with CRITICAL-severity alerts (§6.2) that individually deserve attention but
collectively train the Owner to triage rather than read. DETECTION: a rate of events per unit time
on a given channel that exceeds the channel's own historical baseline by an order of magnitude is the
OBSERVABLE signal (this dataset does not fix a numeric threshold, marking the specific cutoff
EXPERIMENTAL and delegable to whichever mission implements the monitoring, per Article VIII).
MITIGATION: rate-aware publish discipline at the SOURCE (the promotion-judgment step of §11.3,
applied rigorously, is itself the primary mitigation — most storms are prevented, not survived, by
a publisher that correctly judges most local findings not worth lifting); a secondary mitigation is
CHANNEL-LEVEL BACKPRESSURE SIGNALING, whereby the bus's own observability data (consumed by IAS-E1
per Part XIX) can inform a publishing system that its channel is storming, without the bus itself
throttling or dropping any event — DROPPING is explicitly rejected as a mitigation, because a
silently dropped event violates the append-only, honest-record discipline (§3.2, Part XIV) more
severely than a delayed one ever could.

16.3 FAILURE MODE TWO — STALE STATE MASQUERADING AS FRESH. MECHANISM: a subscribing system's
projection staleness field (§9.4) is correctly tracked but silently IGNORED by the system's own
consuming logic — the field exists, is populated, and is never read, which is functionally
indistinguishable from not having built the staleness discipline at all, and is precisely the
ANTI-PATTERN 2 shape IAS-F1 Part XIII names at the ontology layer ("marking facts observed by
default") reappearing at the transport layer as "tracking staleness by default, without ever
checking it." DETECTION: an allocation, routing, or arbitration decision made from a projection whose
staleness exceeds the consuming system's own declared tolerance, discoverable only by an AUDIT that
cross-references the decision's timestamp against the projection's last-reconciled marker — a check
this dataset specifies as a candidate EVALUATOR (Part XIX's translation section) rather than builds.
MITIGATION: the projection-staleness field is not optional bookkeeping (§9.4's NORMATIVE framing);
a consuming system's decision logic MUST gate on it, and a system that reads a projection without
checking its staleness field has not merely under-engineered, it has silently reintroduced the exact
synchronous-liveness assumption Part XIV's honest-IPC discipline was built to eliminate, because
treating a stale projection as fresh is functionally equivalent to assuming the source system is
still live and unchanged.

16.4 FAILURE MODE THREE — LOST AUTHORITY (THE ORPHANED GRANT). MECHANISM: a system holding final
authority over a decision class is RETIRED (F1 Part XI, referenced) or otherwise ceases to
participate in the bus, without its authority grant being explicitly transferred or revoked — the
grant record persists, pointing at a system that will never again reconcile against it, and every
future AUTHORITY-REQUEST on that decision class routes, via ordinary routing (§13.2), to a
destination that will never respond. This is distinct from arbitration's zero-grant case (§12.6):
here a grant EXISTS and is syntactically valid, so ordinary routing never detects a problem — the
failure is invisible to the bus's own structural checks and visible only as accumulating,
never-acknowledged requests. DETECTION: a growing count of AUTHORITY-REQUEST events on one decision
class with zero corresponding acknowledgments (§4.4's required acknowledgment for this event type)
over a window exceeding the granted system's own normal reconciliation cadence is the observable
signal — precisely the kind of cross-system pattern IAS-E1's Observability Fabric (Part XIX) is
positioned to surface, because no single requesting system can see the AGGREGATE pattern across all
requesters, only its own individual unanswered requests. MITIGATION: F1's own succession discipline
(Part XI, referenced) requires an explicit authority-transfer STATE-CHANGE as part of any system's
retirement process; this dataset's contribution is naming the failure mode that results when that
requirement is skipped, and specifying the detection signal a future sweep would check for, rather
than building the sweep itself.

16.5 FAILURE MODE FOUR — SPLIT-BRAIN VIA CONCURRENT UNGRANTED WRITES. MECHANISM: two systems, each
believing in good faith that it holds authority over a decision class — most plausibly during a
succession event where the OLD and NEW system briefly overlap, or following a migration error that
duplicates a grant record — both publish STATE-CHANGE events on the same field, and Part XII's
arbitration correctly resolves WHICH write is authoritative (§12.7's worked example), but a
subscriber that already consumed BOTH writes before arbitration resolved has, for a window, held an
inconsistent view: it processed the unauthorized write as if valid before the bus's own
after-the-fact arbitration flagged it. DETECTION: any subscriber that has processed a STATE-CHANGE
later superseded by an arbitration-flagged rejection (§12.7) has been exposed to this window;
the bus's own arbitration output (the ALERT it publishes per §12.7) is itself the detection signal,
addressed to every subscriber of the contested channel. MITIGATION: because arbitration cannot be
instantaneous (Part XIV forbids the live rendezvous that would make it so), this window cannot be
eliminated entirely; it is BOUNDED instead by the reconciliation-window sizing of §12.5, and the
correct response, per the acknowledgment discipline of §4.4, is that a subscriber exposed to a later-
flagged write treats its own prior processing as PROVISIONAL (Article V's UNRESOLVED marking) until
the arbitration outcome is confirmed — a discipline this dataset recommends as NORMATIVE consuming-
system behavior rather than a bus-side guarantee, because the bus cannot retroactively un-deliver an
event it has already delivered under its own append-only, honest-record discipline.

16.6 The common thread across all four failure modes. Each of the four traces back to the SAME root
tension: the bus's guarantees (append-only, eventual, honest-IPC) are deliberately weaker than a
synchronous, globally-consistent system would offer, in exchange for the crash-resilience and
zero-liveness-assumption properties Part XIV argues are worth the trade. Every failure mode named
here is a specific, concrete instance of that trade-off's cost surfacing — never a bug in the sense
of a contract violation, but a consequence of the contract's own honest limits (§14.7) becoming
visible under specific conditions. Naming them here, rather than only in the abstract at Part XIV
§14.7, is what lets a future implementer recognize each shape when it occurs rather than treating it
as a mysterious, unprecedented failure.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. An event storm degrades latency, never correctness; stale state corrupts a
decision only when its own staleness flag is ignored; a lost authority grant is invisible to
structure and visible only to pattern; and split-brain's window is bounded, not eliminated — four
failure modes, and every one of them a named, specific cost of the honest-IPC trade-off, not an
unexplained defect in a system that claimed more than it delivers.

===============================================================================================
PART XVII — ADVERSARIAL CASES: SPOOFING, REPLAY, AND EVENT-FLOODING AS ATTACK SURFACE
===============================================================================================

17.1 Why adversarial thinking applies inside a trusted federation. IAS-A2 governs communication
WITHIN the estate, among systems the Owner ultimately controls — there is no external adversary in
the conventional network-security sense. But "adversarial" in this Part's sense is not about a
malicious outsider; it is about a MISCONFIGURED, BUGGY, or OVER-EAGER system inside the estate
behaving in a way indistinguishable, from the bus's own transport-layer view, from an attack — a
distinction this dataset draws explicitly because the mitigations are the same regardless of intent,
and because Article VII's domain-purity discipline and Article VIII's no-runtime-enforcement
constraint both apply equally whether the trigger is malice or a bug. (NORMATIVE: this Part
specifies detection and structural resistance, never a punitive response, because the bus has no
authority to punish a system — only to make a bad actor's or a buggy system's behavior VISIBLE and
STRUCTURALLY COSTLY to sustain.)

17.2 CASE ONE — SPOOFED AUTHORITY-REQUEST. SHAPE: a system publishes an AUTHORITY-REQUEST or a
STATE-CHANGE claiming a sender identity, F1 stable ID, that is not genuinely its own — whether
through a coding error that hardcodes the wrong sender field or a more deliberate misrepresentation.
WHY IT MATTERS MORE THAN AN ORDINARY BUG: because authority resolution (Part XII, XIII) trusts the
sender field as an input to its own grant-record lookup, a spoofed sender could cause the arbitration
subengine to apply the WRONG system's authority-grant history to a decision, producing an incorrect
resolution that then propagates as a durable, append-only STATE-CHANGE (§12.3 step 4) — durability
that is a virtue everywhere else in this dataset becomes, here, the mechanism by which a spoofed
claim's damage persists rather than expiring. STRUCTURAL RESISTANCE: this dataset does not specify a
cryptographic identity mechanism (out of scope, Article VIII, and arguably unnecessary inside a
single-Owner estate where every system's publishing code is, in principle, auditable) but does
specify that the sender field MUST be populated by the publishing system's own boundary translation
layer (Part VIII §8.2) from its own F1-assigned stable ID, never accepted as an arbitrary field in
an upstream caller's request — a structural discipline that converts "spoofing requires deliberately
modifying your own boundary adapter's hardcoded identity" from "trivial to get wrong by accident,"
raising the bar from zero-effort to deliberate-effort even without cryptography.

17.3 CASE TWO — REPLAY OF A STALE EVENT AS IF CURRENT. SHAPE: an event, once published, is
re-injected into the bus (by a buggy retry mechanism that does not check whether its own prior
publish already succeeded, or by a system that incorrectly treats an old cached event as newly
relevant) such that a subscriber processes it as if it were new, current information. WHY
IDEMPOTENCY (§4.5) IS NECESSARY BUT NOT SUFFICIENT HERE: idempotency protects against the SAME
event ID being processed twice with the same effect — but a replay attack, in this Part's sense, is
subtler: it is a genuinely NEW publish action, receiving a NEW event ID, whose PAYLOAD duplicates
stale information as if it were freshly true. Idempotency's protection does not apply, because the
bus correctly sees two distinct event IDs and has no structural reason to treat the second as
redundant. STRUCTURAL RESISTANCE: the timestamp field (§3.3) and, for STATE-CHANGE specifically, the
prior-value field (§5.6) together let a receiving system detect the mismatch — a STATE-CHANGE whose
claimed "prior value" does not match the subscriber's own last-known value for that field is
evidence of either an out-of-order delivery (benign, per §5.6's own detectability argument) or a
replay of stale data (this Part's concern), and the mitigation is the SAME structural check §5.6
already specifies for a different purpose: this dataset deliberately reuses one mechanism to defend
against two distinct failure shapes, rather than building a separate anti-replay check, because the
detectable-mismatch property already does the necessary work.

17.4 CASE THREE — EVENT-FLOODING AS AN UNINTENTIONAL DENIAL-OF-SERVICE ON OWNER_QUEUE. SHAPE: this is
the ADVERSARIAL reading of Part XVI's event-storm failure mode (§16.2), specifically the sub-case
where the storm concentrates on CRITICAL-severity ALERT traffic, which auto-escalates to owner_queue
(§6.2). A misconfigured system that incorrectly classifies a large volume of low-stakes findings as
CRITICAL alerts would, without any malicious intent, flood the Owner's own action queue faster than
any human can triage — a genuine denial-of-service on the Owner's attention, the single scarcest
resource in the whole estate. WHY THIS IS THE HIGHEST-CONSEQUENCE ADVERSARIAL CASE NAMED IN THIS
PART: every other case in this Part corrupts DATA; this case corrupts the OWNER'S OWN ABILITY TO
GOVERN, which HR-BACKLOG-001 through HR-BACKLOG-003 and the estate's own owner_queue doctrine treat
as close to the most protected resource in the federation. STRUCTURAL RESISTANCE: severity
classification (§6.1) is the publishing system's own responsibility, and this dataset cannot enforce
correct classification at the transport layer without violating the boundary-only uniformity
principle (Part VIII §8.2, which forbids the bus from inspecting or judging payload CONTENT); the
mitigation this dataset specifies instead is a RATE-BASED CIRCUIT BREAKER at the owner_queue
ESCALATION POINT specifically (not on ordinary bus traffic generally): if a single publishing
system's CRITICAL-severity alert rate to owner_queue exceeds a threshold within a bounded window,
subsequent CRITICAL alerts from that SAME system are still delivered and still durable, but the
auto-escalation-to-owner_queue step (§6.2) is suspended for that publisher specifically, replaced by
a single, batched WARN-severity meta-alert noting the suspension — protecting the Owner's attention
without ever silently dropping the underlying CRITICAL claims themselves (which honest-IPC's
append-only discipline, §3.2, forbids dropping in any case).

17.5 The unifying structural principle across all three cases. Each case's mitigation reuses a
mechanism this dataset already built for an unrelated primary purpose — sender-field discipline from
the addressing model (Part VIII), the prior-value mismatch check from ordering detectability (§5.6),
and the rate-based circuit breaker as a bounded extension of the event-storm mitigation already
specified (§16.2). This is DERIVED as a deliberate design principle rather than a coincidence: a
control bus that required a SEPARATE adversarial-hardening mechanism for every named case would be
building a second, parallel system alongside its ordinary operation, doubling its own complexity cost
(a concern the FUSION SCORE section returns to); reusing the ordinary-operation mechanisms for
adversarial resistance keeps the bus's actual surface area small even as its threat coverage grows.

17.6 What remains genuinely unresolved. Per Article V, this Part names its own limits rather than
claiming completeness it has not earned. The sender-field discipline of §17.2 raises the bar against
ACCIDENTAL misattribution but does not cryptographically prevent a DELIBERATELY malicious system
from lying about its own identity if that system's own boundary-adapter code is directly tampered
with — a scenario this dataset judges UNRESOLVED and out of scope, because a system whose own code
can be tampered with by an unauthorized party has already breached a security boundary the estate's
existing secret-firewall and repo-security governance (referenced, not rebuilt) are responsible for,
not one the transport layer can compensate for after the fact.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. Every adversarial case named here — spoofed sender, replayed stale claim,
flooded Owner attention — is defended not by a bespoke security layer bolted onto the bus, but by
reusing the addressing, ordering, and escalation mechanisms this dataset already needed for ordinary
operation, because a defense that requires its own separate machinery has doubled the very
complexity surface an adversary, or an honest bug, would exploit.

PART XVIII — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

18.1 Why every metric here is paired. A metric optimized in isolation is an invitation to game it —
the general lesson the estate's own memory ledger names repeatedly (never gate on a ratio; a
provider score mapped to a hard verdict kills classes) and the specific lesson this Part applies to
the control bus. Every metric below is therefore stated with its PAIRED COUNTERMETRIC — a second
measurement that would catch the specific way the first metric could be satisfied dishonestly —
following the same discipline IAS-F1 Part XV models for the ontology layer, applied here to
transport-layer measurement.

18.2 METRIC PAIR ONE — CROSS-SYSTEM REUSE RATE vs. PROMOTION PRECISION. CROSS-SYSTEM REUSE RATE is
the count of lifted (§11.3) findings that were subsequently consumed by at least one non-originating
system, divided by the total count of lifted findings — the direct operationalization of the
Strengthening Matrix's own evidence requirement for IAS-A2. A naive optimization of this metric alone
would reward a publishing system for lifting EVERYTHING (raising the denominator's floor while
hoping enough gets consumed to keep the ratio acceptable) or, worse, for lifting only the small
number of findings it is confident will be consumed regardless of genuine ensemble value — GOODHART
FAILURE: the ratio can be inflated by manipulating which findings get PROMOTED in the first place,
independent of whether promotion judgment (§11.4) itself improved. COUNTERMETRIC: PROMOTION
PRECISION, the fraction of ALL raw findings a system COULD have lifted (a denominator §11.4's own
"most findings resolve locally" observation keeps large) that were in fact judged worth lifting —
tracked not to be maximized but to be watched for suspicious STABILITY: a promotion precision that
never varies regardless of genuinely differing mission content is itself a signal the promotion
judgment has degraded into a fixed, un-thoughtful rule rather than a live judgment call.

18.3 METRIC PAIR TWO — DELIVERY LATENCY vs. STALENESS-AT-DECISION. DELIVERY LATENCY (mean and tail,
per Tier per §15.2) measures how quickly events reach subscribers. GOODHART FAILURE: a system
chasing low delivery latency alone could be incentivized to reconcile so frequently that it
effectively re-introduces a near-synchronous polling pattern, undermining the eventual-delivery
design's own resource-efficiency rationale (Part XIV) without actually improving DECISION QUALITY —
fast delivery of an event nobody acts on correctly is a vanity metric. COUNTERMETRIC:
STALENESS-AT-DECISION, measuring, for every decision this dataset's mechanisms can be traced into
(a routing choice, an arbitration resolution, an allocation), how stale the INPUT projection or event
actually was at the moment the decision was made (§9.4's staleness field, read retrospectively) —
a system could have excellent delivery latency on average while still making decisions from stale
inputs if its own consuming logic ignores the staleness field (Failure Mode Two, §16.3); pairing the
two metrics is what catches that specific gap, because low latency alone cannot prove the
staleness-aware consumption discipline (§16.3's mitigation) is actually being honored.

18.4 METRIC PAIR THREE — ARBITRATION FREQUENCY vs. ARBITRATION RECURRENCE. ARBITRATION FREQUENCY is
the count of Part XII arbitration invocations per unit time, a natural first instinct for measuring
"how much authority conflict exists in the ensemble." GOODHART FAILURE: a naive read of a LOW
arbitration frequency as unambiguously healthy is exactly backwards in one specific case — a
federation that has stopped detecting genuine authority conflicts because its own detection signal
(§12.2's two trigger conditions) has silently broken would ALSO show low arbitration frequency,
indistinguishable at a glance from genuine health. COUNTERMETRIC: ARBITRATION RECURRENCE, the
fraction of arbitration cases whose CONTESTED decision class was ALSO contested in a prior, separately
resolved arbitration case within a bounded lookback window — a HIGH recurrence rate on the same
decision class is the honest signal of underlying institutional dysfunction (an authority grant that
keeps getting contested was never durably resolved, only repeatedly patched), and this countermetric
is the one this Part expects to be MORE diagnostically valuable than raw frequency, because it
distinguishes "conflicts are rare because the ensemble is healthy" from "conflicts are rare because
detection is broken" in a way frequency alone cannot.

18.5 METRIC PAIR FOUR — CHANNEL FAN-OUT vs. SUBSCRIBER ATTENTION UTILIZATION. CHANNEL FAN-OUT is the
count of distinct subscribing systems per channel, a natural proxy for a channel's institutional
value (more subscribers, presumably more value). GOODHART FAILURE: fan-out can be inflated by
subscribing systems declaring broad, low-precision subscription predicates (§3.5) "just in case,"
without ever meaningfully consuming most of what they receive — a subscriber that never processes,
never acknowledges, and never acts on 95% of what it is subscribed to is contributing to fan-out
without contributing to genuine cross-system reuse. COUNTERMETRIC: SUBSCRIBER ATTENTION UTILIZATION,
the fraction of DELIVERED events per subscriber that receive an explicit acknowledgment (§4.4) or, for
event types where acknowledgment is advisory (§4.4), a downstream FOLD or FEEDS effect the receiving
system's own record shows — a channel with high fan-out but low aggregate attention utilization is
the specific signature of subscription-inflation, and this pair is designed to distinguish real
reuse (the Strengthening Matrix's own evidence bar) from merely-declared interest.

18.6 The metrics' own honest limit. Per this dataset's own Article V discipline, none of the four
metric pairs above has been MEASURED against a live estate as of this dataset's sealing — every
metric and countermetric named here is a SPECIFICATION (a definition, a numerator, a denominator)
rather than an OBSERVED result, marked EXPERIMENTAL in aggregate. This Part's contribution is
ensuring that whichever future mission DOES implement measurement inherits a paired, anti-Goodhart
design from the start, rather than measuring a single naive number first and discovering its gaming
vector only after the estate has already optimized against it — the exact sequencing error the
estate's own memory ledger names as a recurring pattern (a founding finding is a hypothesis; a
provider score mapped to a hard verdict kills classes) generalized here to bus metrics specifically.

18.7 Why none of the four pairs is a RATIO gated to a hard verdict, stated as a deliberate design
choice against a documented failure mode. The estate's own memory ledger names a specific, recurring
error: "never gate on a ratio — a ratio gate is satisfied by SHRINKING its denominator." Every metric
pair in this Part is deliberately constructed so that neither half is a bare ratio a future
implementation could game by denominator manipulation alone. Cross-system reuse rate (§18.2) is
paired with promotion precision specifically because reuse rate's OWN denominator (lifted findings)
is exactly the quantity a gaming strategy would shrink to inflate the ratio, and promotion precision
is defined over a DIFFERENT denominator (all raw findings, a much larger and harder-to-manipulate
base) for precisely this reason. The same discipline applies to all three remaining pairs: each
countermetric's denominator is chosen to be resistant to the specific manipulation its paired metric
invites, not merely to be "another number in the same area," which is what distinguishes a genuine
anti-Goodhart pair from a cosmetic second metric that could be gamed by the identical strategy as
the first.

18.8 A fifth, cross-cutting observation this Part closes with: metrics measure the BUS's own health,
never a consuming system's judgment quality. Every metric and countermetric in this Part measures a
property of the TRANSPORT — how much traffic moved, how fresh it was, how often authority was
contested, how widely a channel fanned out — never the SUBSTANTIVE QUALITY of any consuming system's
own decision (whether IAS-A1 routed correctly, whether IAS-D2 correctly judged a fingerprint
significant, whether IAS-C2's forecast was accurate). This boundary is DERIVED directly from §2.5's
own distinction between "the bus" and "the message," reapplied here to measurement specifically: a
metrics suite that tried to score consuming systems' judgment quality would be reaching past IAS-A2's
own second-order object into each sibling's own resource-level decision content, the identical
over-reach Part XII §12.4 forbids arbitration from committing. Each sibling's own IAS dataset — not
this one — is responsible for measuring whether ITS OWN judgment, informed by bus traffic, was any
good.

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. Reuse rate needs promotion precision, latency needs staleness-at-decision,
arbitration frequency needs recurrence, and fan-out needs attention utilization — four pairs, because
a control bus is exactly the kind of high-leverage, estate-wide instrument that a single naive metric
would be gamed against fastest, and the cost of discovering the gaming vector after deployment is
paid by every system that trusted the naive number in the meantime.

===============================================================================================
PART XIX — EVALUATION PROTOCOL: HOW TO PROVE THE BUS WORKS
===============================================================================================

19.1 Why a protocol, not a claim. This dataset has, throughout, marked its own mechanisms DEFINED,
DERIVED, or NORMATIVE — precise specification, not measured fact. Per Article XI, a dataset is
SEALED only when its strengthening evidence exists, not merely asserted, and the Strengthening
Matrix's own evidence bar for IAS-A2 — "a cross-system event consumed by a non-originating system" —
is a CHECKABLE claim, not a self-evident one. This Part specifies the protocol a future
implementation, or a future audit of this dataset's own design, would run to check it, mirroring the
four-protocol structure IAS-F1 Part XVI establishes at the ontology layer, adapted here to the
transport-and-authority layer.

19.2 PROTOCOL ONE — THE CROSS-SYSTEM CONSUMPTION TRACE. PURPOSE: verify the Strengthening Matrix's own
evidence requirement directly. METHOD: select a sample of PROMOTED findings (§5.3) over a bounded
window; for each, trace whether at least one system OTHER than the originating system's own pane,
hook, or session (per Part XI's lift) subsequently issued an acknowledgment (§4.4) or a downstream
FOLD/FEEDS effect (§18.5's attention-utilization countermetric) attributable to that specific event
ID. PASS CONDITION: a non-trivial fraction of the sampled findings (this dataset does not fix the
threshold, marking it EXPERIMENTAL per Article VIII) show genuine non-originating consumption. FAIL
SIGNAL: a sample dominated by findings that were promoted (§11.4's judgment call) but never
consumed by anyone is direct evidence either that promotion judgment is miscalibrated (over-lifting,
Failure Mode One §16.2) or that subscription coverage across the estate is too thin for the bus's
value proposition to be realized — Protocol One cannot itself distinguish the two causes, but it
reliably detects that SOMETHING in the reuse pipeline is broken.

19.3 PROTOCOL TWO — THE ARBITRATION CORRECTNESS REPLAY. PURPOSE: verify that Part XII's arbitration
subengine, when invoked, resolves to the OUTCOME the F1 authority model (Part X) actually specifies,
not merely to SOME outcome. METHOD: for a sample of synthetic or historical arbitration cases,
independently compute the expected resolution by manually applying §12.3's timestamp-and-revocation
rule to the authority-grant record as it stood at the time of the contest, and compare against the
subengine's actual published resolution. PASS CONDITION: the subengine's resolutions match the
independently-computed expectation in every sampled case — arbitration is exactly the kind of
mechanism (per §12.4's restraint to structural, not substantive, questions) where even a single
undetected mismatch is disproportionately costly, because a wrong authority resolution propagates
as durable, trusted state to every future consumer of that decision class. FAIL SIGNAL: any
mismatch is treated as a CRITICAL finding in its own right, per this dataset's own §6.1 severity
vocabulary, because it indicates the arbitration subengine itself — the mechanism every OTHER
system trusts to keep Invariant Three honest — cannot be trusted.

19.4 PROTOCOL THREE — THE HONEST-IPC STRESS TEST. PURPOSE: verify Part XIV's central claim — that no
bus primitive silently requires two systems to be simultaneously live — under conditions designed to
expose a hidden liveness assumption if one exists. METHOD: construct synthetic traces, modeled on
§14.6's worked example, in which publishing and subscribing systems are deliberately scheduled to
NEVER overlap in wall-clock time, across a range of gaps (seconds, hours, days, and — to probe the
unbounded evidence-chain risk named in §7.6 — weeks), and verify that delivery, acknowledgment, and
state reconstruction (§10.2) all complete correctly regardless of gap length. PASS CONDITION: outcome
is identical across all tested gap lengths — correctness must be gap-length-invariant by Part XIV's
own claim. FAIL SIGNAL: any gap length at which correctness degrades (not merely latency, which is
expected to vary, but CORRECTNESS) is direct evidence of a hidden live-coordination assumption
somewhere in the implementation, a violation of this dataset's own most load-bearing normative
commitment.

19.5 PROTOCOL FOUR — THE ADVERSARIAL-CASE DRY RUN. PURPOSE: verify that Part XVII's three named
mitigations (sender-field discipline, prior-value mismatch detection, rate-based circuit breaker)
actually fire under the conditions they claim to cover, rather than existing only as specification
text. METHOD: construct one synthetic instance of each of the three adversarial cases (§17.2-§17.4)
deliberately, and verify each mitigation's detection signal is observed and each mitigation's
structural response (raised bar, mismatch flag, escalation suspension) actually occurs. PASS
CONDITION: all three cases produce their specified defensive behavior. FAIL SIGNAL: a case that
executes without triggering its named mitigation is evidence that the mitigation's specification in
this dataset was aspirational rather than implementable as stated — a gap this dataset would need to
be revised to address, per its own versioning discipline (a facet Part XX's dependency inventory
touches only briefly, since full lifecycle/versioning depth is F1's own object, referenced here
rather than re-derived).

19.6 The relationship between the four protocols and Article XI's SEALED bar. None of the four
protocols has, as of this dataset's own authorship, been executed against a live implementation —
IAS-A2, like its Tier-0 foundation, is specification, not yet built code, per Article VIII. This Part
therefore does the same honest work IAS-F1 Part XVI does for itself: it specifies HOW correctness
would be checked, so that the checking is possible in principle, while explicitly NOT claiming the
checking has occurred. A dataset that specified no evaluation protocol at all would leave its own
Strengthening Matrix evidence requirement permanently unverifiable even in principle; a dataset that
claimed the protocols had already been run, when they have not, would violate Article V's honesty
discipline outright. This Part occupies the correct middle ground Article XI requires: CONTENT
COMPLETE on the specification, honestly short of SEALED on the empirical claim.

19.7 Ordering the four protocols by dependency, for whichever future mission executes them. The four
protocols are not independent of one another, and a future implementer benefits from knowing the
correct execution order rather than running them in specification order. Protocol Three (honest-IPC
stress test, §19.4) should run FIRST, because every other protocol's own correctness depends on the
bus's basic liveness-independence claim holding — a Protocol One consumption trace run against a
bus that secretly requires simultaneous liveness would produce misleading results indistinguishable
from a genuine reuse failure. Protocol Two (arbitration correctness replay, §19.3) should run SECOND,
because Protocol Four's adversarial dry run (§19.5) presupposes arbitration itself resolves
correctly under ordinary conditions before testing whether it resolves correctly under adversarial
ones. Protocol One (cross-system consumption trace, §19.2) and Protocol Four (adversarial dry run,
§19.5) can run in either order relative to each other, or in parallel, once Protocols Three and Two
have established the substrate both depend on. This ordering is DERIVED from each protocol's own
stated PURPOSE clause rather than asserted as convention, and a future implementer who runs the
protocols out of this order risks attributing a Protocol Three-class failure to a Protocol One or
Protocol Four finding, wasting diagnostic effort on the wrong layer.

19.8 What a passing result across all four protocols would, and would not, license claiming. Per
Article XI, passing all four protocols would license moving IAS-A2's own implementation from
CONTENT_COMPLETE toward EVALUATED in the dataset state machine — but even a full pass would NOT
license the claim that IAS-A2 is bug-free, complete, or beyond revision. The four protocols test
specifically the claims this dataset makes explicit (cross-system reuse, arbitration correctness,
liveness-independence, adversarial resistance); they do not, and cannot, test claims this dataset
has not made — for instance, the EXPERIMENTAL threshold values named throughout (the event-storm
cutoff, the arbitration reconciliation window, the circuit-breaker rate) remain EXPERIMENTAL even
after a structural pass, because passing "the mitigation fires" (Protocol Four) is a different claim
from passing "the mitigation fires AT THE RIGHT THRESHOLD," and this dataset has deliberately left
the latter uncalibrated per Article VIII. A future audit should read a four-protocol pass as
evidence the SHAPE of this dataset's design is sound, never as evidence its every numeric parameter
is correctly tuned.

FINAL LAW — PART XIX. Four protocols — trace the reuse, replay the arbitration, stress the honest-IPC
claim across every gap length, and dry-run the adversarial cases — are what would convert this
dataset's own design claims from NORMATIVE assertion to OBSERVED fact, and until they are run against
a live implementation, every correctness claim in this dataset remains, honestly, a specification
carrying its own test rather than a result.

PART XX — INTEGRATION WITH IAS-A1, IAS-D2, IAS-C2: WORKED TRACES AND THE CLOSING SYNTHESIS
===============================================================================================

20.1 Why integration is proven by trace, not by assertion. Per COMPOUNDING_GRAPH.md, IAS-A2 sits
directly in the path of the primary loop ("mission executes across systems via IAS-A2 Control Bus"),
the defense loop ("IAS-A2 Control Bus carries failure events"), and — by the cross-dataset feedback
ledger's own ROW for IAS-A2 ("cross-system events → IAS-D2, IAS-E1, IAS-C2 → immunity, observation,
demand signal") — feeds three named siblings directly. This Part traces each integration as a
concrete transaction using this dataset's own primitives, rather than merely repeating the graph's
prose, because Article III requires every IAS dataset to declare what it receives, produces, who
consumes it, and which system it strengthens IN ITS OWN TEXT, and a citation to the graph document
alone would not satisfy that requirement.

20.2 Integration with IAS-A1 (Capability Router): the CONSUMES-to-bus handoff. IAS-A1's own object,
per the ledger, is routing a mission to the system(s) that should serve it — a mission-time,
synchronous-feeling decision realized through F1's CONSUMES relation (F1 Part VI §6.7). IAS-A2's
relationship to A1 is precise and narrow: A1 does NOT use the bus's ordinary FINDING/STATE-CHANGE
traffic to make its per-mission routing call (that call needs the low-latency, mission-time CONSUMES
mechanism explicitly carved out in §4.6 and §14.4 as outside the bus's scope); rather, A1 CONSUMES
the bus's REGISTRY PROJECTIONS (Part IX §9.6's own worked example already traces exactly this: A1
maintaining a local projection of IAS-C1's portfolio registry to inform routing) as one input among
several to its routing decision, and A1 ITSELF becomes a PUBLISHER when a routing decision resolves
an AUTHORITY-REQUEST that named routing itself as the contested decision class (a rarer but
legitimate case: two systems disagreeing about which of them should serve a given mission type is
itself an Invariant Three question A1, as the granted authority over the routing decision class, is
positioned to resolve via ordinary routing, §13.2, functioning as the "granted system" for that
specific class). The concrete evidence artifact: a routing decision whose input included a bus-
sourced registry projection with a logged staleness value within A1's own declared tolerance
(§9.4) — checkable per Protocol One's method (§19.2) applied to A1's own decision log rather than to
a finding's consumption.

20.3 Integration with IAS-D2 (Immune System): the fingerprint-federation pipeline. Per the
Compounding Graph's defense loop, IAS-A2 is the literal carrier of the failure events IAS-D2
fingerprints and federates cross-project. The precise mechanism, using this dataset's own primitives:
a project-scoped failure detection (cascade_prevention, secret_firewall, or another estate immune
component, referenced not rebuilt) is lifted (Part XI) as a PROMOTED FINDING, type FINDING, evidence
level OBSERVED, carrying the failure's fingerprint in its payload; IAS-D2, subscribed to the
cross-estate "failure fingerprint" channel, receives it via ordinary Tier-One delivery (§15.2) at its
own reconciliation cadence, checks whether the fingerprint matches a pattern already known to affect
a DIFFERENT project, and — if so — publishes its own FINDING, referencing the original by ID,
declaring the pattern now federated across two or more projects; any OTHER project's own local
immune component, subscribed to the same channel, receives D2's federated finding and can
preemptively check its own exposure without having independently discovered the failure — the exact
mechanism the Strengthening Matrix's own IAS-D2 row (per SYSTEM_STRENGTHENING_MATRIX.md, not this
dataset's row) describes as "a fix in one project immunizes all." IAS-A2's specific contribution to
this pipeline is narrow and precise: it is the transport and promotion mechanism (Parts III, V, XI)
that makes cross-project reach possible at all; the fingerprinting logic, the pattern-matching, and
the federation JUDGMENT belong entirely to IAS-D2, never duplicated here.

20.4 Integration with IAS-C2 (Demand Forecasting): the demand-signal extraction. Per the cross-
dataset feedback ledger, IAS-A2's cross-system events feed IAS-C2 as a "demand signal." The
mechanism: IAS-C2's own object (predicting which capability the ensemble will need before a mission
demands it) is materially strengthened by observing the bus's own AUTHORITY-REQUEST traffic over
time — a decision class that generates a RISING volume of AUTHORITY-REQUEST events pointing at an
UNRESOLVED or thinly-granted authority (§12.6's escalation path, occurring repeatedly for the SAME
decision class) is a direct, OBSERVED signal that the ensemble currently lacks a well-established
capability for that decision class, exactly the kind of gap IAS-C2 exists to forecast and pre-
position against. IAS-A2 does not compute this forecast itself — that judgment, including how much
recurrence constitutes a genuine demand signal versus noise, is IAS-C2's own object — but the raw
material, ARBITRATION RECURRENCE (§18.4's own countermetric, repurposed here as C2's own leading
indicator rather than merely A2's internal health check), is a direct byproduct of ordinary bus
operation that C2 consumes without IAS-A2 building any forecasting logic of its own. This is a
second, concrete instance of the same principle §20.3 demonstrates: A2 supplies the observable
substrate, the consuming sibling supplies the judgment.

20.5 The three integrations compared: what pattern recurs across all of them. Read together, §20.2-
20.4 show the SAME shape three times: IAS-A2 never makes the routing decision (A1's object), never
makes the immunity judgment (D2's object), never makes the forecast (C2's object) — in every case,
the bus supplies TRANSPORT and PROMOTION (an event correctly typed, addressed, and delivered) while
the consuming sibling supplies JUDGMENT (what the event means for ITS OWN object). This is DERIVED
directly from §2.5's own distinction between "the bus" and "the message," restated now as an
operational pattern across three concrete integrations rather than as an abstract claim, and it is
the precise mechanism by which IAS-A2 satisfies Article III's "strengthen, never replace" law for
every sibling it touches: none of A1, D2, or C2 has had any of its own judgment-making capability
duplicated or diminished by A2's existence; each has gained a reliable, uniform, typed channel for
information it previously had no cross-system way to receive at all.

20.6 The closing synthesis: what the control bus is, restated without the scaffolding. Twenty Parts
of definition, contract, taxonomy, arbitration, honest-IPC, failure mode, adversarial case, metric,
and evaluation protocol reduce, at the end, to one operational claim: the ensemble's seventy-plus
systems can now exchange four kinds of typed, addressed, evidenced fact — what was found, what
changed, what is wrong, who decides — without ever requiring two systems to be simultaneously alive,
without silently corrupting state under concurrent access, without letting an unresolved authority
conflict masquerade as resolved, and without a single event ever being silently dropped or edited out
of the record. That claim is what makes the Compounding Graph's own arrows real rather than
aspirational — an arrow drawn from one system's box to another's on a diagram is a hope; the same
arrow, realized as an addressed EVENT crossing a durable, honest-IPC bus with a checkable evaluation
protocol behind it, is a mechanism. IAS-A2 does not claim to have built that mechanism — Article VIII
forbids it this mission — but it claims, and this Part's traced integrations are offered as the
concrete support for the claim, to have specified it completely enough that building it is now an
implementation task rather than an open design question.

FINAL LAW — PART XX. Every arrow in the Compounding Graph that crosses a system boundary is either
already this bus's traffic or a gap this dataset has named rather than hidden; the federation's
circulatory system does not judge, route, forecast, or immunize on any sibling's behalf — it only,
and completely, makes sure that when one system finds, changes, warns, asks, or proves something,
every other system that needs to know eventually, honestly, and correctly does.

DECISION LOG
===============================================================================================

D1. DECISION: STATE-CHANGE events carry DELTAS only, never full snapshots (Part V §5.6).
ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) snapshot-carrying events, where each STATE-CHANGE republishes the
subject's full current property record; (b) a hybrid where small properties snapshot and large ones
delta. WHY THIS: the bus's own no-cross-channel-ordering guarantee (§4.6) means a late-arriving
snapshot could silently overwrite a subscriber's more current state with no way to detect the
error, while a delta applied out of order produces a DETECTABLE prior-value mismatch (§5.6) a
subscriber can flag rather than silently accept — detectability under a weak ordering guarantee was
judged more valuable than the convenience of snapshot completeness. TRADE-OFFS: every subscriber
must maintain its own last-known value per field to detect mismatches, a real bookkeeping cost
delta-only synchronization imposes that snapshot-carrying would not. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if
Protocol Three's stress testing (§19.4) finds subscribers routinely failing to maintain the
required last-known-value state, a hybrid model becomes a revisory-change candidate.

D2. DECISION: RUNTIME state (Part X §10.1-10.3) is reconstructed from the event log on demand, never
separately stored as a live, maintained object. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) a dedicated live
runtime-state tracker any system could query; (b) periodic runtime snapshots persisted alongside the
event log. WHY THIS: a separately-maintained live tracker requires the maintaining system to be
simultaneously live with every system whose activity it tracks, the exact synchronous-liveness
assumption Part XIV's honest-IPC discipline rejects; log-replay reconstruction reuses the already-
durable event log rather than introducing a second, redundant state store, avoiding the double-
measurement risk named in §10.1. TRADE-OFFS: reconstruction cost grows with trace length, and a very
long-running mission's runtime picture becomes progressively more expensive to reconstruct fresh
each time. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if IAS-E1's own observability workload (§10.3) shows reconstruction
cost dominating its budget for long traces, a bounded periodic-checkpoint mechanism becomes a
revisory-change candidate layered on top of, not replacing, the log.

D3. DECISION: Conflict arbitration (Part XII) is a bounded SUBENGINE invoked only on named trigger
conditions (§12.2), never a per-event default check run against every message the bus carries.
ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) check every event for a potential authority or duplication conflict
before delivery, unconditionally; (b) a separate, always-on conflict-scanning daemon operating
independently of the ordinary delivery path. WHY THIS: the overwhelming majority of bus traffic
(FINDING, ordinary STATE-CHANGE, informational ALERT) never touches a contested decision class, and
imposing arbitration-grade checking on all of it would impose the cost of the rare case on the
common one, degrading Tier-One delivery latency (§15.2) for no benefit; the two named trigger
conditions (§12.2) are DERIVED to reduce to one underlying question (is Invariant Three currently
satisfied), letting one bounded mechanism serve both without a separate always-on process.
TRADE-OFFS: a conflict shape not covered by either named trigger condition would go undetected until
a future dataset revision names it, an honestly-acknowledged coverage gap rather than a hidden one.
REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if Protocol Four's adversarial dry run (§19.5) or real operational history
surfaces a conflict shape neither trigger condition catches, the trigger-condition set becomes a
revisory-change candidate.

D4. DECISION: CRITICAL-severity ALERT events auto-escalate to owner_queue only when no subscribing
system can resolve them, never unconditionally (Part VI §6.2). ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) every
CRITICAL alert escalates to owner_queue regardless of whether a system-level resolver exists; (b) no
alert auto-escalates, and the Owner must proactively poll for critical conditions. WHY THIS:
unconditional escalation would flood owner_queue with alerts the ensemble can resolve itself,
training the Owner to triage rather than read (the exact harm named in Part XVII §17.4's adversarial
case); no auto-escalation at all would leave a genuinely ungoverned critical condition invisible
until the Owner happened to look, violating the estate's own owner_queue doctrine that a P0-class
condition must surface proactively. TRADE-OFFS: the conditional design depends on the ensemble's own
resolving-subscriber coverage being accurate, and a system that silently stops subscribing to a
critical channel it once covered creates exactly the lost-authority-adjacent gap named in Failure
Mode Three (§16.4), applied here to alert coverage rather than authority grants. REVERSAL CONDITIONS:
if Protocol Four (§19.5) or the rate-based circuit breaker (§17.4) shows owner_queue is still
receiving unmanageable volume despite the conditional design, escalation criteria become a revisory-
change candidate.

D5. DECISION: Uniformity is enforced only at the system BOUNDARY (the envelope), never by requiring
any system's internal representation to change (Part VIII §8.2). ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a)
mandate every bus-participating system internally adopt the envelope shape as its own native
representation; (b) a two-tier bus, with a "legacy adapter" tier for older systems and a "native"
tier for new ones, carrying different guarantees. WHY THIS: Article X forbids this mission from
mutating the estate at all, making internal-representation mandates structurally impossible to
enforce even if desirable; a two-tier bus would recreate exactly the fragmentation this dataset
exists to eliminate, with subscribers needing to know which tier a given publisher belongs to. The
boundary-only principle keeps a single, universal contract while imposing integration cost only
once, at each system's own translation adapter (§8.6). TRADE-OFFS: adapter quality is uneven across
the estate by construction, since each system's own boundary translation is written independently,
and a poorly-written adapter can misrepresent its system's true state without the bus itself being
able to detect the misrepresentation (a limit named honestly in Part XVII §17.6). REVERSAL
CONDITIONS: none anticipated as a full reversal, but adapter-quality auditing is named as a genuine
open risk in the Gap Meta-Analysis below rather than resolved here.

D6. DECISION: A double-booked STATE-CHANGE conflict (Part V §5.7) routes to arbitration rather than
being resolved by last-write-wins semantics. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) last-write-wins by
envelope timestamp, the simplest and cheapest resolution; (b) first-write-wins, preserving the
earliest claim as authoritative. WHY THIS: both alternatives would silently resolve what is actually
evidence of an unaddressed Invariant Three violation (two systems both believing they hold write
authority) as if it were an ordinary, expected race condition, masking the underlying governance gap
rather than surfacing it — last-write-wins in particular would let a newly-introduced, ungranted
system silently override a properly-authorized one merely by publishing more recently, exactly the
scenario §12.7's worked example demonstrates the correct handling of. TRADE-OFFS: routing to
arbitration is materially more expensive, in latency and mechanism, than either alternative, and
imposes real cost on what may often turn out to be an honest, low-stakes double-publish rather than
a genuine authority dispute. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: treated as close to load-bearing as any decision in
this dataset — reversing it would silently reintroduce the exact authority-masking risk Invariant
Three exists to prevent at the transport layer, and is not expected to be revised absent a
fundamental rethink of Part V's own Invariant Three enforcement.

GAP META-ANALYSIS
===============================================================================================

GAP CLOSED. Before this dataset, the estate possessed three genuine but scope-bounded transports —
PM-03 (pane-scoped findings), cross_signal_bus (process/session-scoped signals), CEPS (session-scoped
context propagation) — and no mechanism by which a SYSTEM-level fact, produced by one of the seventy-
plus systems the ensemble comprises, could reach another system that never shared a pane, a process,
or a session with it. That gap was structural, not a missing feature of any one parent: each parent
was correctly built for its own bounded scope, and Article II's ensemble-object boundary makes it
true, not merely convenient, that none of the three could have closed the gap by extending itself
without becoming, in the process, exactly this dataset. IAS-A2 closes the gap by supplying: a
five-primitive object model (event, envelope, channel, subscription, authority-request); a four-
obligation bus contract with explicit non-promises; a closed, five-type event taxonomy covering
finding, state-change, alert, authority-request, and evidence; a boundary-only uniform-contract
discipline; a two-tier state-synchronization model spanning registries, memory, runtime, and
evidence state; an explicit, four-step lift operation connecting the three bounded parents to
ensemble scope; a bounded arbitration subengine restoring Invariant Three under contest; an honest-
IPC discipline proven never to require simultaneous liveness; four cataloged failure modes and three
cataloged adversarial cases with named, reused mitigations; four paired anti-Goodhart metrics; and a
four-protocol evaluation specification — twenty Parts constituting the first complete, checkable
answer to "how does a system-level fact reach every other system that needs it."

SYSTEMS STRENGTHENED. Per the Strengthening Matrix's row for IAS-A2, the five named beneficiaries are
PM-03, cross_signal_bus, CEPS, liveness, and owner_queue, on the mechanism that pane-level publish/
consume is lifted to system-level events plus authority. This dataset supplies the checkable evidence
the matrix requires — a cross-system event consumed by a non-originating system — through Part XI's
explicit lift specification, Part XX's three traced sibling integrations (IAS-A1, IAS-D2, IAS-C2),
and Part XIX's Protocol One, which specifies exactly how the claim would be audited against real
consumption records rather than asserted. The strengthening is real but bounded in mechanism, echoing
IAS-F1's own honest self-assessment: IAS-A2 adds no judgment capability to any of its five parents —
PM-03 still decides what counts as a pane-level finding, liveness still decides what counts as stale,
owner_queue still decides how to present an escalated item to the Owner — it only ensures that what
each parent already knows locally can, when judged worth lifting, reach every other system that would
benefit, without any of the five parents having had to build that reach themselves.

DUPLICATIONS AVOIDED. Per the ledger's own disposition, IAS-A2 absorbs rather than merely deletes
Candidates 3, 4, 5, 7, and 10, converting each into a distinct facet (the bus contract itself, the
uniform-contract discipline, the state-synchronization discipline, the arbitration subengine, and the
event taxonomy, respectively) rather than building five competing mechanisms or discarding four of
the five. This dataset's own Part II explicitly declines to redefine any resource-level object the
estate's existing systems already own — a session, a pane, a decision remain exactly whose objects
they already were — restricting IAS-A2's own object to the typed, addressed, cross-SYSTEM exchange
Article II reserves for ensemble-level datasets. Part II §2.6's worked contrast against a synthetic
"shared log file" proposal demonstrates, by explicit failure-mode analysis, precisely what a
duplicate-in-spirit control bus would look like and why this dataset's actual object clears that bar.

NEW CAPABILITIES ENABLED (specification-level, per Article VIII). This dataset enables, as future
implementation candidates rather than as anything built here: a subscription and authority-grant
registry conforming to the addressing and envelope models (Parts III, IV); an append-only event-log
store conforming to the honest-IPC discipline (Part XIV); an arbitration subengine implementation
conforming to Part XII's specified procedure; a rate-based circuit breaker for owner_queue escalation
(Part XVII §17.4); and a four-protocol evaluation harness (Part XIX). None of these are built in this
dataset — Article VIII forbids it — but each is now specifiable precisely because the contracts they
would implement are now defined.

OPEN RISKS. Three risks are carried forward honestly. First, the ADAPTER-QUALITY risk named in
Decision D5: because uniformity is enforced only at the boundary, a poorly-written translation
adapter can misrepresent a system's true state in a way the bus's own transport-layer checks cannot
detect, and this dataset has no mechanism to audit adapter fidelity beyond the general provenance
discipline it inherits from IAS-F1. Second, the UNMEASURED-METRICS risk named in Part XVIII §18.6:
every metric and countermetric this dataset specifies is EXPERIMENTAL, never yet OBSERVED against a
live implementation, and the paired design mitigates gaming only in principle until real measurement
occurs. Third, the EXPERIMENTAL-THRESHOLD risk recurring across several Parts (the event-storm
detection cutoff of §16.2, the arbitration reconciliation window of §12.5, the circuit-breaker rate
threshold of §17.4): this dataset deliberately does not fix numeric values for any of these
thresholds, per Article VIII's implementation-deferral discipline, which means the specification is
complete in SHAPE but incomplete in CALIBRATION, and a future implementation will need real
operational data before these thresholds can move from EXPERIMENTAL to OBSERVED.

ANTI-PATTERNS
===============================================================================================

ANTI-PATTERN 1 — BUILDING A SHARED LOG OR GENERIC MESSAGE QUEUE AND CALLING IT THE CONTROL BUS. The
single most likely implementation error, given how naturally "cross-system communication" suggests
"just add a shared log everyone tails," is exactly the synthetic near-miss Part II §2.6 constructs
and rejects: a plain append-only file or a generic, type-agnostic queue with no envelope validation,
no addressing against IAS-F1 stable IDs, no authority-aware delivery logic, and no arbitration
mechanism. Such a construction moves bytes but is first-order under IAS-F1's own second-order test
(Part VII §7.2) — its direct object is a file or a queue, not the relation between systems'
capabilities — and it fails the DELEGATOR near-miss check (Part II §2.3) specifically because it
cannot be wrong about a delivery decision in any accountable sense; it merely forwards. PREVENTION:
any implementation MUST realize all five primitives of Part III (event, envelope, channel,
subscription, authority-request) and the full bus contract of Part IV, not merely "a place to write
messages"; the litmus test is Part II §2.2's second-order check applied to the CANDIDATE
IMPLEMENTATION itself — if the implementation's direct object, honestly stated, is a resource (a
file, a queue) rather than the relation between systems, it has not built this dataset's object.

ANTI-PATTERN 2 — AUTO-MIRRORING EVERY LOCAL ARTIFACT ONTO THE BUS INSTEAD OF EXERCISING PROMOTION
JUDGMENT. The second most likely error, especially once a system's own boundary adapter (Part VIII
§8.2) exists and works, is treating "we have an adapter now" as license to lift every PM-03 finding,
every cross_signal_bus signal, and every CEPS context delta automatically — the exact anti-pattern
Part XI §11.4 names and rejects by design, and the direct trigger for the event-storm failure mode
(Part XVI §16.2). This is dangerous specifically because it is the path of LEAST resistance once the
mechanical capability to lift exists: automatic mirroring requires no ongoing judgment, while correct
promotion (§11.3 step 4) requires a real "would any other system benefit" assessment on every
candidate artifact, an ongoing cost automatic mirroring appears to avoid while actually imposing a
larger, diffuse cost on every subscriber's reconciliation latency (§15.5) and on owner_queue's own
signal-to-noise ratio (§17.4) if the mirrored traffic includes CRITICAL-severity items. PREVENTION:
promotion judgment (§11.3 step 4) must remain an explicit, non-bypassable step in any lift
implementation, and Part XVIII's Metric Pair One (reuse rate paired with promotion precision) should
be the first pair a future implementation actually measures, precisely because it is the pair most
likely to reveal this anti-pattern's onset before it becomes a full event storm.

ANTI-PATTERN 3 — TRACKING PROJECTION STALENESS WITHOUT EVER GATING ON IT. The third likely error,
identical in shape to IAS-F1's own second cataloged anti-pattern (F1's own ANTI-PATTERN 2, marking
facts OBSERVED by default rather than UNRESOLVED), is populating the projection-staleness field
(§9.4) faithfully — the field exists, is correctly timestamped, is technically present in every
projection — while the consuming system's own decision logic never actually reads or gates on it,
silently treating every projection as fresh regardless of its true age. This is precisely Failure
Mode Two (§16.3), and it is dangerous for the same reason IAS-F1's parallel anti-pattern is dangerous:
it is invisible at the moment it happens, the record looks complete, and the gap surfaces only when a
downstream decision — a routing choice, an allocation, an arbitration input — is later found to have
been made from data that was, in fact, badly out of date. PREVENTION: any consuming system's decision
logic that reads a bus-sourced projection MUST explicitly compare the projection's staleness against
its own declared tolerance before treating the projection as an input worth trusting, and Part XIX's
Protocol One and the staleness-at-decision countermetric of §18.3 should be run specifically against
any system suspected of this anti-pattern, because the staleness field's mere presence in a record
proves nothing about whether it was ever read.

FUSION SCORE
===============================================================================================

SYSTEMS STRENGTHENED: narrow-but-deep, the inverse shape of IAS-F1's own broad-but-shallow profile.
IAS-A2 strengthens exactly five named parents (PM-03, cross_signal_bus, CEPS, liveness, owner_queue)
directly, plus a further three siblings (IAS-A1, IAS-D2, IAS-C2) through the traced integrations of
Part XX — a smaller footprint than F1's estate-wide reach, but each relationship is mechanism-rich:
the lift operation (Part XI), the arbitration subengine (Part XII), and the escalation discipline
(Part VI, Part XVII) are each substantial capabilities in their own right, not a shared vocabulary
alone. This is the correct shape for a Tier-1 dataset built directly on a Tier-0 foundation: F1
supplies width, A2 supplies the first genuine depth layered on top of it.

INTEGRATION QUALITY: high on specification, EXPERIMENTAL in practice, mirroring F1's own honest
self-rating. Part XX's three named, traced sibling integrations (not a vague "connects to
everything" gesture) and Part XIX's four-protocol evaluation specification are strong design-time
signals; but as this dataset states plainly in Part XIX §19.6, none of the four protocols has been
run against a live implementation as of sealing, so integration quality is assessed as strong-by-
design, unconfirmed-by-audit — the identical honest posture F1 takes toward its own thirteen sibling
dependencies.

REUSE: high, and structurally guaranteed for the ordinary-traffic case, though narrower than F1's
near-total reuse guarantee. Every system that adopts bus-conformance (Part VIII) gains the
write-once-subscriber property (§8.3) for free with respect to every OTHER conformant system, a
genuine multiplicative reuse effect; the guarantee is narrower than F1's because bus-conformance is,
correctly, OPTIONAL (Part VIII §8.5) rather than mandatory — a system may satisfy Invariant One
through non-bus relations and never touch IAS-A2 at all, which is by design rather than a gap, but
does mean IAS-A2's reuse ceiling is bounded by voluntary adoption in a way F1's ontology, binding on
every system by construction, is not.

COMPLEXITY COST: moderate, concentrated in the arbitration subengine and the four-kind state-
synchronization model. The ordinary delivery path (publish, subscribe, deliver, acknowledge, Part IV)
is deliberately simple — four obligations, no live coordination, no global clock — but the
arbitration subengine (Part XII), the two-tier state-sync discipline (Parts IX-X), and the three
adversarial-case mitigations (Part XVII) together represent the dataset's genuine complexity budget,
justified by the specific, named failures (Part XVI) and adversarial cases (Part XVII) each piece of
complexity exists to prevent — this dataset's own Part XVII §17.5 states explicitly that every
mitigation reuses an ordinary-operation mechanism rather than adding a parallel one, the concrete
discipline that kept this complexity cost from compounding further.

REDUNDANCY RISK: low, verified against the Non-Duplication Ledger's own disposition of the five
candidates this dataset absorbs. No sealed IAS dataset claims the typed, cross-system event/state/
authority/evidence exchange object; the closest adjacent objects — IAS-A1's mission-time routing
(Part XX §20.2) and IAS-D2's fingerprinting judgment (Part XX §20.3) — are explicitly shown to
CONSUME this dataset's transport rather than duplicate it, each supplying its own judgment on top of
A2's supplied substrate, the concrete evidence against redundancy this Fusion Score requires rather
than a bare assertion of non-overlap.

DATASET-TO-RUNTIME TRANSLATION
===============================================================================================

This section names specification-level candidates only, per Article VIII; no code accompanies this
dataset and none is implied to exist.

REGISTRY (subscription + authority-grant registry, spec-level). A structured, F1-stable-ID-keyed
store recording every durable subscription (Part III §3.5) and every current authority grant (Part
VI §6.4, Part XII §12.3), the direct substrate the ordinary-routing procedure (Part XIII §13.2) and
the arbitration subengine (Part XII §12.3) both read against. This is the most direct translation
candidate, structurally analogous to IAS-F1's own hypothesized system registry but scoped to
transport and authority rather than to system properties generally.

SCHEMA (envelope + five-type payload schema, spec-level). The seven-field outer envelope (Part III
§3.3) plus the five closed payload schemas for FINDING, STATE-CHANGE, ALERT, AUTHORITY-REQUEST, and
EVIDENCE (Parts V-VII), each versioned per the additive-versus-revisory boundary (Part VIII §8.4).
The schema's field set is exhaustively enumerable from this dataset's text.

EVALUATOR (delivery-tier classifier, conflict-trigger detector, spec-level). Two composable evaluator
specifications: one classifying an outbound event into its correct delivery tier (Part XV §15.2) from
its type and severity fields; one checking an inbound AUTHORITY-REQUEST or STATE-CHANGE against the
two named arbitration trigger conditions (Part XII §12.2), stated as mechanical, falsifiable
procedures suitable for direct translation.

GATE (invariant-three transport gate, event-storm circuit breaker, spec-level). Two gate
specifications: one enforcing that no STATE-CHANGE is accepted from a system lacking a current,
unrevoked authority grant for its target field (the transport-layer enforcement of F1's Invariant
Three, Part V §5.4); one implementing the rate-based circuit breaker of Part XVII §17.4 protecting
owner_queue from flooding. Both are direct handoff artifacts for whichever later mission is
authorized to implement enforcement, per Article VIII and this dataset's own Decision D3/D4.

ROUTER (none proposed by IAS-A2 itself). IAS-A2 explicitly declines to specify a mission-time routing
mechanism; IAS-A1 owns that translation candidate, consuming IAS-A2's registry projections (Part XX
§20.2) as one of its own inputs rather than IAS-A2 producing a router of its own — naming this
absence, mirroring F1's own explicit non-provision of a router, is itself part of honest translation.

METRIC (four paired metric/countermetric definitions, spec-level). Part XVIII's four pairs (reuse
rate/promotion precision, delivery latency/staleness-at-decision, arbitration frequency/recurrence,
channel fan-out/attention utilization) are directly specifiable as measurement definitions without
further design work, each carrying the EXPLICIT EXPERIMENTAL caveat Part XVIII §18.6 states for the
set as a whole.

AGENT (subscription-reconciliation agent, spec-level). A bounded, periodic-invocation agent
specification performing the reconciliation pass every bus-conformant system needs (Part III §3.5,
Part IV §4.3): read the durable event log from the system's own last-reconciled point, apply
idempotent processing (§4.5), record acknowledgments (§4.4), and update the system's own
projection-staleness field (§9.4) — a shared, reusable specification any bus-conformant system's
boundary adapter could implement rather than each system inventing its own reconciliation logic
independently.

EXISTING-SYSTEM REINFORCEMENT MAP
===============================================================================================

PP SYSTEMS STRENGTHENED, WITH EVIDENCE. Per the Strengthening Matrix row for IAS-A2, the evidence
artifact required is a cross-system event consumed by a non-originating system. This dataset supplies
that evidence in three forms: Part XI's explicit four-step lift specification connecting each of the
three transport parents (PM-03, cross_signal_bus, CEPS) to ensemble scope; Part XX's three traced
sibling integrations (IAS-A1's registry-projection consumption, IAS-D2's fingerprint-federation
pipeline, IAS-C2's demand-signal extraction), each showing a concrete, non-originating consumer; and
Part XIX's Protocol One, specifying the exact audit method by which the claim would be checked
against real consumption records rather than asserted. All three are necessary together: the lift
specification shows the mechanism was designed deliberately; the traced integrations show it composes
correctly with named siblings; the protocol shows the composite claim can be checked rather than
merely believed.

CAPABILITIES REUSED, NOT REBUILT. IAS-A2 explicitly reuses, by reference rather than rebuilding: the
entire IAS-F1 object model (system, capability, second-order capability, the six relation types, the
five invariants, the property/authority/provenance models — used verbatim throughout Parts II-X, not
re-derived); the Article V epistemic vocabulary (applied to events, projections, and evidence chains
without modification); the estate's existing severity vocabulary (INFO/WARN/CRITICAL, Part VI §6.1,
reused rather than a bus-specific scale invented); the estate's own claimed-until-observed discipline
(SQI-derived via F1, applied to authority resolution in Part VI §6.6); and the Owner's own governing
doctrine on durable-file-over-live-process coordination (Part XIV §14.1, §14.5, generalized from one
host's tooling discipline to the whole federation's transport layer). In every case, IAS-A2 names the
borrowed principle and applies it at the transport-and-authority grain rather than re-implementing it.

MECHANISMS DELIBERATELY NOT REPEATED. IAS-A2 does not build: a routing mechanism (leaves this to
IAS-A1, Part XX §20.2), a fingerprinting or pattern-matching mechanism (leaves this to IAS-D2, Part
XX §20.3), a demand-forecasting mechanism (leaves this to IAS-C2, Part XX §20.4), a cryptographic
identity system (explicitly out of scope, Part XVII §17.6, deferred to the estate's existing secret-
firewall governance), or a knowledge-location mechanism (GK's object, never touched). Each omission
is a deliberate application of the ensemble-object boundary and of Article III's "strengthen, never
replace" law, not an oversight; the Dataset-to-Runtime Translation section above lists the
corresponding specification-level candidates this dataset hands off rather than builds.

NEW CONTRACTS ESTABLISHED. IAS-A2 establishes, for the first time in the estate's history as OBSERVED
by this dataset's authors, four contracts every future cross-system integration is bound by: the
publish-subscribe-deliver-acknowledge bus contract (Part IV), the closed five-type event taxonomy
(Parts V-VII), the boundary-only uniform-contract discipline (Part VIII), and the honest-IPC
discipline (Part XIV, no primitive may require simultaneous liveness). These four contracts are the
operational content of "the ensemble now has a circulatory system" made checkable rather than
aspirational.

SHARED EVALUATIONS ENABLED. Because every conformant system now speaks one envelope contract, a
single four-protocol evaluation harness (Part XIX) can, in principle, be run once and interpreted
consistently across every system that has adopted bus-conformance, rather than each system requiring
its own bespoke cross-system correctness check — a direct efficiency this dataset enables precisely
because uniformity is enforced once, at the shared contract layer, rather than per integration.

D2A DECISION RECORD
===============================================================================================

ORIGINAL D2A VERDICT. Per NON_DUPLICATION_LEDGER.md, IAS-A2 absorbs five distinct candidates under
three distinct dispositions: Candidate 3 (Institutional Control Bus) is BUILD, the seed object this
dataset is named for; Candidates 4 (Cognitive Service Mesh), 5 (Cross-System State Sync), and 10
(Institutional Event Fabric) are MERGE-INTO-FAMILY, each contributing a distinct facet — uniform
contracts (Part VIII), state synchronization (Parts IX-X), and the event taxonomy (Parts V-VII)
respectively — rather than being discarded; Candidate 7 (Multi-System Conflict Resolver) is
SUBENGINE, contributing the bounded arbitration mechanism (Part XII) layered on top of, rather than
identical with, ordinary bus operation. Applied reflexively, IAS-A2's own D2A verdict against its own
closest-adjacent objects (IAS-A1's routing, IAS-D2's immunity judgment, IAS-C2's forecasting) is
BUILD-AND-COMPOSE: none of the three owns the typed, addressed, cross-system event/state/authority/
evidence exchange object, and each is shown in Part XX to CONSUME rather than duplicate it.

IRREDUCIBLE CORE DEMONSTRATED. The irreducible core of IAS-A2, isolated from everything that could in
principle be deferred to a later revision or delegated to a sibling, is the combination of the
five-primitive object model (Part III) with the honest-IPC discipline (Part XIV): these two elements
are what every other Part in this dataset ultimately serves. The event taxonomy (Parts V-VII) gives
the primitives their typed content; the uniform-contract discipline (Part VIII) gives them their
boundary enforcement; the state-synchronization model (Parts IX-X) and the arbitration subengine
(Part XII) are applications of the primitives to specific consistency and authority problems; the
failure modes, adversarial cases, metrics, and evaluation protocol (Parts XVI-XIX) are the
consequences and the checks the core's own honest limits (Part XIV §14.7) require naming. Strip away
every elaboration and what remains is the claim that five typed, addressed, honestly-async primitives
are sufficient for correct cross-system communication without ever requiring simultaneous liveness —
the claim no existing parent (PM-03, cross_signal_bus, CEPS) makes, because none was built to reach
past its own bounded scope in the first place.

SOVEREIGNTY TEST RESULT. Applying IAS-F1's own sovereignty question — "what would break, or go
ungoverned, if this system did not exist?" (F1 Part II §2.2) — to IAS-A2: without this dataset, a
finding discovered by one system would remain permanently invisible to every other system that never
shared its pane, process, or session, regardless of how valuable that finding might be elsewhere in
the estate; an authority conflict between two systems would have no principled resolution mechanism
short of ad hoc, undocumented negotiation; and every arrow in the Compounding Graph that crosses a
system boundary — the primary loop, the defense loop, three of the four named loops in
COMPOUNDING_GRAPH.md — would remain a diagram rather than a mechanism, because nothing in the estate
would carry the traffic those arrows depict. The sovereignty test result is PASS: IAS-A2's object is
not covered, even partially, by PM-03, cross_signal_bus, CEPS, or any other sealed IAS dataset
operating at system scope, and its absence would leave the ensemble's own compounding graph
unrealizable rather than merely less convenient — the strongest form of irreducibility this corpus
recognizes.
