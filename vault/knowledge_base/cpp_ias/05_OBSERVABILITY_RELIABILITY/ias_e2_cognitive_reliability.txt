===============================================================================================
PART I — THE BOUNDARY: RELIABILITY ENGINEERING IS NOT CRASH RECOVERY
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding distinction. Claude Power Pack already owns, as OBSERVED on disk, five systems that
answer "what happens when something breaks": zero-crash (prevents a crash from destroying state),
rollback (reverts a bad change to a known-good point), backup (preserves state against loss), session
resilience (restores a session's continuity across an interruption), and cascade_prevention (stops one
failure from propagating into a second). Every one of these five systems shares a common temporal
posture: each activates AFTER a failure event has occurred, or immediately upon detecting one, and each
is scoped to ONE resource — one session, one repo, one pane, one command. None of the five can answer a
question that has no "after" in it at all: how much failure, this month, was this capability ALLOWED to
have before its behavior counted as a defect rather than a variance? None can answer what a capability
should DO, by design, when it is running at seventy percent of its normal reliability rather than either
one hundred percent or zero. (OBSERVED: the five parents' interfaces are all triggered by a detected
failure event; DERIVED: a system triggered only by failure cannot, by construction, answer a question
about the ACCEPTABLE RATE of failure, because that question must be answerable in the absence of any
single failure event to trigger on.)

1.2 Why "better crash recovery" would be a duplicate, not an advance. The natural error, building a
sixth system in this family, is to make the crash handler smarter — catch more exception types, restore
more granular state, roll back more surgically. Under the D2A test (IAS-F1 Part II §2.2), the honest
answer to "what would go ungoverned if a smarter crash handler did not exist" is: nothing new. The five
parents already own that object; a smarter version of any one of them is an EXTEND verdict against that
parent, never a new system. IAS-E2 exists only because a genuinely different question is currently
ungoverned by any of the five, and that question is economic and predictive rather than reactive: given
that failure is not eliminable at the ensemble scale (seventy-plus systems, non-deterministic model
calls, network-dependent tool use), how much of it is BUDGETED, how is a capability's DEGRADED-BUT-
FUNCTIONING state DESIGNED rather than accidental, and how is a fix PROVEN to have raised reliability
rather than merely felt like it did. (NORMATIVE: this is the second-order test of IAS-F1 Part VII applied
here — IAS-E2's direct object is the reliability behavior of the five parents and of every capability in
the estate, considered as a governed economy, not any one capability's crash.)

1.3 The temporal reframe: before, during, after. The clearest way to state the boundary is temporal.
zero-crash and cascade_prevention act DURING a failure, containing it as it happens. rollback and backup
act AFTER a failure, restoring a prior state. session_resilience acts ACROSS an interruption, bridging
continuity. IAS-E2 acts BEFORE any of the three moments arrive: it sets the tolerance (the SLO, Part
III) before a mission runs; it sets the budget (Part V) before a system is allowed to spend its permitted
failure; it tests the failure deliberately (chaos engineering, Part XI) before a real failure would have
found the same gap; and it designs the degraded state (Part XIII) before the degradation occurs, so that
when a capability does fall short of one hundred percent, its behavior at seventy percent was chosen, not
improvised. (DERIVED: "before" is not merely a different clock position than the five parents — it is a
different EPISTEMIC posture, planning under uncertainty rather than responding to certainty, which is
precisely why the object is ensemble-level: planning under uncertainty requires a model of the whole
federation's failure statistics, not one incident's forensics.)

1.4 What IAS-E2 explicitly does not do, stated as a negative specification. IAS-E2 does not restore state
(backup's object), does not revert a bad commit or config (rollback's object), does not catch an
exception before it corrupts memory (zero-crash's object), does not resume a pane's terminal after a
Cursor reload (session_resilience's object), and does not stop one system's error from cascading into a
sibling's (cascade_prevention's object). Every one of those five remains the exclusive first-order
mechanism for its resource, and IAS-E2 invokes them by name rather than reimplementing any of their
logic — a graceful-degradation path (Part XIII) that needs a state restored calls backup; a chaos
experiment (Part XI) that needs a rollback point calls rollback; a degradation detector (Part IX) that
observes a cascade calls cascade_prevention's own containment rather than building a second containment
mechanism. (NORMATIVE: every mechanism this dataset specifies is defined in terms of WHEN and WHY the
five parents are invoked, never as a substitute invocation path.)

1.5 The reliability engineering discipline as an estate import, not a novel invention. The vocabulary
this dataset uses — service-level objective, error budget, chaos engineering, graceful degradation,
blast radius — is DERIVED from the site-reliability-engineering discipline as it is practiced across
distributed-systems institutions generally: a body of practice built for systems that are large,
imperfectly observable, and cannot be made perfectly reliable at any affordable cost. Claude Power Pack's
seventy-plus systems share exactly those three properties: large (more systems than any one session can
hold in view), imperfectly observable (a model call's failure mode is not fully enumerable in advance),
and imperfectly reliable at any affordable cost (a system that never degrades under any load has spent
more engineering than the mission it serves is worth). (DERIVED: importing a mature discipline is not
naming-only novelty, because the discipline is applied here to a genuinely new object — COGNITIVE
capabilities, whose failure modes include hallucination, drift, and non-reproducibility, not only crash
and network partition, and Part II specifies exactly how the reliability object differs for a
non-deterministic capability.)

1.6 Why the boundary must be stated first, before any mechanism. Every one of the ten candidate
mechanisms this dataset absorbs — SLOs, failure budgets, the mission-reliability predictor, the
degradation detector, chaos engineering, recovery readiness, the redundancy planner, graceful
degradation, the postmortem, and Cognitive Reliability Engineering itself as the umbrella name — is
vulnerable to the same misreading: each one, described loosely, SOUNDS like it could be folded into one
of the five parents ("isn't an SLO just a fancier crash alarm?", "isn't a postmortem just what rollback
already logs?"). Part I exists to foreclose that misreading before it recurs mechanism by mechanism: the
five parents react to failure; IAS-E2 governs the ECONOMY of failure across the whole federation, before,
independent of, and in aggregate over any single failure event. Every subsequent Part inherits this
boundary and states, where relevant, which of the five parents it invokes and for what, rather than which
it replaces.

1.7 The boundary's concrete interface to the two named edges. The preamble's inbound and outbound edges
are not decorative citations; each names a specific handoff this Part's temporal boundary makes necessary.
IAS-D2 (Immune System) feeds IAS-E2 a FAILURE FINGERPRINT — a signature of a failure mode it has already
classified from crash-recovery telemetry across the estate — and IAS-E2 consumes that fingerprint
specifically as an input to Part IX's degradation detector and Part XI's chaos-experiment candidate
ranking (Part XI §11.6): a fingerprint IAS-D2 has already seen recur across several of the five parents is
exactly the shape of failure a BEFORE-the-fact mechanism should prioritize testing deliberately, per §1.3's
temporal reframe, rather than waiting to observe it a further time. The interface is strictly
one-directional at this seam — IAS-D2 classifies AFTER a failure occurs, using the five parents' own
telemetry, and IAS-E2 imports the classification as a prior, never re-deriving it from raw crash logs
itself, which would duplicate IAS-D2's own object. Outbound, IAS-E2 feeds IAS-F3 (Digital Twin) two
specific artifacts, not a general reliability opinion: a Mission-Reliability Predictor forecast (Part VII)
whose low confidence interval is exactly the trigger condition Part VII §7.5 names for preferring a
simulated over a live run, and a graceful degradation contract (Part XIV) whose UNTESTED status (Part XIII
§13.6) makes it a priority candidate for simulated exercise before any real mission depends on it.
(DERIVED: both edges exist because IAS-E2's own object sits at the midpoint of the estate's causal order,
downstream of what has already broken and upstream of what has not yet run, and a dataset at that midpoint
must name both the classifier feeding it and the simulator it feeds, or its BEFORE-boundary claim in §1.3
has no concrete downstream consumer to justify the planning effort.) An implementer that logs a fingerprint
import or a forecast export with no actual consuming mechanism on the other end has built the citation
without the interface — the Scaffold Illusion applied to a cross-dataset seam rather than to a single file.

FINAL LAW — PART I. Crash recovery answers "what do we do now that it broke"; reliability engineering
answers "how much breaking did we plan for, what does partial function look like by design, and how do
we prove the plan is working" — and a federation that cannot ask the second set of questions cannot
budget for, design around, or learn from failure, no matter how well it recovers from any single instance
of it.

===============================================================================================
PART II — THE RELIABILITY OBJECT: WHAT "RELIABLE" MEANS FOR A NON-DETERMINISTIC CAPABILITY
===============================================================================================

2.1 Why reliability needs its own definition here. Classical reliability engineering, as imported in
Part I §1.5, was built for systems whose correct behavior is specifiable in advance: a database either
returns the right row or it does not; a network call either completes within budget or times out. A
Claude Power Pack CAPABILITY (per IAS-F1 Part II §2.4) frequently has no single correct output — a
summarization capability, a routing decision, a knowledge-synthesis step may each have a RANGE of
acceptable outputs, and "reliable" cannot mean "always produces the one correct answer" because for many
capabilities there is no one correct answer to converge on. This Part therefore DEFINES the reliability
object precisely enough for the mechanisms of Parts III through XXII to compute over it without silently
importing the deterministic assumption the classical discipline takes for granted.

2.2 RELIABILITY, defined for a cognitive capability. RELIABILITY, as DEFINED in this corpus, is the
probability that a capability's output, over a bounded set of comparable invocations, falls within a
DECLARED ACCEPTABILITY ENVELOPE — not a single correct value, but a region of outputs the capability's
owner has stated, in advance, as fit for the capability's purpose. The envelope may be narrow (a routing
decision that must select one of a small enumerated set of valid systems has a narrow envelope, close to
the classical binary case) or wide (a synthesis capability whose envelope is "coherent, grounded in the
source material, under N words" admits many acceptable outputs). (DEFINED: reliability is a probability
over an envelope, not a probability over a single value; DERIVED: this generalizes the classical
deterministic case, which is the special case where the envelope has exactly one member.) A capability
whose owner has never declared an envelope has, by definition, an UNRESOLVED reliability — the failure
mode Part XIX §19.4 names ENVELOPE ABSENCE, and it is distinct from and worse than a wide but declared
envelope, because an undeclared envelope cannot be measured against at all.

2.3 The four axes of the reliability object. A capability's reliability record, as DEFINED, carries four
axes, each independently measurable and independently degradable. AVAILABILITY is whether the capability
responds at all within its declared time budget, the axis closest to the classical meaning and the one
zero-crash and cascade_prevention most directly protect. CORRECTNESS-WITHIN-ENVELOPE is whether a
response that DID arrive falls inside the declared acceptability envelope of §2.2 — a capability can be
perfectly available and unreliable on this axis if it answers promptly but wrongly. CONSISTENCY is
whether comparable invocations, given comparable inputs, land in the SAME region of the envelope rather
than swinging unpredictably across it — a capability whose outputs are each individually acceptable but
wildly different from one invocation to the next is unreliable on this axis even though no single output
fails the envelope test, and this axis has no classical deterministic analogue at all, because a
deterministic system by definition cannot be inconsistent given identical inputs. DEGRADATION GRADE
(specified fully in Part X) is the capability's own self-reported or externally-measured position on a
ladder from full function to non-function, the axis that lets "reliable" mean something more granular
than a boolean. (DEFINED, all four; NORMATIVE: a reliability claim that reports only availability, which
is the axis the five parents already measure well, and is silent on the other three, is an incomplete
claim regardless of how good the availability number is.)

2.4 Why consistency is the axis most specific to cognitive capabilities, worked. Consider a synthetic
capability, illustrative only, "extract the three highest-priority action items from a mission
transcript." Run against the same transcript five times, a reliable version of this capability should
return substantially the same three items each time (consistency), each time within a time budget
(availability), and each time as genuine action items rather than summary sentences (correctness-within-
envelope). Suppose an observed run returns a DIFFERENT set of three items on four of five invocations,
each individually plausible as "an action item" and each arriving promptly. Classical reliability
engineering, measuring only availability and a pass/fail correctness check per invocation, would score
this capability as reliable — every invocation was available and every individual output passed a
plausibility check. The consistency axis catches what the other three miss: a capability this volatile
cannot be depended upon by a downstream system that expects a stable answer, and DEPENDS-ON edges (IAS-F1
Part VI §6.3) into a low-consistency capability are, per Part VI of this dataset, a specific risk category
requiring either envelope narrowing or a fallback chain (Part XVII).

2.5 Reliability is a distribution, not a snapshot, and must be read at a bounded window. A single
invocation cannot establish reliability on any of the four axes except availability (which is
observable per-invocation); correctness-within-envelope, consistency, and degradation grade are all
DEFINED over a SET of invocations within a bounded time window, because a cognitive capability's
behavior is a distribution, and a distribution requires a sample. (DERIVED: this is the reason SLOs, Part
III, are always stated over a window — "ninety-five percent within envelope over the trailing thirty
missions" — never over a single mission; a reliability claim scoped to one invocation is a description of
one draw from the distribution, not a reliability measurement.) This has a direct consequence for the
Mission-Reliability Predictor of Part VII: because reliability is a distributional property, predicting
it for a MISSION THAT HAS NOT YET RUN is fundamentally a forecast over a distribution the predictor has
not itself observed for this exact mission, and the predictor's honesty (Part VIII) depends on stating
its confidence interval rather than a point estimate.

2.6 The reliability object's relationship to the SLO and the budget. The reliability object of this Part
is the MEASURED THING; the SLO of Part III is the DECLARED TARGET against that measured thing; the
failure budget of Part V is the PERMITTED SHORTFALL between the two. A system cannot declare an SLO
without first having a reliability object with defined axes to declare a target over, and cannot compute
a failure budget's consumption without a reliability measurement to compare the target against — the
three concepts form a strict dependency chain, object before target before budget, and a dataset or
implementer that skips the object (jumps straight to "we need an SLO of 99%") without first defining
WHICH AXIS and WHAT ENVELOPE that 99% applies to has produced a number with no referent, the specific
failure mode Part XIX §19.2 catalogs as SLO THEATER.

2.7 Edge case: capabilities with no meaningful envelope. Some capabilities — a pure random-sampling
function, a capability whose entire purpose is to introduce controlled variation (an idea-generation
step designed to diverge, not converge) — resist envelope declaration altogether, because low consistency
is, for such a capability, not a defect but the point. F1's UNRESOLVED-field discipline (Part IV §4.6)
applies directly: rather than force a fabricated envelope onto such a capability, this dataset permits an
explicit EXEMPT marking — a capability whose owner has declared, with justification, that the consistency
axis does not apply to its intended function. An EXEMPT marking is not the same as an UNRESOLVED one: the
former is a considered decision with a stated reason, auditable and reviewable; the latter is an
unaddressed gap. Treating an EXEMPT capability's low consistency as a defect, or treating an UNRESOLVED
capability's absent envelope as tacit exemption, are the two symmetric errors this distinction exists to
prevent.

FINAL LAW — PART II. Reliability, for a capability that has no single correct output, is the measured
probability that a bounded set of invocations lands inside a declared envelope, arrives on time, agrees
with itself, and sits at a known point on a degradation ladder — four axes, not one — and a claim of
reliability that reports only whether the capability responded has measured availability and called it
the whole.

===============================================================================================
PART III — COGNITIVE SLOs, DEFINED: SERVICE-LEVEL OBJECTIVES FOR CAPABILITIES
===============================================================================================

3.1 From measured object to declared target. Part II defined what can be measured; this Part defines
what is DECLARED as acceptable, because a measurement with no target attached licenses no decision — a
capability whose correctness-within-envelope runs at eighty-one percent is neither good news nor bad news
until someone has stated what percentage was expected. A COGNITIVE SLO, DEFINED, is a stated, owner-
authored target for one of the four reliability axes of Part II §2.3, over a stated window, at a stated
confidence, for one named capability or system. "The routing capability IAS-A1 provides shall select a
valid system in its envelope on at least ninety-five percent of invocations, measured over the trailing
thirty days" is a complete Cognitive SLO: it names the axis (correctness-within-envelope), the target
(95%), the window (30 days), and the subject (IAS-A1's routing capability).

3.2 Why "cognitive" and not merely "SLO," and the departure from the classical service SLO. Classical SLOs
are typically stated over availability and latency, because classical services are largely deterministic
in their correctness and the interesting variance is in whether and how fast they respond. A Cognitive
SLO must be able to target any of the four axes of Part II, including consistency and degradation grade,
which have no classical analogue, and must be stated with an explicit ENVELOPE DEFINITION rather than
inheriting an implicit "correct answer" the classical discipline assumes. (DERIVED: this is the direct
consequence of Part II's generalization — an SLO discipline imported without modification from classical
practice would be able to state availability and latency targets only, and would be silent on the two
axes most distinctive of cognitive capabilities, reproducing the exact gap Part I exists to close.)

3.3 The SLO record's required fields. A Cognitive SLO record, NORMATIVE, carries: SUBJECT (the capability
or system the SLO targets, addressed per IAS-F1 Part IX's stable-ID convention, never a file path), AXIS
(one of the four from Part II §2.3, or an explicitly composite target naming more than one axis with its
own combination rule), ENVELOPE DEFINITION (the acceptability region for correctness-within-envelope
targets, or N/A for pure availability/latency targets), TARGET (a percentage, threshold, or rate), WINDOW
(the bounded period over which the target is evaluated, per Part II §2.5's distributional requirement),
CONFIDENCE (the statistical confidence at which the target is asserted, honest about sample-size
limitations for low-volume capabilities), OWNER (who authored the SLO and who is accountable for revising
it), and EVIDENCE LEVEL (per Article V — an SLO derived from OBSERVED historical performance is a
different kind of claim than one that is purely NORMATIVE aspiration with no observed baseline). A record
missing any field is INCOMPLETE and, per Part XIX §19.2, is the precursor condition for SLO theater.

3.4 Setting an SLO: from aspiration to evidence. An SLO's initial value, before any observation exists,
is necessarily NORMATIVE — a statement of what SHOULD be true, set by the owner's judgment about the
capability's purpose and the mission's tolerance for its failure. (DERIVED: a brand-new capability cannot
have an OBSERVED SLO on day one, because Part II §2.5 requires a window of invocations to measure a
distribution over, and no window exists yet.) F1's evidence-level discipline (Part IV §4.2) applies
directly: an SLO's evidence level should be recorded honestly as NORMATIVE at inception and is expected to
migrate toward OBSERVED as invocations accumulate and the target is checked against real outcomes; a
downstream consumer (IAS-C1's portfolio math, IAS-D1's ecology judgment) that treats a still-NORMATIVE
SLO as if it were OBSERVED performance is committing the false-confidence error IAS-F2 Part XII §12.5
already names for scores generally, applied here to targets.

3.5 Worked example: setting and evaluating an SLO on a synthetic capability. Consider a synthetic
capability, illustrative only, "generate a one-paragraph risk summary before a deploy." The owner declares
an SLO: axis = correctness-within-envelope, envelope = "names at least one concrete risk and does not
recommend proceeding without qualification when a HIGH-severity finding exists in the input," target =
98%, window = trailing 60 deploys, confidence = the SLO is asserted at 95% statistical confidence given
sample size, owner = the deploy-governance maintainer, evidence level = NORMATIVE at inception. After 60
observed deploys, the capability is measured at 93% — six deploys in which either no concrete risk was
named or a HIGH-severity finding was present without the required qualification. The SLO is now BREACHED,
and the evidence level of the SLO itself migrates to OBSERVED (the target has now been checked against
real outcomes, whether or not it was met), while the capability's own reliability record shows 93% on
this axis for this window. This single worked trace shows the SLO functioning as intended: a precise,
falsifiable, dated claim rather than a vague sense that "the summaries are usually fine."

3.6 SLOs as inputs, not verdicts: what happens on breach. A Cognitive SLO breach is NORMATIVE information,
not an automatic action — per Article IX's governed-self-evolution boundary, IAS-E2 proposes and observes;
it does not autonomously throttle, disable, or replace a breaching capability. A breach consumes failure
budget (Part V), which may trigger a budget-exhaustion protocol (Part VI §6.4) with its own escalation
path, and a breach is exactly the kind of signal the reliability postmortem (Part XVIII) exists to
investigate, but the SLO record itself is a measurement-and-target pair, not an enforcement mechanism. This
separation matters because conflating the two — letting the SLO layer itself halt a capability — would
grant IAS-E2 final authority over a decision class (whether a capability keeps running) that properly
belongs to whichever system already holds authority over that capability, violating IAS-F1's Invariant
Three (Part V §5.4) if IAS-E2 asserted it unilaterally.

3.7 The SLO portfolio: many small targets over one heterogeneous capability set. Because the estate holds
seventy-plus systems each with potentially several capabilities, the realistic unit of work is not one
SLO but an SLO PORTFOLIO — the full set of declared targets across the estate, which is itself an object
IAS-E1 (Observability Fabric) reads for coverage (what fraction of capabilities have a declared SLO at
all, distinct from what fraction meet their SLO) and IAS-C1 (Capability Portfolio) reads alongside cost
and reuse-horizon data when weighing whether a capability's low reliability offsets its high value. A
portfolio with wide SLO coverage but low SLO ambition (targets set trivially low to guarantee compliance)
is a distinct pathology from narrow coverage, and both are named explicitly in Part XIX so that "we have
SLOs" is never mistaken for "we have meaningful SLOs."

3.8 SLO revision and the re-baselining hazard, worked. Part IV §4.6 names UNDER REVISION as a distinct
lifecycle state from BREACHED, and this Part closes the gap between the two by specifying, NORMATIVE, what
a legitimate revision requires versus what it must never be mistaken for. A LEGITIMATE revision is one
triggered by new evidence about the capability's actual purpose or actual input distribution — a
postmortem (Part XVIII) discovering that the original envelope excluded a class of input the capability was,
in fact, always expected to handle competently, or a capability's mission scope genuinely expanding since
the SLO was first set. An ILLEGITIMATE revision is one triggered solely by the SLO having breached
repeatedly with no corresponding evidence that the ORIGINAL target was miscalibrated — lowering the target
from 95% to 88% because 88% is what the capability has actually been delivering is not re-baselining, it is
SLO THEATER's slower cousin (Part XIX §19.2), because the capability's real behavior has not changed; only
the institution's tolerance for reporting it honestly has. The distinguishing test, NORMATIVE: every
revision record must cite the SPECIFIC new evidence that justifies the change, using the same justification
discipline Part III §3.4 requires of an SLO's initial setting, and a revision record that cites only "the
target was unrealistic" without naming what changed about the capability or its mission is presumptively
illegitimate until an owner supplies the missing evidence. Worked example, synthetic and illustrative: a
capability's SLO, set at 97% correctness-within-envelope, breaches for four consecutive windows. A
postmortem (Part XVIII) traces the breaches to a single recurring input shape — mission transcripts
containing embedded tables — that the original envelope definition never explicitly addressed because no
transcript in the capability's initial calibration sample happened to contain one. The revision, properly
justified, narrows the envelope to explicitly exclude table-bearing transcripts (routing them to a
different, table-aware capability instead) rather than lowering the target — the SLO itself remains at 97%,
unchanged, because the capability's true behavior on its now-correctly-scoped envelope was never actually
below target; the envelope, not the target, was the defect. This is the shape a legitimate revision should
take whenever the underlying evidence permits it: fix the envelope's scope before touching the target's
number, because loosening the number first destroys the falsifiability Part III §3.1 exists to protect
before the cheaper, more honest fix has even been considered.

FINAL LAW — PART III. A Cognitive SLO is a dated, falsifiable, owner-accountable target on one of four
named axes over a bounded window — never a vague sense that a capability is "usually fine" — and a target
with no envelope, no window, or no evidence level attached is not an SLO; it is an unfalsifiable opinion
wearing an SLO's name.

===============================================================================================
PART IV — THE SLO TAXONOMY AND THE SLO RECORD
===============================================================================================

4.1 Why a taxonomy, beyond the four axes of Part II. Part II's four axes classify WHAT is measured; this
Part classifies HOW an SLO is shaped once a subject, axis, and window are chosen, because not every SLO
of the same axis behaves the same way operationally, and the mission-reliability predictor (Part VII) and
the degradation detector (Part IX) both need to know which shape of SLO they are reading before they can
interpret a breach correctly.

4.2 THRESHOLD SLOs. A THRESHOLD SLO, DEFINED, states a single target the capability must clear on
aggregate over the window ("95% within envelope"), the shape used in the worked example of Part III §3.5.
Threshold SLOs are simple to state and simple to breach-detect, but they have a known blind spot: a
capability that is exactly at 95.1% every window looks identical, to a threshold SLO, to one that swings
between 100% and 90.2% across sub-windows, even though the second capability's volatility is a distinct
risk the threshold form cannot see. This blind spot is precisely why Part II §2.3 separated consistency
from correctness-within-envelope as its own axis: a threshold SLO on correctness-within-envelope must be
paired with a separate consistency SLO to catch the volatility a single aggregate threshold hides.

4.3 BUDGET-RATE SLOs. A BUDGET-RATE SLO, DEFINED, states the maximum PACE at which failure budget (Part V)
may be consumed, rather than a target on the underlying reliability percentage directly — "no more than
two budget-consuming breaches per rolling seven-day period," independent of the total budget remaining.
This shape exists because a capability can be well within its aggregate failure budget for the month
while burning it in a dangerous cluster (all six permitted failures in the first three days), and a
budget-rate SLO catches the clustering pattern a pure threshold SLO, evaluated only at month's end, would
miss until the budget was already gone. (DERIVED: budget-rate SLOs are the mechanism that lets Part VI's
budget-consumption tracking distinguish a slow, sustainable burn from a fast, alarming one at the SAME
total consumption level.)

4.4 DEGRADATION-GRADE SLOs. A DEGRADATION-GRADE SLO, DEFINED, targets the fourth axis of Part II directly:
"this capability shall spend no more than 5% of invocations at Grade 2 or below," referencing the
degradation ladder of Part X. This shape is the one most distinctive to the cognitive-reliability
discipline, because it treats partial function as a first-class, measurable state rather than folding it
into a binary available/unavailable count, and it is the SLO type the graceful degradation contract
(Part XIV) is checked against directly.

4.5 COMPOSITE SLOs and their combination rule. A COMPOSITE SLO, DEFINED, targets more than one axis
simultaneously with a stated combination rule — commonly a conjunction ("both correctness-within-envelope
at 95% AND consistency at 90%, both required"), occasionally a weighted aggregate for capabilities where
one axis is known to matter more than another. F1's property-honesty invariant (Part V §5.6) applies at
the SLO layer directly: a composite SLO reported as a single passing/failing verdict without disclosing
which underlying axis drove a failure is dishonest by omission, because it hides exactly the diagnostic
information the reliability postmortem (Part XVIII) needs to act on; every composite SLO record, NORMATIVE,
must retain and expose its per-axis sub-results, never only the combined verdict.

4.6 The SLO record's lifecycle: draft, active, breached, revised, retired. An SLO record moves through a
lifecycle DERIVED, in structure, from IAS-F2's score lifecycle (Part XV of that dataset) applied to targets
rather than scores: DRAFT (proposed, not yet evidenced against any window), ACTIVE (evaluated on schedule
against real invocations), BREACHED (the most recent window failed the target — a status, not a
terminal state, since the next window may return to ACTIVE), UNDER REVISION (the owner has opened a
change to the target itself, distinct from a breach of the existing target, and revision requires the
same justification discipline as initial setting per Part III §3.4), and RETIRED (the subject capability
no longer exists or the SLO has been superseded, with the record preserved for historical postmortem
reference rather than deleted). (NORMATIVE: RETIRED SLOs are never deleted, because IAS-D2's cross-project
fingerprinting and IAS-E2's own postmortem discipline both depend on being able to trace a capability's
full reliability history, including targets it no longer carries.)

4.7 Worked example: a composite SLO catching a failure a threshold SLO alone would have missed. Consider a
synthetic capability, illustrative only, "classify an incoming mission by priority tier." A pure threshold
SLO on correctness-within-envelope, set at 96%, is met — 96.4% of classifications land in a defensible
tier over the trailing window. But a paired consistency sub-target, part of a composite SLO, reveals that
of the borderline missions (those near a tier boundary), the SAME mission classified twice produces a
different tier 40% of the time — well within the aggregate correctness envelope (both tiers were
individually defensible) but a consistency failure invisible to the threshold alone. The composite SLO,
by exposing both sub-results, surfaces a genuine reliability problem — a downstream router depending on
this classification cannot depend on it consistently for borderline missions — that the threshold-only
form would have certified as healthy at 96.4%.

4.8 The SLO taxonomy's relationship to the failure budget and the predictor. Each SLO shape feeds Part V's
failure-budget mechanics differently: threshold SLOs generate a simple over/under-budget signal per
window; budget-rate SLOs generate the consumption-pace signal Part VI's dangerous-clustering check reads
directly; degradation-grade SLOs generate the input Part IX's detector needs to distinguish a genuine
degradation event from ordinary variance. The Mission-Reliability Predictor of Part VII reads across all
four shapes for a given capability and produces a single forecast, and Part VIII's feature model specifies
how a heterogeneous mix of SLO shapes is combined into one predictive signal without collapsing their
distinct diagnostic value the way a naive average would.

4.9 Choosing an SLO shape: a decision procedure, worked. Parts 4.2 through 4.5 define the four shapes; this
section closes the taxonomy with the procedure, NORMATIVE, an owner applies when a capability's first SLO
is authored, because leaving the choice to unguided intuition reproduces the same under-specification risk
Anti-pattern One names for the envelope discipline generally, here applied to shape selection instead. STEP
ONE: examine the capability's dominant historical failure mode, per the same reliability record (Part II
§2.3) the redundancy tier-matching rule (Part XVI §16.5) already reads for a related purpose — a capability
whose misses cluster as availability failures (timeouts, transient unavailability) with little variance in
output quality when it DOES respond is well served by a simple THRESHOLD SLO on availability alone, because
the other three axes carry little independent signal for this capability's actual risk profile. STEP TWO:
if the capability has, or is expected to accumulate, a meaningful failure budget whose CONSUMPTION PATTERN
matters as much as its aggregate total — a mission-critical capability where a cluster of three failures in
one day is materially worse than the same three failures spread across a month — a BUDGET-RATE SLO is
layered on top of the threshold, never substituted for it, because the two answer different questions (how
much, versus how fast) and Part IV §4.3 specifies the rate form as a supplement, not a replacement. STEP
THREE: if the capability has an explicit graceful degradation contract (Part XIV) already authored or
planned, a DEGRADATION-GRADE SLO should be added specifically to give that contract's Grade 2/3 behavior a
falsifiable target of its own, because a contract with no corresponding SLO has no way to detect when its
own designed degradation is itself degrading further than intended. STEP FOUR: if more than one axis is
independently load-bearing for the capability's actual mission stakes — correctness AND consistency both
matter, as in Part IV §4.7's classification worked example — a COMPOSITE SLO is required, and a single-axis
SLO is an under-specification masquerading as simplicity. Worked example, synthetic and illustrative:
applying this procedure to a capability that drafts a compliance-check summary, Step One finds its dominant
historical risk is correctness-class (confidently wrong summaries, not absent ones), ruling out a
pure-availability threshold as sufficient. Step Two finds the capability's failure budget history shows no
meaningful clustering pattern to date, so a budget-rate layer is deferred rather than added prematurely.
Step Three finds the capability already carries a graceful degradation contract with a Grade 2 narrowed
envelope, so a degradation-grade SLO is added to keep that contract's own behavior falsifiable. Step Four
finds both correctness-within-envelope and consistency are independently mission-critical for a compliance
check specifically, so the final SLO shape is a COMPOSITE of a threshold correctness target, a
degradation-grade target, and a consistency sub-target — three of the four shapes combined, arrived at by
following the procedure rather than by defaulting to whichever shape the owner happened to author first.

FINAL LAW — PART IV. An SLO is not one shape; a threshold catches the aggregate, a budget-rate catches the
pace, a degradation-grade catches partial function, and a composite catches what no single axis alone
would reveal — and a reliability layer that only ever writes threshold SLOs has built one quarter of the
instrument and is reading three-quarters of a capability's real behavior as silence.

===============================================================================================
PART V — FAILURE BUDGETS: THE ECONOMICS OF PERMITTED FAILURE
===============================================================================================

5.1 The premise a failure budget makes explicit. Every SLO of Part III implies, by simple arithmetic, an
amount of failure that is EXPLICITLY TOLERATED: an SLO of 95% is a statement that 5% of invocations are
permitted to miss the envelope without that fact alone constituting a crisis. Most institutions that
declare targets never take the next step of naming that 5% as a resource to be deliberately spent,
tracked, and reasoned about — the target sits as an aspiration while every individual miss is treated,
inconsistently, as either an ignorable blip or a five-alarm incident depending on who notices it. A
FAILURE BUDGET, DEFINED, converts the tolerated gap between 100% and an SLO's target into an explicit,
countable, spendable resource for a stated window, so that the estate can reason about failure the way it
already reasons about cost: as a finite allocation that funds risk-taking rather than an undifferentiated
bad thing to be minimized at any expense.

5.2 Why a budget is more useful than a floor. A floor ("never let reliability drop below 95%") is a
binary trip-wire with no positive use until it is crossed. A BUDGET, by contrast, is a resource that can
be SPENT deliberately: a system with budget remaining can, NORMATIVE, be the deliberate target of a chaos
experiment (Part XI), a candidate for a riskier but higher-value change, or the subject of an accepted
architectural trade-off, because its owner knows precisely how much room exists before the SLO itself is
breached. (DERIVED: this is the central insight the site-reliability discipline contributes and the one
most alien to a pure crash-prevention posture — zero-crash's implicit target is zero failure, full stop,
which is the correct posture for state corruption but the wrong posture for a cognitive capability whose
every improvement cycle requires SOME tolerance for trying something that might not work.) A budget
reframes 5% permitted failure not as a shameful residual but as the estate's allocated capacity for
controlled risk.

5.3 Computing the budget: from SLO target to spendable units. For a threshold SLO with target T over
window W with N observed or forecast invocations, the failure budget, DERIVED, is (1-T) × N — the count of
invocations permitted to miss the envelope before the SLO itself would show a breach at window's end. A
95% threshold SLO over a forecast 200 invocations carries a budget of 10 permitted misses. Each observed
miss CONSUMES one unit of budget; the budget's remaining balance is 10 minus the count of misses so far in
the current window. For budget-rate SLOs (Part IV §4.3), the budget is expressed directly as a pace cap
rather than derived arithmetically — "no more than two per rolling seven days" IS the budget, not a
derivation from a threshold. Degradation-grade SLOs (Part IV §4.4) derive a budget analogously, in units of
permitted below-full-grade invocations rather than envelope misses.

5.4 The budget ledger: a per-capability, per-window record. Every capability with an active SLO carries a
BUDGET LEDGER, NORMATIVE, recording total budget for the current window, units consumed to date, units
remaining, the consuming events (each tied to a specific invocation, per the falsifiability discipline
Part III §3.3 requires of SLO records generally), and a running BURN RATE — consumption per unit time,
compared against the pace implied by even, sustainable spending across the window. A ledger showing 8 of
10 units consumed with 25% of the window elapsed is burning at nearly four times sustainable pace, a
signal Part VI's freeze protocol reads directly, independent of whether the aggregate SLO has technically
breached yet.

5.5 What a budget licenses, and what it does not. A remaining budget balance licenses, NORMATIVE, three
specific institutional actions and no others: continuing normal operation without escalation despite
recent misses (the ordinary case), authorizing a deliberate chaos experiment against the capability (Part
XI §11.4's budget-gated activation rule), and informing IAS-C1's Capability Portfolio that this capability
currently has "headroom" for a riskier change without immediate SLO consequence. A budget balance does
NOT license ignoring the postmortem discipline of Part XVIII for individual misses merely because budget
remains — every consuming event is still logged and remains available for pattern analysis, because a
budget that is being spent on the SAME recurring root cause, rather than on varied, independent risk, is
itself a signal (Part XVIII §18.5) regardless of whether the aggregate balance still shows headroom.

5.6 Worked example: two capabilities at the same aggregate reliability, different budget stories. Consider
two synthetic capabilities, illustrative only, both measured at exactly 94% correctness-within-envelope
against a 95% SLO — both technically breached by the same one-point margin. Capability Alpha's budget
ledger shows an even burn rate across the window, six distinct root causes each occurring once, no
clustering, and a burn rate matching sustainable pace until the final week when three unrelated edge
cases arrived in quick succession. Capability Beta's ledger shows the identical total miss count but all
misses traced to the SAME root cause (a specific malformed-input shape the capability handles poorly),
consumed in the FIRST three days of the window, with the remainder of the window budget-exhausted and
therefore operating, unmonitored past that point, at true reliability the ledger cannot even measure
because further misses in an exhausted budget are not separately distinguished from the first ten. The two
capabilities are aggregate-identical and budget-story-opposite: Alpha's breach is diffuse variance worth a
mild note; Beta's is a single fixable defect masquerading, in the aggregate number alone, as ordinary
noise. This is the specific diagnostic value a ledger adds over a bare percentage, and it is the direct
input Part XVIII's postmortem discipline consumes to decide where investigative effort belongs.

5.7 The budget's relationship to authority and escalation. A failure budget does not, by itself, hold
final authority over any decision class (per IAS-F1's Invariant Three) — it is an ADVISORY signal that
routes, per Part VI's mechanics, to whichever system holds final authority over the capability in
question. This matters because a poorly designed budget mechanism could tempt an implementer to let
budget exhaustion AUTOMATICALLY disable a capability, which would grant the reliability layer authority it
was never assigned. IAS-E2's role, NORMATIVE throughout this dataset per Article IX, is to surface the
exhaustion, its pattern, and its recommended response with enough evidence that the capability's actual
owner can act — never to act unilaterally in the owner's place. The one narrow exception, specified in
Part VI §6.5, is a pre-authorized, owner-configured automatic freeze the owner opted into in advance,
which is a delegation of the owner's own authority rather than an assertion of new authority by IAS-E2.

5.8 A metric for budget health, and its anti-Goodhart countermetric. The natural metric a dashboard reaches
for first is BUDGET UTILIZATION — the fraction of allocated budget actually consumed by window's end, on
the intuition that a capability consistently near zero utilization is safely reliable and one consistently
near full utilization is at risk. NORMATIVE, this metric is genuinely useful as a coarse first read, but it
is directly gameable in a specific, quiet way: a capability owner who wants to look good on this metric can
simply avoid ever attempting the riskier, higher-value change §5.2 explicitly licenses budget for,
converging on near-zero utilization not because the capability is more reliable but because nothing was
ever risked against it. A federation that rewards low utilization without qualification therefore rewards
STAGNATION indistinguishably from genuine reliability, the same incentive failure the imported discipline
(§1.5) polices in its origin practice, where a team permanently far under its error budget is read as
shipping too conservatively, not as an unqualified success. The COUNTERMETRIC, DERIVED to close this gap,
pairs utilization with CHANGE VELOCITY ATTRIBUTABLE TO BUDGET — the count of deliberate, budget-gated
actions (a chaos experiment per Part XI §11.4, an accepted trade-off per §5.2, a riskier deploy IAS-C1
approved citing this capability's headroom) actually taken against the remaining balance in the same
window. A capability posting near-zero utilization AND near-zero attributable velocity is not healthy by
this pairing; its owner has not exercised the option the budget exists to fund, and Part XVIII's postmortem
discipline reads that combination as its own investigable pattern, distinct from and less urgent than a
hard breach but no longer invisible the way it would be under utilization read alone. (NORMATIVE: neither
metric is published without the other.)

FINAL LAW — PART V. An SLO without a budget is an aspiration with no mechanism for spending the tolerance
it implies; a budget converts "5% permitted failure" from a shameful residual into a resource — tracked,
spendable, and diagnostic — and two capabilities at the identical aggregate reliability can carry
opposite budget stories that only a ledger, never a percentage alone, can tell apart.

===============================================================================================
PART VI — BUDGET MECHANICS: ALLOCATION, CONSUMPTION, REPLENISHMENT, FREEZE
===============================================================================================

6.1 Why mechanics deserve their own Part beyond the ledger's definition. Part V defined what a budget is
and what it licenses; this Part specifies HOW a budget moves through its lifecycle — how it is initially
allocated, how consumption is attributed correctly to the right consuming event, how it replenishes across
window boundaries, and what happens, precisely, at exhaustion — because each of these four mechanics has a
failure mode of its own if left unspecified, and a budget mechanism specified only at the definitional
level (Part V) but not at the operational level (this Part) is not yet implementable.

6.2 Allocation: window-start and mid-window. At WINDOW-START, a capability's budget is allocated per Part
V §5.3's derivation from its SLO's target and forecast invocation volume — a simple, deterministic
computation given the SLO record. The harder case is MID-WINDOW ALLOCATION for a capability whose SLO is
newly declared or revised after the window has already begun: NORMATIVE, the budget is pro-rated to the
remaining window fraction, and the consumption that occurred before the SLO existed is NOT retroactively
charged against the new budget, because charging pre-declaration misses against a budget the capability
did not yet carry would penalize a capability for a target it could not have known to meet — the specific
unfairness Part XIX §19.6 names RETROACTIVE BUDGET CHARGING as an implementation anti-pattern.

6.3 Consumption: attribution and the double-counting hazard. Each consuming event — one invocation
missing its envelope, one degradation-grade drop, one budget-rate cap crossing — is attributed to exactly
one capability's ledger, identified per IAS-F1's stable-ID addressing (Part IX of that dataset) rather
than by file path, so that a capability's ledger survives a refactor of its implementation. The
double-counting hazard arises when a single underlying failure propagates through a DEPENDS-ON chain
(IAS-F1 Part VI §6.3): if capability A depends on capability K, and K's failure causes A's invocation to
also miss its envelope, a naive attribution charges BOTH A's and K's budgets for what is, causally, one
event. The resolution, NORMATIVE, is that the consuming event is attributed to K (the ROOT capability
whose own reliability axis actually failed) as a primary charge, and to A as a SECONDARY, distinctly
tagged charge that Part XVIII's postmortem discipline reads differently from a primary charge — A's
ledger shows the miss, honestly, but A's own reliability engineering is not indicted for a defect that
belongs to K, and a fix to K should be expected to clear both charges simultaneously, a prediction Part
XVIII §18.6 uses as a verification test for exactly this shape of fix.

6.4 The dangerous-burn-rate check and pre-exhaustion escalation. Independent of the aggregate balance,
Part V §5.4's burn-rate figure is checked, NORMATIVE, against the sustainable pace implied by even
spending across the window; a burn rate exceeding roughly twice sustainable pace (a threshold each
capability owner may tune, since a universal number cannot fit both a high-volume and a low-volume
capability equally well) triggers a PRE-EXHAUSTION ESCALATION — an advisory signal, per Part V §5.7,
surfaced to the capability's owner before the budget is actually gone, giving the owner lead time to
investigate rather than discovering exhaustion only at the moment it has already occurred. This is
DERIVED directly from the general principle that a signal fired only at the terminal state (full
exhaustion) wastes the lead time a rate-based check can provide, and it is the specific mechanism that
makes a budget-rate SLO (Part IV §4.3) operationally useful rather than merely descriptive.

6.5 Exhaustion and freeze: the narrow, owner-authorized exception. At full exhaustion — the ledger's
remaining balance reaching zero before the window closes — the DEFAULT behavior, NORMATIVE per Part V
§5.7, is an escalation to the capability's owner with the full ledger as evidence; IAS-E2 does not, by
default, alter the capability's operation. A capability owner MAY, in advance and explicitly, configure a
PRE-AUTHORIZED FREEZE: an instruction that upon exhaustion, the capability should be automatically routed
to a fallback (per Part XVII's redundancy ladder) or a more conservative operating mode (per Part XIII's
graceful degradation) rather than continuing to operate uninstrumented past its own budget. This is framed
explicitly as the OWNER pre-delegating a decision that remains theirs, not as IAS-E2 asserting new
authority — the distinction Part V §5.7 draws between advisory surfacing and unilateral action is preserved
because the freeze behavior was authored and consented to by the party who already holds authority over
the capability's decision class, satisfying IAS-F1's Invariant Three rather than violating it.

6.6 Replenishment at window rollover, and the memory question. At each window's close, a new window opens
with a freshly allocated budget per §6.2 — reliability budgets are, by design, PERIODIC rather than
cumulative, mirroring the classical error-budget discipline's own choice, because a budget that never
replenishes would eventually reach permanent exhaustion for any capability with a nonzero long-run failure
rate, making the mechanism useless past its first bad month. (NORMATIVE.) The unresolved question this
periodicity raises — whether a capability's history of REPEATED exhaustion across successive windows
should itself trigger an ESCALATION BEYOND the routine per-window advisory, since a capability that
exhausts every month is a different institutional situation than one that exhausted once — is addressed
directly in Part XVIII §18.7's repeated-postmortem pattern rather than in the budget mechanics themselves,
because that judgment belongs to the postmortem's cross-window pattern analysis, not to any single
window's ledger.

6.7 Worked example: a full budget lifecycle traced end to end. A synthetic capability, illustrative only,
opens a 30-day window with a 95% threshold SLO over a forecast 300 invocations, yielding a budget of 15
units. By day 6 (20% of the window), 6 units are consumed — a burn rate roughly three times sustainable
pace — triggering a pre-exhaustion escalation to the owner per §6.4. The owner investigates, per Part
XVIII, and identifies a single root cause; a fix ships on day 9. Consumption slows to roughly sustainable
pace for the remainder of the window, closing at 11 of 15 units consumed — the SLO itself was not
breached (11/300 misses is under the 5% budget line), but the ledger's shape, preserved per §6.3's
attribution discipline, shows the escalation-and-fix story clearly for the postmortem record even though
no hard breach ever occurred. On day 31, a fresh 30-day window opens with a newly computed 15-unit budget,
and the prior window's ledger is archived, retained (per Part IV §4.6's RETIRED-record discipline analogue)
rather than discarded, because Part XVIII's cross-window pattern analysis needs the history.

6.8 Edge case: correlated exhaustion across a shared-dependency cluster. Part VI §6.3's double-counting
resolution handles ONE consuming event propagating through a single DEPENDS-ON chain; a harder case arises
when a single root cause — a shared knowledge-lookup capability degrading, the same shape Part IX §9.5
names for trend detection — drives SEVERAL independent capabilities toward exhaustion within the same
narrow interval, each with its own ledger, none individually flagged as unusual by §6.4's per-capability
burn-rate check because each capability's own burn rate, read in isolation, may sit only moderately above
sustainable pace. NORMATIVE: the budget mechanism therefore carries a CLUSTER-LEVEL check, distinct from
and layered atop the per-capability check of §6.4 — when three or more capabilities sharing a common
DEPENDS-ON root cross a pre-exhaustion escalation (§6.4) within the same rolling short window, the
escalation is elevated from an independent per-capability advisory to a single CLUSTER escalation naming
the shared root explicitly, routed to the root's owner first, mirroring the attribution discipline §6.3
already applies to single-event double-counting but applied here at the pattern level rather than the event
level. This is the specific seam at which IAS-E2's budget mechanics interface with cascade_prevention (Part
I §1.4): a cluster escalation is not itself a containment action, but it is exactly the shape of evidence
cascade_prevention's own containment logic is best positioned to consume as an early input, since a
correlated multi-capability budget drain is observable evidence that propagation is already under way even
before any single capability's containment threshold has individually fired. Worked example, synthetic and
illustrative: a shared context-retrieval capability, K, begins degrading; capabilities P, Q, and R, each
depending on K, individually cross pre-exhaustion escalation across a single 48-hour span, each burn rate
moderately elevated but none dramatically so alone. The cluster check fires on the third crossing, naming K
as the shared root, and routes one escalation to K's owner rather than three uncoordinated ones each
investigating in isolation and finding nothing locally wrong.

FINAL LAW — PART VI. A budget's definition (Part V) is inert without mechanics; allocation must not
retroactively punish a capability for a target it did not yet carry, consumption must not double-charge a
dependent for its dependency's fault, burn-rate checks must fire before exhaustion rather than only at it,
and exhaustion's only automatic consequence is one the capability's own owner explicitly pre-authorized —
four rules, and a budget mechanism silent on any one of them will eventually produce an unfair charge, a
missed warning, or an unauthorized action.

===============================================================================================
PART VII — THE MISSION-RELIABILITY PREDICTOR: ARCHITECTURE
===============================================================================================

7.1 From measuring the past to forecasting a mission that has not run. Parts II through VI specify how
reliability is measured, targeted, and budgeted AFTER invocations occur. The MISSION-RELIABILITY
PREDICTOR, DEFINED, is the mechanism that estimates, BEFORE a specific mission runs, the probability that
the capabilities it will invoke will together stay within their respective envelopes for THIS mission —
answering "how likely is this particular mission to succeed reliably," not merely "how reliable has this
capability been on average." This is a genuinely different question from the historical SLO record,
because a mission is a specific composition of capabilities under specific conditions (input size, novel
input shape, chained dependencies), and a capability's average historical reliability can be a poor
predictor of its reliability for an atypical mission.

7.2 The predictor's three subengines. The predictor, NORMATIVE architecture, decomposes into three
subengines (per IAS-F1 Part II §2.3's subsystem discipline — none of the three independently passes the
systemhood test; each is a facet of the predictor's own object). The HISTORICAL-BASELINE SUBENGINE reads
each candidate capability's current SLO record and budget ledger (Parts III-VI) to establish a
per-capability baseline probability. The COMPOSITION SUBENGINE reads the mission's actual invocation
chain — which capabilities feed which, per IAS-F1's typed relation graph (Part VI of that dataset,
specifically FEEDS and DEPENDS-ON edges) — and combines the per-capability baselines according to the
chain's structure, since a mission with capabilities in a DEPENDS-ON chain has different composite
reliability arithmetic than one where capabilities operate independently in parallel. The NOVELTY
SUBENGINE adjusts the composite estimate downward when the mission's actual input diverges from the
historical distribution the baseline was measured against — a mission with an unusually large or unusually
shaped input is, DERIVED, less well-covered by historical baseline data regardless of how good that
baseline looks in aggregate.

7.3 The composition subengine's arithmetic, specified. For capabilities in a DEPENDS-ON chain, mission
reliability is DERIVED as approximately the product of the individual capability reliabilities along the
critical path — because a depends-on relation (Part I §1.4's reminder of IAS-F1 Part VI §6.3) propagates
failure, a chain of five capabilities each individually at 98% reliability compounds to roughly 90% for
the chain as a whole, a materially worse number than any single link suggests, and a predictor that reports
only the WEAKEST link's reliability (a common naive shortcut) systematically overstates the mission's true
composite reliability. For capabilities related only by FEEDS (not depends-on), the composition is
gentler: a feeds-edge failure degrades rather than breaks the downstream capability (per Part I §1.4's own
distinction), so the composition subengine's arithmetic there is DERIVED from the graceful degradation
contract's stated behavior (Part XIV) rather than from a simple product, because the downstream
capability's actual response to an upstream feeds-failure is precisely what that contract specifies.

7.4 The novelty subengine and its honest uncertainty. When a mission's input shape has no close precedent
in the historical baseline's coverage, the predictor's honest response, NORMATIVE, is to WIDEN its
confidence interval rather than to silently keep the point estimate and drop the uncertainty — a predicted
"85% reliable, wide interval, low confidence" is a materially different and more useful statement than "85%
reliable" bare, because a downstream consumer (a chaos-experiment gate, Part XI §11.4, or an owner deciding
whether to proceed with a high-stakes mission) needs to know whether 85% is a well-evidenced number or a
guess dressed as one. (DERIVED directly from IAS-F2's evidence-level discipline for scores generally,
applied here to a forecast rather than a retrospective measurement — a forecast under high novelty is
closer to HYPOTHESIZED than to DERIVED, and the predictor's output record must carry that marking.)

7.5 What the predictor is for: three consuming decisions. A mission-reliability forecast, once produced, is
NORMATIVE input to exactly three decisions, and the predictor itself makes none of them. IAS-A1's routing
decision (per the Compounding Graph's primary loop) may prefer a lower-cost but lower-forecast-reliability
path for a low-stakes mission and a higher-cost, higher-forecast-reliability path for a high-stakes one — a
trade-off IAS-A1 makes, using the predictor's number as one input among its own routing criteria, never a
trade-off the predictor makes on IAS-A1's behalf. An Owner or automated pre-flight check may decline to
proceed with a mission whose forecast falls below a stated risk tolerance, surfacing the forecast and its
confidence interval as the basis for that human or governed decision. And IAS-F3's Digital Twin (per the
defense loop, Part XXII) may use a low forecast as the specific trigger to simulate the mission in advance
rather than running it live first, closing the loop this dataset's outbound edge to IAS-F3 describes.

7.6 Worked example: forecasting a three-capability mission. A synthetic mission, illustrative only, chains
capability P (depends-on) into capability Q (depends-on) into capability R (feeds only, optional
enrichment). Historical baselines: P at 99% correctness-within-envelope, Q at 96%, R at 90%. The
composition subengine computes the depends-on chain P→Q at approximately 95% (0.99 × 0.96), and folds in
R's feeds relation per the graceful-degradation-informed arithmetic of §7.3 — because R's failure degrades
rather than breaks the mission, R's 90% contributes a smaller reliability penalty than a strict product
would imply, landing the composite estimate around 93%. The novelty subengine then checks the mission's
actual input against each capability's historical coverage; P and Q's inputs are well within precedent
(narrow confidence interval, high confidence), but R is being invoked with an input shape it has handled
only twice before in the historical record, widening R's contribution's uncertainty and, by extension, the
composite forecast's interval, though not materially shifting its point estimate. The final predictor
output — "approximately 93% composite reliability, moderate confidence driven by limited precedent for R's
input shape" — is a materially richer signal than any single capability's SLO record alone, and it is
exactly this composite, chain-aware, novelty-adjusted number that IAS-A1 and any pre-flight gate consume.

7.7 The predictor's own reliability, and why it must be measured too. The predictor is itself a capability
per Part II's object model, and its forecasts, per this dataset's own discipline applied reflexively, carry
a reliability record: how often has a forecast of "X% likely reliable" actually landed within a reasonable
band of the mission's observed outcome. A predictor whose forecasts are systematically miscalibrated —
consistently too optimistic, or too pessimistic — is itself a reliability defect this dataset must be able
to detect in itself, and Part XXI's evaluation protocol specifies exactly this self-check as one of its
required benchmarks, closing the reflexivity gap explicitly rather than exempting the predictor from the
discipline it applies to everything else.

7.8 Edge case: a diamond-shaped chain, and why linear-chain arithmetic understates its risk. Part VII §7.3
specified the composition subengine's arithmetic for a simple linear DEPENDS-ON chain and a single FEEDS
hop; a mission whose chain instead DIAMONDS — two capabilities, S and T, both depending independently on a
shared upstream capability U, then both feeding into a single downstream capability V — cannot be composed
by the same product-along-the-critical-path rule without a correction, because S's and T's failures are not
independent draws once both trace back to the same U: if U is degrading, S and T are more likely to fail
TOGETHER than the product rule assumes, and a naive composition that treats them as independent will
systematically OVERSTATE the diamond's true composite reliability. NORMATIVE: the composition subengine
detects a diamond shape directly from IAS-F1's relation graph (a shared ancestor reachable from two or more
paths converging on the same downstream capability) and applies a CORRELATION-ADJUSTED composition —
discounting the naive independent product by a factor derived from U's own current grade (Part X) and
recent trend (Part IX), since a U sitting at Grade 0 justifies near-independence between S and T's
remaining risk, while a U at Grade 1 justifies a materially larger discount. Worked example, synthetic and
illustrative: S and T each independently forecast at 96%, both depending on U, currently flagged DEGRADING.
A naive independent-product composition would compute roughly 92% (0.96 × 0.96); the correlation-adjusted
composition, discounting for U's shared degrading state, instead reports approximately 88% with a widened
interval — a U-caused failure is likely to take out S and T together, not as two separate coin flips. This
correction is precisely the kind of forecast IAS-F3's Digital Twin is best positioned to validate
empirically (per §7.5's third consuming decision): simulating the diamond under injected U-degradation and
comparing the joint-failure rate against the correlation-adjusted forecast is a calibration check neither
system can perform alone.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. A historical SLO tells you how a capability has behaved on average; a
mission-reliability forecast tells you how THIS mission, with its specific chain and its specific input, is
likely to behave — and a predictor that reports a bare point estimate without a novelty-adjusted confidence
interval has answered a harder question with an easier one's precision.

===============================================================================================
PART VIII — THE PREDICTOR'S FEATURE MODEL AND WORKED PREDICTION
===============================================================================================

8.1 Why the feature model needs its own Part beyond the architecture. Part VII specified the predictor's
three subengines and their arithmetic; this Part specifies WHAT DATA feeds those subengines — the concrete
feature set the historical-baseline, composition, and novelty subengines each read — because an
architecture with an unspecified feature set is not yet a measurable mechanism, and two implementations of
"the same" predictor built against different feature sets would produce silently incomparable forecasts.

8.2 The feature model's four categories. CAPABILITY FEATURES, DEFINED, are the per-capability inputs the
historical-baseline subengine reads directly from Parts III-VI's records: current SLO target and axis,
trailing-window observed reliability per axis, budget ledger balance and burn rate, and evidence level of
the underlying SLO. GRAPH FEATURES, DEFINED, are the composition subengine's inputs, drawn from IAS-F1's
typed relation graph (Part VI of that dataset): the specific chain of feeds and depends-on edges the
mission will traverse, the depth of that chain, and any competes-with edges among the mission's candidate
capabilities that might indicate resource contention affecting availability rather than correctness. INPUT
FEATURES, DEFINED, are the novelty subengine's inputs: the mission's actual input size, shape, and
similarity (by whatever grounded comparison the estate's knowledge systems already support) to the
historical distribution each capability's baseline was measured against. CONTEXT FEATURES, DEFINED, are
ensemble-state signals external to any single capability — current estate-wide load, any active chaos
experiment (Part XI) that might be perturbing a capability the mission depends on, and any open reliability
postmortem (Part XVIII) currently investigating a capability in the chain, since an under-investigation
capability carries elevated uncertainty regardless of what its ledger currently shows.

8.3 Feature weighting is capability-specific, not universal. A single fixed weighting across all four
feature categories, applied identically to every capability, would be a false-precision error of the exact
shape IAS-F2 Part XVI catalogs for scores generally: a capability with a long, stable history should weight
capability features heavily and novelty features lightly (little in a typical mission is actually novel to
it), while a newly deployed capability with almost no history must weight novelty features heavily by
necessity, because capability features alone are too thin to trust. (NORMATIVE: the predictor's weighting
scheme is itself parameterized per capability, informed by the capability's own reuse-horizon and evidence
level per IAS-F1's property model, Part IV of that dataset, rather than fixed globally.)

8.4 The cold-start problem and its honest resolution. A capability with zero or near-zero invocation
history presents the predictor's hardest case: capability features are nearly empty, and the novelty
subengine cannot calibrate "how novel is this input" against a baseline that barely exists. The honest
resolution, NORMATIVE, is not to fabricate a plausible-looking number by borrowing another capability's
baseline uncritically, but to report the forecast at UNRESOLVED or, if a coarse category-level baseline
genuinely exists (this capability resembles a well-established CLASS of capability closely enough to
borrow that class's typical reliability as an explicitly-labeled proxy), to report the borrowed estimate
tagged HYPOTHESIZED with the borrowing made explicit in the record. (DERIVED directly from IAS-F1's
UNMEASURED-PROPERTY discipline, Part IV §4.6 of that dataset, applied to a forecast rather than a static
property.) A predictor that silently interpolates a confident-looking number for a cold-start capability
manufactures exactly the false confidence Part II §2.5 warns against for distributional claims generally.

8.5 Worked prediction with full feature disclosure. Returning to the synthetic P→Q→R mission of Part VII
§7.6, the feature model underlying that forecast is now made explicit. Capability features: P (SLO 99%,
evidence OBSERVED, budget 12/15 remaining, burn rate sustainable), Q (SLO 96%, evidence OBSERVED, budget
9/15 remaining, burn rate slightly elevated), R (SLO 90%, evidence NORMATIVE — R's SLO was set recently and
has only two prior windows of observation). Graph features: P depends-on Q, Q feeds R (correcting Part VII
§7.6's informal description to the precise relation types), chain depth two plus one feeds hop, no
competes-with edges detected among the three for this mission's resource envelope. Input features: P and
Q's inputs score high similarity to historical distribution (narrow interval); R's input scores low
similarity (only two precedents), driving the width of R's contribution to the composite interval. Context
features: no active chaos experiment touches P, Q, or R currently; no open postmortem exists for any of the
three. This full disclosure is what lets a downstream consumer audit WHY the forecast landed where it did,
rather than trusting a bare number — the specific transparency Part XII's provenance discipline (imported
from IAS-F1 Part XII by reference) requires of any ensemble-level claim.

8.6 Feature drift and the recalibration trigger. A feature model's weighting, once tuned, does not stay
correct indefinitely: as a capability accumulates more history, its appropriate weighting shifts (per §8.3)
from novelty-heavy toward capability-feature-heavy, and a predictor that never recalibrates its weighting
scheme will under-trust an increasingly well-evidenced capability's own track record. NORMATIVE: the
predictor's weighting is reviewed at a cadence tied to capability lifecycle events — window rollovers (Part
VI §6.6), SLO revisions (Part IV §4.6), and postmortem closures (Part XVIII) — rather than on a fixed
calendar unrelated to the actual accrual of new evidence, so that recalibration tracks the pace at which
genuine uncertainty is actually resolving.

8.7 The feature model's failure surface: garbage-in and the parent-system dependency. The predictor's
entire feature model is DERIVED from records the five parent systems and Parts III-VI's own mechanics
produce; it computes nothing from raw observation itself. This is a deliberate non-duplication choice — the
predictor is a second-order consumer of first-order measurement, never a second measurement apparatus — but
it means the predictor's forecast quality is strictly bounded above by the quality of its inputs: a
capability with a stale, unreconciled SLO record (per IAS-F1's staleness discipline, Part IV §4.3 of that
dataset) will produce a stale, unreliable forecast regardless of how sound the predictor's own arithmetic
is, and Part XIX §19.5 names this specific failure surface explicitly so it is never mistaken for a defect
in the predictor's own logic.

8.8 Feature schema versioning and the backward-compatibility rule. As the estate's capability count grows
and new failure classes are discovered, NORMATIVE, the four feature categories of §8.2 are themselves
expected to grow — a new context feature (an active incident on a shared infrastructure component, for
instance) may be added years after the predictor's initial design once experience shows it materially
improves calibration. This raises a specific hazard the feature model must resolve explicitly: a forecast
produced under an OLDER feature schema is not directly comparable to one produced under a NEWER schema with
additional inputs, because the older forecast's stated confidence interval did not, and could not, account
for a feature category that did not yet exist at the time it was computed. The resolution, NORMATIVE, is
that every forecast record carries an explicit FEATURE-SCHEMA VERSION tag alongside its point estimate and
interval, and Part XXI's calibration benchmark (§21.3) segments its retrodictive analysis by schema version
rather than pooling forecasts from different schema generations into one undifferentiated calibration
curve — pooling them would silently attribute a miscalibration that is actually a schema-vintage artifact to
the predictor's underlying arithmetic instead, the same category of false attribution Part VI §6.3's
double-counting hazard warns against for budget consumption, applied here to forecast provenance. A schema
upgrade is, itself, NORMATIVE, a re-calibration trigger per §8.6's cadence, since a new feature category
changes what "well-weighted" means for every capability the predictor covers, not merely the ones the new
feature was specifically added to observe. Worked example, synthetic and illustrative: the predictor's
original feature schema (v1) has no CONTEXT FEATURE for "shared infrastructure incident in progress." A
mission forecast under v1, for a capability depending on a knowledge-lookup system that happens to be
degraded at forecast time but has no active chaos experiment or open postmortem (the only two context
signals v1 tracks), reports a confident 92% forecast that does not reflect the shared degradation at all,
because v1's feature model has no slot to carry that information. Following an evaluation-benchmark finding
that v1 forecasts systematically over-predicted reliability during periods of shared-infrastructure stress,
a v2 schema adds exactly this context feature. Under v2, the identical mission scenario now forecasts
correctly at a wider interval and lower point estimate, reflecting the shared incident. Every v1 forecast in
the historical archive remains tagged v1 rather than being silently reinterpreted as if v2's feature had
been available, preserving the honest record of what the predictor actually knew at each point in its own
evolution — the same RETIRED-record-preservation discipline Part IV §4.6 applies to superseded SLOs, applied
here to superseded feature schemas.

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. A forecast is only as honest as the features beneath it; capability, graph, input,
and context features must be weighted per capability rather than uniformly, a cold-start capability must be
marked UNRESOLVED or explicitly HYPOTHESIZED rather than confidently guessed, and a predictor built on
stale parent records will compute a precise number from an imprecise world without ever knowing it has done
so.

===============================================================================================
PART IX — THE CAPABILITY DEGRADATION DETECTOR
===============================================================================================

9.1 The gap between a breach and a slide. An SLO breach (Part III §3.6) is a WINDOW-LEVEL event, visible
only once enough invocations have accumulated to compute the window's aggregate. Between breaches, a
capability can be sliding — trending steadily worse invocation over invocation — for a considerable stretch
before that slide accumulates into an aggregate number low enough to register as a breach, and by the time
the breach fires, the slide has often already run for long enough that its root cause has drifted, new
deploys have layered on top of it, and the postmortem (Part XVIII) faces a harder reconstruction than it
would have faced at the slide's earlier, cleaner stage. The CAPABILITY DEGRADATION DETECTOR, DEFINED, is
the mechanism that watches for the SLIDE ITSELF — a directional trend across recent invocations — rather
than waiting for the aggregate window to cross its threshold, closing the detection-lag gap the SLO
mechanism alone cannot close by design.

9.2 Distinguishing degradation from failure from noise: the three-way test. The detector's central
technical problem is that a cognitive capability's per-invocation reliability is naturally noisy (Part II
§2.5's distributional framing), and a naive detector that alarms on any string of misses will alarm
constantly on ordinary variance. The detector therefore applies a three-way test to any observed run of
recent misses. NOISE is a pattern indistinguishable, at the observed sample size, from random draws at the
capability's own established baseline rate — the null hypothesis a proper statistical test must fail to
reject before anything is flagged. FAILURE is a discrete, bounded event — one or a small cluster of misses
tied to an identifiable, non-recurring cause (a single bad deploy, a transient upstream outage) — the shape
zero-crash and cascade_prevention are already built to contain and that this detector explicitly defers to
those parents rather than re-detecting. DEGRADATION, DEFINED as this detector's own object, is a
STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT DIRECTIONAL TREND across a rolling sub-window — reliability measurably and
monotonically (or near-monotonically) worsening invocation over invocation, distinct from noise by its
significance and distinct from a discrete failure by its gradualness and its lack of a single bounded
trigger event.

9.3 The trend-detection mechanism, specified. NORMATIVE: the detector maintains a rolling sub-window
(materially shorter than the full SLO window of Part III, so that a slide is caught well before the full
window's aggregate would reflect it) and applies a trend test comparing the sub-window's trajectory against
the capability's own established baseline variance — not an absolute reliability floor, because a
capability whose baseline is naturally noisier requires a correspondingly higher bar of significance before
a trend is declared real, the same discipline Part VII §7.4 applies to the predictor's confidence
intervals. A trend crossing the significance bar is flagged DEGRADING; a trend that reverses before crossing
is logged but not escalated, preserving a record (per Part XVIII's postmortem discipline) of near-misses
that never became full degradation events, which are themselves useful signal for tuning the significance
bar over time.

9.4 What the detector does upon flagging: routing, not remediation. Consistent with this dataset's
Article-IX-governed posture throughout, a DEGRADING flag is NORMATIVE input routed to three places and
triggers no autonomous remediation. It updates the capability's degradation grade (Part X) if the trend has
crossed a grade boundary. It feeds the Mission-Reliability Predictor's context features (Part VIII §8.2),
so that any mission currently forecasting against this capability inherits the elevated uncertainty a
known-degrading capability represents, even before its aggregate SLO shows any breach. And it surfaces to
the capability's owner as an early warning — materially earlier than the SLO breach the same underlying
slide would eventually produce — giving the owner the same lead-time advantage Part VI §6.4's burn-rate
check gives the budget mechanism, applied here to the trend itself rather than to budget consumption.

9.5 Distinguishing capability degradation from ensemble-level degradation. A single capability's own trend
is the detector's primary object, but the detector must also distinguish a LOCAL degradation (this
capability alone is sliding, likely a defect specific to it) from an ENSEMBLE-LEVEL degradation shared
across many capabilities simultaneously (a shared dependency, such as a common knowledge-lookup system, is
itself degrading and dragging every capability that depends on it down together, per IAS-F1's DEPENDS-ON
propagation, Part VI §6.3 of that dataset). DERIVED: when multiple capabilities in the same DEPENDS-ON
subgraph flag DEGRADING within the same rolling window, the detector escalates the ROOT of that subgraph
as the primary flag and the downstream capabilities as secondary, propagated flags — the identical
attribution discipline Part VI §6.3 already specifies for budget-consumption double-counting, applied here
to trend detection, so the same worked mechanism serves both the economic layer and the detection layer
without duplicating the attribution logic.

9.6 Worked example: catching a slide two weeks before the SLO would have caught it. A synthetic capability,
illustrative only, holds a 95% threshold SLO over a 30-day window. Its true underlying reliability begins
drifting downward from day 10 onward — a slow decay from 97% to 89% by day 24 — but because the SLO's
window aggregates all 30 days together, the aggregate figure at day 24 (blending 10 good days against 14
progressively worse ones) sits at approximately 93%, still visibly close to breach but not yet crossed, and
would not clearly cross the 95% line until several more bad days accumulate. The degradation detector,
watching a rolling 5-day sub-window against the capability's pre-drift baseline variance, flags DEGRADING
on approximately day 16 — roughly a week into the true drift and more than a week before the aggregate SLO
would show unambiguous breach — because the sub-window trend crosses its significance bar well before the
full-window aggregate is dragged low enough to register. This six-to-eight-day lead time is the detector's
entire institutional value: the same underlying defect is found and can begin investigation (Part XVIII)
before it has consumed more than a fraction of the window's failure budget, rather than after.

9.7 The detector's own false-positive and false-negative costs, stated honestly. A trend-detection
mechanism trades off two error costs that a pure threshold-SLO check does not face: a FALSE POSITIVE
(flagging DEGRADING on what is, in fact, ordinary noise) generates an unnecessary early-warning escalation,
consuming an owner's attention on a non-issue — a real cost, though smaller than the cost of a missed real
slide, and one Part XIX §19.7 names explicitly as DEGRADATION ALARM FATIGUE when it recurs often enough to
erode an owner's trust in the flag. A FALSE NEGATIVE (a real slide that never crosses the significance bar
because it is gradual enough, or the rolling sub-window short enough, to hide within normal variance)
reproduces the exact detection-lag problem this Part exists to close, just less completely than the SLO
alone would. NORMATIVE: the significance bar and sub-window length are therefore explicit, owner-tunable
parameters per capability rather than fixed constants, and Part XXI's evaluation protocol requires both
error rates to be measured and reported, never only the detector's true-positive catches, so that its own
tuning can be assessed honestly rather than celebrated only for its wins.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. An SLO breach is the smoke alarm that fires once the room is already full of smoke; a
degradation detector watches the trend line before the room fills, distinguishing a real, significant slide
from ordinary noise and from a discrete bounded failure the crash-prevention parents already own — and the
whole of its institutional value is measured in the days of lead time it buys an owner over the aggregate
window alone.

===============================================================================================
PART X — THE DEGRADATION LADDER AND GRADE TRANSITIONS
===============================================================================================

10.1 Why a ladder, not a binary. Part IX's detector flags a trend; this Part specifies WHAT STATE a
capability occupies once degradation is real, because "available" versus "unavailable" is too coarse a
partition for a capability that can be meaningfully useful at reduced function — exactly the state Part I
§1.1 observed the five crash-recovery parents have no vocabulary for. The DEGRADATION LADDER, DEFINED, is a
small, closed, ordered set of grades a capability occupies at any moment, each with a stated meaning
independent of any one capability's specifics, so that "Grade 2" means the same thing across the whole
estate the way IAS-F1's property model (Part IV of that dataset) fixed one shared meaning for "cost" across
every system.

10.2 The five grades, defined. GRADE 0 — FULL FUNCTION: the capability operates within its declared
envelope at its historical baseline rate; no active degradation signal. GRADE 1 — WATCHED: the degradation
detector (Part IX) has flagged a trend crossing its significance bar, but the capability's output remains,
so far, within its envelope; this grade exists specifically to make the detector's early-warning state
visible as a first-class capability state rather than a hidden internal signal only the owner sees. GRADE 2
— REDUCED ENVELOPE: the capability is operating with a NARROWED acceptability envelope — accepting a
smaller class of inputs, offering a coarser output, or explicitly declining classes of request it would
normally serve — a state entered either automatically per a pre-declared graceful degradation contract
(Part XIV) or manually by the owner in response to a Grade 1 flag. GRADE 3 — FALLBACK-SERVED: the
capability's normal implementation is bypassed and a designated fallback (Part XVII's redundancy ladder)
is serving requests in its place, at a stated, typically lower, standard of output. GRADE 4 — UNAVAILABLE:
no capability, primary or fallback, is serving the request at all — the terminal state, and the only one of
the five that corresponds to the crash-recovery parents' existing binary vocabulary.

10.3 Grade transitions are governed events, not silent state changes. NORMATIVE: every transition between
grades is a LOGGED, TIMESTAMPED, ATTRIBUTED event — what triggered it (a detector flag, a manual owner
action, an SLO breach, a pre-authorized freeze per Part VI §6.5), who or what authorized it, and what grade
preceded it — because a capability's grade history is exactly the record Part XVIII's postmortem discipline
needs to reconstruct how long a degraded state persisted and whether the transition rules themselves
behaved as designed. A capability whose grade changes are not logged has, in effect, no ladder at all — only
an informal, unauditable sense that "it's been acting up," which is the precise condition this Part exists
to replace with a governed, comparable record.

10.4 Ascending and descending the ladder: asymmetric caution. Transitions DOWN the ladder (toward more
degraded states) are, NORMATIVE, permitted on a lower evidentiary bar than transitions UP (toward recovery)
— a single strong signal (a hard SLO breach, a detector flag crossing significance) is sufficient to
descend one grade as a precaution, but ASCENDING back to a lower grade number requires a SUSTAINED period
of clean, in-envelope operation at the new, better grade before the capability is trusted back at the
former one. (DERIVED: this asymmetry mirrors the general engineering principle that the cost of a false
descent — briefly over-cautious, serving at reduced capacity when full capacity was actually fine — is
materially lower than the cost of a false ascent — declaring recovery prematurely and re-exposing missions
to a defect that has not actually cleared.) The specific sustained-period requirement is owner-tunable per
capability, analogous to the detector's significance bar (Part IX §9.7), because a high-volume capability
can accumulate sufficient clean evidence for a confident ascent far faster than a low-volume one.

10.5 The ladder's relationship to the SLO and the budget, made precise. A capability sitting at Grade 1 or
Grade 2 is NOT automatically in SLO breach — Grade 1 explicitly permits in-envelope operation, and Grade 2's
narrowed envelope may itself be the capability's OWN new envelope for the duration (per the graceful
degradation contract, Part XIV, which may declare a distinct, honestly lower SLO target that applies only
while at Grade 2). This separation matters because conflating grade with breach would double-count the same
underlying signal in two different records and would penalize a capability for correctly, deliberately
entering a designed reduced-capacity state exactly as intended — the opposite of the outcome graceful
degradation (Part XIII) is meant to reward. A Grade 3 fallback-served capability, by contrast, is measured
against the FALLBACK's own SLO record (which may be a distinct capability entirely, per Part XVII), not the
primary's, because the primary is, by definition, not the one currently operating.

10.6 Worked example: a full ladder traverse and recovery. A synthetic capability, illustrative only, sits at
Grade 0 through day 15. On day 16, the degradation detector flags per Part IX §9.6's worked trace, entering
Grade 1 — WATCHED — with the transition logged, attributed to the detector, and routed per Part IX §9.4. By
day 19, the trend has worsened enough that the capability's declared graceful degradation contract (Part
XIV) auto-triggers entry to Grade 2, narrowing its accepted input class to exclude the specific malformed
shape identified as the likely root cause, a transition attributed to the pre-declared contract rather than
a manual owner action. The narrowed-envelope Grade 2 operation holds cleanly — no further misses — through
day 26, when a fix ships addressing the root cause directly. Per §10.4's asymmetric-caution rule, the
capability does not immediately return to Grade 0; it re-widens to its full envelope but remains logged at
an OBSERVATION status pending a fourteen-day clean run at full envelope before the ladder record marks it
back at Grade 0 with full confidence. This traverse — 0 to 1 to 2, sustained, then a governed, evidence-
gated return toward 0 — is the complete institutional behavior this Part specifies, and every step of it is
visible in the capability's logged grade history for the postmortem to read as one coherent narrative rather
than a scattering of disconnected alerts.

10.7 Why five grades and not more. A finer-grained ladder (ten grades, a continuous percentage) was
considered and rejected, NORMATIVE, for the same reason IAS-F1 Part II §2.5 bounded its primitive object set
deliberately small: a ladder fine enough to require judgment calls at every one-percent boundary produces
more disagreement about WHICH grade a capability currently occupies than it produces useful signal, and the
five grades chosen here map cleanly onto five operationally distinct RESPONSES (do nothing / watch / narrow
/ substitute / stop) rather than onto five arbitrary reliability-percentage bands, which is the property
that makes the ladder actionable rather than merely descriptive.

10.8 The flapping failure mode, and its detection signal. Part X §10.4's asymmetric-caution rule governs a
single descent-then-sustained-ascent cycle; a distinct failure mode arises when a capability's grade
OSCILLATES repeatedly across a short span — descending to Grade 1 or 2, beginning its sustained-clean-period
toward ascent, then descending again before the sustained period completes, cycling several times within
what should be one coherent recovery window. FLAPPING, DEFINED, is this repeated-oscillation pattern,
distinct from a single legitimate descent-and-recovery traverse (Part X §10.6's worked example) by its
REPETITION COUNT within a bounded span rather than by any single transition's own evidentiary basis, each of
which may individually satisfy §10.3's logging and §10.4's evidentiary-bar requirements without the pattern
as a whole being healthy. (OBSERVED, by analogy: the identical pathology recurs in classical infrastructure
health-checking, where a service repeatedly marked up-then-down produces more operational harm through
alerting and re-routing churn than a single sustained outage would.) The DETECTION SIGNAL, NORMATIVE: a
FLAP COUNTER tracks the number of DOWNWARD transitions within a rolling window sized to the capability's
own sustained-period parameter (§10.4); a counter crossing three within that window, independent of whether
any single transition individually looks justified, flags the capability FLAPPING — a status distinct from
any single grade on the ladder, logged as a metadata annotation on the grade history rather than as a sixth
grade, because flapping is a property of the TRANSITION PATTERN, not an additional state the capability
occupies. A FLAPPING flag routes to the capability's owner as an escalation more urgent than a routine
Grade 1 flag, and is a MANDATORY trigger for the reliability postmortem (Part XVIII), because a capability
whose individual transitions each look locally justified but whose aggregate pattern is unstable exhibits
exactly the defect a window-by-window read of the ladder alone cannot see — the same blind spot Part IV
§4.2 names for a threshold SLO masking sub-window volatility, recurring here at the grade-transition layer.

FINAL LAW — PART X. Five grades, not two — full function, watched, reduced envelope, fallback-served,
unavailable — because a capability that can no longer do everything can often still do something, and an
estate with no vocabulary for "something" will either pretend the capability is fine or declare it dead,
both of which are lies about the state actually in front of it.

===============================================================================================
PART XI — INSTITUTIONAL CHAOS ENGINEERING: DOCTRINE AND GOVERNANCE
===============================================================================================

11.1 Why a federation deliberately breaks itself. Every mechanism specified so far measures, targets, and
budgets failure that ARRIVES on its own. INSTITUTIONAL CHAOS ENGINEERING, DEFINED, inverts the posture: it
is the deliberate, governed injection of controlled failure into a live or shadow capability, undertaken
specifically to discover a reliability gap on the estate's own schedule, under observation, with a rollback
point ready, rather than discovering the same gap for the first time when a real mission hits it
unobserved and unprepared. The founding intuition, DERIVED from the site-reliability discipline imported in
Part I §1.5, is that an untested failure path is not a safe path; it is an unmeasured one, and an unmeasured
path's first real test will, eventually, be a live mission that cannot afford to be the test case.

11.2 Why this is an ensemble-level mechanism, not a per-system testing practice. A single system's own unit
and integration tests already exercise many of its failure paths in isolation; chaos engineering, as
DEFINED here, is distinct because its object is the ENSEMBLE'S relation graph (IAS-F1 Part VI), not one
system's internals — a chaos experiment injects a failure into ONE capability specifically to observe how
the DEPENDS-ON and FEEDS edges around it in the live estate actually behave, information no per-system test
suite can produce because a per-system test suite does not have the rest of the seventy-plus-system estate
wired into it. (DERIVED: this satisfies IAS-F1's second-order test, Part VII §7.2 of that dataset, directly
— the experiment's object is the relation between capabilities under stress, not any single capability's
correctness in isolation.)

11.3 The chaos doctrine's four non-negotiable governance rules. NORMATIVE, and stated before any protocol
mechanics because a chaos-engineering capability that skipped these rules would itself be the single most
dangerous system this dataset could specify. Rule One — BLAST RADIUS BOUNDING: every experiment declares,
in advance, the maximum scope of systems its injected failure is permitted to reach, and the experiment
mechanism itself, not merely the experiment's design intent, enforces that boundary — an unbounded chaos
injection is not an experiment, it is an unauthorized production incident wearing an experiment's name.
Rule Two — BUDGET-GATED ACTIVATION: per Part V §5.5, a chaos experiment against a capability may only be
authorized while that capability holds sufficient remaining failure budget to absorb the experiment's
worst-case consumption without risking an involuntary SLO breach the experiment itself would cause — chaos
engineering spends budget deliberately; it does not exceed it. Rule Three — INSTANT ABORT: every experiment
carries a pre-verified, tested rollback or abort path (invoking rollback and backup, the parents named in
Part I §1.4, directly) that can halt the injection and restore prior state faster than the experiment's own
worst-case blast-radius damage could compound — an experiment without a faster-than-damage abort path is
not authorized to run, full stop. Rule Four — OWNER CONSENT: the capability's own owner, who holds authority
over its decision class per IAS-F1's Invariant Three, must explicitly authorize any experiment against
their capability — IAS-E2 proposes candidate experiments (Part XI §11.6) but never runs one without the
owning system's consent, the Article IX discipline applied at its sharpest point in this entire dataset.

11.4 Experiment eligibility: what may be chaos-tested and what may not. Per Rule Two, a capability is
ELIGIBLE for a chaos experiment only while its budget ledger (Part VI) shows sufficient headroom; a
capability already at Grade 2 or below on the degradation ladder (Part X) is INELIGIBLE by default,
because injecting additional controlled failure into an already-degraded capability risks compounding a
real problem with an artificial one in a way the abort path's speed calculus (Rule Three) did not account
for. A capability whose DEPENDS-ON fan-out (IAS-F1 Part VI §6.3) is unusually wide — many other capabilities
depend on it — requires a correspondingly TIGHTER blast-radius bound and a stronger abort-path verification
before eligibility, because the propagation risk scales with fan-out exactly as Part IX §9.5's ensemble-
level degradation detection already established for organic failures.

11.5 What an experiment produces: evidence, not just an outcome. A completed chaos experiment's primary
product, NORMATIVE, is an EVIDENCE RECORD distinct from a simple pass/fail: what failure was injected, what
blast radius was actually observed versus predicted, which downstream systems responded per their declared
graceful degradation contracts (Part XIV) and which did not, how long recovery actually took versus the
recovery-readiness estimate (Part XV) predicted, and — critically — whether any UNDECLARED dependency
surfaced, meaning a system was affected by the injected failure despite no DEPENDS-ON or FEEDS edge
recording that relationship in IAS-F1's graph beforehand. This last finding is, DERIVED, often the single
most valuable output of an experiment, because an undeclared dependency is a gap in the ensemble's own
self-knowledge (IAS-F1's ontology) that no amount of passive observability could have surfaced without the
active perturbation chaos engineering specifically provides.

11.6 Who proposes an experiment, and the candidate-ranking logic. Candidate experiments are proposed,
NORMATIVE, primarily from three sources: a capability newly promoted to a higher-stakes role (a candidate
for its first-ever chaos test, since its declared graceful degradation contract has never actually been
exercised under real perturbation), a capability whose Fusion Score profile (per IAS-F2 Part V, imported by
reference) shows a WEAK Integration Quality slot — a system other systems lean on without a clean,
documented seam is precisely the shape a chaos experiment is best suited to expose — and a capability the
Mission-Reliability Predictor (Part VII) has flagged as carrying an unusually wide forecast confidence
interval, since a wide interval is itself evidence of thin, untested coverage the experiment can narrow.
Candidates are RANKED, not run in arrival order, by expected information gain per unit of budget spent —
the same scarce-resource discipline this entire family (per IAS-C1's portfolio logic, referenced but not
duplicated here) applies to every other form of cognitive capital.

11.7 The failure mode this doctrine exists to prevent, named directly. A federation that skips deliberate
chaos testing does not thereby avoid the failures chaos engineering would have found — it merely defers
discovering them from a scheduled, bounded, consented experiment to an unscheduled, unbounded, unconsented
real mission, and the deferred version of the same discovery is strictly worse on every one of the four
governance axes of §11.3 simultaneously: unbounded because no blast radius was declared, unbudgeted because
no capacity was reserved, unaborted because no verified rollback path was staged in advance, and
unconsented because the affected owner learns of the gap only after their capability has already failed a
real mission. Institutional chaos engineering does not add risk to the estate; it relocates a risk that was
always present into a controlled setting where the estate can afford to pay it.

11.8 Interface to IAS-D2: chaos evidence as a fingerprint-library contribution. The preamble names IAS-D2
(Immune System) as feeding IAS-E2 failure fingerprints (Part I §1.7); institutional chaos engineering is
the specific mechanism through which the traffic runs the other direction as well. An evidence record (Part
XI §11.5) from a WIDER-THAN-PREDICTED experiment — an undeclared dependency surfacing under deliberate
injection — is, DERIVED, a higher-quality input to IAS-D2's own fingerprint library than an
organically-discovered failure of the same shape would be, because the chaos record carries exactly the
controlled conditions (declared hypothesis, bounded radius, graduated injection, per-step instrumentation
per Part XII §12.8) that let IAS-D2 attribute the fingerprint to its TRUE root cause with far less ambiguity
than a real incident's noisier, unplanned telemetry typically permits. NORMATIVE: every chaos experiment's
evidence record, on closure, is offered to IAS-D2 as a candidate fingerprint contribution regardless of
whether the experiment's outcome was CONFIRMED, NARROWER-THAN-PREDICTED, or WIDER-THAN-PREDICTED (Part XII
§12.5) — even a CONFIRMED outcome, where nothing surprising happened, is a negative-evidence contribution,
confirming that a previously-suspected coupling does NOT in fact exist, which is itself valuable for IAS-D2
to retire a stale fingerprint rather than continue carrying it forward as an open suspicion. This closes a
loop the preamble's edge list states only in one direction: IAS-D2 informs which capabilities IAS-E2 should
prioritize testing (Part XI §11.6's candidate-ranking sources), and IAS-E2's testing, in turn, sharpens the
fingerprints IAS-D2 uses to make that same prioritization better on the next cycle — a compounding
relationship rather than a one-way import, and the specific mechanism by which chaos engineering's
institutional value (§11.7) extends beyond the single experiment's own capability to the estate's general
failure-classification capacity. A chaos program that runs experiments but never routes evidence back to
IAS-D2 has captured only half of this interface's value: the half that improves the tested capability
directly, while forfeiting the half that would have improved every future fingerprint-informed decision
across the rest of the estate.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. A federation that never deliberately breaks itself is not safer than one that does — it
has simply chosen to let its first real failure be the experiment, unbounded, unbudgeted, unaborted, and
unconsented, and institutional chaos engineering exists to move that same discovery onto terms the estate
actually chose.

===============================================================================================
PART XII — THE CHAOS PROTOCOL: HYPOTHESIS, BLAST RADIUS, INJECTION, OBSERVATION
===============================================================================================

12.1 From doctrine to procedure. Part XI established WHY and under what governance chaos experiments run;
this Part specifies the procedural sequence every experiment follows, NORMATIVE, so that "run a chaos
experiment" names one repeatable protocol rather than an ad hoc improvisation each time, and so that the
evidence record Part XI §11.5 requires has a consistent shape across every experiment the estate ever runs.

12.2 Stage One — HYPOTHESIS. Every experiment begins with an explicit, falsifiable HYPOTHESIS about the
system's expected behavior under the planned injection — "if capability K's output is delayed by 3x its
normal latency, capability Q, which feeds from K, is expected to enter Grade 2 within one invocation cycle
per its declared graceful degradation contract, and no downstream capability beyond Q should show any
grade change at all." A hypothesis this specific does two things a vague "let's see what happens" cannot:
it names the PREDICTED blast radius (Q only) against which the OBSERVED blast radius (Stage Four) can be
compared, and it makes the experiment's outcome falsifiable rather than merely descriptive, satisfying
Article V's epistemic-honesty discipline applied to a proposed test rather than to a retrospective claim.

12.3 Stage Two — BLAST RADIUS DECLARATION AND BOUNDING. Building on the hypothesis's predicted radius, this
stage names the MAXIMUM permitted radius — deliberately wider than the predicted radius, to allow the
experiment to detect an undeclared dependency (Part XI §11.5) without that discovery itself being read as a
containment failure — together with the concrete technical boundary (which systems, which mission types,
which time window) the injection mechanism itself enforces. NORMATIVE: the declared maximum radius is
reviewed and approved by every owner of every system inside it, not only the primary target's owner,
because Rule Four's consent requirement (Part XI §11.3) extends to any system the experiment could
plausibly reach, not merely the one it is aimed at.

12.4 Stage Three — INJECTION, WITH THE ABORT PATH ARMED. The actual failure — a delay, an error response, a
degraded-quality output, a simulated unavailability — is introduced under the pre-verified abort path (Rule
Three) held ready and monitored continuously throughout, never merely staged and forgotten once injection
begins. NORMATIVE: injection intensity ramps rather than starting at full severity, so that if the observed
blast radius begins exceeding the declared maximum at low severity, the abort path triggers before full
severity is ever reached — a graduated injection is strictly safer than an instant full-severity one for
identical eventual test coverage, at the cost of a longer experiment duration, a trade-off this dataset
resolves NORMATIVE in favor of safety.

12.5 Stage Four — OBSERVATION AND COMPARISON AGAINST HYPOTHESIS. Throughout and immediately following
injection, the experiment records every grade transition (Part X §10.3's logged-event discipline, applied
here to experiment-triggered transitions specifically so they are distinguishable in the historical record
from organically-triggered ones), every SLO and budget consequence (Parts III-VI), and every graceful
degradation contract invocation (Part XIV) among systems inside the declared blast radius. The observed
radius and behavior are then compared, explicitly, against the Stage One hypothesis: CONFIRMED (observed
matched predicted), NARROWER-THAN-PREDICTED (a positive surprise — the ensemble is more resilient at this
edge than believed, itself valuable evidence for Part XV's recovery-readiness record), or WIDER-THAN-
PREDICTED (an undeclared dependency surfaced, per Part XI §11.5, and this outcome routes directly to a
mandatory postmortem, Part XVIII, regardless of whether the experiment stayed within its bounded maximum
radius).

12.6 Stage Five — RESTORATION AND VERIFICATION. The abort or natural experiment conclusion restores every
affected system to its pre-experiment state, and NORMATIVE, restoration is not assumed complete merely
because the injection mechanism reports it stopped — a verification pass confirms every system inside the
declared radius has returned to its pre-experiment grade and SLO status before the experiment is marked
CLOSED, because an experiment that silently leaves one system at a degraded grade after "restoration" has
produced exactly the organic-looking degradation Part IX's detector would otherwise have to catch later,
unnecessarily, with no record connecting it back to its true chaos-experiment cause.

12.7 Worked example: a full protocol trace with an undeclared-dependency discovery. A synthetic experiment,
illustrative only, hypothesizes that injecting a 3x latency delay into capability K will push its direct
feeds-consumer Q to Grade 2 and affect nothing further. The blast radius is declared as {K, Q} with a
maximum bound of {K, Q, and any system within one additional hop of either}. Injection ramps from 1.5x to
3x delay over four graduated steps, abort path armed and monitored at each step. Observation confirms Q
enters Grade 2 as predicted at the 2x step — but also shows a THIRD system, R, entering Grade 1 at the 3x
step, despite no FEEDS or DEPENDS-ON edge from K to R recorded in IAS-F1's relation graph at experiment
start. The result is logged WIDER-THAN-PREDICTED, restoration confirms all three systems return to Grade 0
within the expected window, and the experiment routes automatically to a mandatory postmortem (Part XVIII)
whose specific charge is to trace HOW R was actually affected — very likely an undocumented shared
dependency (a common knowledge-lookup or shared context resource) that the graph itself must now be
corrected to reflect, which is, per Part XI §11.7, exactly the class of institutional value this entire
mechanism exists to produce: a gap in the estate's own self-knowledge, found on a Tuesday afternoon by
design, rather than found in a real mission six weeks later by accident.

12.8 Instrumentation requirements: what must be logged at every ramp step. NORMATIVE, and distinct from
Stage Four's post-hoc observation record (§12.5), the injection stage itself carries a mandatory
per-step instrumentation contract: at each graduated intensity level, the mechanism logs the injected
severity, the observed grade of every system inside the declared blast radius at that moment, the latency
and error-rate profile of the primary target, and — critically — a continuous read of the abort path's own
readiness (Rule Three, Part XI §11.3), never assumed constant once the experiment has begun. This
per-step granularity exists specifically because a WIDER-THAN-PREDICTED outcome (§12.5) is far more useful
diagnostically if the postmortem can see AT WHICH RAMP STEP the undeclared dependency first began
responding, rather than only the final aggregate outcome — a dependency that only manifests at the highest
severity step is a different institutional finding than one that manifests immediately at the lowest step,
because the former indicates a threshold-triggered coupling and the latter indicates a coupling active even
under light stress, and Part XVIII's Stage Two root-cause attribution reads this per-step trace directly
when the routed postmortem (§12.5) begins its reconstruction.

12.9 The abort-path-failure edge case, worked. Rule Three (Part XI §11.3) requires every experiment to carry
a pre-verified abort path faster than the experiment's own worst-case blast-radius damage — but the abort
path is itself a mechanism that can fail, and this Part specifies, NORMATIVE, what happens when it does.
Consider a synthetic experiment, illustrative only, in which the abort path itself invokes rollback (Part I
§1.4) to restore a capability's pre-experiment configuration, and at the third ramp step, the monitoring
instrumentation of §12.8 detects the blast radius exceeding its declared maximum — triggering the abort — but
rollback's own restoration takes measurably longer than its own recovery-readiness record (Part XV §15.3)
predicted, because the very stress the experiment induced has itself degraded the storage-access path
rollback depends on, the identical circular-dependency hazard Part XV §15.4 names for organic recovery,
surfacing here under a deliberate experiment instead. NORMATIVE: an abort path that fails to complete within
its own predicted window is itself an AUTOMATIC MANDATORY-POSTMORTEM trigger (Part XVIII §18.4), added
explicitly to that Part's trigger list, distinct from and more urgent than a routine WIDER-THAN-PREDICTED
finding, because an abort path that does not perform as verified is a defect in the estate's own safety net,
not merely a surprising discovery about the target capability's dependencies — and every future chaos
candidate ranking (Part XI §11.6) for any capability sharing that same recovery mechanism is penalized,
NORMATIVE, until the abort path's own recovery-readiness record has been re-verified and re-confirmed CLEAN.
This is the specific mechanism that keeps Rule Three from being a one-time pre-flight checkbox: an abort path
is only as trustworthy as its most recent exercise, and a chaos protocol that never revisits that trust after
a slow abort has quietly downgraded its own single most safety-critical guarantee without anyone noticing.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. Hypothesis before injection, bounded radius before intensity, graduated ramp before
full severity, verified restoration before closure — a chaos experiment run out of this order is not a
faster experiment, it is an unbounded incident that happens to have started on purpose.

===============================================================================================
PART XIII — GRACEFUL DEGRADATION: THE DESIGNED PARTIAL-CAPABILITY STATE
===============================================================================================

13.1 From detecting degradation to designing it. Parts IX and X specify how degradation is DETECTED and
GRADED once it occurs, largely as a response to conditions arising organically. GRACEFUL DEGRADATION,
DEFINED, is the complementary, forward-looking discipline: designing, in advance, exactly what a
capability's Grade 2 (reduced envelope) and Grade 3 (fallback-served) states should DO, so that when
degradation occurs — organically or via chaos experiment — the capability's reduced-function behavior is a
DESIGNED response to an anticipated condition rather than an improvised one discovered for the first time
under real failure pressure. This is the mechanism the Strengthening Matrix names directly as this
dataset's headline evidence requirement: "an SLO + a failure-budget + a graceful-degradation path defined."

13.2 Why undesigned degradation is worse than either full function or clean unavailability. A capability
with no designed reduced-function behavior does not skip straight and cleanly from Grade 0 to Grade 4 when
stressed; it typically limps — producing output that is neither reliably correct nor cleanly absent,
consuming downstream capacity on results a consumer cannot trust and has no signal to distinguish from
trustworthy ones. (DERIVED: this is strictly worse than clean unavailability, because a downstream
DEPENDS-ON consumer that receives a silently-degraded, unflagged output will propagate that degraded
output as if it were reliable, compounding the original problem rather than containing it — precisely the
propagation zero-crash and cascade_prevention are built to stop for STATE corruption, but which those
parents cannot catch when the corruption is in OUTPUT QUALITY rather than in a crash signature.) Graceful
degradation exists specifically to close this gap: a capability at a designed Grade 2 announces, structurally,
that it is operating at reduced capacity, so every consumer can make an informed choice rather than
unknowingly trusting a quietly worse answer.

13.3 The three shapes of a designed degradation response. ENVELOPE NARROWING, the most common shape,
restricts the class of inputs the capability accepts or the precision of output it promises, while
remaining the SAME implementation — the capability that normally handles arbitrary mission transcripts
might, at Grade 2, decline transcripts above a length threshold it has identified as correlated with its
own recent misses, continuing to serve the narrower, well-covered case reliably rather than serving the
full case unreliably. QUALITY STEPPING DOWN offers a materially simpler, cheaper, or more conservative
output at reduced confidence — a synthesis capability that normally produces a nuanced, multi-source answer
might, at Grade 2, produce a single-source, explicitly-flagged "low confidence" answer rather than
attempting the harder synthesis under conditions it currently trusts less. EXPLICIT REFUSAL WITH REASON,
the most conservative shape, declines the request outright but with a structured, machine-readable reason
a consumer can act on programmatically (retry later, route to a Part XVII fallback, escalate to a human) —
distinct from a silent failure precisely because the refusal itself is the designed, informative output.

13.4 The contract's relationship to consumers: a promise, not merely an internal state. A capability's
degraded behavior is only genuinely graceful if its CONSUMERS know what to expect from it — a Grade 2
narrowed-envelope capability whose consumers were never told the envelope could narrow will still surprise
them, defeating the purpose. NORMATIVE: the graceful degradation contract (specified fully in Part XIV) is
therefore published, per IAS-F1's addressing discipline (Part IX of that dataset), as a discoverable
property of the capability itself, so that a consuming system (or IAS-A1's router, at mission-composition
time) can read, in advance, what a capability WILL do under each grade, and can plan its own DEPENDS-ON or
FEEDS relationship accordingly — a consumer that cannot tolerate any narrowing should, per this visibility,
prefer a different capability or arrange its own fallback rather than discovering the incompatibility only
when degradation actually occurs.

13.5 Worked example: three capabilities, three designed responses to the same stress. Consider a synthetic
scenario, illustrative only, in which a shared upstream knowledge-lookup capability, K, begins operating at
elevated latency (a Grade 1 condition for K itself). Three downstream capabilities, each depending on K
differently, respond per their own designed contracts. Capability P, a routing decision that needs K's
output only as a tie-breaker among otherwise-close candidates, is designed to fall back to its own
secondary heuristic when K exceeds a latency threshold — envelope narrowing, invisible to P's own consumers
because P's overall output shape is unchanged. Capability Q, a synthesis step that genuinely needs K's
content to produce a grounded answer, is designed to step down to a lower-confidence, explicitly-flagged
output when K is slow rather than waiting indefinitely — quality stepping down, visible to Q's consumers
via the confidence flag. Capability R, a compliance-check step for which an ungrounded answer would be
actively dangerous rather than merely lower quality, is designed to refuse explicitly with a structured
"upstream dependency degraded, retry recommended" reason rather than attempting any output at reduced
confidence — explicit refusal, the most conservative of the three shapes, chosen deliberately because R's
mission stakes do not tolerate the other two. The same upstream stress produces three DIFFERENT, each
individually correct, designed responses, because graceful degradation is calibrated per capability's own
stakes, never applied as one uniform policy across the estate.

13.6 Designing a contract without ever having observed the failure it targets. A capability's very first
graceful degradation contract is necessarily authored, NORMATIVE, before any real degradation has been
observed for it — an EXPERIMENTAL claim in Article V's vocabulary, carrying its own test (the chaos
experiment of Parts XI-XII is the natural, and often the first, occasion on which a newly-authored contract
is actually exercised and either CONFIRMED or found wanting). A contract that has never been exercised,
organically or via chaos test, carries an explicit UNTESTED marking in its record, distinct from a contract
that has been confirmed working at least once, so that a consumer relying on it (per §13.4's discoverability)
knows whether the promise is a design intention or a demonstrated behavior — the same evidence-level
discipline this whole dataset applies to every other claim, here applied to a designed response rather than
a measured one.

13.7 The cost calculus across the three shapes, made explicit. Part XIII §13.3 named the three designed
shapes without ranking their cost; this section closes that gap, NORMATIVE, because a capability owner
choosing among them without a cost model will default to whichever shape is easiest to implement rather than
whichever shape actually matches the capability's stakes, the same failure-mode-match error Part XVI §16.5
names for redundancy tiering, applied here to degradation design instead. ENVELOPE NARROWING carries the
LOWEST implementation cost of the three, because it requires no new output path — only an input-side filter
identifying and excluding the class of request correlated with recent misses — but it carries a distinct
OPPORTUNITY cost: every narrowed-out request is a request the capability could, in principle, have attempted,
and a capability that narrows too aggressively or too permanently converts a temporary reliability problem
into a permanent capability gap the estate never revisits. QUALITY STEPPING DOWN carries a MODERATE
implementation cost, because it requires a genuinely alternate, simpler output path to be built, tested, and
maintained alongside the primary path — a second thing to keep working, not merely a filter on the first — but
it preserves service to every consumer, at reduced confidence, which is often the better trade for a
capability whose consumers can tolerate a flagged lower-confidence answer more easily than an outright
narrowing of what they can even ask for. EXPLICIT REFUSAL WITH REASON carries the LOWEST ongoing maintenance
cost of the three (there is no alternate output path to build at all) but the HIGHEST opportunity cost per
invocation, since every refused request produces zero value rather than reduced value, and this is precisely
why §13.5's worked scenario reserves refusal for capability R specifically — the shape's low build cost is
irrelevant when the mission stakes make even a low-confidence wrong answer actively dangerous, and the
opportunity cost of refusing is, in that specific case, the cheaper of the two costs to bear. NORMATIVE: a
capability's owner, when authoring the contract of Part XIV, is required to record which shape was chosen and
a one-line justification citing this cost calculus explicitly, so that Part XIV §14.3's completeness review
can distinguish a deliberate cost trade-off from a shape chosen merely because it was the first one the owner
thought of — the same justification discipline this dataset applies to every other design decision, here
extended to the graceful degradation shape itself rather than only to the SLO or the fallback tier.

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. Undesigned degradation does not fail cleanly; it limps, producing untrustworthy
output with no signal distinguishing it from trustworthy output — graceful degradation exists to replace
that limp with one of three deliberate, calibrated, consumer-visible responses, chosen per capability by
what its own downstream stakes can and cannot tolerate.

===============================================================================================
PART XIV — THE GRACEFUL DEGRADATION CONTRACT
===============================================================================================

14.1 From design principle to record. Part XIII established the three shapes a designed degradation
response may take and why the choice among them is calibrated per capability; this Part specifies the
CONTRACT — the concrete, published record that makes a capability's degradation design discoverable,
enforceable in the sense that Part X's ladder mechanics can check a transition against it, and auditable by
the postmortem discipline of Part XVIII.

14.2 The contract's required fields. A GRACEFUL DEGRADATION CONTRACT, NORMATIVE, carries: SUBJECT (the
capability it governs, addressed per IAS-F1's stable-ID convention), TRIGGER CONDITIONS (the specific
signals — a degradation-detector flag per Part IX, an SLO breach per Part III, a chaos-experiment injection
per Part XII, an upstream dependency's own grade change per §13.5's worked pattern — that cause a grade
transition to auto-fire rather than requiring a manual owner action), PER-GRADE BEHAVIOR (for each of
Grades 1 through 4, which of Part XIII §13.3's three shapes applies, and the specific technical detail of
that shape — which inputs are excluded at Grade 2, which fallback is invoked at Grade 3 per Part XVII, what
structured reason accompanies a Grade 3 or 4 refusal), CONSUMER NOTIFICATION MECHANISM (how a consuming
system learns the capability's current grade — a discoverable status field, an event on IAS-A2's Control
Bus, or both), and TESTED STATUS (per Part XIII §13.6, whether the contract has been exercised and
confirmed, or remains UNTESTED design intent).

14.3 Contract authorship and review. A contract is authored, NORMATIVE, by the capability's own owner —
consistent with every authority-preserving pattern this dataset has established — but is REVIEWED, not
authored, by IAS-E2's own mechanisms for two specific properties a busy owner is prone to miss:
COMPLETENESS (does the contract cover all four non-zero grades, or does it silently assume Grade 2 never
needs a distinct design from Grade 3, collapsing two genuinely different states into one under-specified
response) and CONSISTENCY WITH THE RELATION GRAPH (does the contract's trigger conditions correctly account
for every DEPENDS-ON edge into this capability per IAS-F1's graph, or does it name only the dependencies
the owner happened to think of, leaving an undeclared dependency — exactly Part XI §11.5's discovery
target — silently uncovered by design rather than merely undiscovered by observation).

14.4 The contract's relationship to the fallback chain. A Grade 3 PER-GRADE BEHAVIOR entry that specifies
"invoke fallback" is INCOMPLETE without a corresponding entry in the redundancy ladder (Part XVII)
identifying WHICH fallback, under what conditions it itself is eligible, and what quality gap exists
between primary and fallback output — a contract that says merely "fall back" without this cross-reference
has deferred the actual design decision rather than made it, and Part XIX §19.8 names this specific gap
UNDERSPECIFIED FALLBACK as a recurring implementation shortfall.

14.5 Contract staleness and the re-review trigger. Per IAS-F1's general staleness discipline (Part IV
§4.3 of that dataset) applied to a contract rather than a property, a graceful degradation contract that
has not been reviewed since the capability's own implementation was last materially changed carries elevated
risk that its PER-GRADE BEHAVIOR no longer accurately reflects what the current implementation actually
does under stress — a contract authored against an old implementation and never re-verified against a
newer one is a false promise waiting to be discovered at the worst possible moment. NORMATIVE: a material
implementation change to a capability is a mandatory re-review trigger for its contract, tracked the same
way Part IV §4.6 tracks an SLO's UNDER REVISION status.

14.6 Worked example: the contract underlying Part XIII's three-capability scenario, made explicit. Returning
to capability Q from Part XIII §13.5, its contract's PER-GRADE BEHAVIOR entry for Grade 2 specifies: trigger
= K's own grade reaching 1 or worse OR Q's direct latency exceeding a stated threshold; behavior = quality
stepping down, producing single-source output with an explicit LOW-CONFIDENCE flag in a stated output
field, rather than the normal multi-source synthesis; consumer notification = the LOW-CONFIDENCE flag
itself, embedded in Q's output, plus a status event on the Control Bus for any consumer polling grade state
directly rather than reading per-invocation flags; tested status = CONFIRMED, following the chaos experiment
worked in Part XII §12.7, which incidentally exercised Q's contract as a secondary effect of K's injected
latency. This single contract record is what let Part XIII's worked scenario be more than a narrative
description — it is the actual specification a real implementer would build against, complete enough that
Part XXI's evaluation protocol can check the implementation against the contract's own stated fields rather
than against an informal sense of what "graceful" should mean.

14.7 The contract as this dataset's clearest single Dataset-to-Runtime translation candidate. Of every
mechanism specified across this dataset's twenty-two Parts, the graceful degradation contract's fixed field
schema (§14.2) is the most directly implementable as a concrete artifact — a per-capability record, checked
by an automated completeness-and-consistency gate (§14.3) at authorship and at every material implementation
change (§14.5), consumed by Part X's ladder mechanics to determine actual transition behavior, and by any
consuming system per §14.6's notification mechanism. This is named explicitly here, ahead of Part XXIII's
dedicated translation section, because it is the single mechanism in this dataset for which the path from
specification to runtime artifact requires no further design decision — every other mechanism in this
dataset (the predictor, the detector, the chaos protocol) requires additional engineering judgment the
contract schema itself does not.

14.8 Contract diffing across a material implementation change, worked. Part XIV §14.5 names a material
implementation change as a mandatory re-review trigger; this section specifies, NORMATIVE, what the
re-review actually compares, because "re-review" left unspecified could mean anything from a full re-write
to a cursory skim. The re-review, NORMATIVE, produces an explicit DIFF between the contract's PER-GRADE
BEHAVIOR fields as authored and the capability's new implementation's ACTUAL observed behavior under a
targeted chaos experiment (Parts XI-XII) or, where a fresh experiment is not immediately available, a
structured code-level inspection of the new implementation's input-handling and fallback-invocation paths
against the contract's stated triggers. A diff finding is FIELD-CLEAN if the new implementation's behavior
at each grade still matches the contract's stated PER-GRADE BEHAVIOR exactly; it is FIELD-DRIFTED if the
behavior has changed in a way the contract does not reflect — the implementation now narrows a different
input class at Grade 2 than the contract states, for instance, following a refactor that changed which
malformed-input shapes the capability actually detects. Worked example, synthetic and illustrative:
capability Q from Part XIII §13.5 and Part XIV §14.6's worked contract specifies Grade 2 behavior as
"quality stepping down, single-source output with a LOW-CONFIDENCE flag, triggered by K's grade or Q's own
latency threshold." A subsequent implementation change replaces Q's underlying synthesis approach entirely,
and the re-review's targeted chaos experiment discovers that under the new implementation, Q's Grade 2
behavior has silently changed to a NARROWER-input-class refusal instead of the contract's stated
quality-stepping-down — the new implementation's authors, focused on the primary-path improvement, never
revisited the degradation path at all. This is logged FIELD-DRIFTED, NORMATIVE, and Q's contract moves back
to UNTESTED status specifically for Grade 2 (not for every grade, since Grade 3 and 4 were unaffected by this
particular refactor and remain at their prior tested status) until the contract is either updated to match
the new, narrower-refusal behavior with a fresh justification per §13.7's cost calculus, or the new
implementation is corrected to restore the originally-contracted quality-stepping-down behavior — the owner's
choice, but a choice that must be made explicitly rather than left as an undetected mismatch between what
the estate believes Q does at Grade 2 and what Q actually does. This diffing discipline is what keeps Part
XIV §14.7's translation-candidate claim honest over time: a contract schema that is easy to implement once is
not automatically a contract that stays correct through every subsequent change to the capability it governs,
and the diff is the mechanism that catches the gap between the two.

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. A graceful degradation contract that is authored but never reviewed for completeness,
never cross-referenced against the relation graph, never re-verified after the implementation changes
beneath it, and never actually exercised is a document, not a contract — and the difference between the two
is exactly the difference between a promise and a promise someone has checked.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — RECOVERY READINESS: MEASURING RECOVERABILITY BEFORE IT IS NEEDED
===============================================================================================

15.1 The question none of the five parents answer in advance. zero-crash, rollback, backup, and session
resilience each execute a recovery mechanism WHEN a failure occurs; none of them, as OBSERVED per Part I
§1.1, publishes a standing answer to "if capability X failed right now, how long would recovery actually
take, and how confident are we in that estimate" BEFORE the failure happens. RECOVERY READINESS, DEFINED,
is that standing, forward-looking answer: a maintained estimate, per capability, of expected time-to-
recovery and confidence in that estimate, computed from evidence about the recovery mechanisms actually
available to it, refreshed on a cadence rather than produced only in the moment of an active incident when
there is least time to compute it carefully.

15.2 Why an estimate computed during a live incident is the wrong time to compute it. A time-to-recovery
figure produced for the first time WHILE a failure is actively occurring is produced under exactly the
conditions least suited to careful measurement — time pressure, incomplete information, and, if the
recovery mechanism itself is what is failing, no live system available to even query for the estimate.
(DERIVED: this is the direct institutional argument for computing recovery readiness in advance and
maintaining it as a standing record, the same argument Part VII makes for computing mission reliability in
advance rather than only retrospectively — both mechanisms move a needed number from "computed too late to
help" to "available exactly when a decision needs it.") A recovery-readiness record answers, before any
incident, the question an incident-response effort would otherwise have to answer for the first time under
duress.

15.3 The recovery-readiness record's components. NORMATIVE, a capability's recovery-readiness record
carries: RECOVERY MECHANISM (which of the five parent systems, or which fallback per Part XVII, would
actually restore this capability, named explicitly rather than assumed), LAST VERIFIED (the most recent
date this mechanism was actually exercised and confirmed to work — per a chaos experiment, Part XII, or a
real incident — distinct from merely having been designed and never tested, the identical UNTESTED-versus-
CONFIRMED distinction Part XIII §13.6 applies to degradation contracts, applied here to recovery mechanisms),
ESTIMATED TIME-TO-RECOVERY (a point estimate with a confidence interval, DERIVED from the last verified
exercise's observed duration where available, or marked HYPOTHESIZED where the mechanism has never actually
been exercised), and DEPENDENCY COVERAGE (whether the recovery mechanism itself depends on other systems
that might be UNAVAILABLE at the same time the primary capability is — a recovery path that itself depends
on the thing that is failing is not a recovery path at all, and this field exists specifically to catch that
circularity before it is discovered mid-incident).

15.4 The circular-dependency hazard, worked. Consider a synthetic capability, illustrative only, whose
recovery mechanism is "restore from the most recent backup, retrieved via a shared storage-access
capability." If that shared storage-access capability is itself in the same DEPENDS-ON subgraph as a common
upstream failure mode — the same shared dependency pattern Part IX §9.5 names for ensemble-level
degradation — then a failure large enough to take down the primary capability may, in the worst case, also
degrade the very storage-access path recovery depends on, and a recovery-readiness record that names only
"restore from backup" without checking this circularity has produced a confident-looking estimate that is
silently invalid in exactly the scenario it matters most. DEPENDENCY COVERAGE analysis, run against IAS-F1's
relation graph the same way Part XI §11.4's eligibility check reads DEPENDS-ON fan-out, is the specific
mechanism that catches this hazard before an incident does.

15.5 Recovery readiness as the chaos protocol's natural downstream consumer. Per Part XII §12.5's Stage
Four, every chaos experiment's restoration step produces an OBSERVED time-to-recovery figure for whatever
recovery mechanism the experiment exercised, and this figure is, NORMATIVE, the preferred source for a
capability's recovery-readiness record's ESTIMATED TIME-TO-RECOVERY field whenever a recent exercise exists
— an OBSERVED figure from a real, governed test strictly outranks a HYPOTHESIZED figure from an
unexercised design, per Article V's evidence-level discipline applied here as everywhere in this dataset.
This is the concrete mechanism by which Part XI's chaos-experiment candidate ranking (§11.6) should weight
"this capability's recovery readiness has never been verified" as an independent factor favoring an
experiment against it, closing a loop between two of this dataset's own mechanisms without either
duplicating the other's function.

15.6 What recovery readiness is for: pre-flight gating and portfolio input. A LOW recovery-readiness
capability — long estimated time-to-recovery, low confidence, or an unresolved circular dependency — is
NORMATIVE input to the Mission-Reliability Predictor's context features (Part VIII §8.2) for any mission
depending on it, to IAS-C1's Capability Portfolio as a cost factor (a capability that is cheap to run but
expensive to recover from carries a different total-cost-of-ownership than its raw operating cost suggests),
and to a pre-flight check an Owner or automated gate may consult before committing a high-stakes mission to
a capability whose recovery, should it be needed, is itself unproven. As with every prior mechanism, IAS-E2
surfaces this signal; it does not itself block a mission — that authority belongs to whichever system holds
final authority over mission dispatch.

15.7 Worked example: comparing two capabilities' recovery-readiness records. Capability S, synthetic and
illustrative, shows recovery mechanism = rollback to last known-good config, last verified = eleven days
prior via a scheduled chaos experiment, estimated time-to-recovery = 40 seconds at high confidence,
dependency coverage = clean (no circularity found). Capability T, equally synthetic, shows recovery
mechanism = restore from backup, last verified = never (the mechanism was designed at T's inception but no
chaos experiment or real incident has ever exercised it), estimated time-to-recovery = HYPOTHESIZED at
roughly five minutes based on the backup system's own documented performance characteristics rather than
T's actual observed recovery, dependency coverage = UNRESOLVED (the analysis has not yet been run). Read
side by side, S is a proven, fast, well-understood recovery story; T is an untested, slower-by-estimate,
and incompletely-analyzed one — and this comparison, computable from the record alone without any live
incident, is precisely the standing answer §15.1 argues no existing parent currently publishes.

15.8 Recovery readiness across a fallback chain, not merely at the primary. Part XV §15.3's record schema is
specified per capability, but a mission-critical capability rarely stands alone — Part XVII's fallback chain
means the REAL recovery question, NORMATIVE, is not "how fast does the primary recover" in isolation, but
"how fast does the CHAIN, considered as a whole, return the mission to its best available service level."
This section closes that gap explicitly, because a recovery-readiness record scoped only to the primary
capability answers a narrower question than the one an owner actually needs before committing a high-stakes
mission. A CHAIN RECOVERY-READINESS RECORD, DEFINED, aggregates the per-link records of Part XV §15.3 for
every link in a Part XVII fallback chain, and adds one further, chain-specific field: TIME-TO-BEST-AVAILABLE,
the estimated interval from the primary's failure to the mission being served by the BEST link currently
capable of serving it — which is NOT simply the primary's own time-to-recovery, because while the primary is
recovering, Part XVII §17.4's sequential activation logic means the chain is already serving from a fallback,
at that fallback's own quality gap, throughout the primary's recovery window. DERIVED: a mission planner
weighing whether to proceed with a high-stakes mission against a capability whose primary has a SLOW
time-to-recovery but a FAST-activating, high-quality first fallback (an N+1 tier per Part XVI §16.4, quality
gap "none — identical implementation") faces a materially different risk profile than one whose primary has
the identical slow recovery time but no fallback chain at all, or a fallback chain whose only link is a
graceful-capability-redundancy tier with a large quality gap — and a recovery-readiness record scoped to the
primary alone cannot express this difference, even though it is exactly the difference a pre-flight gate
(Part XV §15.6) needs to weigh. Worked example, synthetic and illustrative: capability M from Part XVII
§17.6's three-link chain (M, M', M'') carries a chain recovery-readiness record showing M's own
time-to-recovery at four minutes (rollback-based, well-verified), M' activating within one invocation cycle
of M's Grade 3 transition (N+1, near-instant, quality gap none), and TIME-TO-BEST-AVAILABLE therefore reading
"under one invocation cycle to acceptable service, four minutes to the primary's own full restoration" — a
materially more reassuring composite figure than M's own four-minute recovery time read in isolation would
suggest, precisely because the chain's fast-activating first link absorbs the gap while the slower primary
recovery proceeds in parallel. This chain-level record is, NORMATIVE, the field Part XV §15.6's pre-flight
consumer should actually read for any mission depending on a chained capability, with the single-link record
of §15.3 retained underneath it as the detail a postmortem needs when a specific link's own recovery
performance requires investigation.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. A recovery mechanism that has never been exercised is a hypothesis wearing a
plan's name, and the worst possible moment to discover that a recovery path depends on the very thing that
just failed is during the incident it was supposed to resolve — recovery readiness exists to move that
discovery onto a calendar the estate controls.

PART XVI — THE COGNITIVE REDUNDANCY PLANNER: USEFUL REDUNDANCY VS DUPLICATION
===============================================================================================

16.1 The tension this Part exists to resolve. Two or more systems providing the SAME first-order capability
is, per IAS-F1's Invariant Two (Part V §5.3 of that dataset) and Article I's supreme law, the exact
condition the D2A engine exists to catch and dispose of as a duplication. Yet reliability engineering, as
imported in Part I §1.5, treats deliberate redundancy — a fallback capability that can serve when the
primary cannot — as a CORE, valuable pattern, not a defect. The COGNITIVE REDUNDANCY PLANNER, DEFINED, is
the mechanism that resolves this apparent tension: it distinguishes USEFUL REDUNDANCY, a deliberately
maintained fallback relationship serving a stated reliability purpose, from DUPLICATION, an unmanaged
overlap the estate did not choose and cannot account for, because the two look structurally similar (two
systems doing "the same thing") while being institutionally opposite in value.

16.2 The formal distinction: managed versus unmanaged overlap. DEFINED: a redundancy relationship is USEFUL
if and only if it satisfies four conditions simultaneously — DECLARED (the relationship is recorded
explicitly, per §16.3's fallback-record schema, not merely discovered incidentally by an auditor), RANKED
(there is a stated primary and a stated fallback order, not two peers each silently believing itself
authoritative, which would itself violate IAS-F1's Invariant Three if both held final authority), PURPOSEFUL
(the fallback exists specifically to serve a named reliability goal — covering a specific failure mode the
primary is known to carry — rather than existing as an accidental byproduct of two teams building similar
things independently), and BOUNDED (the redundancy's cost, per IAS-F2's Complexity factor, is periodically
re-justified against its reliability value rather than persisting indefinitely on the strength of its
original justification alone). An overlap missing any of the four is, by definition, drifting toward
DUPLICATION, and the D2A engine's own disposition vocabulary — MERGE-INTO-FAMILY, SUBENGINE, EXTEND — is
the correct remedy for an overlap that fails this test, never a remedy the redundancy planner itself
attempts to apply.

16.3 The fallback record. NORMATIVE, a declared useful-redundancy relationship is recorded as a FALLBACK
RECORD: PRIMARY (the capability normally serving requests), FALLBACK (the capability that serves at Grade 3,
per Part X §10.2), ACTIVATION CONDITION (the specific grade-transition or trigger, cross-referenced against
the primary's own graceful degradation contract per Part XIV §14.4's completeness requirement — an
UNDERSPECIFIED FALLBACK entry in a degradation contract is exactly the gap this record is meant to close),
QUALITY GAP (an honest, stated description of what is LOST by falling back — the fallback's own reliability
record, per Part II, at its own axes, compared against the primary's, so that a consumer knows in advance
what "served by fallback" actually means rather than assuming silent equivalence), and LAST VERIFIED (per
Part XV §15.3's identical discipline, whether the fallback path has actually been exercised, ideally via a
chaos experiment per Part XII, or remains untested design intent).

16.4 The redundancy ladder: three tiers of useful redundancy. Not every fallback relationship carries the
same value, and the planner distinguishes three tiers, DEFINED. N+1 REDUNDANCY is the simplest: a second
instance of substantially the SAME implementation, serving as a fallback against availability failures of
the first instance specifically — cheap to maintain, but structurally unable to help against a CORRECTNESS
failure shared by both instances, since an identical implementation carries an identical defect. DIVERSITY
REDUNDANCY is a fallback built on a MEANINGFULLY DIFFERENT approach — a different model, a different
algorithm, a different data source — specifically so that a correctness failure affecting the primary's
particular approach is less likely to affect the fallback identically; diversity redundancy costs more to
build and maintain (a second, genuinely distinct implementation is more expensive than a second copy of the
first) but is the only tier that helps against the correctness-failure class N+1 cannot address. GRACEFUL-
CAPABILITY REDUNDANCY is not a full second implementation at all but a deliberately simpler, narrower
capability — the "quality stepping down" shape of Part XIII §13.3 formalized as a standing fallback
relationship rather than an in-place behavior change — accepting a known, bounded quality gap in exchange
for materially lower maintenance cost than either of the other two tiers.

16.5 Choosing a tier: the failure-mode-match rule. NORMATIVE: the appropriate redundancy tier for a given
capability is determined by matching the tier's protective scope against the capability's OWN dominant
failure mode, drawn from its reliability record (Part II §2.3's four axes) and its degradation history (Part
X's logged grade transitions) — a capability whose historical failures are overwhelmingly availability-class
(timeouts, transient unavailability) is well served by cheap N+1 redundancy; a capability whose historical
failures are correctness-class (confidently wrong outputs, not absent ones) requires diversity redundancy
or it is not actually protected at all, since an N+1 fallback sharing the same defect would fail identically
under the same triggering condition. Choosing N+1 redundancy for a capability whose dominant failure mode
is correctness-class is, per Part XIX §19.9, the specific FALSE REDUNDANCY failure mode this Part exists to
name and prevent.

16.6 Worked example: auditing three "redundant" pairs for the useful-versus-duplicate test. Consider three
synthetic pairs, illustrative only. Pair One: two capabilities that both "summarize a document," built
independently by different teams, neither aware of the other, no declared fallback record, no stated
ranking — this fails all four conditions of §16.2 and is a genuine DUPLICATION, correctly routed to the
D2A engine for disposition rather than retained under any redundancy framing. Pair Two: a primary
capability and a second instance of the identical implementation, declared, ranked, with a fallback record
whose activation condition is the primary's availability-grade transitions specifically, quality gap
recorded as "none — identical implementation," last verified via a recent chaos experiment — this passes
all four conditions and is USEFUL N+1 redundancy, correctly protecting against availability failures and
honestly silent about correctness failures it cannot help with. Pair Three: a primary capability built on
one grounding approach and a second, genuinely different capability built on an independently-sourced
grounding approach, declared, ranked, with a fallback record whose quality gap honestly states "fallback
covers 80% of the primary's input range at a materially lower confidence band, verified against held-out
cases" — this is USEFUL DIVERSITY redundancy, the tier best matched to protect against a shared-defect
correctness failure the identical-implementation Pair Two could never catch. The same surface pattern —
"two things that do something similar" — resolves to a duplication verdict, an N+1 verdict, and a diversity
verdict respectively, and only the fallback-record discipline of §16.3, applied honestly, distinguishes the
three.

16.7 The planner's relationship to IAS-D1's ecology and to cost. A declared, well-tiered redundancy
relationship is exactly the SYMBIOSIS pattern IAS-D1's ecology mapping (per the ledger's Family D routing)
is built to recognize as healthy; an undeclared, unranked, unbounded overlap is exactly what that same
ecology mapping should flag as either a PARASITE or an unresolved duplicate requiring D2A disposition. The
redundancy planner does not build IAS-D1's ecology map itself — that remains IAS-D1's own object — but it
supplies the fallback-record evidence (§16.3) that lets IAS-D1's judgment be evidence-based rather than
inferred from structural similarity alone, the same STRENGTHENS relation (IAS-F1 Part VI §6.6) this dataset
carries toward every sibling it feeds rather than replaces.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. Two systems doing something similar are not automatically a duplication and not
automatically healthy redundancy; they are a duplication unless the overlap is declared, ranked, purposeful,
and bounded — and even a declared, ranked overlap is FALSE redundancy if its tier does not actually match
the failure mode it claims to protect against.

===============================================================================================
PART XVII — THE REDUNDANCY LADDER AND FALLBACK CHAINS
===============================================================================================

17.1 From a single fallback to a chain. Part XVI specified the fallback record for a single primary-
fallback pair; this Part extends the mechanism to the realistic case of a FALLBACK CHAIN — a capability
whose Grade 3 behavior itself has its own fallback, which may itself have a further fallback, because a
mission-critical capability's reliability requirement often exceeds what a single fallback tier can provide
alone. A FALLBACK CHAIN, DEFINED, is an ordered sequence of fallback records (Part XVI §16.3) chained by
each entry's PRIMARY being the prior entry's FALLBACK, terminating either in a capability judged reliable
enough to serve as the chain's floor or in an explicit, structured refusal (Part XIII §13.3's third shape)
as the chain's designed terminal state.

17.2 Chain depth and diminishing returns. Each additional link in a fallback chain adds coverage against a
narrower slice of remaining failure modes while adding its own maintenance cost (per IAS-F2's Complexity
factor) and its own quality gap compounding on top of every prior link's gap — a mission served by the
third fallback in a four-deep chain has, DERIVED, typically accepted a materially larger cumulative quality
gap than the fallback record of any single link would suggest in isolation, because quality gaps compound
along a chain the way reliability itself compounds along a DEPENDS-ON path (Part VII §7.3's composition
arithmetic). NORMATIVE: a chain's CUMULATIVE quality gap at each depth is computed and published explicitly,
never left implicit as the simple concatenation of each link's own individually-stated gap, because a
consumer choosing to accept service from the third link needs to know the cumulative degradation from the
original primary, not merely the marginal degradation from the second link to the third.

17.3 Chain design: matching tiers to depth, not repeating the same tier. A well-designed chain, NORMATIVE,
does not stack multiple N+1 links in sequence — since each additional identical-implementation instance
protects against the same narrow availability-failure class the first N+1 link already covers, adding a
third instance behind the second buys materially less additional protection than the maintenance cost it
adds. A well-designed chain instead diversifies DOWN the chain: an N+1 first fallback (cheap, covers the
common availability case), a diversity-redundancy second fallback (more expensive, covers the correctness
case the first fallback cannot), and a graceful-capability-redundancy terminal fallback (cheapest per-unit,
covers the case where genuine grounded synthesis of any kind has become infeasible and a conservative,
narrower answer is the honest ceiling of what can still be offered).

17.4 Activation logic across a chain: sequential, not simultaneous. NORMATIVE: a fallback chain activates
SEQUENTIALLY — the primary's own Grade 3 trigger (per its graceful degradation contract, Part XIV) invokes
the first fallback; only if the first fallback ITSELF is unavailable or degraded past its own acceptable
grade does activation cascade to the second link, and so on. This sequential logic is what keeps a chain's
actual behavior legible and its cumulative quality gap (§17.2) meaningful as a per-depth figure rather than
an ambiguous blend — a consumer told "served by link two" knows unambiguously that link one was tried and
found unavailable, information the postmortem discipline (Part XVIII) depends on for reconstructing exactly
how deep a real incident actually reached into a prepared chain.

17.5 The chain's interaction with the failure budget. Each link in a chain carries its OWN reliability
record and, where it holds its own declared SLO, its own failure budget (Part V) — a fallback link being
invoked frequently enough to be consuming its own budget at an elevated rate is itself a signal, per Part VI
§6.4's burn-rate check applied here, that the PRIMARY is degrading more often than its own SLO would suggest
in isolation, since a healthy primary should rarely push traffic deep into its chain. NORMATIVE: a chain's
aggregate activation frequency — how often ANY fallback beyond the primary is serving traffic, regardless of
which specific link — is tracked as a standing signal distinct from any single link's own SLO, because a
rising aggregate activation frequency is often the earliest available evidence of a primary's slow
degradation, available even before Part IX's own trend detector crosses its significance bar on the
primary's direct measurements.

17.6 Worked example: a three-link chain under sustained primary degradation. A synthetic mission-critical
capability, illustrative only, maintains a three-link chain: primary M, N+1 fallback M', diversity fallback
M''. Over a stressful two-week period, M's own reliability slides per Part IX's detector, and the chain's
aggregate activation frequency climbs from a baseline of roughly 2% of invocations served by any fallback to
nearly 30%, with the majority landing at M' (the cheap N+1 tier, correctly absorbing the bulk of what
appears to be an availability-class stress on M) and a smaller but non-trivial and growing fraction reaching
M'' (the diversity tier, indicating some fraction of M's degradation is correctness-class, not merely
availability-class, since only the diversity link protects against that). This pattern — rising aggregate
activation with a growing SECOND-link share specifically — is read, per §17.5, as evidence that M's
degradation has a correctness component the primary's own SLO record (which may still show only a modest
threshold breach) understates, and it routes, per Part XVIII's postmortem discipline, as a specific,
actionable diagnostic lead: investigate M's correctness axis first, not merely its availability, because
the chain's own activation pattern has already narrowed the search.

17.7 The chain's terminal state and the honest limit of redundancy. Every chain, NORMATIVE, terminates —
either in a link judged to have a genuinely independent, sufficiently low correlated-failure probability
with everything above it (rare, and requires explicit justification per §16.5's failure-mode-match rule
applied at the terminal link specifically), or in Part XIII §13.3's explicit structured refusal. A chain
that never terminates in either — one that simply keeps adding cheaper, lower-quality links indefinitely in
the hope that SOME answer is always better than none — has, per Part XIX's failure-mode catalog, quietly
substituted an ever-degrading output for the honest refusal that a mission's actual stakes may require, and
this dataset is explicit, NORMATIVE, that a well-designed chain's terminal refusal is not a failure of the
redundancy planning — it is redundancy planning's most conservative and, for the highest-stakes
capabilities, most honest possible output.

17.8 Chain ownership across multiple owners, and the coordination hazard. Every link in a fallback chain
carries its own PRIMARY per Part XVI §16.3's fallback record, but a realistic multi-link chain frequently
spans DIFFERENT owning systems — the primary belongs to one team, the N+1 fallback is a second instance the
same team maintains, but the diversity-redundancy second fallback (Part XVI §16.4) is, by definition, a
MEANINGFULLY DIFFERENT approach, and a meaningfully different approach is often owned by a different system
entirely. NORMATIVE: a chain spanning multiple owners requires an explicit CHAIN COORDINATOR role, distinct
from any single link's own owner, whose sole responsibility is maintaining the chain's aggregate view — the
cumulative quality gap of §17.2, the aggregate activation frequency of §17.5, and the chain recovery-readiness
record of Part XV §15.8 — because no single link's owner has visibility into the chain as a whole by default,
and a chain with no coordinator risks each owner independently believing their own link is functioning
correctly while the CHAIN's aggregate behavior — the pattern §17.6's worked example depends on reading —
goes unobserved by anyone. The coordinator does not hold authority over any individual link's implementation
(that remains each link owner's exclusive authority per IAS-F1's Invariant Three), but does hold the
NORMATIVE obligation to convene link owners when a chain-level signal (rising aggregate activation, a
widening cumulative quality gap) indicates the chain's aggregate health requires attention no single link's
own SLO would surface in isolation. Worked example, synthetic and illustrative: a three-link chain whose
primary and N+1 fallback belong to Team Alpha and whose diversity fallback belongs to Team Beta experiences
the rising-aggregate-activation pattern of §17.6's worked trace. Team Alpha, monitoring only its own two
links' individual SLOs, sees each link's own aggregate reliability still within target and has no signal
prompting escalation. Team Beta, monitoring only the diversity fallback's own activation frequency, sees a
rising count but has no visibility into whether this reflects the primary's genuine degradation (Team Alpha's
concern) or the diversity fallback's own increasing popularity for unrelated reasons. Only the chain
coordinator, reading the aggregate figure across both teams' links per §17.5's tracking discipline, correctly
identifies the pattern as evidence of the primary's correctness-class degradation and convenes both teams for
the resulting investigation — a finding neither team's own siloed monitoring would have produced
independently, and the specific institutional gap the coordinator role exists to close.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. A fallback chain is not infinite insurance; each link buys protection against a
narrower slice of failure at a compounding quality cost, and a chain that never terminates in either a
genuinely independent final link or an honest structured refusal has traded the appearance of resilience
for the reality of a quietly worsening answer no consumer was told to expect.

===============================================================================================
PART XVIII — RELIABILITY POSTMORTEM: FROM FAILURE TO VERIFIED IMPROVEMENT
===============================================================================================

18.1 Why a postmortem is a distinct mechanism from every prior one. Every mechanism specified so far —
SLOs, budgets, the predictor, the detector, the ladder, chaos experiments, graceful degradation, the
redundancy planner — either MEASURES a capability's reliability or DESIGNS its response to a shortfall.
None of them, on their own, closes the loop back to VERIFIED, PROVEN improvement. The RELIABILITY
POSTMORTEM, DEFINED, is the mechanism that takes a failure, a degradation event, or a chaos-experiment
surprise as its input and produces, as its required output, not merely an explanation but a FOLLOWING
MEASUREMENT WINDOW showing the proposed fix actually raised the reliability record — the discipline this
dataset's own Strengthening Matrix entry names directly: "graceful degradation instead of hard failure" is
only a claim the estate may make once a postmortem has actually verified it, not merely proposed it.

18.2 The postmortem's four stages, distinguished from a generic incident report. STAGE ONE, RECONSTRUCTION,
assembles the full evidence trail — the SLO and budget ledger (Parts III-VI), the degradation grade history
(Part X §10.3's logged transitions), any chaos-experiment record if the event originated there (Part XII
§12.5), and the fallback chain's activation pattern if one was engaged (Part XVII §17.6) — into one coherent
timeline, never relying on any single record type alone, because Part V §5.6's worked contrast (Alpha versus
Beta, two capabilities at identical aggregate reliability with opposite root-cause stories) is precisely the
kind of distinction only a multi-source reconstruction can draw. STAGE TWO, ROOT-CAUSE ATTRIBUTION, applies
Part VI §6.3's dependency-aware attribution discipline to determine whether the failure's TRUE root sits in
the capability under investigation or propagated from a DEPENDS-ON ancestor, avoiding the double-counting
hazard that Part's mechanics already name. STAGE THREE, FIX PROPOSAL, names a specific, falsifiable change —
never merely "we will be more careful" — with an explicit PREDICTED EFFECT on the reliability record (which
axis, which SLO, by roughly how much) that the following stage can check. STAGE FOUR, VERIFICATION WINDOW,
is the stage most postmortem disciplines outside this one skip: a mandatory, dated re-measurement of the
affected capability's reliability record over a subsequent window, comparing the OBSERVED effect against
Stage Three's PREDICTED effect, with the postmortem only marked CLOSED-VERIFIED if the observed effect
actually matches — a postmortem whose fix shipped but was never re-measured remains, permanently, CLOSED-
UNVERIFIED, a status this dataset treats as materially different from CLOSED-VERIFIED and never collapses
into a generic "closed."

18.3 Why Stage Four is the postmortem's load-bearing stage. Without a mandatory verification window, a
postmortem's Stage Three fix proposal is merely a HYPOTHESIS about what would help — plausible, well-
reasoned, but unconfirmed — and an estate that treats every plausible fix as though it were a proven one is
committing exactly the false-confidence error this dataset's evidence-level discipline exists everywhere
else to prevent. (DERIVED: this is the postmortem-specific instance of Article V's general law that a
mechanism asserted without a level defaults to HYPOTHESIZED — a fix ships as a HYPOTHESIZED improvement and
only Stage Four's re-measurement can promote it to OBSERVED.) The Strengthening Matrix's evidence
requirement for this whole dataset — a graceful-degradation path DEFINED — is itself only as trustworthy as
the postmortems that verified the contracts underlying it actually closed CLOSED-VERIFIED rather than
CLOSED-UNVERIFIED.

18.4 What triggers a mandatory postmortem versus an optional one. NORMATIVE: a postmortem is MANDATORY
following any hard SLO breach (Part III §3.6), any chaos experiment resolving WIDER-THAN-PREDICTED (Part
XII §12.5), any Grade 3 or Grade 4 transition on a capability whose graceful degradation contract marks it
UNTESTED at that grade (Part XIII §13.6), and any budget exhaustion pattern repeating across successive
windows (Part VI §6.6's cross-window question, resolved here as the trigger it defers to). A postmortem is
OPTIONAL, at the owner's discretion, for a Grade 1 flag that self-resolves without crossing further grades,
or a single, isolated budget-consuming event with no repeat pattern — not every miss merits the full
four-stage discipline, and requiring one for every isolated variance would itself produce the alarm-fatigue
pathology Part IX §9.7 already names, applied here to postmortem overhead rather than to detector flags.

18.5 The recurring-root-cause pattern across postmortems. A single postmortem examines one event; a
PATTERN across postmortems — the same root cause named in Stage Two recurring across multiple, nominally
unrelated incidents, possibly on different capabilities that happen to share the same underlying dependency
— is itself a distinct, higher-value finding, DERIVED from Part V §5.6's Beta-capability pattern generalized
across the whole postmortem archive rather than one capability's single ledger. NORMATIVE: the postmortem
archive is searched, on a cadence, for exactly this cross-postmortem recurrence, and a recurring root cause
found this way routes as a HIGH-PRIORITY candidate to IAS-B1's Cognitive Multiplier (per the amplification
spine, Compounding Graph), because a fix addressing a root cause recurring across N capabilities' incidents
amplifies N capabilities' reliability simultaneously — the exact leverage IAS-B1's own object is built to
rank highly.

18.6 Verifying a dependency-propagated fix: the double-clearance test. Following Part VI §6.3's secondary-
charge attribution, a fix to a ROOT capability K that was causing propagated failures in a downstream
consumer A predicts, per Stage Three, that BOTH K's own primary charges AND A's secondary charges should
clear simultaneously in the verification window — and Stage Four's re-measurement checks BOTH ledgers, not
merely K's, because a fix that clears K's own record while A's secondary charges continue would reveal
either an incomplete root-cause attribution at Stage Two (a second, independent cause also affecting A) or
an undeclared additional dependency exactly analogous to Part XI §11.5's chaos-experiment discovery, surfaced
here through the postmortem's own double-clearance check rather than through a deliberate experiment.

18.7 Worked example: a postmortem's full four-stage trace with a downstream double-clearance. Following the
degradation-detector worked trace of Part IX §9.6 and its ladder traverse in Part X §10.6, the mandatory
postmortem opens upon the Grade 2 entry (per §18.4, since the underlying degradation contract had been
UNTESTED at Grade 2 before this event). Stage One reconstructs the timeline from the detector's flag on day
16 through the contract's auto-triggered narrowing on day 19. Stage Two attributes root cause to a specific
malformed-input handling gap in the capability itself, not a propagated dependency — a clean, non-
propagated case, simplifying the attribution. Stage Three proposes a specific input-validation fix with a
predicted effect: "correctness-within-envelope returns to baseline 97% within one window; degradation grade
returns to 0 within the fourteen-day observation period the ladder mechanics already specify." Stage Four,
opened fourteen days after the fix ships, observes correctness-within-envelope at 96.8% (within the
predicted range) and confirms the Grade 0 return exactly on the ladder's own asymmetric-caution schedule
(Part X §10.4) — the postmortem closes CLOSED-VERIFIED, and its record, including the specific input-
validation fix, is now archived as a candidate root-cause pattern per §18.5's cross-postmortem search should
a similar malformed-input gap ever surface in an unrelated capability.

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. A fix that ships without a verification window is a hypothesis wearing a
resolution's name; the postmortem's mandatory Stage Four is what converts "we believe this helped" into
"we measured that this helped," and a reliability layer that only ever proposes fixes, never re-measures
them, has built three-quarters of a learning loop and called it complete.

===============================================================================================
PART XIX — FAILURE MODES OF THE RELIABILITY LAYER ITSELF: SLO THEATER, BUDGET GAMING, CHAOS FATIGUE
===============================================================================================

19.1 Why the reliability layer must audit itself. Every mechanism in Parts III through XVIII was specified
with an implicit assumption: that the mechanism is used in good faith, tuned honestly, and read for what it
actually says. That assumption fails predictably under institutional pressure — a target missed repeatedly
creates incentive to weaken the target rather than fix the capability; a budget nearly exhausted creates
incentive to reclassify a miss rather than log it. This Part catalogs the recurring failure shapes this
dataset's own mechanisms are vulnerable to, NORMATIVE throughout, because a reliability system blind to its
own gameability is not a reliability system — it is a scoreboard someone can quietly repaint.

19.2 SLO THEATER, named directly. SLO THEATER, DEFINED, is the pattern of an SLO record (Part III §3.3)
that exists, is formally complete, and is nonetheless institutionally meaningless — most commonly a target
set so low from the outset that it is essentially unbreachable regardless of the capability's real
behavior, or an envelope defined so broadly that nearly any output qualifies as within it. The tell, per
Part III §3.4's evidence-level discipline, is an SLO that has NEVER breached across many successive windows
despite the capability's own consistency axis (Part II §2.3) showing real volatility — a genuinely well-
performing capability and a theatrically-targeted one both show zero breaches, but only the theatrical one
shows zero breaches ALONGSIDE high measured volatility on axes the target itself was careful not to cover.
PREVENTION: NORMATIVE, an SLO's envelope and target are reviewed against the capability's OWN observed
distribution at initial setting (per §3.4) rather than set to guarantee compliance, and a target that has
never once come within a stated margin of breach across an extended history is itself flagged for review
as a candidate theater instance, inverting the usual alarm logic — here, an ALWAYS-GREEN record is the
suspicious pattern, not the reassuring one.

19.3 BUDGET GAMING, named directly. BUDGET GAMING, DEFINED, is the pattern of consuming events being
reclassified, under-attributed, or simply not logged as budget-consuming when a capability's ledger is
running low, so that the ledger's remaining balance overstates true health. The specific mechanical
vulnerability, DERIVED from Part VI §6.3's attribution discipline, is the secondary-charge boundary: an
owner under pressure has an incentive to attribute every miss upward to a DEPENDS-ON ancestor as a
"secondary charge" (which per §6.3 is logged more leniently than a primary charge) even when the true root
cause sits in the capability's own logic. PREVENTION: NORMATIVE, root-cause attribution (Part XVIII's Stage
Two) is never self-certified by the capability's own owner alone for a disputed case — a pattern of
secondary-charge attribution disproportionate to the capability's actual DEPENDS-ON fan-out (a capability
attributing most of its misses upstream despite depending on very few other systems) is itself a flagged
anomaly per the same cross-postmortem search Part XVIII §18.5 already runs for recurring root causes,
repurposed here as an audit signal rather than a leverage-finding one.

19.4 ENVELOPE ABSENCE, named directly. Per Part II §2.2, a capability with no declared envelope has
UNRESOLVED reliability by definition — but ENVELOPE ABSENCE as a failure mode is specifically the pattern
of this UNRESOLVED status being allowed to persist indefinitely, quietly exempting a capability from the
entire reliability discipline rather than being treated as the urgent gap it is. PREVENTION: NORMATIVE,
IAS-E1's Observability Fabric (per the Compounding Graph's inbound edge) is the natural consumer for a
standing count of UNRESOLVED-envelope capabilities across the estate, surfaced as a coverage gap rather
than allowed to sit invisibly outside every SLO, budget, and predictor mechanism this dataset specifies —
an UNRESOLVED capability is not exempt from risk merely because it is exempt from measurement.

19.5 STALE-INPUT FORECAST CONFIDENCE, named directly. Per Part VIII §8.7, the Mission-Reliability
Predictor's forecast quality is bounded by the freshness of the parent records it reads; this specific
failure mode is the pattern of a forecast being TRUSTED at its stated confidence level despite its
underlying capability or graph features having gone stale (per IAS-F1's staleness discipline) without that
staleness being visibly propagated into the forecast's own confidence interval. PREVENTION: NORMATIVE, the
predictor's output record carries the STALENESS of its most-stale input feature as a first-class field
alongside its point estimate and interval, so a consumer reading "93% confident, moderate interval" can
also see "based in part on a capability-feature record last reconciled 47 days ago" and discount
accordingly, rather than the staleness silently vanishing into an interval computed as though every input
were fresh.

19.6 RETROACTIVE BUDGET CHARGING, named directly. Already named at the mechanics level in Part VI §6.2, this
failure mode recurs here as an institutional pattern worth flagging independently of its specific mechanic:
the temptation, when a capability's reliability history looks bad in retrospect, to apply a NEWLY tightened
SLO or budget rule backward across a window that predates the rule, manufacturing an appearance of chronic
breach that did not exist under the rules actually in force at the time. PREVENTION: NORMATIVE, every SLO
and budget record's EFFECTIVE-FROM date is immutable once set, and any historical report comparing periods
across an SLO revision explicitly labels which rule version governed which period rather than silently
applying the current rule to historical data.

19.7 DEGRADATION ALARM FATIGUE, named directly. Already introduced at the mechanics level in Part IX §9.7,
this failure mode's institutional shape is the pattern of an owner, having been burned by enough false-
positive DEGRADING flags, beginning to systematically dismiss or delay-review every subsequent flag
regardless of its actual significance — at which point the detector's entire lead-time value (Part IX
§9.6) is lost not because the detector stopped working but because its output stopped being trusted.
PREVENTION: NORMATIVE, the detector's own false-positive rate (Part IX §9.7) is tracked and, when it
exceeds a stated threshold for a given capability's tuning, triggers a mandatory re-tuning of that
capability's significance bar rather than leaving the owner to silently absorb the cost of over-alarming
through learned distrust.

19.8 UNDERSPECIFIED FALLBACK, named directly. Already introduced in Part XIV §14.4, this failure mode's
recurring institutional shape is a graceful degradation contract's Grade 3 entry naming "invoke fallback"
without a corresponding, cross-referenced fallback record (Part XVI §16.3) — a contract that looks complete
on inspection of its own fields but is, in fact, deferring the actual design decision to the moment of
failure, precisely the outcome graceful degradation exists to prevent (Part XIII §13.2). PREVENTION:
NORMATIVE, the contract completeness review (Part XIV §14.3) treats a Grade 3 entry with no linked fallback
record as an automatic INCOMPLETE finding, never a passing one, regardless of how well-specified the
contract's other fields are.

19.9 FALSE REDUNDANCY, named directly. Already introduced in Part XVI §16.5, this failure mode's
institutional shape is an estate that believes itself protected because a fallback record exists, without
ever checking whether the fallback's TIER (Part XVI §16.4) actually matches the primary's dominant failure
mode — an N+1 fallback maintained against a capability whose real risk is correctness-class provides an
appearance of resilience that a correctness failure will pass straight through, unprotected, at precisely
the moment protection was assumed to exist. PREVENTION: NORMATIVE, the failure-mode-match rule (§16.5) is
re-checked at the SAME cadence as the graceful degradation contract's staleness re-review (Part XIV §14.5),
because a capability's dominant failure mode can shift over its lifetime even when its fallback tier was
correctly matched at original design time.

19.10 CHAOS FATIGUE, named directly. The institutional cost of Part XI's chaos-engineering doctrine, left
unmanaged, is CHAOS FATIGUE: capability owners, having consented to and absorbed several experiments,
begin treating consent requests (Rule Four, Part XI §11.3) as a formality to be rubber-stamped rather than
a genuine review, eroding the governance discipline the whole doctrine depends on. PREVENTION: NORMATIVE,
the candidate-ranking logic (Part XI §11.6) explicitly weighs an owner's recent experiment load as a cost
factor lowering a candidate's priority, spreading experimental burden across the estate's capabilities
rather than concentrating it on the same willing, cooperative owners repeatedly, and an owner's consent
review time is tracked as a coarse proxy for whether review has become genuinely perfunctory.

FINAL LAW — PART XIX. Every mechanism this dataset specifies has a corresponding way to be gamed, faked, or
worn down by fatigue — a target set to never breach, a charge attributed away from its true cause, an
undeclared gap left unmeasured, a stale input trusted as fresh, a rule applied backward, a real warning
ignored after enough false ones, a fallback that exists in name only, a tier that protects against the
wrong failure, and a consent process rubber-stamped into meaninglessness — and a reliability layer that has
not named its own nine ways to fail has only half specified itself.

PART XX — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

20.1 Why metrics need their own Part beyond the mechanisms that produce them. Parts III through XVIII each
produce records — SLOs, budgets, forecasts, grades, contracts, fallback chains, postmortems — but an
institution reasoning about its OWN reliability discipline, at the ensemble level, needs a small set of
ESTATE-WIDE metrics distinct from any single capability's own record, the same way IAS-F2 (Part I §1.1 of
that dataset) established a shared unit above individual system scores. This Part specifies those metrics
and, per this family's own anti-Goodhart discipline (imported from IAS-F2 Part X by reference), a
countermetric for each, because a metric optimized in isolation is a metric eventually gamed, and Part
XIX's catalog already demonstrates exactly how each of this dataset's own mechanisms can be.

20.2 SLO COVERAGE and its countermetric. SLO COVERAGE, DEFINED, is the fraction of the estate's capabilities
carrying an ACTIVE, non-EXEMPT, non-UNRESOLVED SLO record (Parts II-III). Raw coverage alone is gameable by
exactly the ENVELOPE ABSENCE-adjacent move of declaring trivial, unfalsifiable SLOs merely to raise the
coverage count without raising real accountability — the countermetric, MEAN SLO AMBITION, DEFINED as the
average gap between each active SLO's target and the capability's own historical baseline at time of
setting, catches a coverage push achieved by setting targets so close to guaranteed compliance that they add
no real discipline; a rising coverage figure paired with a falling ambition figure is the specific signature
of gamed coverage, and the two are reported together, never coverage alone.

20.3 BUDGET HEALTH INDEX and its countermetric. BUDGET HEALTH INDEX, DEFINED, is the estate-wide fraction
of active failure-budget ledgers (Part V-VI) with a sustainable burn rate and no pre-exhaustion escalation
in the current window. Raw health-index optimization is gameable via BUDGET GAMING (Part XIX §19.3) —
under-attributing consuming events to keep the index high without the underlying capabilities actually
being healthier. The countermetric, SECONDARY-CHARGE RATIO, DEFINED as the estate-wide fraction of
consuming events attributed as secondary rather than primary charges, is tracked against each capability's
own DEPENDS-ON fan-out (per §19.3's prevention rule); a rising health index paired with a disproportionately
rising secondary-charge ratio is the specific signature of attribution gaming rather than genuine
improvement.

20.4 DEGRADATION LEAD TIME and its countermetric. DEGRADATION LEAD TIME, DEFINED, is the average interval,
across confirmed degradation events, between the detector's DEGRADING flag (Part IX) and the point at which
the aggregate SLO window would independently have shown breach — the direct measure of the detector's
institutional value per Part IX §9.6's worked claim. Raw lead-time optimization is gameable by loosening the
detector's significance bar (Part IX §9.3) to flag earlier and more often, trading lead time for a higher
false-positive rate. The countermetric, DETECTOR PRECISION, DEFINED as the fraction of DEGRADING flags that
were later confirmed as genuine (versus reversed-before-escalation near-misses per Part IX §9.3, or outright
false positives per Part IX §9.7), is reported alongside lead time; a rising lead-time figure paired with a
falling precision figure is the signature of a loosened bar bought at the cost of trustworthiness, exactly
the DEGRADATION ALARM FATIGUE precursor Part XIX §19.7 names.

20.5 GRACEFUL-DEGRADATION COVERAGE and its countermetric. GRACEFUL-DEGRADATION COVERAGE, DEFINED, is the
estate-wide fraction of capabilities at Grade 2 or 3 (per Part X) operating under a CONFIRMED (not merely
UNTESTED) graceful degradation contract (Part XIV) at the moment of transition. Raw coverage optimization is
gameable by UNDERSPECIFIED FALLBACK (Part XIX §19.8) — contracts that pass a shallow completeness check
without their fallback records actually being sound. The countermetric, FALLBACK TIER MATCH RATE, DEFINED
as the fraction of active fallback records whose tier (Part XVI §16.4) has been verified, per §19.9's
re-check cadence, to actually match its primary's dominant failure mode, is reported alongside coverage; a
rising coverage figure paired with a falling match rate is the signature of FALSE REDUNDANCY accumulating
under the coverage number's shadow.

20.6 POSTMORTEM VERIFICATION RATE and its countermetric. POSTMORTEM VERIFICATION RATE, DEFINED, is the
fraction of closed postmortems (Part XVIII) that closed CLOSED-VERIFIED rather than CLOSED-UNVERIFIED — the
single most direct measure of whether this dataset's reliability-postmortem discipline is producing PROVEN
improvement rather than merely plausible fixes, per Part XVIII §18.3's load-bearing argument. This metric is
comparatively resistant to direct gaming (a postmortem cannot be marked CLOSED-VERIFIED without an actual
re-measurement showing the predicted effect), but it carries an indirect vulnerability: an owner under
pressure to raise the verification rate may propose only SAFE, easily-confirmed fixes at Stage Three (Part
XVIII §18.2) while avoiding harder, higher-leverage but riskier-to-verify fixes. The countermetric, FIX
AMBITION, DEFINED as the average magnitude of predicted effect across Stage Three proposals (a large
predicted jump in reliability versus a marginal one), is reported alongside the verification rate for the
same reason SLO AMBITION pairs with coverage in §20.2 — a high verification rate achieved only by proposing
trivially-confirmable fixes is not the institutional learning this dataset exists to produce.

20.7 CHAOS EXPERIMENT YIELD and its countermetric. CHAOS EXPERIMENT YIELD, DEFINED, is the fraction of
completed chaos experiments (Part XII) that surfaced a genuinely new finding — an undeclared dependency
(Part XI §11.5), a contract confirmed for the first time (Part XIII §13.6), or a recovery-readiness estimate
refreshed with fresh evidence (Part XV §15.5) — versus experiments that merely confirmed an already well-
understood behavior with no new information gained. Raw yield optimization is gameable by preferentially
selecting easy, high-yield-looking candidates and avoiding harder, more uncertain ones where a CONFIRMED
result (no surprise) is a more likely outcome, precisely inverting the candidate-ranking logic Part XI §11.6
intends. The countermetric, CANDIDATE COVERAGE BREADTH, DEFINED as the fraction of the estate's ELIGIBLE
capabilities (per Part XI §11.4) that have received at least one experiment within a rolling period,
prevents yield-chasing from concentrating all experimental effort on a small, reliably-surprising subset of
the estate while leaving the rest permanently unexercised.

20.8 Reporting cadence and the dashboard-design discipline. Parts 20.2 through 20.7 define WHAT is measured;
this section specifies, NORMATIVE, HOW OFTEN and in WHAT FORM the six metric-countermetric pairs reach an
Owner's attention, because a metric computed correctly but surfaced too rarely, too late, or too flatly
provides little more institutional value than a metric never computed at all. CADENCE: each pair is computed
on a rolling basis aligned to the shortest window among its own inputs — SLO COVERAGE and MEAN SLO AMBITION
(§20.2) recompute at every SLO record change, since coverage is a point-in-time fact; DEGRADATION LEAD TIME
and DETECTOR PRECISION (§20.4) recompute only as confirmed degradation events accumulate, since a lead-time
figure computed from a single new event is too thin a sample to move the estate-wide average meaningfully.
FORM: NORMATIVE, every pair is reported as a PAIRED TIME SERIES, never as two independent single-point
numbers on separate views, because the diagnostic signature Part XX's own closing law names — a rising
primary metric paired with a falling countermetric — is only visible when both are plotted against the same
time axis; a dashboard that reports SLO COVERAGE on one page and MEAN SLO AMBITION on another, each looking
healthy in isolation, reproduces exactly the failure Part XIX's catalog warns against, merely relocated from
the metric layer to the dashboard layer. THRESHOLD ALERTING: a pair's DIVERGENCE — the primary metric rising
while its countermetric falls beyond a stated tolerance band in the same window — is itself, NORMATIVE, a
distinct alertable condition routed to the estate's governance review, independent of either metric's own
absolute value, because a primary metric that looks good in isolation while its countermetric is actively
degrading is precisely the gaming signature the countermetric exists to catch, and a dashboard that only
alerts on an absolute threshold crossing (coverage below 80%, say) while ignoring the divergence pattern
misses the earliest and most diagnostic warning the pairing was designed to produce. Worked example, synthetic
and illustrative: over a six-month rolling window, GRACEFUL-DEGRADATION COVERAGE (§20.5) climbs steadily from
40% to 72% — an apparently healthy trend an isolated dashboard would celebrate — while FALLBACK TIER MATCH
RATE, plotted on the same time axis, falls from 85% to 61% across the identical window. Neither number alone
crosses an alarming absolute threshold (72% coverage still looks reasonable; 61% match rate is degraded but
not catastrophic), but the DIVERGENCE between a rising primary and a falling countermetric, visible only in
the paired time series, is flagged automatically per this section's threshold-alerting rule and routes to
governance review before either individual number would have triggered a standalone alert — the concrete
mechanism by which Part XX's closing law is enforced operationally rather than left as a reporting aspiration.

FINAL LAW — PART XX. Every metric this dataset reports is paired with a countermetric that catches the
specific way the metric alone can be gamed — coverage against ambition, health against attribution honesty,
lead time against precision, degradation coverage against tier match, verification rate against fix
ambition, chaos yield against candidate breadth — and a dashboard reporting only the first metric of each
pair is reporting exactly the number most likely to look good while the underlying discipline quietly
erodes.

PART XXI — EVALUATION PROTOCOL: PROVING THE RELIABILITY LAYER WORKS
===============================================================================================

21.1 The reflexivity requirement. Per IAS-F2 Part XIX's precedent — an algebra that will not compute its own
score has not earned trust in the scores it computes for others — this dataset's own claims of institutional
value (faster degradation detection, better-forecast missions, proven-not-merely-proposed fixes) must be
checkable against the estate's real history, not merely asserted as design intent. This Part specifies the
evaluation protocol, NORMATIVE, that this dataset's implementation is checked against before any claim of
"IAS-E2 is working" may be stated as fact rather than as a still-HYPOTHESIZED design.

21.2 Benchmark One — retrodictive detector lead time. Per Part IX §9.6's worked claim, the degradation
detector's core value proposition is measurable lead time over the aggregate SLO's own breach detection.
This benchmark, EXPERIMENTAL, runs the detector's trend-test logic RETROACTIVELY against the estate's own
historical reliability records (where such records exist), for every historical degradation episode already
identified by other means (a postmortem, a known incident), and reports the DISTRIBUTION of lead times the
detector WOULD HAVE produced had it been running at the time, alongside Part XX §20.4's detector-precision
countermetric computed over the same retrodictive set. A distribution showing consistently near-zero or
negative lead time (the detector would have flagged no earlier than the SLO itself, or later) falsifies the
detector's core value claim and is reported honestly as such, not smoothed over.

21.3 Benchmark Two — predictor calibration. Per Part VII §7.7's reflexivity requirement, the
Mission-Reliability Predictor's forecasts are checked, EXPERIMENTAL, against actual mission outcomes over an
accumulating sample: for missions where a forecast of "X% likely reliable" was produced, what fraction of
those missions actually stayed within envelope. A well-calibrated predictor's forecasts should track
observed outcomes closely across the full range of stated confidence — missions forecast at 90% confidence
should succeed at approximately 90%, not systematically higher (an overly pessimistic predictor whose
excess caution has its own cost, per Part XX's ambition-pairing logic applied here) or systematically lower
(an overly optimistic predictor providing false reassurance). This benchmark's output is a calibration curve,
not a single pass/fail verdict, because a predictor can be well-calibrated at some confidence bands and
poorly calibrated at others, and only the full curve reveals which.

21.4 Benchmark Three — the graceful-degradation confirmation rate. Per Part XIV §14.7's identification of
the degradation contract as this dataset's clearest translation candidate, this benchmark tracks, across the
estate, what fraction of authored contracts have moved from UNTESTED to CONFIRMED (per Part XIII §13.6)
within a stated period after authorship — a contract that sits UNTESTED indefinitely provides none of the
value Part XIII claims for designed degradation, since an unexercised design is, per this whole dataset's
epistemic discipline, indistinguishable in evidentiary weight from no design at all. A low confirmation rate
falsifies the claim that "graceful degradation instead of hard failure" (the Strengthening Matrix's stated
expected gain) is actually being delivered rather than merely documented.

21.5 Benchmark Four — postmortem verification completeness. Directly consuming Part XX §20.6's metric, this
benchmark checks not merely the CLOSED-VERIFIED rate among CLOSED postmortems, but the fraction of
postmortems that never reach Stage Four at all — opened, Stage Three fix proposed, and then abandoned
without a verification window ever being scheduled or run. A high abandonment rate before Stage Four is a
distinct and, per Part XVIII §18.3, arguably more damaging failure than a low verification rate among those
that DO reach Stage Four, because it represents the postmortem discipline being adopted only through its
easier stages while its single load-bearing stage is systematically skipped.

21.6 Benchmark Five — false-positive and false-negative cost accounting. Per Part IX §9.7 and Part XIX's
catalog generally, every detection mechanism in this dataset (the degradation detector, the chaos
eligibility check, the recovery-readiness circularity check) carries both a false-positive and a
false-negative failure mode, and this benchmark requires BOTH rates to be reported for every such mechanism,
never only the true-positive success stories a dataset is naturally inclined to showcase — an evaluation
report that lists only "the detector caught N real slides" without also stating its false-positive count and
its estimated false-negative rate (via retrodiction per Benchmark One against episodes the detector did NOT
catch) is an incomplete evaluation regardless of how impressive the true-positive count looks alone.

21.7 The evaluation protocol's own honesty requirement. NORMATIVE, and closing the reflexive loop this Part
opened: the evaluation protocol itself is subject to the same evidence-level discipline as every claim this
dataset makes — a benchmark run once, on a small or favorable sample, produces a HYPOTHESIZED verdict on this
dataset's own effectiveness, not an OBSERVED one, and this dataset explicitly declines to claim OBSERVED
institutional value for itself until each of the five benchmarks has been run against a real, accumulated
estate history large enough to support the statistical confidence Part III §3.3's own SLO records require of
every OTHER claim in this corpus. This dataset does not exempt itself from the standard it sets for the
seventy-plus systems it governs.

21.8 Benchmark execution cadence and the sample-size gate. NORMATIVE, none of the five benchmarks specified
in §21.2 through §21.6 are run on a fixed calendar independent of the underlying data they consume — each is
gated, instead, by a stated MINIMUM SAMPLE SIZE below which a benchmark's output is reported as INSUFFICIENT
DATA rather than as a verdict, because a calibration curve (Benchmark Two) computed over four missions, or a
confirmation-rate figure (Benchmark Three) computed over three contracts, carries statistical noise wide
enough to make the reported number actively misleading rather than merely imprecise. The minimum sample size
differs per benchmark, DERIVED from each one's own statistical shape: Benchmark One's retrodictive lead-time
distribution requires enough historical degradation episodes (a double-digit count, at minimum, per Part III
§3.3's own confidence-reporting convention) to report a distribution rather than a handful of anecdotes;
Benchmark Two's calibration curve requires enough missions AT EACH CONFIDENCE BAND to bin meaningfully, so a
predictor that has only ever forecast in the 90-95% range cannot yet be calibration-checked at the 60-70%
band regardless of how many total missions have run; Benchmark Four's abandonment-rate figure requires enough
opened postmortems to distinguish a genuine abandonment pattern from a handful of postmortems still
legitimately mid-window and not yet due for Stage Four. PREVENTION, NORMATIVE, of a specific gaming
temptation this gate itself creates: an owner under pressure to produce a favorable benchmark result before
the true minimum sample has accumulated might be tempted to lower the stated minimum until the current data
happens to clear it — the same INSUFFICIENT-DATA-avoidance move Part XIX's catalog would recognize as a cousin
of SLO THEATER (§19.2) if applied to a target rather than to a benchmark gate. The minimum sample size for
each benchmark is therefore itself an EFFECTIVE-FROM-dated, immutable-once-set parameter subject to the
identical RETROACTIVE-CHARGING protection Part XIX §19.6 specifies for SLO and budget rules, so that a
minimum cannot be quietly lowered after the fact to manufacture a passing report.

21.9 Worked example: a benchmark suite's honest first run against a young estate. Consider a synthetic
snapshot, illustrative only, of this dataset's evaluation protocol run for the first time against six months
of accumulated estate history. Benchmark One (detector lead time) clears its sample-size gate with fourteen
confirmed historical degradation episodes and reports a median lead time of five days at a detector precision
of 78% — a genuinely OBSERVED, reportable result. Benchmark Two (predictor calibration) clears its gate only
for the 85-95% confidence band, where enough missions have accumulated to bin meaningfully, and reports
INSUFFICIENT DATA for every band below 85%, because the estate's early missions were disproportionately
high-confidence, low-novelty ones — an honest gap in coverage, not a fabricated calibration curve padded to
look complete. Benchmark Three (contract confirmation rate) reports 34% of authored contracts CONFIRMED
within the stated period, a genuinely unflattering number that the dataset's own evaluation discipline
requires be reported exactly as measured rather than rounded generously or contextualized away. Benchmark
Four (postmortem abandonment) clears its gate and reports a 22% pre-Stage-Four abandonment rate, flagged per
§21.5 as the more damaging of the two postmortem failure shapes despite the CLOSED-VERIFIED rate among
postmortems that DO reach Stage Four looking comparatively healthy at 81%. Benchmark Five reports the
detector's false-positive rate at 15% and an estimated false-negative rate, via Benchmark One's retrodictive
method applied to episodes the detector missed, at approximately 20% — both numbers published alongside the
true-positive figures rather than the true-positive count alone. Read together, this snapshot is neither a
triumphant nor a damning report — it is an honest, sample-size-gated, partially-INSUFFICIENT-DATA picture of
a young reliability layer, exactly the HYPOTHESIZED-not-yet-OBSERVED status §21.7 requires this dataset to
claim about itself at this stage, and exactly the kind of unflattering-where-warranted report Part XIX's own
failure-mode catalog exists to make possible rather than to paper over.

FINAL LAW — PART XXI. A reliability layer that has not retrodicted its own detector's lead time, calibrated
its own predictor's confidence, measured its own contracts' confirmation rate, tracked its own postmortems'
abandonment before verification, and reported its own false-positive and false-negative costs honestly has
not proven itself reliable — it has only asserted that it is, which is precisely the unverified-fix pattern
Part XVIII exists to forbid, applied here to the dataset's own claim about itself.

PART XXII — INTEGRATION: THE DEFENSE LOOP, D2 IMMUNE SYSTEM, F3 DIGITAL TWIN, ESTATE EVOLUTION
===============================================================================================

22.1 IAS-E2's position in the Compounding Graph's defense loop. Per the Compounding Graph's explicit
statement: "IAS-A2 Control Bus carries failure events → IAS-D2 Immune System fingerprints + federates them
cross-project → IAS-E2 Cognitive Reliability turns recurring failures into SLOs + failure budgets → IAS-F3
Digital Twin simulates the failure before it recurs → IAS-D2 immunizes exposed projects preventively → fewer
recurrences → IAS-E1 observes lower failure flow → capacity freed for IAS-C1." This Part specifies the two
integration seams named explicitly in this dataset's own brief — inbound from IAS-D2, outbound to IAS-F3 —
in the same procedural detail every other mechanism in this dataset has received, because a compounding
edge asserted only at the Compounding Graph's summary level, without a specified interface, is exactly the
kind of undocumented coupling Part XVI's redundancy planner already warns against for a different relation
type.

22.2 The inbound seam: IAS-D2's failure fingerprints become IAS-E2's SLO and budget inputs. IAS-D2, per the
Strengthening Matrix, federates failure fingerprints across projects — a recurring failure SHAPE (not merely
one instance) that has been observed independently in multiple projects. NORMATIVE: when IAS-D2 identifies a
fingerprint matching a capability this dataset already tracks, that fingerprint is consumed as a direct input
to two mechanisms specifically: it informs the capability's SLO envelope definition (Part III §3.3) if the
fingerprint reveals an input class the envelope did not previously account for, and it informs the failure
budget's expected consumption baseline (Part V §5.3) if the fingerprint indicates the capability's true
long-run failure rate differs from what a shorter, single-project observation window alone would suggest.
This is the specific mechanism by which "IAS-E2 turns recurring failures into SLOs and failure budgets," per
the Compounding Graph's own language: IAS-D2 supplies the CROSS-PROJECT PATTERN, and IAS-E2 supplies the
TARGET-AND-BUDGET FORMALISM the pattern is converted into, neither system duplicating the other's mechanism.

22.3 The outbound seam: IAS-E2's findings become IAS-F3's simulation targets. Three of this dataset's own
outputs are, NORMATIVE, prioritized simulation candidates for IAS-F3's Digital Twin, closing the defense loop
in the direction the Compounding Graph names: a capability whose Mission-Reliability Predictor (Part VII)
forecast carries an unusually wide confidence interval is a candidate for simulation specifically because a
wide interval indicates thin real-world coverage a simulated run can supplement without spending real
failure budget; a graceful degradation contract marked UNTESTED (Part XIII §13.6) at a grade the capability
has never organically reached is a candidate for simulated exercise as a lower-cost alternative or precursor
to a full chaos experiment (Part XI-XII) against a live capability; and a recurring root-cause pattern
surfaced by Part XVIII §18.5's cross-postmortem search is a candidate for IAS-F3 to simulate PREVENTIVELY
across every OTHER capability sharing the same dependency, testing whether the pattern would recur elsewhere
before it actually does, precisely the "simulates the failure before it recurs" language the Compounding
Graph uses to describe this exact edge.

22.4 Why the defense loop tightens rather than merely repeats. The loop's institutional value, per the
Compounding Graph's own closing claim ("fewer recurrences → IAS-E1 observes lower failure flow → capacity
freed for IAS-C1"), depends on each traversal of the loop producing a MEASURABLY better starting condition
for the next traversal, not merely repeating the same cycle at constant quality. This dataset's own
contribution to that tightening is concrete and traceable: each CLOSED-VERIFIED postmortem (Part XVIII)
raises a capability's demonstrated reliability baseline, which lowers that capability's contribution to
future Mission-Reliability Predictor uncertainty (Part VII §7.4's novelty-driven interval widening shrinks
as precedent accumulates), which in turn lowers the rate at which IAS-F3 needs to flag that specific
capability as a high-priority simulation candidate per §22.3 — a genuine, mechanism-level tightening, not
merely an assertion that the loop "should" improve over time.

22.5 Estate-wide evolution: how this dataset's own mechanisms mature. Per Article IX's governed-self-
evolution boundary, none of this dataset's mechanisms are permitted to self-modify the estate autonomously;
every mechanism specified across Parts III-XVIII proposes and surfaces, and Part XIX's failure-mode catalog
exists precisely to keep that proposing honest. The dataset's own EVOLUTION, over time, is expected —
DERIVED from Part XX §20.1's admission that estate-wide metrics are themselves subject to review — to take
the shape of periodic re-calibration events: the detector's significance bars (Part IX §9.3), the redundancy
tier-matching cadence (Part XVI §16.5), the chaos candidate-ranking weights (Part XI §11.6), and the
predictor's own feature weighting (Part VIII §8.6) are all, individually, owner-tunable parameters this
dataset specifies but does not fix permanently, and their re-tuning is itself an OWNER-authorized action per
every prior Part's authority-preserving discipline, never an autonomous adjustment this dataset's own
mechanisms perform on themselves.

22.6 Open questions this dataset does not resolve. Three questions, UNRESOLVED, are named honestly rather
than papered over. First, whether an estate-wide, cross-capability failure-budget POOL — allowing one
capability's unused budget headroom to be temporarily reallocated to a sibling capability under acute stress
— is a healthy extension of this dataset's economics (Part V) or a dangerous erosion of each capability's own
accountability for its own reliability; this dataset takes no position and flags the question for IAS-C1's
Capability Portfolio to weigh alongside its own allocation logic. Second, whether the degradation ladder's
five grades (Part X §10.7) remain the right granularity as the estate's capability count grows well beyond
its current OBSERVED seventy-plus, or whether a larger, more heterogeneous estate eventually needs a richer
ladder this dataset's own argument against over-granularity (Part X §10.7) would then need to revisit.
Third, whether chaos experiments should ever be permitted to target combinations of capabilities
simultaneously (multi-capability blast radii) rather than the single-primary-target model this dataset
specifies throughout Parts XI-XII, a genuinely harder governance problem this dataset defers rather than
under-specifies a partial answer to.

22.7 The closing integration statement. IAS-E2 receives cross-project failure patterns from IAS-D2 and
converts them into the estate's shared vocabulary of targets, budgets, and designed degraded states; it
sends its own highest-uncertainty forecasts, untested contracts, and recurring root causes onward to IAS-F3
for preventive simulation; and every one of its internal mechanisms — SLOs, budgets, the predictor, the
detector, the ladder, chaos experiments, graceful degradation, the redundancy planner, the postmortem — is
built, per Part I's founding boundary, on the explicit premise that the five parent systems it strengthens
already own the moment of failure itself, so that this dataset's entire institutional contribution sits
strictly BEFORE that moment: in the budget an owner is given to spend, the trend an owner is warned about
early, the response an owner designed in advance, the experiment an owner consented to run, and the fix an
owner is required to actually verify rather than merely believe.

FINAL LAW — PART XXII. The defense loop does not close at IAS-E2; it passes through it — cross-project
patterns arrive from the immune system, formalized targets and budgets and designed degradation states leave
toward the digital twin, and the loop's entire claim to tightening rather than merely repeating rests on
every postmortem this dataset runs actually reaching, and passing, its own mandatory Stage Four.

DECISION LOG
===============================================================================================

DL-1. Decision: define reliability as a probability over a DECLARED ACCEPTABILITY ENVELOPE (Part II §2.2)
rather than importing the classical single-correct-value model unmodified. Alternatives considered: (a)
adopt the classical deterministic-service SLO model as-is, treating every capability's output as either
correct or incorrect; (b) define reliability purely in terms of availability, deferring correctness
entirely to whichever downstream system consumes the output. Why this: alternative (a) is falsified
immediately by any capability with a genuinely wide space of acceptable outputs (Part II §2.2's synthesis
example), and alternative (b) would leave the two axes most distinctive to cognitive capabilities —
correctness-within-envelope and consistency — completely ungoverned, reproducing the exact gap Part I exists
to close. Trade-offs: an envelope-based model requires every capability owner to do real definitional work
(declaring an envelope) before any SLO can be set, a real up-front cost. Reversal conditions: if, in
practice, the estate's capabilities turn out to be overwhelmingly narrow-envelope (close to the classical
single-correct-value case), the added generality of Part II's four-axis model may prove to be more
apparatus than the observed capability mix actually needs, and a simplified two-axis model (availability
plus a single correctness check) could be revisited.

DL-2. Decision: separate the failure BUDGET (Part V-VI) from the SLO TARGET (Part III-IV) as two distinct
records rather than treating budget consumption as a derived, implicit quantity computed only when needed.
Alternatives considered: (a) compute budget consumption on demand from the raw invocation log whenever a
budget question arises, with no standing ledger; (b) fold budget tracking directly into the SLO record
itself as a running field rather than a separate object. Why this: alternative (a) would make Part V §5.6's
worked Alpha-versus-Beta distinction (same aggregate reliability, opposite budget stories) impossible to
compute cheaply, since the diagnostic value specifically requires a standing, attributed ledger rather than
a raw log re-scanned each time; alternative (b) would conflate two records with different lifecycles (an
SLO target changes rarely, a budget ledger changes with every invocation) and different consumers (Part
VII's predictor reads SLO records structurally but budget ledgers for burn-rate signals specifically).
Trade-offs: maintaining two linked records instead of one is more implementation surface. Reversal
conditions: if a future implementation finds the SLO-budget linkage is never actually exercised
independently (every consumer that reads one always reads the other), the separation could be collapsed
without losing the diagnostic value, provided the attribution discipline of Part VI §6.3 is preserved.

DL-3. Decision: require chaos experiments to be OWNER-CONSENTED per experiment (Part XI §11.3, Rule Four)
rather than pre-authorized as a blanket estate-wide policy once, or authorized centrally by IAS-E2 itself.
Alternatives considered: (a) a single estate-wide chaos policy, approved once, authorizing any future
experiment meeting bounded-blast-radius criteria without per-instance owner review; (b) centralized
authorization by IAS-E2 alone, treating chaos engineering as this dataset's own final-authority decision
class. Why this: alternative (a) risks exactly the CHAOS FATIGUE erosion Part XIX §19.10 catalogs, but in
reverse — a blanket policy removes the review step fatigue would otherwise erode, producing the same
under-scrutinized outcome from a different direction; alternative (b) would grant IAS-E2 final authority over
a decision class it does not hold per IAS-F1's Invariant Three, since authority over whether a capability is
perturbed belongs to that capability's own owning system. Trade-offs: per-experiment consent is slower and
creates real friction, which is precisely what Part XI §11.6's candidate-ranking logic exists to spend
efficiently rather than accept as a reason to skip consent. Reversal conditions: none stated — this decision
is treated as load-bearing for the entire chaos doctrine's legitimacy and is not proposed as revisable absent
a fundamental rethink of Article IX itself.

DL-4. Decision: make the reliability postmortem's Stage Four (Part XVIII §18.2) VERIFICATION WINDOW
mandatory for CLOSED-VERIFIED status, with CLOSED-UNVERIFIED as a distinct, permanently visible terminal
state rather than allowing postmortems to simply close upon a shipped fix. Alternatives considered: (a) treat
a postmortem as closed once Stage Three's fix ships, with verification as an optional follow-up; (b) require
verification but silently relabel an unverified postmortem as closed without distinguishing it from a
verified one in the record. Why this: alternative (a) reproduces exactly the "we believe this helped" pattern
Part XVIII §18.3 identifies as the core failure this mechanism exists to prevent; alternative (b) hides the
distinction downstream consumers (IAS-B1's leverage ranking, Part XVIII §18.5's cross-postmortem search) need
to weight a CLOSED-VERIFIED finding more heavily than an unconfirmed one. Trade-offs: mandatory verification
windows add latency (a postmortem cannot be considered fully resolved until an additional measurement period
elapses) and create a visible metric (Part XX §20.6) that can look unflattering if verification rates are
low. Reversal conditions: if Benchmark Four (Part XXI §21.5) repeatedly shows near-universal abandonment
before Stage Four across a mature estate, the mandatory requirement may need a lighter-weight verification
option for low-stakes postmortems rather than abandoning the requirement outright.

DL-5. Decision: define the degradation ladder at exactly five grades (Part X §10.1-10.2, 10.7) rather than a
continuous percentage or a larger discrete set. Alternatives considered: (a) a continuous 0-100 reliability
percentage as the sole state representation, with no discrete grades at all; (b) a ten-or-more-grade ladder
offering finer distinctions between states. Why this: alternative (a) provides no natural mapping to
DISTINCT OPERATIONAL RESPONSES (Part X §10.7's core argument) — a percentage alone does not tell a consuming
system what to DO, only how to feel about the number; alternative (b) was rejected on the same reasoning
IAS-F1 Part II §2.5 applied to its own primitive object count — more grades produce more disagreement about
boundary placement than they produce useful signal, without a corresponding gain in actionable distinctness.
Trade-offs: five grades may be too coarse for an unusually large or heterogeneous future estate (Part XXII
§22.6's second open question). Reversal conditions: explicitly named in Part XXII §22.6 — if the estate's
capability count and diversity grow substantially beyond current OBSERVED levels, this decision is the first
candidate for revisiting.

DL-6. Decision: attribute a propagated failure's budget consumption as a PRIMARY charge to the root capability
and a distinctly-tagged SECONDARY charge to the dependent (Part VI §6.3), rather than charging only the root,
only the dependent, or splitting the charge proportionally between them. Alternatives considered: (a) charge
only the root capability, leaving the dependent's ledger untouched by a propagated failure; (b) charge only
the dependent capability, since it is the dependent's invocation that actually missed its envelope; (c) split
the charge by some proportional formula between root and dependent. Why this: alternative (a) would make a
dependent's own ledger blind to real reliability impact it actually experienced, undermining Part VII's
predictor which needs the dependent's own record to forecast future missions through it; alternative (b)
would falsely indict the dependent's own logic for a defect that is not its own, the exact BUDGET GAMING
inversion Part XIX §19.3 warns against; alternative (c), a proportional split, was rejected as introducing an
arbitrary weighting formula with no principled basis for the split ratio, adding false precision the way
IAS-F2 Part XVI warns against generally. Trade-offs: the dual primary-secondary attribution requires every
consumer of a ledger to understand and correctly interpret the tag, adding a small comprehension burden.
Reversal conditions: none currently proposed; this is treated as a settled mechanic load-bearing for Part
XVIII §18.6's double-clearance verification test.

===============================================================================================
GAP META-ANALYSIS
===============================================================================================

Gap closed. Before this dataset, Claude Power Pack's reliability posture was entirely reactive: five parent
systems (zero-crash, rollback, backup, session_resilience, cascade_prevention) each respond WHEN a failure
has already occurred or is actively occurring, and none of them, individually or together, can answer how
much failure a capability is expected to tolerate, what that capability should do at reduced function rather
than full function or none, whether a proposed fix actually raised reliability rather than merely shipped, or
whether the estate's own recovery mechanisms have ever actually been exercised rather than merely designed.
This dataset closes that gap by supplying the missing BEFORE-the-failure layer: cognitive SLOs and failure
budgets (Parts III-VI) as the economic substrate, the mission-reliability predictor and degradation detector
(Parts VII-X) as the forecasting and early-warning substrate, institutional chaos engineering and graceful
degradation (Parts XI-XIV) as the deliberate-testing and designed-response substrate, recovery readiness and
the redundancy planner (Parts XV-XVII) as the recoverability-in-advance substrate, and the reliability
postmortem (Part XVIII) as the closing verification substrate that converts a proposed fix into a proven one.

Systems strengthened. Per the Strengthening Matrix's row for IAS-E2 and this dataset's own Parts I and
XXII: zero-crash gains a budget-and-SLO context for what counts as an acceptable rate of the crashes it
already prevents, rather than treating every crash as equally alarming regardless of whether it falls within
a knowingly-accepted tolerance. rollback and backup gain a recovery-readiness record (Part XV) that tells the
estate, in advance, how fast and how confidently each restores state, rather than discovering that answer
only during a live incident. session_resilience gains the same forward-looking readiness discipline applied
to continuity restoration specifically. cascade_prevention gains a degradation detector (Part IX) that
catches a slide toward a cascade-worthy state before the cascade itself begins, extending containment
earlier in the failure's lifecycle than reactive containment alone can reach.

Duplications avoided. Per this dataset's Part I boundary and the Non-Duplication Ledger's own verdict for
Family J: this dataset does not rebuild any of the five parents' reactive mechanisms — every graceful
degradation contract (Part XIV) invokes backup or rollback by name rather than reimplementing state
restoration; every chaos experiment's abort path (Part XII §12.4) invokes the same parent mechanisms rather
than a second, competing recovery apparatus. The redundancy planner (Part XVI) explicitly routes genuine
duplications it discovers to the D2A engine's own disposition vocabulary rather than adjudicating duplication
verdicts itself, preserving D2A's exclusive authority over that decision class per IAS-F1's Invariant Three.

New capabilities enabled. Three capabilities did not exist anywhere in the estate before this dataset and
are, per Article VIII, specified here as specification rather than shipped as code: a standing,
per-capability answer to "how much failure is this capability permitted before its behavior counts as a
defect" (the SLO-and-budget pair); a designed, published, discoverable answer to "what does this capability
do when it cannot do everything" (the graceful degradation contract); and a verified, dated answer to "did
our last fix actually work" (the postmortem's mandatory Stage Four) — three questions the estate could not
previously answer with evidence rather than intuition.

Open risks. The three UNRESOLVED questions of Part XXII §22.6 (cross-capability budget pooling, ladder
granularity at estate scale, multi-capability blast radii) remain genuinely open and are not resolved by
this dataset's own text. Additionally, Part XIX's nine-failure-mode catalog is, by this dataset's own
admission, a catalog of PREDICTED gameability rather than an OBSERVED one — no chaos experiment, budget
ledger, or postmortem has yet accumulated real institutional history at the time of this dataset's authoring,
and every worked example throughout Parts I-XXII is explicitly synthetic. The single largest open risk,
named honestly, is that this dataset's entire mechanism set remains NORMATIVE and EXPERIMENTAL until Part
XXI's five benchmarks are actually run against real accumulated data — a risk this dataset states about
itself rather than obscures, per Part XXI §21.7's closing discipline.

===============================================================================================
ANTI-PATTERNS
===============================================================================================

Anti-pattern one: SLO-first implementation without envelope discipline. The single most likely
implementation error is standing up the SLO and budget mechanics of Parts III-VI first, because they are the
most familiar and most classically-documented pieces of this dataset, while skipping Part II's envelope-
definition discipline as "obvious" or "something we'll fill in later." The predictable failure mode is
ENVELOPE ABSENCE (Part XIX §19.4) at scale: dozens of SLO records exist with vague or copy-pasted envelope
definitions that do not actually reflect each capability's real acceptable output space, producing SLO
THEATER (Part XIX §19.2) across the board before anyone notices. Prevention: treat Part II's envelope
definition as a mandatory, reviewed prerequisite artifact for any SLO, never an implicit assumption, and
apply the always-green-is-suspicious heuristic of Part XIX §19.2 to any capability whose SLO has never come
close to breach.

Anti-pattern two: shipping the detector, predictor, and chaos engine without the postmortem's Stage Four.
Parts VII through XII (the predictor and the chaos protocol) are the most technically interesting mechanisms
in this dataset and the most likely to attract early implementation effort, while Part XVIII's postmortem
discipline — comparatively unglamorous, requiring patience for a verification window to elapse — is the most
likely to be deprioritized or implemented only through Stage Three. The predictable failure mode is a
reliability layer that generates impressive-looking forecasts and well-designed chaos experiments while never
actually closing the loop back to PROVEN improvement, leaving every fix at permanent CLOSED-UNVERIFIED or
worse, never postmortem'd at all — precisely Benchmark Five's (Part XXI §21.5) targeted failure. Prevention:
implement Stage Four's verification-window scheduling as part of the SAME unit of work as Stage Three's fix
proposal, never as a separately-prioritized follow-up task that competes for attention against newer
incidents.

Anti-pattern three: treating grade transitions and budget consumption as internal telemetry rather than
published, consumer-visible contracts. Because Parts IX, X, and XIV's mechanisms are naturally implemented as
internal state on the capability being measured, the tempting shortcut is to track degradation grade and
budget status as private, undocumented fields visible only to the capability's own maintainers — which
technically satisfies "we have a degradation ladder" while completely failing Part XIII §13.4's requirement
that a capability's degraded behavior only be genuinely graceful if its CONSUMERS know what to expect from
it. The predictable failure mode is every DEPENDS-ON and FEEDS consumer across the estate continuing to treat
a Grade 2 or Grade 3 capability as though it were still at Grade 0, propagating unflagged degraded output
exactly as Part XIII §13.2 describes for undesigned degradation, even though a perfectly good grade record
exists — it simply was never published where a consumer could read it. Prevention: treat Part XIV §14.2's
CONSUMER NOTIFICATION MECHANISM field as non-optional at contract authorship, and audit, per Part XIX §19.8's
completeness check, that every Grade 2 or 3 capability's current grade is actually discoverable by at least
one mechanism external to the capability's own internals before considering its degradation ladder
implementation complete.

arise from poor discoverability rather than deliberate re-invention.
