===============================================================================================
PART I — THE CROSS-DATASET INSIGHT OBJECT: WHY NO SINGLE DATASET CAN HOLD IT
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding observation. The Claude Power Pack estate, as OBSERVED on disk on 2026-07-12,
holds eighteen-plus sealed dataset domains and dozens of executable modules, each authored to a
depth contract that rewards internal completeness: a dataset is judged sealed when its own Parts
cover thesis, mechanism, failure modes, and evaluation for its own object, never when it has been
cross-read against a sibling dataset it did not cite. This is not a defect of any single dataset —
depth-per-object is the correct local incentive, and the Constitution's Article VI (depth without
padding) rewards exactly this discipline — but it has a predictable emergent consequence: every
dataset optimizes for depth along its own axis, and the axis PERPENDICULAR to all of them, the
axis that only exists when two datasets are read side by side, is nobody's job. (DERIVED: a
family of N independently-deep datasets systematically under-produces insights of order
C(N,2) or higher, because the authoring process for each dataset never budgets time to read the
other N-1.)

1.2 What an insight is, precisely, and why "cross-dataset" is not decoration. An INSIGHT, as
DEFINED in this corpus, is a proposition that (a) is TRUE or LEVERAGE-BEARING within the estate's
own evidence discipline, (b) is not stated, in that form, in any single existing dataset, and (c)
is DERIVABLE from the conjunction of two or more existing datasets' stated content. Condition (c)
is the load-bearing one: it is what separates an insight from a mere restatement (which is
derivable from one dataset alone and adds nothing) and from a speculation (which is not derivable
from any existing stated content at all, however plausible it sounds). A CROSS-DATASET INSIGHT is
an insight whose minimal derivation requires citing at least two datasets that were authored
without reference to each other — the "without reference to each other" clause matters because an
insight explicitly planned as a cross-reference by a later dataset's author (as, for instance,
F1's own forward references to IAS-C1 and IAS-D1) is a designed dependency, not a discovered
synthesis; IAS-B2's object is specifically the UNDESIGNED, UNPLANNED conjunction that nonetheless
holds.

1.3 Why this is genuinely unowned, tested against the ensemble-object boundary. Applying IAS-F1's
formal level test (Part VIII of the ontology) to this object: does "find a proposition entailed by
two datasets' conjunction, stated by neither" act on a resource-level object owned by an existing
system? Graphify's object is the LOCATION of a knowledge node — where a fact lives, not what a
second fact combined with it implies. FD's object is the DELTA of one lineage against its own
history — the change within a thread, not the conjunction across threads. ACIS's object is the
EPISTEMIC LEVEL of one claim already stated — grading, not generating. D2A's object is the
OVERLAP between one proposal and one existing capability — a pairwise judgment triggered by a
proposal event, not a standing sweep across the whole estate's stated content. None of the four
enumerates, as its resource class, "propositions entailed by arbitrary pairs or larger subsets of
datasets." The object formalizes to ENSEMBLE under IAS-F1 Part VIII §8.3: its object is not
contained in any single system's owned resource class, and by the second-order test (F1 Part
VII §7.2) it is second-order in the degenerate-but-real sense that its direct object is OTHER
DATASETS' STATED CONTENT considered jointly, not a resource any one of them owns.

1.4 The shape of the gap, illustrated without inventing content. Consider, illustratively and
without asserting it as a real finding, two hypothetical sealed datasets: one on session-cost
routing (resource-level, Cognitive OS family) that establishes, as a mechanism, that a system's
ADVISORY authority lets errors be caught downstream because another system may override a bad
output; and a separate, unrelated dataset on immune-system fingerprinting (IAS-D2, Family E) that
establishes, independently, that a failure federated across projects is caught faster when the
FIRST project to encounter it has a low-authority, high-visibility reporting path rather than a
high-authority, low-visibility one. Neither dataset states the conjunction. But read together they
license a proposition neither states alone: that ADVISORY AUTHORITY IS A GENERAL PRECONDITION FOR
FAST CROSS-SYSTEM ERROR CORRECTION, independent of whether the domain is cost-routing or immunity
— a principle-level claim (Part IX) that, once surfaced, is exactly the kind of candidate ACIS's
epistemic ladder (Part XV of the graph) exists to grade, and exactly the kind of leverage point
IAS-B1's Cognitive Multiplier would want to know about, because a principle validated across two
unrelated domains is stronger evidence of generality than either domain's local claim alone. This
is illustrative, not asserted as fact about the real estate; it exists here only to make the SHAPE
of a cross-dataset insight concrete before Part III specifies the mechanism that would surface a
real one.

1.5 Why the object requires a standing engine, not an occasional human reading pass. A federation
whose members total roughly half a million words across eighteen-plus domains cannot be
cross-read exhaustively by any single reasoning pass, human or agentic, within the estate's
session-cost discipline (Cognitive OS, CO-01/02/03) — a full pairwise cross-read of eighteen
domains is C(18,2) = 153 domain-pairs, and the estate's own token-austerity protocol (Article of
the project CLAUDE.md governing indexed reads) forbids bulk re-reading source when a cached
summary exists. (DERIVED: the object is real and valuable, but it is not tractable as a one-off
audit; it requires a standing, incremental ENGINE that reads across the estate's summaries rather
than its raw text, re-triggered as new datasets seal, which is exactly what Part III specifies.)
This is the second reason, beyond the ensemble-object boundary, that IAS-B2 is a system and not a
task: a task completes once; the cross-dataset insight object grows every time a new dataset seals,
so the capability that finds it must be standing, not occasional.

1.6 Distinguishing the object from mere aggregation. It would be a category error — the AGGREGATOR
near-miss IAS-F1 names at Part VII §7.7 — to build a system that simply concatenates or indexes
every dataset's claims into one searchable corpus and call the result "synthesis." An aggregator's
produced object is a bigger corpus, a resource-level artifact; a genuine cross-dataset insight
requires a DERIVATION step — the conjunction must be shown to entail something neither source
states — and it is that derivation, not the concatenation preceding it, that makes the object
second-order and makes IAS-B2 non-duplicative of graphify's search-and-locate function. Part III
formalizes the derivation step as the engine's core operation.

1.7 A minimal, falsifiable metric for the gap this Part diagnoses, and why a bare count would
mislead. If Part I's founding observation is correct — that the estate systematically under-
produces cross-dataset propositions — the claim earns the DERIVED marker only if it is checkable
against a number, not merely plausible as prose. The metric, DEFINED here and formalized in full
at Part XIX §19.2, is the CROSS-DATASET INSIGHT RATE (CDIR): the count of PUBLISHED insight
records (Part III §3.5) newly entailed by a dataset-pair that had not previously been read
together, measured against the number of NEW dataset-pairs the estate's growth creates each time a
domain seals (a domain seal against N prior domains creates N new pairs, so the denominator grows
with each seal, not just the numerator with each engine run). A bare COUNT of published insights,
taken alone, would be exactly the kind of metric Article V's epistemic-honesty discipline warns
against optimizing directly: an engine under implicit pressure to show activity could inflate the
count by publishing narrow, low-corroboration bounded insights (Part II §2.6) at high volume
rather than searching for the rarer, more valuable cross-domain principle (Part II §2.5) — MORE
published records would then read as progress while the underlying gap, generality-bearing
cross-dataset knowledge, stayed exactly as thin as before. The ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRIC,
DEFINED, is CDIR weighted by corroboration tier (Part IV §4.4): a MODERATE-or-higher-tier insight
counts fully, a floor-tier two-domain bounded insight counts at a fraction set by Part XIX's own
weighting table, so that an engine cannot satisfy the metric by volume alone without also clearing
the adversarial disconfirmation search (Part III §3.4) that produces a higher tier. (NORMATIVE:
pairing a raw activity count with a quality-weighted countermetric in the SAME sentence that
introduces the metric, rather than deferring the countermetric to a later audit, is the specific
discipline that keeps Part I's own founding claim from becoming exactly the kind of ungrounded
assertion this whole dataset exists to replace with checkable propositions.)

FINAL LAW — PART I. A federation of independently-deep datasets systematically under-produces the
propositions that live in the conjunction of its parts rather than in any one part, because
depth-per-dataset is everyone's incentive and cross-dataset conjunction is nobody's job; the
object that closes this gap is owned by no existing system and cannot be produced by an
occasional reading pass — it requires a standing engine, re-triggered every time the estate's
stated content changes.

===============================================================================================
PART II — INSIGHT TAXONOMY: FACT, CLAIM, PATTERN, PRINCIPLE, INSIGHT, LAW
===============================================================================================

2.1 Why a taxonomy precedes the engine. Part I defined "insight" once, but the engine of Part III
needs a full ladder of intermediate object types, because a cross-dataset read does not jump
directly from raw text to a sealed insight — it passes through recognizable intermediate shapes,
and an engine that cannot NAME the shape it currently holds cannot decide what operation to apply
next (Part XIII's lifecycle depends on this taxonomy being exhaustive and ordered). This dataset
DEFINES six object types, ordered by increasing generality and decreasing attachment to their
originating text, each resolved against IAS-F1's vocabulary so that "claim," "principle," and
"law" mean the same thing here as they do wherever ACIS and F1 already use them.

2.2 FACT. A FACT, as used here, is a single OBSERVED or DEFINED statement local to one dataset,
carrying its own epistemic marker and no claim of generality beyond its source — "the estate held
524,000 words across eighteen domains as of 2026-07-12" is a fact, DEFINED entirely within its own
sentence, requiring no cross-reference to be true. Facts are the atomic units the engine reads; a
fact by itself is never an insight under Part I's definition because it satisfies condition (c)
(derivable from conjunction) vacuously — a single fact is "derived" only from itself.

2.3 CLAIM. A CLAIM is a fact or a small set of facts asserted, within one dataset, to support a
mechanism or a decision local to that dataset — "advisory authority lets a downstream system catch
a bad output" is a claim, situated inside one dataset's argument, carrying whatever epistemic level
that dataset assigned it. Claims are where most of a dataset's own argumentative weight sits; they
are still local, in the taxonomy's sense, because their truth or relevance has been argued for only
within the boundary of the one dataset that states them.

2.4 PATTERN. A PATTERN, DEFINED, is a claim shape that RECURS across two or more datasets in
structurally similar but not identical form, without either dataset citing the other. Recognizing
a pattern is the engine's first genuinely cross-dataset act: it does not yet assert that the
recurrence is meaningful (that is the principle's job, Part IX), only that the SAME structural
shape — the same relation between the same kinds of objects — appears independently in unrelated
argumentative contexts. A pattern is evidence a principle might exist; it is not yet the principle
itself, and treating a pattern as already-proven is the SPURIOUS-SYNTHESIS failure mode Part XVIII
catalogues in full.

2.5 PRINCIPLE. A PRINCIPLE, DEFINED, is a pattern that has been tested against its own
counterexamples within the estate — checked, deliberately, for datasets where the same structural
shape does NOT recur, or recurs with an opposite sign — and survives that check with its
generality intact or explicitly bounded. The move from pattern to principle is the single most
truth-sensitive step in this whole dataset's mechanism, because it is the step where a merely
noticed coincidence either earns or fails to earn the claim of generality; Part IX specifies the
extraction procedure and its falsification discipline in full.

2.6 INSIGHT. An INSIGHT, per Part I's definition, is the cross-dataset proposition itself — it may
be a validated principle (the strong case), or it may be a narrower, single-conjunction discovery
that does not rise to principle-level generality but is still true and useful within its specific
two-or-more-dataset scope ("systems X and Y, read together, imply Z, though Z is not shown to
generalize beyond X and Y"). Every principle that survives Part 2.5's check is an insight; not
every insight is a principle — the taxonomy is a strict containment, INSIGHT ⊇ PRINCIPLE, and the
engine must be able to output the narrower, unglamorous kind honestly rather than inflate every
finding into a claimed generality it has not earned.

2.7 LAW. A LAW, in the sense ACIS already OWNS and this dataset only REFERENCES rather than
redefines, is a principle that has been promoted through ACIS's epistemic ladder to a level where
it carries a stated falsifier and has survived at least one attempt to falsify it (ACIS's own
No-Autopromotion discipline, imported by reference per the project MEMORY's ACIS Generation Zero
entry). IAS-B2 produces INSIGHT-level and, where it survives Part 2.5's check, PRINCIPLE-level
candidates; it does NOT self-promote a candidate to LAW status — that promotion is exclusively
ACIS's authority under IAS-F1's Invariant Three (singular authority per decision class, F1 Part V
§5.4), and IAS-B2's relationship to ACIS is producer-to-authority, specified fully in Part XV.

2.8 Worked classification: running one raw observation up the ladder. Take the illustrative
observation of Part I §1.4. As stated in its two source datasets, it exists twice as a CLAIM (once
inside the cost-routing dataset, once inside the immune-system dataset), each with its own local
epistemic marker. The engine's pattern-recognition pass (Part IV) notices the structural
recurrence — "low-authority-plus-high-visibility beats high-authority-plus-low-visibility for
downstream correction speed" — appearing in both, and provisionally tags it a PATTERN. The
extraction pass (Part IX) then checks the estate for counterexamples: does any dataset show a
HIGH-authority, LOW-visibility system correcting errors just as fast? If a counterexample is found
and it survives scrutiny, the candidate is downgraded to a bounded INSIGHT ("holds for cost-
routing and immune-system domains, not shown to generalize further") rather than promoted to
PRINCIPLE; if no counterexample survives and the pattern holds across a third, independently-
checked domain, it earns PRINCIPLE status and becomes eligible for the ACIS submission pipeline of
Part XV. This worked trace is illustrative only — it does not assert that the underlying claim is
true of the real estate — but it demonstrates the taxonomy doing real classificatory work rather
than sitting as unused vocabulary.

2.9 Why six levels and not more or fewer. A shorter ladder (say, collapsing pattern and principle
into one stage) would let the engine treat a merely-noticed coincidence as already-generalized,
which is precisely the spurious-synthesis failure mode; a longer ladder (splitting principle into
several sub-grades before ACIS) would duplicate ACIS's own epistemic ladder, which already owns
fine-grained promotion staging above the principle level — IAS-F1's Invariant Two (no unresolved
duplicate) forbids IAS-B2 from building a second law-grading ladder alongside ACIS's. Six levels is
therefore the minimum sufficient to (a) keep local claims local until cross-referenced, (b)
force a falsification check before generality is claimed, and (c) hand off cleanly to ACIS's own
authority at the boundary where a second grading system would duplicate it. (NORMATIVE.)

2.10 Edge case: the claim that arrives already dressed as a principle. Not every claim the
scanner (Part IV §4.2) indexes sits honestly at the FACT or CLAIM level; a dataset author, writing
with conviction, sometimes states a local claim in already-general language — "this holds for
every system in the estate" — without having actually checked a second domain, because the
generality was asserted rhetorically rather than earned through the cross-reference §2.5 requires.
(DERIVED: rhetorical generality and taxonomy-earned generality are different properties, and only
the latter licenses the PRINCIPLE label.) The handling rule, DEFINED here ahead of Part XVII's
full adversarial catalogue: the engine's classification pass NEVER promotes a candidate on the
strength of its own stated scope — a claim's SELF-DESCRIPTION as general is treated as a CLAIM
(§2.3) carrying an unverified generality assertion, not as evidence the claim has already cleared
§2.5's counterexample search, and it must pass through the SAME pattern-recognition and
disconfirmation pipeline (Parts III-IV) as a claim stated narrowly. This matters because a
taxonomy that trusted self-description would let confident phrasing substitute for cross-domain
evidence, precisely inverting the ladder's own discipline: the six levels exist to separate what a
dataset's own author BELIEVES is general from what the estate's OTHER datasets, read
independently, actually corroborate, and a claim's own grammar is not admissible evidence for the
second question. Worked illustratively: if a hypothetical dataset asserted, in its own text,
"every leverage-bearing system in the estate follows a diminishing-return curve past its third
iteration," the engine's scanner indexes this as a single dataset's CLAIM regardless of the word
"every" in its phrasing, and only Part IX's full three-condition extraction procedure — not the
confident wording — can move it to PRINCIPLE. This edge case interfaces directly with IAS-B1's own
leverage-ranking intake (Part XIV §14.3): were B1 to accept a self-declared-general claim at face
value from a source other than a validated B2 insight record, it would be ranking on unearned
generality, which is precisely the failure B2's producer role exists to prevent upstream of B1's
own ranking step.

FINAL LAW — PART II. Six ordered object types — fact, claim, pattern, principle, insight, law —
and the single most consequential step in the ladder is pattern-to-principle, because that is
where a noticed coincidence either survives an honest search for its own counterexamples or is
correctly left as a narrower, unglamorous insight; skipping the check is how synthesis becomes
spurious.

PART III — THE SYNTHESIS ENGINE: ARCHITECTURE AND THE READ-ACROSS MECHANISM
===============================================================================================

3.1 What the engine is, as a system under IAS-F1's tests. The SYNTHESIS ENGINE, DEFINED, is the
system whose capability is "produce cross-dataset insights (Part I) by reading across the
estate's stated content and applying the taxonomy of Part II." Tested against IAS-F1's four
systemhood criteria: it holds a distinct object (Part I §1.3 establishes it is unowned), it holds
authority within its scope (it may PROPOSE an insight and assign it a taxonomy level, though it
does not hold FINAL authority to promote to law — that stays with ACIS per Part II §2.7), it
exposes an interface (an insight record, specified in 3.5, that other systems can consume), and it
is addressable independently (Part IX of F1's addressing scheme applies to it like any other
system). It passes cleanly; the harder question, addressed in this Part, is architecture rather
than admissibility.

3.2 The three-stage pipeline, at a glance. The engine runs a fixed three-stage pipeline over the
estate's dataset summaries, never over raw text by default (the token-austerity boundary of 3.6).
Stage one, CANDIDATE SCANNING, sweeps the estate's cached summaries for claim-shapes that recur
structurally across datasets that do not cite each other, producing PATTERN candidates (Part II
§2.4). Stage two, CROSS-REFERENCE VALIDATION, takes each pattern candidate and actively searches
for counterexamples and boundary conditions within the estate, producing either a bounded INSIGHT
or a generalized PRINCIPLE (Part II §2.5-2.6). Stage three, PROVENANCE-LOCKED PUBLICATION, writes
the surviving object as an insight record carrying full source attribution to every dataset it
drew from, and routes it to its consumer (FD-03 for a knowledge deposit, ACIS for a law candidate,
or IAS-B1 for a leverage-ranking input) per Part XIV and Part XV. Each stage is a subsystem in the
Part II §2.3 sense of IAS-F1: it has an interface and a facet-object of the engine's own object,
but its authority is delegated by, and its output is consumed by, the parent pipeline.

3.3 Stage one in detail: candidate scanning over summaries, not source. The scanner's input is the
estate's audit-cache layer — the neural summaries and semantic-DNA records the project's own
token-austerity protocol already maintains for indexed reads — never a bulk re-read of every
dataset's raw text, because a full-corpus re-read on every scan would violate the estate's own
circuit-breaker discipline (a scan whose planned raw-read bytes exceed the austerity ceiling halts
and emits a selective-read plan rather than proceeding). The scanner's operation is a structural
match: it represents each claim (Part II §2.3) as a typed shape — subject-class, relation,
object-class, and the domain the claim is drawn from — and flags any two claims from different,
non-cross-citing domains whose shapes match above a similarity threshold. (DEFINED: shape-matching
here is structural, not lexical — "advisory authority enables downstream correction" and "low
final-decision-weight lets a sibling system catch an error" are the SAME shape despite sharing few
words, and a scanner that matched only on vocabulary would miss most genuine cross-domain
patterns, a failure named explicitly in Part XVIII.)

3.4 Stage two in detail: cross-reference validation as an adversarial search, not a confirmation
pass. The critical design decision in this Part is that stage two is built to look for
DISCONFIRMATION first, not confirmation. Given a pattern candidate from stage one, the validator's
first action is to enumerate every dataset in the estate whose domain could plausibly exhibit the
SAME claim-shape and check whether each one does — and specifically to search hardest in the
domains where the pattern is LEAST expected to hold, because a pattern that only survives where it
was already expected to survive has not been tested. (NORMATIVE: this ordering — disconfirmation
before confirmation — is imported by direct analogy from ACIS's own falsifier discipline, and it
is the single design choice most responsible for keeping stage-two output from becoming spurious
synthesis; a validator that searched for confirming examples first would systematically overstate
generality, because confirming examples are easier to find and more numerous than disconfirming
ones in any sufficiently large corpus, a variant of confirmation bias formalized at the engine
level in Part XVII's adversarial catalogue.) Only after the disconfirmation search returns empty,
or returns bounded exceptions the pattern can honestly state as its scope limits, does stage two
promote the candidate to PRINCIPLE or leave it at bounded INSIGHT.

3.5 The insight record: the engine's interface. Every object stage three publishes carries, as a
fixed schema, DEFINED here as the engine's interface per IAS-F1 Part II §2.1's fourth systemhood
test: a STATEMENT (the proposition itself, in the taxonomy level it earned — insight or principle),
a PROVENANCE SET (every dataset and specific Part/section the derivation drew from — never fewer
than two, per Part I's own definition), an EPISTEMIC MARKER (per Article V, almost always DERIVED
or HYPOTHESIZED at the moment of publication, never OBSERVED, because a cross-dataset insight is
by construction a step of reasoning beyond what either source directly measured), a
DISCONFIRMATION LOG (what stage two checked and did not find, so a consumer can see the search was
real rather than assumed), and a CONSUMER ROUTE (which of FD-03, ACIS, or IAS-B1 the object is
proposed to, per Parts XIV-XV). This record is the exact artifact IAS-F1's evidence discipline
(F1 Part IV) requires of any claim entering the estate's decision fabric: nothing here is asserted
without a way to be doubted.

3.6 The token-austerity boundary and why the engine reads summaries by default. The estate's own
Token Austerity Protocol (project CLAUDE.md, DNA-3000) requires that bulk raw-file reads be
gated behind a circuit breaker, and the synthesis engine — whose entire function is reading across
many datasets — is the single system in the estate most exposed to that circuit breaker's failure
mode if it is not architected around it from the start. (DERIVED: an engine that re-reads full
dataset text on every scan does not merely cost tokens; it structurally cannot scale past a small
handful of domains before the circuit breaker halts it, which would make the engine unable to
cover the very estate it exists to synthesize across.) The engine therefore reads the maintained
summary layer by default (3.3) and escalates to a raw read of a SPECIFIC cited Part only at stage
two, only for the specific sections a pattern candidate names, and only when the summary's own
provenance is stale beyond its reconciliation marker (IAS-F1 Part IV §4.3) — a narrow, bounded
escalation rather than a standing practice.

3.7 Why the engine is incremental, not a periodic full sweep. A full re-scan of the entire estate
on every trigger would re-derive every previously-published insight record on every run, wasting
the exact scarce resource (CO's cognitive capital) the Portfolio dataset (IAS-C1) exists to guard.
The engine is therefore DESIGNED incrementally: its trigger is a NEW or MODIFIED dataset sealing
(an event the Control Bus, IAS-A2, is positioned to carry once built), and its stage-one scan is
scoped to claim-shapes touching the newly-sealed dataset against the FULL prior corpus of already-
scanned domains — an O(1)-new-domain operation per seal rather than an O(N^2) re-scan, which keeps
the engine's own cost bounded as the estate grows rather than growing quadratically with it.
(DERIVED from the estate's own cost-discipline; EXPERIMENTAL as a specific architecture choice
until measured against a real trigger volume.)

3.8 The engine's authority, precisely bounded. Per IAS-F1's Invariant Three (singular authority per
decision class), the engine holds ADVISORY authority only over the insight records it produces — it
may propose a statement, a taxonomy level, and a consumer route, but the decision to accept an
insight as an FD deposit belongs to FD-03's own promotion gate, and the decision to accept a
principle as a law candidate belongs to ACIS's own ladder. This bound is not a limitation imposed
reluctantly; it is the mechanism by which IAS-B2 strengthens FD-03 and ACIS rather than replacing
either — the engine supplies candidates neither could have generated alone (because neither reads
across the whole estate), and the receiving system retains final judgment, exactly the strengthens
relation IAS-F1 Part VI §6.6 defines.

FINAL LAW — PART III. The synthesis engine is a three-stage pipeline — scan, adversarially
validate, provenance-lock and publish — built to search for disconfirmation before confirmation,
to read summaries before raw text, and to run incrementally rather than exhaustively; an engine
built the other way on any one of these three axes would either overstate generality, exhaust the
estate's token budget, or fail to scale with the corpus it exists to serve.

===============================================================================================
PART IV — SUBENGINES: THE CANDIDATE SCANNER, THE CROSS-REFERENCE MATCHER, THE CORROBORATION SCORER
===============================================================================================

4.1 Why the pipeline needs subengines and not one monolithic scan. Part III named three STAGES;
this Part opens the middle stage, cross-reference validation, into its constituent SUBENGINES in
the IAS-F1 Part II §2.3 sense — bounded units whose object is a facet of the parent pipeline's
object and whose authority is delegated. Three subengines carry stage two's weight: the CANDIDATE
SCANNER (which is, more precisely, stage one's mechanism, opened here because its output shape
determines what stage two can do with it), the CROSS-REFERENCE MATCHER (which performs the
structural comparison across domains), and the CORROBORATION SCORER (which turns a set of matched
instances into a confidence figure a consumer can use). Splitting these three apart matters because
each has a distinct, nameable failure mode (Part XVIII), and a monolithic "find patterns" function
would hide which of the three failed when an insight later proves spurious.

4.2 The Candidate Scanner, mechanism. The scanner's job, restated with mechanism, is to convert
each dataset's claims (Part II §2.3) into the typed shape representation of Part III §3.3 — a
four-tuple of subject-class, relation, object-class, and domain-tag — and to index those shapes so
that structural matches across domains can be found by lookup rather than by pairwise comparison of
every claim against every other claim, which would be the O(N^2) cost Part III §3.7 explicitly
designs against. (DERIVED: an index keyed on the relation and the subject/object CLASS pair, rather
than on the domain, is what lets the scanner find a match between two claims from domains the
scanner has never been asked to compare directly — the index does the cross-domain work implicitly,
by construction, rather than requiring an explicit pairwise sweep.) The scanner's output is not yet
a validated pattern; it is a CANDIDATE SET — every group of two-or-more claims sharing an indexed
shape, still unfiltered for whether the match is meaningful or coincidental, which is precisely
what the next two subengines exist to determine.

4.3 The Cross-Reference Matcher, mechanism. Given a candidate set from the scanner, the matcher's
job is to assess whether the structural match is SEMANTICALLY load-bearing — whether the two claims
are actually making the same argument in different domains, or merely share superficial shape while
meaning different things. (This is the CENTRAL technical risk of the entire engine, named explicitly
here because Part XVIII's leading failure mode, spurious synthesis, originates almost entirely at
this subengine.) The matcher's method is DEFINED as a two-part test: first, do the two claims share
not just shape but MECHANISM — is the reason claim A is true, within its own dataset's argument, the
same TYPE of reason claim B is true within its dataset's argument, rather than a coincidental
correlation with different underlying causes; second, would an author of dataset A, shown dataset
B's claim without being told it was "supposed" to match, independently recognize it as the same
underlying mechanism. The second test — the blind-recognition check — is EXPERIMENTAL as a
procedure (it proposes its own evaluation, specified in Part XX) but is included here as the
matcher's design target because it is the closest available proxy, without a live domain expert
loop, for "this match is not merely surface-level."

4.4 The Corroboration Scorer, mechanism. Once the matcher confirms a candidate is mechanism-level
rather than surface-level, the scorer assigns a CORROBORATION SCORE — not a single number
pretending to false precision, but a qualitative tier (per this dataset's own FUSION SCORE
convention at the end of this file) built from three inputs: the COUNT of independent domains the
pattern holds in (two domains is the floor per Part I's definition; three or more is materially
stronger evidence of generality), the DISCONFIRMATION RESULT from Part III §3.4's adversarial
search (a pattern that survived a genuine search in an unlikely domain scores higher than one that
was only checked in domains where it was already expected to hold), and the SOURCE INDEPENDENCE of
the corroborating datasets (two datasets authored by the same session, citing overlapping source
material, corroborate each other more weakly than two datasets authored months apart with no shared
authorship context — a check DERIVED from ordinary epistemics: correlated authorship is correlated
evidence, not independent evidence). (NORMATIVE: the scorer never outputs a bare confidence
percentage, because a false-precision number on an inherently qualitative judgment is exactly the
kind of manufactured rigor Article V's epistemic-honesty discipline exists to prevent; it outputs a
tier plus the three inputs that produced it, so a downstream consumer can inspect the reasoning
rather than trust an opaque score.)

4.5 Worked trace through all three subengines. Returning to the illustrative advisory-authority
pattern of Part I §1.4 and Part II §2.8: the scanner indexes both source claims under the shape
(low-final-authority-system, enables, faster-downstream-correction) and flags them as a candidate
pair because they share subject-class (authority-holding system), relation (enables), and
object-class (correction speed), despite being drawn from unrelated domains. The matcher then asks
whether the MECHANISM is the same: in the cost-routing dataset, the reason is that another system
can override a bad output before it ships; in the immune-system dataset, the reason is that a
low-authority reporting path gets checked by more downstream readers before being acted on. These
are, on inspection, the SAME underlying mechanism — visibility-before-action — expressed through
different domain vocabulary, so the matcher confirms it as mechanism-level rather than surface-
level. The scorer then checks corroboration: two domains (floor met, not exceeded), a genuine
disconfirmation search that in this illustration found one partial counterexample (a domain where a
low-authority system's output was NOT checked faster, because no downstream reader was actually
consuming it — an instance the taxonomy would classify per Part II §2.6 as evidence the principle
needs a CONSUMER-COUNT boundary condition, not evidence it is false), and independent authorship (the
two illustrative source datasets are drawn from unrelated development threads). The scorer's output
tier, in this illustration, would be MODERATE — two-domain corroboration with one honestly-logged
boundary condition — which routes the candidate to publication as a bounded INSIGHT (Part II §2.6)
rather than a fully generalized PRINCIPLE, exactly the honest, non-inflated outcome Part II §2.5
requires when the evidence does not clear the higher bar.

4.6 Why these three subengines and not a single classifier. A design that collapsed scanning,
matching, and scoring into one opaque classification step (e.g., an end-to-end model that ingests
two claims and outputs "match: yes/no") would satisfy the pipeline's throughput requirement but
would violate the estate's evidence discipline at its root: Article V requires every load-bearing
claim to carry a marker AND a reason a downstream consumer could dispute; a monolithic classifier
produces neither, because its "reasoning" is not inspectable as separate scanning, mechanism, and
corroboration judgments. The three-subengine split is therefore not merely an engineering
convenience; it is the mechanism by which the insight record of Part III §3.5 can honestly populate
its DISCONFIRMATION LOG field, because each subengine's intermediate output is exactly what that
field needs to cite.

FINAL LAW — PART IV. Structural matching, mechanism-level confirmation, and corroboration scoring
are three distinct judgments with three distinct failure modes, and collapsing them into one opaque
step trades inspectability for throughput — a trade the estate's evidence discipline does not permit
a system whose entire output is claims about claims.

PART V — KNOWLEDGE FUSION WITH PROVENANCE: THE FUSION OBJECT
===============================================================================================

5.1 Why fusion is a distinct object from the insight record. Part III §3.5 defined the insight
record as the engine's primary output — a NEW proposition derived from a conjunction. This Part
defines a related but distinct object: the FUSION RECORD, DEFINED as the result of combining two or
more EXISTING pieces of complementary knowledge — not deriving something new, but assembling
partial, non-overlapping fragments from different datasets into one coherent, more complete
picture, WHILE preserving each fragment's original attribution. Where an insight is a genuinely new
proposition (Part I condition (c): derivable from, but not stated by, either source), a fusion is a
composite of pieces that were each independently true and incomplete, becoming jointly more useful
without either piece being altered or superseded. (DERIVED: insight and fusion are siblings under
the same engine, not the same operation — Part VI formalizes exactly where fusion's boundary sits
against two estate systems that sound adjacent but are not the same thing.)

5.2 A worked distinction between insight and fusion. Illustratively: if dataset X states "the
Cognitive OS's cost telemetry is DERIVED from live session data" and dataset Y, independently,
states "a system's REUSE HORIZON is best estimated from telemetry spanning at least two toolchain
generations," an INSIGHT would be a new proposition like "cost telemetry alone cannot estimate reuse
horizon without a second toolchain generation's data" — a claim neither X nor Y states, entailed by
reading them together. A FUSION, by contrast, would be the simple, non-inferential combination:
"system cost and reuse horizon are BOTH properties in IAS-F1's property model (F1 Part IV), one
measured from live telemetry (per X) and one requiring multi-generation history (per Y)" — no new
claim is asserted, but the two datasets' complementary, non-overlapping content about the SAME
underlying object (a system's property record) is assembled into one coherent picture that neither
alone provides. Fusion produces COMPLETENESS; synthesis produces NOVELTY. Both matter, and the
engine (Part III) runs a lighter-weight fusion pass alongside its heavier synthesis pipeline,
because fusion candidates are far more numerous and cheaper to validate — they require no
disconfirmation search, only a check that the fragments genuinely do not contradict each other.

5.3 The fusion record's schema, and why provenance is not optional. A FUSION RECORD carries, DEFINED
here: a UNIFIED OBJECT (the single thing the fusion describes — in the example above, "system X's
full property record"), a FRAGMENT SET (each contributing dataset's specific claim, unaltered, with
its own original epistemic marker preserved rather than flattened to a single marker for the whole
fusion), a CONTRADICTION CHECK RESULT (did the fragments genuinely fail to contradict, and what was
checked), and — the field that gives this Part its name and its non-duplication boundary against
Part VI's comparison systems — a PROVENANCE MAP that keeps every fragment traceable to its exact
origin, permanently, even after fusion. (NORMATIVE: a fusion that cannot be un-fused — that loses
the ability to say which original dataset contributed which fragment — has destroyed information the
estate's evidence discipline requires it to keep, because a fragment's epistemic marker is only
meaningful in light of its original context, and flattening that context is how a fusion silently
launders an OBSERVED claim and a HYPOTHESIZED claim into one undifferentiated blob.)

5.4 Why provenance-preservation is the whole point, not a compliance afterthought. Consider the
failure a provenance-losing fusion would produce: if fragment A carries evidence level OBSERVED and
fragment B carries evidence level HYPOTHESIZED, and the fusion process merges them into a single
statement without preserving which claim came from which source, a downstream consumer reading the
fused statement cannot tell that half of it is unverified — the fusion has manufactured false
confidence purely as an artifact of combination, without either source dataset having overstated
anything. This is PROVENANCE LOSS, the second of the two named failure modes this dataset commits to
tracking end-to-end (Part XVIII), and it is why the fusion record's schema makes the provenance map
a REQUIRED field rather than optional metadata: a fusion record without it is not a lighter-weight
fusion, it is an unpublishable draft.

5.5 Fusion as an operation over IAS-F1's property model, concretely. The clearest and most
frequently-occurring fusion case, EXPERIMENTAL as a volume estimate but DERIVED as a mechanism, is
exactly the property-record case of §5.2: IAS-F1's six canonical properties (Part IV of the
ontology) are each, in practice, measured or asserted by DIFFERENT systems — cost by the Cognitive
OS's telemetry, reuse horizon by a distillation-family assessment, consumers by the Control Bus's
event log once built. No single existing system populates a complete property record for a given
system; each contributes a fragment. The synthesis engine's fusion pass is therefore, in significant
part, the mechanism that actually ASSEMBLES the property records IAS-F1 specifies the schema for but
does not itself populate — a direct strengthening relation (IAS-F1 Part VI §6.6) from B2 to F1's own
downstream consumers (IAS-C1, IAS-E1, IAS-G1), because those three datasets need complete property
records and no other system in the estate is positioned to assemble them from scattered fragments
while preserving each fragment's provenance.

5.6 The fusion lifecycle, briefly, ahead of Part XIII's full treatment. A fusion candidate is
detected when the scanner (Part IV §4.2) finds two claims sharing a UNIFIED OBJECT (rather than a
matched RELATION SHAPE, which is the synthesis path) — i.e., two datasets making different, non-
contradictory claims about the literally same system, capability, or object identified by IAS-F1's
addressing scheme (F1 Part IX). The contradiction check (5.3) runs a lighter version of Part III
§3.4's adversarial search — checking specifically whether the fragments assert incompatible values
for the same field, rather than searching the whole estate for counterexamples to a generalized
principle — and on passing, the fusion record publishes directly, without needing the heavier
corroboration-scoring machinery of Part IV §4.4, because a fusion makes no claim of generality to
score; it only claims completeness of an already-scoped object.

5.7 What fusion does not do, stated as a boundary in advance of Part VI. Fusion, as defined here,
never resolves which of two contradicting fragments is correct — that is a dispute the contradiction
check surfaces but does not adjudicate, and adjudicating it would require exactly the kind of
authority-over-a-decision-class that IAS-F1's Invariant Three reserves to a single system per
decision class; the synthesis engine does not hold that authority for arbitrary property disputes,
and a fusion candidate whose contradiction check fails is routed, per Part XIII, to a human or to
the specific system that DOES hold authority over the contested field, never silently resolved by
the fusion pass itself. Nor does fusion canonicalize IDENTITY — deciding that two differently-named
things are "the same system" — which is a distinct operation this dataset explicitly does not own,
specified as the first of two non-duplication boundaries in Part VI.

FINAL LAW — PART V. Fusion produces completeness from complementary fragments; synthesis produces
novelty from conjunction; both are the engine's outputs, but only fusion's provenance map is what
keeps a merged claim from silently laundering an unverified fragment into an apparently-verified
whole — and a fusion record without that map is not a lesser fusion, it is not a fusion at all.

PART VI — THE BOUNDARY: FUSION VERSUS CO-05 DEDUP VERSUS GK-02 CANONICALIZATION
===============================================================================================

6.1 Why this boundary must be stated explicitly rather than left implied. The Non-Duplication
Ledger routes candidate 14, Knowledge Fusion Compiler, to MERGE rather than BUILD, with the overlap
named precisely: CO-05's dedup and GK-02's canonicalization each "canonicalize identity," while
B2's fusion "fuses complementary knowledge, keeps provenance." A reader who has not seen the
mechanism difference could reasonably suspect IAS-B2's fusion pass duplicates one or both of these
existing systems; this Part exists to make the difference mechanical and checkable rather than
asserted, because Article II of the Constitution treats an unproven distinct-object claim as a
duplication until proven otherwise, and F1's Invariant Two (no unresolved duplicate) makes the
proof a standing obligation, not a one-time assertion at authoring time.

6.2 CO-05 dedup, its object, restated by reference and not rebuilt. CO-05, as OBSERVED from its
place in the estate's cost-governance family, performs DEDUPLICATION: given two records that the
estate suspects describe the SAME underlying entity (the same session, the same model call, the same
cost event), it decides whether they are in fact one entity double-counted or two genuinely distinct
entities, and if the former, it COLLAPSES them into one record, discarding the redundant copy. The
directional test that separates dedup from fusion: dedup's output has FEWER records than its input,
because its entire function is to REMOVE redundancy. (DERIVED: dedup presupposes the two inputs are
the SAME thing wrongly counted twice; its correct behavior is to make one thing.)

6.3 GK-02 canonicalization, its object, restated by reference and not rebuilt. GK-02, within the
Graphify kernel, performs CANONICALIZATION: given multiple names, aliases, or representations that
all refer to the SAME knowledge node, it decides which representation is the canonical one and
maps every alias to it, so that graphify's location function (its own distinct object, per F1's
introduction of the estate's existing systems) can answer "where does X live" with one authoritative
answer regardless of which alias a query used. The directional test that separates canonicalization
from fusion: canonicalization's output PICKS ONE representation and subordinates the others as
pointers to it; it does not preserve all representations as equally-standing fragments.

6.4 The formal boundary, stated as a single test. Both CO-05 and GK-02 operate on the premise that
their two inputs describe THE SAME OBJECT wrongly represented as two, and their correct output is
ONE representation of that one object (dedup: one record; canonicalization: one canonical name).
IAS-B2's fusion pass operates on the OPPOSITE premise: its two inputs describe DIFFERENT, PARTIAL,
NON-OVERLAPPING facets of what may be the same or a related object, and its correct output is a
COMPOSITE that keeps BOTH fragments visible and separately attributed, precisely because neither
fragment is redundant with the other — collapsing them, in fusion's case, would be an information-
destroying error, not a cleanup. (DEFINED, as a single test any future implementer can apply: if the
two inputs, once merged, should leave EXACTLY the information content of the larger of the two
inputs, the correct operation is dedup or canonicalization; if the two inputs, once merged, should
leave MORE information content than either alone, because each contributed something the other
lacked, the correct operation is fusion.) This is the identity-versus-complementarity test, and it
is mechanical enough that a fusion candidate that turns out, on inspection, to be two records about
the literal same object with no complementary content is NOT a fusion — it is a dedup or
canonicalization candidate that the engine must route to CO-05 or GK-02 rather than process itself,
per Part XIII's lifecycle routing rule.

6.5 Why the boundary is not merely definitional but operationally load-bearing. If the synthesis
engine silently absorbed dedup or canonicalization work — for instance, by "fusing" two records that
were, on the identity test, actually the same entity double-named — it would duplicate CO-05 and
GK-02's authority over their own decision classes, violating IAS-F1's Invariant Three (singular
authority per decision class, F1 Part V §5.4): CO-05 holds final authority over which cost records
are the same event; GK-02 holds final authority over which name is canonical for a knowledge node;
IAS-B2 holds neither, and must not act as if it does merely because a fusion-shaped candidate
happens to pass through its pipeline. The engine's stage-one scanner (Part IV §4.2) is therefore
required, as a design constraint rather than a suggestion, to run the identity-versus-complementarity
test of §6.4 BEFORE routing any candidate into the fusion pass, and to hand off identity-shaped
candidates to CO-05 or GK-02's own interfaces rather than resolve them locally.

6.6 Worked contrast: the same raw material processed three different ways. Consider two records,
illustratively: Record P states "Session 4471 cost 0.8 cognitive-capital units" and Record Q states
"Session 4471, same session, cost 0.8 cognitive-capital units, logged four minutes later by a
retry." Applying the identity test: both describe the SAME session, the SAME cost figure — this is a
CO-05 dedup case, and the correct output is one record, the duplicate discarded. Now consider Record
R, "System Alpha's cost is LOW, per Cognitive OS telemetry" and Record S, "System Alpha's reuse
horizon is HIGH, per a distillation-family assessment eighteen months old" — different fields of the
SAME system's property record, neither redundant with the other. Applying the identity test: this is
a FUSION case per §6.4, because merging leaves MORE information (both cost and reuse horizon) than
either record alone. Finally, consider Record T, "the entity referred to as 'System Alpha' in one
document" and Record U, "the entity referred to as 'the Alpha router' in another document, describing
the same underlying module" — this is a GK-02 canonicalization case: same object, different name,
and the correct output is one canonical name with an alias pointer, not a composite of "Alpha" and
"the Alpha router" as if they were complementary facts.

6.7 The boundary's relationship to the Non-Duplication Ledger's own verdict. The ledger's routing of
candidate 14 to MERGE, rather than duplicating it as a second BUILD, is itself the institutional
enactment of this boundary: knowledge fusion IS a real, distinct capability (which is why it survives
as part of B2's object rather than being rejected outright), but it is real specifically BECAUSE it
does something CO-05 and GK-02 structurally cannot — preserve complementary, non-redundant fragments
with provenance — and it is folded into B2 rather than standing alone specifically because it shares
an engine (Part III) and a taxonomy (Part II) with synthesis, not because it duplicates either
existing system. The MERGE verdict and the BUILD verdict for candidate 13 land in the same dataset
for architectural reasons (shared engine), not because fusion and synthesis are the same operation —
Part V already established they are siblings, not identical twins.

6.8 Failure mode specific to this boundary: silent misrouting, and its detection signal. The
single failure this Part's mechanism exists to prevent, named explicitly and given a detection
signal rather than left as an abstract risk: SILENT MISROUTING occurs when the scanner (Part IV
§4.2) proposes a candidate to the fusion pass without first clearing the identity-versus-
complementarity test of §6.4 — for instance, because the two source records' domain tags differ
superficially (one calls the entity "System Alpha," the other "the Alpha router," per the §6.6
worked GK-02 case) even though they in fact name the same object, and the scanner's structural
match treats the surface difference as evidence of two DIFFERENT, complementary facets rather than
one entity under two names. The consequence, if uncaught: a fusion record publishes that
"combines" what is actually a single object's two mis-attributed names, producing an artifact that
reads as richer than it is — a composite whose PROVENANCE MAP (Part V §5.3) correctly lists two
sources, but whose UNIFIED OBJECT field is, on inspection, one object double-counted as two, which
is the CO-05/GK-02 failure mode imported into B2's own output. The DETECTION SIGNAL, DEFINED: a
fusion record whose two fragments, when checked against IAS-F1's own addressing scheme (F1 Part
IX), resolve to the SAME address once canonical names are checked — a check the fusion pass runs
as a mandatory pre-publication gate, not an optional audit, specifically because §6.5 already
establishes that absorbing GK-02's authority even by accident violates Invariant Three regardless
of intent. A fusion candidate that fails this check at publication time is not published as a
weaker fusion; it is re-routed to GK-02 for canonicalization, exactly as §6.5 requires, and the
misrouting is logged as a near-miss in B2's own lifecycle tracker (Part XIII §13.6) so a recurring
pattern of near-misses against the same alias pair becomes visible as a signal that GK-02's own
canonicalization coverage, not B2's routing discipline, has a gap upstream.

FINAL LAW — PART VI. Dedup and canonicalization resolve the SAME object wrongly represented as two
into one; fusion resolves DIFFERENT, complementary facets of a related object into a composite that
keeps both, with provenance — and the identity-versus-complementarity test is the one mechanical
question that tells an implementer, in every case, which of the three operations a candidate record
actually needs.

PART VII — PATTERN TRANSFER BETWEEN DOMAINS: MECHANISM AND THE TRANSFER OPERATOR
===============================================================================================

7.1 Distinguishing pattern transfer from pattern recognition. Part II §2.4 defined a PATTERN as a
claim shape that recurs across domains; Part IV's scanner and matcher DETECT such recurrence. This
Part defines a further, forward-looking operation the ledger names separately (candidate 18, Pattern
Transfer, MERGE-routed into B2): TRANSFER, DEFINED, is the deliberate application of a pattern
CONFIRMED in one domain to a SECOND domain where it has not yet been checked, in order to generate a
TESTABLE PREDICTION in that second domain — not a retrospective observation that a pattern already
recurred, but a prospective claim that it SHOULD recur, stated before the second domain's own data
has been consulted for it. (DERIVED: recognition looks backward across data that already exists;
transfer looks forward, proposing where else the SAME mechanism ought to apply, and is therefore
falsifiable in a way recognition alone is not.)

7.2 The transfer operator, formalized. Given a validated pattern P, holding in domain D1 with
mechanism M (Part IV §4.3's mechanism-level confirmation), the TRANSFER OPERATOR, DEFINED, proposes
that P should also hold in domain D2 if and only if D2 contains an object that plays the SAME
structural role as P's subject-class in D1, under the SAME relation, for the SAME reason M — not
merely a superficially similar-sounding domain. (NORMATIVE: transfer is licensed by shared MECHANISM,
never by shared VOCABULARY or shared SURFACE DOMAIN; two domains that both happen to use the word
"router," for instance, are not thereby transfer-eligible unless the underlying mechanism —
what makes the pattern true in D1 — is actually present in D2's structure.) This is the same
mechanism-versus-surface discipline Part IV §4.3 applies backward (confirming an existing match); the
transfer operator applies the identical discipline forward, generating a new candidate rather than
validating an old one.

7.3 Why transfer is riskier than recognition, and why the engine marks it accordingly. A recognized
pattern (Part II §2.4) is grounded in data that already exists in both source domains; a transferred
prediction is grounded in data that exists in only ONE domain and is being extrapolated to a second
where it has not yet been checked. The engine therefore marks every transfer-operator output with
epistemic level HYPOTHESIZED, never DERIVED, regardless of how strong the source pattern's own
corroboration score (Part IV §4.4) is — because the strength of the SOURCE pattern says nothing about
whether the TARGET domain actually contains the structural analog the operator assumes it does, and
that assumption is exactly the part that has not yet been checked. (DERIVED from Article V: an
epistemic marker attaches to the specific claim being made, not to the pedigree of the reasoning that
produced it; a HYPOTHESIZED transfer built on a well-corroborated PRINCIPLE is still HYPOTHESIZED,
because the transfer step itself introduces new uncertainty the source pattern's own corroboration
cannot retire.)

7.4 The transfer lifecycle: propose, then require empirical closure. A transfer candidate does not
publish as a sealed insight; it publishes as a TESTABLE PREDICTION record — DEFINED as an insight
record (Part III §3.5) with an additional required field, a CLOSURE CONDITION: the specific
observation that, if made in the target domain, would confirm or disconfirm the transfer. This
mirrors ACIS's own falsifier discipline by design (imported by reference, not rebuilt), because a
transferred pattern that cannot state what would prove it wrong is not a prediction, it is a
speculation wearing a prediction's grammar. The prediction record routes, per Part XIII's lifecycle,
to whichever system owns the target domain's own evidence — often the domain's own dataset authors,
or, where the target domain is itself an IAS sibling, that sibling's own evaluation mechanism — and
the transfer is only promoted from HYPOTHESIZED prediction to a confirmed cross-domain PATTERN once
that closure condition is actually observed, never on the strength of the source domain's
corroboration alone.

7.5 Worked example: transferring a reliability-domain pattern into an economics-domain target.
Illustratively, and without asserting this as a validated finding: suppose a confirmed pattern in
IAS-E2's reliability family (once built) establishes that a FAILURE BUDGET — a bounded, spendable
allowance for a known failure rate — produces better operator behavior than a bare "minimize
failures" directive, because a budget makes the acceptable failure RATE explicit and stops
operators from over-investing in marginal reliability past the point of diminishing return. The
transfer operator would ask: does IAS-C1's capability-economics domain contain a structural analog —
some bounded, spendable allowance whose explicit statement would similarly prevent over-investment
past diminishing return? If IAS-C1 already has an unstated version of this — teams informally
capping investment in a mature capability without a named mechanism for why — the transfer predicts
that NAMING an explicit "investment budget" analogous to a failure budget would produce the same
behavioral improvement in the economics domain. The closure condition would be a specific,
observable test: does a system whose investment is bounded by an explicit budget receive
measurably more disciplined allocation than a comparable system without one, across a sample the
economics domain can actually observe. Until that observation is made, the transfer remains a
HYPOTHESIZED prediction — a candidate for IAS-C1 to test, not a settled fact IAS-B2 has established
on IAS-C1's behalf.

7.6 The transfer operator's relationship to D2A's `abstract` operation, and why it is not the same
op. The ledger notes candidate 17, Cross-System Abstraction, overlaps D2A's own `abstract` operation
(one of D2A's fifteen dispositions) and routes it to SUBENGINE status under B2 rather than treating
it as identical. The distinction, DERIVED and stated precisely here because Part VIII returns to it
in full: D2A's `abstract` operation fires on a SINGLE overlap event — two specific proposals or
capabilities that a live duplication review has already identified — and produces a shared
abstraction for THAT pair. The transfer operator, by contrast, fires PROSPECTIVELY across the whole
estate's confirmed-pattern inventory, without waiting for a duplication event to trigger it, and its
output is a PREDICTION about an as-yet-unobserved domain, not an abstraction resolving an
already-identified pair. D2A's `abstract` is reactive and pairwise; B2's transfer operator is
proactive and estate-wide — related in spirit (both generalize beyond a single instance) but
operating on different triggers, different inputs, and different authority (D2A's abstract concludes
a live duplication review with authority to actually merge two proposals; B2's transfer concludes
with a hypothesis routed for someone else's test, holding no merge authority at all).

7.7 Failure risk specific to transfer: analogy without mechanism. The single most common way transfer
goes wrong, named here ahead of Part XVIII's full catalogue because it is transfer-specific rather
than a general synthesis failure, is ANALOGICAL OVERREACH: proposing a transfer on the strength of a
compelling-sounding analogy whose surface similarity is real but whose underlying mechanism (§7.2's
requirement) is absent. "Both domains involve routing decisions" is a surface similarity; it licenses
a transfer only if the SPECIFIC mechanism that made the source pattern true — not "routing" in
general, but the specific causal story of why THIS pattern held — is actually present in the target.
The mechanism-level test of §7.2 exists precisely to catch this before a prediction record is
published, and a transfer candidate that fails the test is not published as a weaker-confidence
prediction; it is discarded, because a transfer without a shared mechanism is not a bounded-confidence
claim, it is not a claim of this taxonomy's type at all.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. A pattern may be transferred to a new domain only when the target contains the
same structural role under the same mechanism that made the source pattern true — never on shared
vocabulary or surface resemblance — and every transfer publishes as a falsifiable, HYPOTHESIZED
prediction with a named closure condition, never as a settled fact borrowed from its source domain's
corroboration.

PART VIII — PATTERN TRANSFER: WORKED EXAMPLES ACROSS SYNTHETIC CROSS-DOMAIN DATA
===============================================================================================

8.1 Purpose of this Part. Part VII specified the transfer operator's mechanism; this Part exercises
it against a set of synthetic, illustrative cross-domain scenarios chosen to cover the operator's
main behavior modes: a successful transfer, a correctly-rejected analogical-overreach candidate, a
transfer that requires a boundary condition rather than a clean yes/no, and a transfer that closes
(is confirmed or disconfirmed) after publication. None of the four scenarios asserts a real finding
about the actual PP estate; each is constructed purely to demonstrate the mechanism operating on
concrete, traceable inputs, consistent with the worked-example discipline IAS-F1 itself uses (F1
Part V §5.7, Part VI §6.9).

8.2 Scenario one — a successful transfer, source to target, fully traced. Source pattern, domain D1
(illustrative "session-recovery" family): "a recovery mechanism that validates its target state
BEFORE resuming outperforms one that resumes first and validates after, because a pre-validated
resume avoids compounding an already-corrupted state." Mechanism M: sequencing the check before the
action prevents the action from amplifying an undetected fault. Candidate target domain D2
(illustrative "deployment" family): does D2 contain an object playing the same structural role — an
action that could compound an undetected fault if performed before validation? Yes: a deploy step
that pushes a build before confirming the build's own integrity plays exactly this role, under the
same mechanism (pre-action validation prevents fault amplification). The transfer operator proposes:
"a deploy pipeline that validates build integrity before push should show fewer compounded
production failures than one that validates after." Closure condition: comparing compounded-failure
rates between pre-validated and post-validated deploy pipelines within domain D2's own observable
history. This transfer passes the mechanism test cleanly because the underlying causal story —
sequencing prevents amplification — is structurally identical in both domains, not merely
verbally similar.

8.3 Scenario two — a correctly-rejected analogical-overreach candidate. Source pattern, domain D1
(illustrative "knowledge-canonicalization" family, GK-02-adjacent): "choosing ONE canonical name for
an entity, with aliases pointing to it, reduces downstream confusion more effectively than allowing
multiple co-equal names to persist." Surface-tempting target, domain D3 (illustrative "capability
portfolio" family): a naive transfer might propose "choosing ONE canonical SYSTEM to provide a given
capability, retiring redundant providers, reduces downstream confusion" — the vocabulary ("canonical,"
"reduces confusion") lines up superficially. Applying §7.2's mechanism test: the source pattern's
mechanism is about NAMING — multiple names for one referent create lookup ambiguity with no
functional cost to keeping the referent itself singular. The target case is different in kind: two
systems providing the same capability are not a naming ambiguity, they are a REDUNDANCY-VERSUS-
RESILIENCE trade-off (IAS-D1's territory, per the ecology family) where eliminating the second
provider has a real functional cost (loss of redundancy) that eliminating a mere alias never has. The
transfer operator correctly REJECTS this candidate at the mechanism-check stage: the surface analogy
("pick one canonical X") is present, but the underlying mechanism is not the same, and publishing the
prediction would be exactly the analogical-overreach failure Part VII §7.7 names. This scenario exists
specifically to show the operator's rejection path working, not only its acceptance path.

8.4 Scenario three — a transfer requiring a boundary condition rather than a clean yes/no. Source
pattern, domain D4 (illustrative "immune/failure-federation" family): "a failure fingerprint shared
across projects immunizes exposed projects faster than a failure discovered independently in each."
Candidate target, domain D5 (illustrative "demand-forecasting" family): does sharing a DEMAND SIGNAL
across projects similarly improve forecast accuracy faster than each project forecasting
independently? The mechanism test finds a PARTIAL match: the underlying mechanism in D4 is that a
failure's SIGNATURE is largely project-INVARIANT (the same bug shape recurs regardless of which
project hits it first), which is why sharing it transfers cleanly. In D5, a demand signal is
frequently NOT project-invariant — one project's spike in a particular capability's demand may be
specific to that project's mission mix and may not predict another project's demand at all. The
transfer operator, applying the mechanism test honestly rather than forcing a binary verdict,
publishes a BOUNDED prediction: "sharing demand signals across projects should improve forecast
accuracy specifically for demand drivers that are estate-structural (e.g., a new dataset sealing,
which affects every project's available capability set equally) and should NOT be expected to help
for project-specific mission-mix drivers." This is the taxonomy's Part II §2.6 distinction in
action at the transfer level: rather than force scenario three into scenario one's clean
acceptance or scenario two's clean rejection, the operator outputs the honest, narrower, bounded
form — the same discipline that keeps a pattern from being inflated to a principle without earning
it.

8.5 Scenario four — closure after publication, both directions. Continuing scenario one: suppose the
prediction publishes and, per Part VII §7.4's lifecycle, routes to the illustrative deployment
domain's own evaluation mechanism. Two outcomes are worth tracing because they are handled
differently. CONFIRMING CLOSURE: the deployment domain's own data shows pre-validated pipelines with
measurably fewer compounded failures — the prediction record is updated from HYPOTHESIZED
(prediction) to DERIVED (confirmed cross-domain pattern, per Part II §2.4), and — because it is now
confirmed in a SECOND domain independent of its source — its corroboration score (Part IV §4.4)
strengthens, potentially qualifying it for the pattern-to-principle extraction procedure of Part IX.
DISCONFIRMING CLOSURE: the deployment domain's data shows NO measurable difference — the prediction
record is updated to CONTESTED or explicitly marked FALSIFIED-IN-THIS-TARGET, and, critically, this
outcome is NOT discarded as a failed run; it is published with equal rigor to a confirming outcome,
because a disconfirmed transfer is itself information — it tells the estate that the source pattern's
mechanism, while real in D1, has a boundary the estate did not previously know, refining the pattern's
own scope rather than merely failing silently. (NORMATIVE: disconfirmation is a first-class output of
this dataset, not a discarded null result — an engine that only ever reports successful transfers is
producing survivorship-biased output, one of the adversarial risks catalogued in Part XVII.)

8.6 Operational guardrails observed across the four scenarios. Running the transfer operator at
estate scale (not as a one-off worked example) surfaces failure modes the single-scenario walkthrough
does not, and each of the four scenarios above maps to one: (a) scenario one's clean acceptance is
the ONLY shape safe to auto-publish without a second reviewer, because the mechanism match is
structural and traceable end to end; (b) scenario two's rejection is the shape most likely to be
SKIPPED by an under-resourced scanner, because rejecting costs the same compute as accepting but
produces no visible output — a scanner tuned to "ship more transfers" will under-invest in the
mechanism check specifically where it matters most, at the surface-tempting rejection boundary; (c)
scenario three's bounded prediction is the shape most often mis-recorded as a binary pass, because
downstream consumers (a dashboard, a summary table) frequently collapse a bounded prediction into a
single confirmed/rejected cell, silently discarding the scope qualifier that made the transfer honest
in the first place — the antidote is that the bounded form is a required output type, not a
fallback, and any consumer that cannot render it is itself the defect; (d) scenario four's
disconfirming closure is the shape most vulnerable to survivorship bias in reporting — an estate
dashboard that only surfaces confirmed transfers, because confirmed transfers are the ones people
want to see cited, quietly trains the next synthesis pass to under-report disconfirmations, which
degrades the corroboration scorer's calibration (Part IV §4.4) over multiple cycles even though no
single cycle looks wrong in isolation. 8.7 Metrics worth tracking per scenario type going forward:
acceptance rate at the mechanism-check gate (should NOT trend toward 100% — a gate that never
rejects is not testing anything), the ratio of bounded-to-binary predictions (a healthy estate
should show a non-trivial bounded share, because most real cross-domain transfers ARE partial), and
the confirm:disconfirm ratio on closure (a ratio that drifts to all-confirm over time is the
Goodhart signal that the closure step itself has stopped being adversarial, addressed further in
Part XIX). None of these three metrics is meaningful alone; together they triangulate whether the
worked-example discipline demonstrated in 8.2 through 8.5 is still operating honestly once the
dataset is no longer being read one scenario at a time by a careful human reviewer but processed at
volume by the synthesis engine itself.

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. A worked transfer either passes the mechanism test and generates a genuine,
falsifiable prediction, fails it and is correctly discarded before publication, or passes partially
and generates an honestly bounded prediction — and whichever closure a published prediction later
receives, confirming or disconfirming, is recorded with equal rigor, because a disconfirmed transfer
still teaches the estate where a pattern's true boundary lies.

PART IX — PRINCIPLE EXTRACTION ACROSS SYSTEMS: METHOD AND RELATION TO THE ACIS LAW SCHEMA
===============================================================================================

9.1 Returning to the pattern-to-principle step, now with full mechanism. Part II §2.5 named the
move from PATTERN to PRINCIPLE as the taxonomy's single most consequential step and deferred its
procedure to this Part. EXTRACTION, DEFINED, is the procedure that takes a pattern already confirmed
in two or more domains (whether by direct recognition, Part IV, or by successful transfer closure,
Part VIII §8.5) and determines whether it should be stated as a PRINCIPLE — a proposition asserted
to hold generally across the estate's relevant domains, not merely in the specific domains checked so
far — or whether it should remain a bounded, multi-domain INSIGHT that makes no claim beyond the
domains actually tested.

9.2 The extraction test, three conjunctive conditions. A pattern is promoted to principle status
only if it clears THREE conditions together, none sufficient alone. First, DOMAIN BREADTH: the
pattern holds, with the same mechanism (Part VII §7.2's standard), across at least three
INDEPENDENTLY-authored domains — two is the taxonomy's floor for a bounded insight (Part I), but
three is required before the extraction procedure even opens, because two domains cannot
distinguish "generalizes" from "happened to coincide twice." Second, SURVIVED DISCONFIRMATION: the
adversarial search of Part III §3.4 — extended, at this stage, to search SPECIFICALLY for domains
where the pattern's own preconditions are met but the pattern does NOT hold — returns empty, or
returns exceptions that themselves resolve into a stated, non-arbitrary boundary condition (as in
Part VIII §8.4) rather than an unexplained gap. Third, MECHANISM STABILITY: the causal story that
explains WHY the pattern holds is the same story across all checked domains, not a different story
in each that happens to produce a similar-looking pattern — a requirement that rules out what might
be called "coincidental convergence," where unrelated mechanisms happen to produce structurally
similar patterns without sharing an underlying cause.

9.3 Why three conjunctive conditions and not a weighted score. A weighted-score approach — say,
breadth counts for 40%, disconfirmation for 40%, mechanism stability for 20%, promote above a
threshold — would let a pattern with excellent breadth but a shaky, inconsistent mechanism story
still cross a numeric threshold, which is precisely the false-precision risk Part IV §4.4 already
rejected for the corroboration scorer. (NORMATIVE: extraction uses a conjunctive gate, not a
weighted sum, because a principle whose mechanism story does not hold together is not a weaker
principle, it is not a principle — the same reasoning IAS-F1 applies to systemhood itself, Part II
§2.1, where the four tests are conjunctive and a system failing even one test is not a "partial
system," it is a component or a duplicate.)

9.4 The relation to ACIS's law schema, precisely bounded. ACIS, per the project MEMORY's Generation
Zero entry, already owns an epistemic ladder with a schema requiring a stated FALSIFIER before a
claim promotes toward LAW status, and a No-Autopromotion discipline that caps promotion absent
independent verification. IAS-B2's extraction procedure is DESIGNED to produce exactly the input
ACIS's own ladder is built to consume: a PRINCIPLE that has already cleared domain breadth,
disconfirmation, and mechanism stability arrives at ACIS's gate with most of the epistemic labor
already done, but — and this is the boundary that keeps B2 from duplicating ACIS's authority — B2's
extraction procedure does not itself assign a FALSIFIER in ACIS's specific schema format, does not
run ACIS's own promotion ladder, and does not grant law status. B2 hands ACIS a well-evidenced
CANDIDATE; ACIS decides, under its own authority (IAS-F1 Invariant Three), whether and how it
becomes a law. This is a strengthens relation, not a merge or an overlap: ACIS's ladder produces
higher-quality laws faster because it is fed candidates that already survived a real disconfirmation
search, rather than having to run that search itself on raw, unvetted claims. (DERIVED, and the
exact mechanism the Strengthening Matrix's IAS-B2 row cites as evidence: "one insight traceable to
>=2 datasets, present in neither" — a criterion B2's extraction procedure satisfies by construction
whenever it promotes a candidate to principle status.)

9.5 Worked extraction, closing the advisory-authority thread from Parts I, II, and IV. Returning to
the illustrative advisory-authority-enables-fast-correction pattern, now confirmed (illustratively)
in the cost-routing and immune-system domains and, per a successful Part VIII-style transfer, closed
CONFIRMING in a third, illustrative "architecture drift" domain (where a system whose flagged-drift
findings are advisory rather than binding is corrected faster by a downstream architecture reviewer
than one whose findings are auto-applied without review). Domain breadth: three independently-
authored domains, condition cleared. Disconfirmation: the search specifically probes domains where a
system HAS advisory authority but correction is NOT fast — and, illustratively, finds one such case,
where an advisory-authority system's output is simply never read by anyone downstream, resolving into
the bounded precondition already surfaced in Part IV §4.5 ("advisory authority speeds correction ONLY
when a downstream reader actually consumes the output") — a stated, non-arbitrary boundary, not an
unexplained gap, so this condition is cleared WITH the boundary attached. Mechanism stability: all
three confirming domains share the identical causal story — visibility-before-action lets an error be
caught before it compounds — so this condition clears cleanly. The extraction procedure therefore
promotes the candidate to PRINCIPLE status, with its boundary condition attached as an integral part
of the principle's own statement (never dropped for tidiness), and routes it to ACIS as a law
candidate per §9.4 — again, illustrative throughout, asserting nothing about the real estate's actual
content.

9.6 What extraction explicitly refuses to do. Extraction does not lower its three-condition bar under
time pressure, does not average a strong pattern in two domains with a weak one in a third to reach an
apparent "three domains," and does not treat a transfer's own successful closure (Part VIII §8.5) as
automatically satisfying the disconfirmation condition — a confirmed transfer adds to domain breadth,
but the disconfirmation search must still be run fresh across the FULL estate, including domains the
transfer never touched, because a pattern confirmed in exactly the domains it was tested in has not
yet been searched for the domains where it might fail. (NORMATIVE: this refusal is what keeps
extraction from becoming a rubber stamp for whatever the transfer operator already produced, and it
is why extraction is a distinct procedure — Part IX — from transfer closure — Part VIII — rather than
the same step under two names.)

9.7 Failure modes specific to the extraction procedure, distinguished from the pattern-level failures
already catalogued in Part IV. Three recur in practice. UNDERCOUNTED DOMAINS: a reviewer counts two
superficially different datasets as "independently authored" when they in fact share an author, a
template, or a common upstream source — the domain-breadth condition then passes on paper while
failing in substance, because two datasets drafted from the same template are not independent
evidence, they are one piece of evidence copied twice; the guard is to check authorship and drafting
lineage, not merely dataset identity, before counting a domain toward the floor of three. NARROW
DISCONFIRMATION SEARCH: a reviewer under time pressure searches only the domains most similar to the
ones that already confirmed the pattern, which structurally cannot find a disconfirming case, because
the search space was chosen to resemble the confirming evidence — the guard is a disconfirmation
search protocol that explicitly samples DISSIMILAR domains first, on the theory that a pattern's
boundary is more likely to live at the edge of its applicability than at its center. MECHANISM
STORY DRIFT: a reviewer, having confirmed a pattern in three domains, retroactively adjusts the stated
mechanism to fit all three after the fact, rather than checking that the SAME mechanism was proposed
BEFORE the third domain was examined — this converts mechanism stability from a real constraint into a
post-hoc narrative fitted to already-known outcomes, and the guard is a pre-registration discipline:
the mechanism claim is written down at the second domain, before the third is searched, so the third
domain's result is a genuine test rather than a confirmation-shaped rationalization. 9.8 Interaction
with promotion pressure over time. As the estate accumulates more principles, there is a structural
incentive for extraction to loosen — a larger principle count reads, superficially, as a healthier
synthesis layer, and a reviewer facing that incentive may unconsciously relax the disconfirmation
search's rigor to keep the count climbing. The countermeasure is the same one Part XIX formalizes for
metrics generally: track the RATE at which extraction candidates are rejected, not only the count that
pass, and treat a rejection rate trending toward zero as a warning sign rather than a success signal,
because a gate that has stopped rejecting anything has, in practice, stopped gating.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. A pattern earns principle status only by clearing domain breadth, a genuine
disconfirmation search, and mechanism stability together, never by a weighted average across the
three, and even then the extraction procedure does not grant law status itself — it hands ACIS a
candidate that has already done the work ACIS's own falsifier discipline would otherwise have to do
from scratch.

PART X — CROSS-SYSTEM ABSTRACTION: SHARED ABSTRACTIONS ACROSS THE ENSEMBLE
===============================================================================================

10.1 What an abstraction is here, distinct from a principle. Part IX's PRINCIPLE is a proposition —
a statement that is true across domains. A CROSS-SYSTEM ABSTRACTION, DEFINED here as the object
candidate 17 names, is different in kind: it is a shared STRUCTURE or SCHEMA — not a claim about what
is true, but a common shape multiple systems could each be BUILT from, such that expressing each
system in terms of the shared abstraction makes their similarities and differences precise rather
than implicit. Where a principle answers "what holds across these domains," an abstraction answers
"what is the common architecture these domains are each instances of." IAS-F1's own property model
(F1 Part IV) is itself an example of exactly this kind of object at the ontology's own foundation
level — a single shared schema (cost, evidence level, reuse horizon, model-dependence, consumers,
authority) that every system's individual record instantiates differently, and IAS-B2's abstraction
subengine is what discovers CANDIDATES for this kind of shared schema in domains where no such schema
has yet been named, feeding them toward whichever system would own the resulting abstraction (often
F1 itself, for genuinely estate-wide schemas, or a specific sibling dataset for narrower ones).

10.2 The abstraction-discovery procedure. Given a set of confirmed patterns and fusion records (Parts
IV-V) that recur across three or more systems, the abstraction subengine asks a structurally different
question than extraction (Part IX): not "is this proposition true everywhere," but "do these several
systems' own internal structures share a common decomposition — the same kinds of parts, playing the
same kinds of roles, connected the same way — even though each system was built independently and
names its parts differently." (DERIVED: this is a structural-isomorphism question, not a truth
question, and it is answered by mapping each system's own internal architecture, as described in its
own dataset, onto a candidate shared schema and checking whether the mapping is faithful — every part
of the candidate schema finds a real counterpart in each system, and no part of any system is left
unaccounted for by the schema in a way that suggests the schema is incomplete.)

10.3 Worked example: a candidate shared abstraction across three illustrative pipeline-shaped systems.
Illustratively: suppose three unrelated estate systems each, independently, turn out to be built as a
CANDIDATE-VALIDATE-PUBLISH pipeline — a scanning stage that proposes unvalidated candidates, a
checking stage that tests each candidate against some form of adversarial or disconfirming search, and
a publication stage that commits only the survivors with a provenance trail. IAS-B2's own engine
(Part III) is one instance; suppose, illustratively, an unrelated quality-gate system and an unrelated
capability-discovery system independently turn out to share the same three-stage shape under different
names. The abstraction subengine would propose CANDIDATE-VALIDATE-PUBLISH as a shared abstraction:
naming it precisely lets a future system architect check any NEW proposed system against the schema
("does this new system have a genuine adversarial validation stage, or does it collapse candidate and
publish into one unchecked step") rather than re-deriving the same design question from scratch each
time, exactly the kind of leverage IAS-B1's Cognitive Multiplier would rank highly, because a named
abstraction, once discovered, applies to every FUTURE system built in the estate, not only the ones
that revealed it.

10.4 The boundary against D2A's own `abstract` operation, restated and sharpened from Part VII §7.6.
D2A's `abstract` disposition fires reactively, on a single live overlap review, and produces an
abstraction scoped to the two specific proposals under review at that moment; its authority extends
only as far as resolving that one duplication event, and its output is typically narrow by design
(broad enough to cover the two colliding proposals, no broader). IAS-B2's abstraction subengine fires
proactively across the estate's full confirmed-pattern and fusion-record inventory, is not triggered
by any single overlap event, and its candidate abstractions are explicitly ESTATE-SCOPED — proposed as
potentially relevant to systems that have not yet been built, not merely to the systems that
happened to trigger the discovery. B2 holds no authority to declare an abstraction canonical or
binding; per IAS-F1's addressing and authority discipline, a discovered abstraction is proposed,
carrying its own provenance (which systems it was derived from) and its own epistemic marker
(virtually always DERIVED or HYPOTHESIZED, never DEFINED, since a structural-isomorphism claim about
independently-built systems is itself an inference), to whichever system or Owner-facing review would
adopt it as a design standard — B2 discovers and proposes; it does not mandate.

10.5 Why abstraction discovery is harder to validate than principle extraction, and how the engine
compensates. A principle (Part IX) is falsifiable by finding a domain where it does not hold — a
relatively crisp test. An abstraction's faithfulness is falsifiable only by finding a system that
CLAIMS to fit the schema but whose actual internal structure does not map cleanly onto it — a softer,
more interpretive test, because "does this system's stage two really play the same role as that
system's stage two" admits more judgment than "does this proposition hold in this domain." The engine
compensates by requiring, for any abstraction candidate, the SAME blind-recognition check Part IV §4.3
specifies for pattern matching: an author of one of the source systems, shown the proposed shared
schema without being told which of their own system's stages is supposed to map to which schema
element, should be able to make the mapping themselves, unprompted, with the same result the
subengine proposed. An abstraction candidate that fails this check — where the mapping only seems to
work because the subengine's own description was written to force it — is discarded as
OVER-ABSTRACTION, catalogued fully among Part XVIII's failure modes.

10.6 What abstraction discovery deliberately does not attempt: prescribing a redesign. The
abstraction subengine's output is a NAMED, DESCRIPTIVE schema — it is not a recommendation that any
existing system be REFACTORED to more closely match the schema, and it explicitly does not carry that
authority. IAS-F1's Article IX (governed self-evolution) and Article X (no estate mutation) both bear
directly here: a discovered abstraction is knowledge about shared structure, and any decision to use
that knowledge to change an existing system's actual architecture belongs to whatever governed
evolution process the estate runs for that purpose (referenced, not owned, by B2), with its own
evidence threshold, validation, and rollback discipline. B2's abstraction output feeds that process as
an input; it is never self-executing.

10.7 Metrics and calibration for the abstraction subengine, distinguished from the extraction metrics
of Part IX. Because faithfulness is validated by a softer, more interpretive test than a
disconfirmation search, the abstraction subengine tracks a different pair of numbers. FALSE-MAPPING
RATE: the fraction of proposed abstractions that fail the blind-recognition check of §10.5 when an
actual author of a source system is asked to map their own system onto the schema unprompted — a
rate near zero across many trials should read as a warning that the check itself has gone soft (an
author primed by prior exposure to the schema's description is not really blind), not as a sign of
engine excellence, mirroring the same Goodhart caution Part IX §9.8 raises for extraction. SCHEMA
REUSE COUNT: how many times a named abstraction is subsequently cited by a NEW system's own design
review, which is the actual leverage event IAS-B1's Cognitive Multiplier framing cares about — an
abstraction discovered once and never referenced again has produced a name but no measurable
leverage, and a healthy abstraction layer should show reuse counts climbing over time as the estate's
system count grows, not staying flat. 10.8 A worked contrast: over-fitted versus genuinely general
abstraction. Illustratively, suppose a candidate abstraction is proposed from exactly two systems that
happen to share an author and a near-identical build date — the schema might describe their shared
shape with high fidelity, yet the blind-recognition check with a THIRD, unrelated author (someone who
built neither source system but is asked to evaluate whether the schema is a natural fit for their own
unrelated system) returns a poor match every time, because the schema was implicitly tuned to the
idiosyncrasies of the two closely-related source systems rather than to the structural feature that
would actually generalize. This is the abstraction-layer analogue of the extraction procedure's
undercounted-domains failure (Part IX §9.7): two non-independent examples can produce a
schema that reads as general but is not, and the guard is identical in spirit — check authorship and
build lineage of the source systems before trusting a two-system schema, and require a third,
genuinely independent confirming system before the abstraction is proposed estate-wide rather than
flagged as a narrower, tentative candidate awaiting a third data point.

FINAL LAW — PART X. An abstraction names the shared STRUCTURE several independently-built systems
turn out to share, not a claim about what is TRUE of them — it is validated by faithful mapping and a
blind-recognition check rather than by disconfirmation search, it is proposed estate-wide rather than
scoped to one overlap event, and it is knowledge for a governed redesign process to use, never a
mandate the discovering engine issues itself.

PART XI — ABSTRACTION WORKED EXAMPLE AND THE D2A `ABSTRACT` OP BOUNDARY, IN FULL TRACE
===============================================================================================

11.1 Purpose: closing the D2A boundary with one complete, side-by-side trace. Parts VII and X each
stated the D2A `abstract`-operation boundary in prose; this Part exists to trace one synthetic
scenario through BOTH paths side by side — what D2A's reactive `abstract` would do, and what B2's
proactive abstraction subengine would do, given the same raw material — so the distinction is
demonstrated operationally rather than only asserted, matching the discipline IAS-F1 applies at Part
VI §6.9 and Part VIII §8.7.

11.2 The shared raw material. Illustratively: two proposals arrive independently for review. Proposal
One describes a mechanism for deciding, when two candidate answer-providers could serve a mission,
which one to route to, based on a cost-versus-quality trade-off local to that single routing decision.
Proposal Two, submitted separately and without reference to Proposal One, describes a mechanism for
deciding, when two candidate deployment targets could receive a build, which one to deploy to, based
on a risk-versus-speed trade-off local to that single deployment decision. A live duplication review
flags these two proposals as overlapping — both are, at bottom, "pick between two options under a
trade-off" — and this overlap event is exactly D2A's trigger.

11.3 The D2A path. D2A's `abstract` operation, fired reactively on this specific overlap, examines
Proposal One and Proposal Two ONLY, and produces a shared abstraction scoped narrowly enough to
resolve THEIR overlap: something like "a two-option trade-off selector, parameterized by which two
axes are being traded (cost/quality for routing, risk/speed for deployment)." D2A's authority extends
to disposing of THIS overlap — likely a MERGE-INTO-FAMILY or EXTEND verdict, converting the two
proposals into one parameterized mechanism — and the abstraction it produces is a byproduct of
resolving the collision, scoped to exactly the two systems involved and not asserted to apply beyond
them. D2A does not, as part of this operation, scan the rest of the estate for a THIRD system that
might independently exhibit the same trade-off-selector shape; that is not its trigger and not its
object.

11.4 The B2 path, given the same raw material plus estate context D2A does not consult. IAS-B2's
abstraction subengine, running independently of any live overlap review, would have already indexed
both Proposal One's and Proposal Two's underlying shapes (once they seal as systems) alongside every
OTHER system's shape in the estate, per Part IV §4.2's scanner. Suppose, illustratively, a third,
unrelated system — an established one, sealed months earlier, governing which of two caching
strategies to apply under a memory-versus-latency trade-off — already exists in the estate and was
never flagged by any duplication review because caching and routing do not read as obviously
overlapping to a human reviewer. B2's abstraction subengine, having indexed all three shapes under the
same structural schema (two-option trade-off selector, parameterized by trade-off axis), proposes the
SAME abstraction D2A derived reactively for Proposals One and Two, but now GENERALIZED across three
independently-built systems, none of which cited each other, and crucially INCLUDING the caching
system D2A's reactive process had no reason to ever examine, because no duplication review was ever
triggered between caching and routing.

11.5 Why the two outputs are related but not redundant, precisely. D2A's abstraction is CORRECT and
SUFFICIENT for its own purpose (resolving the Proposal One/Two overlap) and requires no correction from
B2. B2's abstraction is a DIFFERENT, BROADER claim — that the shape recurs a third time, in a system
D2A's process never touched — and this is exactly the kind of finding Part I defined as unownable by
any resource-level system: D2A's object is one overlap event; it structurally cannot notice a third,
non-overlapping-on-the-surface system sharing the same underlying shape, because nothing about that
third system ever entered D2A's review queue. B2's estate-wide scan is the only mechanism in the
federation positioned to notice it. (DERIVED: this is the concrete, traced demonstration of the
Strengthening Matrix's claimed mechanism for the IAS-B2-to-D2A relationship — B2 supplies D2A's
`abstract` operation with cross-system abstraction candidates it could not discover from a single
overlap event — because D2A's own `abstract` output, once produced, becomes exactly the kind of
confirmed-pattern input B2's scanner indexes for its own broader search, and B2's broader finding, once
validated, becomes a resource D2A's FUTURE overlap reviews can consult to recognize a fourth instance
faster next time, closing a genuine feedback loop rather than a one-way dependency.)

11.6 The authority question, resolved for both directions of the loop. In the D2A-to-B2 direction, B2
holds no authority to override or second-guess D2A's disposition of the Proposal One/Two overlap — that
verdict is sealed under D2A's own authority the moment it is rendered, per IAS-F1's Invariant Three,
and B2's later, broader finding does not reopen it; B2's finding is additive context for FUTURE
reviews, not a challenge to a past one. In the B2-to-D2A direction, B2's estate-wide abstraction finding
(including the caching system) does not itself trigger a retroactive duplication review of the caching
system against the merged routing/deployment mechanism — sharing an abstraction is not, by itself,
evidence of DUPLICATION (IAS-F1's Invariant Two, unresolved duplicates, applies to systems governing
the SAME distinct object, and caching and routing govern different objects even while sharing a
trade-off-selector shape) — B2's finding is knowledge that the shape recurs, routed, per §10.6, to
whatever governed process might use it, not an automatic trigger for D2A to re-review a system that was
never actually a duplicate in the first place.

11.7 The general lesson this trace licenses. A reactive, single-event mechanism (D2A's `abstract`) and
a proactive, estate-wide mechanism (B2's abstraction subengine) can derive structurally IDENTICAL
abstractions from DIFFERENT triggers and DIFFERENT scopes, and the fact that their outputs can coincide
is not evidence of duplication between D2A and B2 themselves — it is exactly the expected behavior of
two systems operating at genuinely different levels (one resource-pairwise, one ensemble-wide) that
happen, on this occasion, to illuminate the same underlying structure from different starting points.
This is the single clearest illustration in this dataset of why the ensemble-object boundary (IAS-F1
Article II) is a boundary of OBJECT and TRIGGER, not of surface output similarity.

11.8 A second worked branch: what changes if the third system is discovered LATE. The trace in §11.4
assumed the caching system already existed in the estate when B2's scanner ran. Consider instead the
more common timing: B2's abstraction subengine proposes the two-option trade-off selector from
Proposals One and Two ALONE (having found no third instance at scan time), and the caching system is
only built and sealed months later. Under B2's own re-scan discipline (Part III §3.6's periodic
re-indexing), the NEW caching system is checked against the estate's existing library of proposed
abstractions as part of its own onboarding, not only against other systems' raw content — and the
match to the already-proposed trade-off-selector schema is caught at THAT point, strengthening the
abstraction's corroboration (now three confirming instances rather than two) without requiring any
human reviewer to have noticed the connection. This is the operational difference between an
abstraction subengine that only scans at proposal time and one that treats its own prior proposals as
a standing checklist every new system is measured against — the second design is strictly more
valuable, because most instances of a recurring shape are NOT discovered simultaneously, they trickle
in over the estate's build history, and a subengine that forgets its own past proposals the moment
they are logged would need to re-derive each abstraction from scratch on every new coincidence rather
than accumulating confirming evidence over time. 11.9 What this second branch implies for provenance.
Each time a new system strengthens an existing abstraction's corroboration, the abstraction's
provenance record (per Part V's fusion-object discipline) is APPENDED, not replaced — the original
two-system proposal, its date, and its original proposer remain visible in the record even after a
fifth or sixth confirming system joins, because collapsing the history down to only the current count
would erase exactly the trail that lets a future reviewer verify the abstraction was never quietly
re-derived to fit new data after the fact, closing the same provenance-integrity requirement Part V
§5.6 states for fusion objects generally and Part XVI's edge-case catalogue treats as a first-class
failure mode when it is violated.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. A reactive, pairwise abstraction and a proactive, estate-wide abstraction can
coincide in content without colliding in authority, because they answer different questions triggered
by different events at different scopes — and the estate-wide finding's genuine value is precisely the
system the pairwise process could never have been triggered to examine in the first place.

===============================================================================================
PART XII — THE DECISION MODEL: WHEN TO SYNTHESIZE VERSUS WHEN TO ABSTAIN
===============================================================================================

12.1 Why abstention needs its own decision model rather than being a default. The preceding Parts
specified how the engine produces a positive output — a pattern, an insight, a principle, a fusion, a
transfer, an abstraction. This Part specifies the opposite and equally load-bearing decision: when the
engine should produce NOTHING, because every one of the engine's positive outputs carries a
reputational cost if wrong (a downstream consumer that trusts a bad insight record inherits its error),
and a synthesis engine that never abstains is an engine that has never encountered a candidate too weak
to publish — which is itself evidence the abstention threshold is set wrong, not evidence the estate's
knowledge is unusually clean.

12.2 The four abstention gates, applied in fixed order. Gate one, INSUFFICIENT BREADTH: fewer than two
independently-authored domains support the candidate (Part I's own floor) — abstain, full stop, no
exception, because a single-domain claim is not a cross-dataset object under this dataset's own
definition and publishing it as one would misrepresent its type. Gate two, MECHANISM MISMATCH: the
matcher (Part IV §4.3) finds shared shape but not shared mechanism — abstain from synthesis, but route
the surface-similarity finding to a lower-stakes log (Part XVI's near-duplicate handling) rather than
silently discarding it, because a rejected candidate is itself a data point about where the estate's
domains merely LOOK similar without being causally connected, useful context for a future scan even
though it is not itself publishable. Gate three, FAILED DISCONFIRMATION SEARCH: a genuine
counterexample was found and does not resolve into a stated boundary condition (contrast Part VIII
§8.4, where a partial counterexample DOES resolve into a boundary and the candidate publishes bounded)
— abstain entirely on the pattern as stated, though a narrower, explicitly-scoped restatement may
re-enter the pipeline as a new candidate. Gate four, PROVENANCE INCOMPLETENESS: even a
mechanism-confirmed, disconfirmation-surviving candidate abstains if its provenance chain (Part III
§3.5, Part V §5.3) cannot be fully traced to its originating claims — an insight the engine cannot
prove its own reasoning path for is not a weaker insight, it is unpublishable regardless of how
plausible it sounds, because Article V's evidence discipline applies to the engine's own outputs with
the same force it applies to every other claim in the estate.

12.3 The decision model as a formal precedence, not a checklist to satisfy loosely. The four gates are
evaluated in the stated order specifically because gates one and two are TYPE questions (is this even a
cross-dataset candidate of the right shape) while gates three and four are EVIDENCE questions (given
the right type, does the evidence support publication) — and IAS-F1's own worked example at Part V §5.7
establishes the general principle this dataset imports directly: structural questions resolve before
evidential ones, because an evidential judgment about a candidate whose very type is unsettled is
premature. A candidate that fails gate one is never even evaluated against gates two through four; the
pipeline halts at the first failing gate, both to save computation (Part III §3.6's austerity
discipline) and to keep the abstention reason precise and auditable rather than a vague composite
score.

12.4 Confidence-calibrated publication below the principle threshold. Not every candidate that clears
all four gates earns PRINCIPLE status (Part IX's separate, stricter three-condition test); a candidate
can clear all four abstention gates and still publish only as a bounded INSIGHT (Part II §2.6), and
the decision model treats this as a SUCCESSFUL outcome, not a partial failure — the engine's job is to
publish exactly what the evidence supports, and an honestly-bounded insight is a complete, useful
output, never a consolation prize for a principle the evidence could not sustain. (NORMATIVE: framing
a bounded insight as a lesser achievement than a principle would create a systematic pressure to
inflate borderline candidates, precisely the Goodhart risk Part XVII's metrics section is built to
resist from the measurement side; the decision model resists it from the publication-framing side by
treating "correctly scoped" as the success criterion, not "maximally general.")

12.5 Abstention under resource pressure, and why it must never silently degrade quality instead. When
the estate's cognitive-capital budget (Cognitive OS, CO-01/02) is constrained, the correct response is
to run the engine LESS OFTEN or over FEWER candidate domains — never to relax the four gates to produce
the same volume of output more cheaply. (DERIVED from IAS-F1's Invariant Five, property honesty:
publishing more insights per unit of validation effort would mean each published insight's true
evidence level no longer matches its claimed epistemic marker, exactly the staleness-masquerading-as-
freshness failure the invariant forbids, generalized here from stale properties to under-validated
insights.) A resource-constrained engine that must choose between fewer, fully-validated insights and
more, partially-validated ones has exactly one correct choice under this dataset's own evidence
discipline, and Part XIX's metrics are constructed specifically so that "output volume" is never, on
its own, a metric this dataset rewards.

12.6 Worked trace: a candidate that abstains at gate three, and what happens to it afterward. Take an
illustrative candidate that clears gates one and two — two independent domains, confirmed shared
mechanism — but at gate three's disconfirmation search, a genuine, unexplained counterexample surfaces:
a third domain where the pattern's stated preconditions appear to hold but the predicted outcome does
not occur, and no boundary condition search (of the kind that rescued Part VIII §8.4's scenario three)
manages to explain the gap. The engine abstains on the candidate as originally stated. But abstention
here is not the end of the record: per §12.2's gate-two handling extended to gate three, the
counterexample itself, together with the original two-domain match, is logged as an OPEN QUESTION (a
distinct, lower-commitment object specified fully in Part XXII) — a flagged, unresolved tension the
estate now knows about even though no insight was published, which is a strictly better outcome than
either publishing an unreliable pattern or discarding the tension entirely, because a logged open
question is exactly the kind of seed a LATER scan, run after a fourth relevant domain seals, might
resolve either by finding the missing boundary condition or by discovering the original two-domain
match itself was coincidental after all.

12.7 The decision model's relationship to the engine's own authority, restated. Every abstention and
every publication decision under this model is exercised under the ADVISORY authority Part III §3.8
already bounds the engine to — the decision model decides what the ENGINE proposes, never what a
downstream consumer (FD-03, ACIS, IAS-B1) is obligated to accept. A downstream consumer retains its own
authority to reject even a candidate that cleared all four gates, and the decision model's job is
narrower and more precise than "decide what is true" — it is "decide what this engine has earned the
right to claim, at what confidence, with what boundary," leaving the final acceptance judgment where
IAS-F1's Invariant Three places it.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. Publication is earned by clearing four ordered gates — sufficient breadth, shared
mechanism, a survived or honestly-bounded disconfirmation search, and complete provenance — and a
candidate that fails any one abstains, with its failure logged as an open question rather than
discarded, because an honest abstention with a preserved trail is worth more to the estate than a
publication the evidence does not support.

===============================================================================================
PART XIII — THE SYNTHESIS LIFECYCLE: STAGES FROM CANDIDATE TO SEALED INSIGHT
===============================================================================================

13.1 Why a lifecycle Part, distinct from the architecture of Part III. Part III specified the engine's
three PIPELINE STAGES (scan, validate, publish) as a processing architecture; this Part specifies the
LIFECYCLE STATES a single candidate object passes through as it moves through that architecture and
beyond it into consumption — a distinction analogous to the difference between a factory's assembly
line (the architecture) and a single unit's journey from raw material to warehoused product (the
lifecycle), because the two views answer different questions: the architecture answers "what does the
engine do," the lifecycle answers "what state is THIS candidate in right now, and what can legally
happen to it next."

13.2 The eight lifecycle states, DEFINED in sequence. RAW: two or more claims have been indexed by the
scanner (Part IV §4.2) but no match has yet been proposed. CANDIDATE: the scanner has flagged a
structural match; the object is now visible to the matcher but unvalidated. MATCHED: the cross-
reference matcher (Part IV §4.3) has confirmed shared mechanism, not merely shared shape; the object
is eligible for the four abstention gates. GATED: the object has been run through Part XII's decision
model and has either cleared all four gates or been ROUTED-ABSTAINED (a state distinct from silently
discarded, per §12.6). SCORED: for objects that clear gating, the corroboration scorer (Part IV §4.4)
has assigned a tier; for identity-versus-complementarity candidates (Part VI §6.4) that resolve as
fusion rather than synthesis, this state is the lighter contradiction-check pass of Part V §5.6 instead.
PUBLISHED: a complete insight, fusion, or transfer-prediction record (Parts III, V, VII) exists with
full provenance and has been routed to a consumer per Part XIV or Part XV. CONSUMED: the routed
consumer (FD-03, ACIS, IAS-B1, or another) has taken its own action on the record — accepted it as a
deposit, opened its own promotion process, used it as a ranking input — an action outside B2's own
authority to compel, but a state B2's own record-keeping tracks so that a later scan does not propose
the same candidate again as if it were new. CLOSED: for transfer predictions specifically (Part VII
§7.4), the closure condition has been observed, confirming or disconfirming, and the record has been
updated accordingly (Part VIII §8.5).

13.3 Legal and illegal transitions. The lifecycle is a strict, forward-moving sequence with exactly
one designed exception: a GATED-abstained object may re-enter as a new CANDIDATE if, and only if, new
source material (a newly-sealed dataset, or a closed transfer prediction feeding back per §12.6) changes
the specific gate it failed — for instance, a boundary condition discovered later resolves what had
been an unexplained gate-three counterexample. No other backward transition is legal: a PUBLISHED
record is never silently un-published and re-scored with a different tier merely because a later scan
would have scored it differently under updated criteria; instead, a materially changed assessment
produces a NEW record that supersedes the old one explicitly, with both remaining in the provenance
trail, because silently rewriting a published record would violate the same append-only, auditable-
history discipline the estate already applies to its own knowledge base writes. (NORMATIVE, DERIVED
from Article V's requirement that a claim's evidence level be checkable against its history, not just
its current stated value.)

13.4 Where fusion and synthesis diverge in the lifecycle, restated operationally. Part V §5.6 already
noted fusion runs a lighter validation path; expressed in this Part's state machine, a fusion candidate
moves RAW to CANDIDATE identically to a synthesis candidate, but at the MATCHED state its "mechanism"
check is the identity-versus-complementarity test of Part VI §6.4 rather than Part IV §4.3's shared-
causal-story test, and it skips the full four-gate decision model of Part XII in favor of the narrower
contradiction check of Part V §5.3, moving directly from MATCHED to SCORED (its lighter corroboration
pass) to PUBLISHED. This is a genuine shortcut, not a corner cut: it is licensed specifically because a
fusion candidate makes no claim of GENERALITY the way a synthesis candidate does — it only claims that
two specific fragments about the same specific object do not contradict — and the heavier gates exist
to guard against overclaiming generality, a risk fusion structurally does not carry.

13.5 Trigger events and the standing versus one-shot distinction. Per Part III §3.7, the primary
trigger for a new scan is a dataset sealing — an event the Control Bus (IAS-A2) is positioned to carry
once built, and, until then, a manual or scheduled trigger the engine's operator invokes directly.
A SECONDARY trigger, specified here for completeness, is a CLOSED transfer prediction (§13.2's eighth
state): closure itself is new source material — a new confirmed-or-disconfirmed fact — and per §13.3's
re-entry exception, a closed prediction always re-triggers a fresh candidate-scan pass scoped to
patterns that touch the domain the closure occurred in, because a closure is exactly the kind of event
that can resolve a previously-abstained gate-three candidate elsewhere in the estate.

13.6 The lifecycle's relationship to provenance permanence. Every state transition APPENDS to the
object's provenance record (Part III §3.5's provenance set, Part V §5.3's provenance map) rather than
overwriting it — a CONSUMED object still carries its full RAW-through-PUBLISHED history, and a CLOSED
transfer prediction still carries the original prediction alongside its closure outcome, never replacing
one with the other. (DERIVED: this is the direct lifecycle-level enactment of Part V's core commitment
— provenance is never destroyed by a later processing step — extended from the fusion-specific case
Part V introduces to every object type this dataset produces.)

13.7 Worked lifecycle trace, end to end, for one illustrative candidate. A claim in a newly-sealed
dataset triggers a scan (RAW). The scanner finds a structural match against an existing claim in an
unrelated, established dataset (CANDIDATE). The matcher confirms shared mechanism (MATCHED). The
decision model runs: sufficient breadth (two domains, gate one clears), mechanism confirmed (gate two
clears), the disconfirmation search finds one partial counterexample that resolves into a stated
boundary condition per Part VIII §8.4's pattern (gate three clears, bounded), provenance is fully
traceable to both source claims (gate four clears) — GATED, cleared. The corroboration scorer assigns a
tier of MODERATE, given two-domain support with a bounded exception (SCORED). A complete insight record
publishes, routed to IAS-B1 as a leverage-ranking input per this Part's consumer-routing rule (PUBLISHED).
IAS-B1, under its own authority, incorporates the finding into its own multiplier ranking (CONSUMED). No
further transfer-prediction closure applies to this particular trace, so it does not reach CLOSED — a
reminder that CLOSED is specific to the transfer-prediction subtype (Part VII) and is not a universal
terminal state every object passes through; PUBLISHED-then-CONSUMED is itself a complete, correct
lifecycle for a bounded insight that was never a prospective prediction in the first place.

13.8 The interface to IAS-A2 (Control Bus), specified precisely rather than left as a forward
reference. §3.7 and §13.5 both name the Control Bus as the engine's primary trigger source "once
built," but neither Part specifies the CONTRACT precisely; this subsection closes that gap because
a lifecycle Part is exactly where a trigger's precise shape belongs. The contract, DEFINED: IAS-A2,
once built, emits a DATASET-SEALED event carrying, at minimum, the sealed dataset's identifier and
the list of Parts newly added or materially revised; B2's own listener consumes this event and
scopes its next scan (per §13.5's O(1)-new-domain design) to claim-shapes touching ONLY the named
Parts, never the whole newly-sealed dataset by default, because a dataset seal frequently touches
only a handful of Parts relative to its own prior stable content, and scanning the untouched Parts
again would violate the same incremental-cost discipline Part III §3.7 already establishes. Until
IAS-A2 is built, this contract degrades gracefully to the MANUAL trigger Part III §3.5 already
names — an operator-invoked scan scoped by hand to the same Parts a Control Bus event would have
named — which means the engine's lifecycle logic does not depend on IAS-A2's existence to function
correctly today; it depends on it only for the trigger to fire WITHOUT a human remembering to
invoke it. (DERIVED: this is the precise sense in which B2 is a CONSUMER of IAS-A2 rather than a
system whose own correctness is coupled to IAS-A2's build status — the RAW state of §13.2 is
reachable by either trigger path, and the lifecycle from RAW onward is identical regardless of
which one fired it, which is the specific design property that lets B2 ship and operate correctly
before IAS-A2 exists at all.)

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. Eight states, one designed re-entry exception, and a hard rule against silent
overwrite: every candidate's history from raw indexed claim to consumed record remains inspectable in
full, because a synthesis engine whose own past reasoning cannot be audited has no standing to ask any
other system in the estate to preserve provenance.

===============================================================================================
PART XIV — THE INSIGHT-TO-FD-DEPOSIT PIPELINE
===============================================================================================

14.1 Why insights need a named handoff to FD-03 rather than a generic "publish and hope." Part III
§3.5 named FD-03 as one of the engine's three consumer routes; this Part specifies the handoff
concretely, because an insight record that merely SITS in B2's own store, unread, has produced no
value — the Compounding Graph's amplification spine (COMPOUNDING_GRAPH.md) explicitly names this
edge ("IAS-B2 Insight Synthesis — cross-dataset insights feed new FD deposits / ACIS laws — strengthens
the whole estate's knowledge"), and a spine with an unrealized edge is exactly the orphan condition
Article IV forbids at the dataset level, here re-applied at the individual-record level: a PUBLISHED
insight that never reaches CONSUMED status is a record-level orphan even inside a non-orphan dataset.

14.2 What FD-03 needs from an insight record to accept it as a deposit, and what B2 supplies. FD-03,
per the Fable Distillation Suite's own doctrine (PR-FABLE-DELTA-ONLY-001: distill the delta, not the
answer), accepts deposits that name a specific DELTA — a change in understanding, not a restatement of
existing knowledge — with a clear lineage. IAS-B2's insight record (Part III §3.5) is built to supply
exactly this shape without modification: its STATEMENT is, by Part I's own definition, a proposition
absent from every single source dataset, which is precisely FD-03's delta requirement; its PROVENANCE
SET supplies the lineage FD-03's own doctrine requires (which specific datasets and sections the delta
traces to); and its EPISTEMIC MARKER carries forward unchanged, because FD-03 does not need B2 to have
already resolved the insight to a higher evidence level — FD-03's own distillation process applies its
own scrutiny on receipt, and B2's role ends at supplying a well-formed, honestly-marked candidate, not
at pre-deciding FD-03's acceptance judgment.

14.3 The routing rule: which insights go to FD-03 versus IAS-B1 versus ACIS. Not every published
insight routes to every consumer; the CONSUMER ROUTE field (Part III §3.5) is set by a simple,
DEFINED rule rather than sent to all three by default (which would burden every consumer with
irrelevant volume, the opposite of the leverage discipline IAS-B1 itself embodies). An insight routes
to FD-03 when its content is a knowledge-shaped delta suitable for the estate's distillation lineage
regardless of whether it has reached principle status. An insight routes to ACIS (Part XV, next) only
once it has cleared Part IX's full three-condition extraction test and reached PRINCIPLE status — a
bounded insight below that bar never routes to ACIS, because submitting an under-evidenced candidate
to ACIS's ladder would waste ACIS's own falsification effort on material B2's own gates were built to
filter first. An insight routes to IAS-B1 when its content bears specifically on LEVERAGE — how many
systems a candidate intervention would amplify — which is a routing judgment orthogonal to the
FD-03/ACIS split and can co-occur with either. A single insight record MAY carry more than one route
simultaneously (FD-03 and IAS-B1 together is the common case for a well-evidenced bounded insight;
FD-03, ACIS, and IAS-B1 together is the case for a fully-extracted principle), and the record's
CONSUMER ROUTE field is a set, not a single value.

14.4 The deposit's own epistemic trajectory after handoff, and why B2 tracks it. Once FD-03 accepts a
deposit, that deposit enters FD-03's own lineage and epistemic trajectory, which B2 does not control —
but B2's own lifecycle tracking (Part XIII §13.2's CONSUMED state) records the handoff and, where
FD-03's own downstream status is itself observable (a later scan finds FD-03's lineage referencing the
deposit), updates the original insight record's provenance forward to note it, closing a feedback loop
symmetrical to the one Part XI traced for D2A: B2 feeds FD-03, and FD-03's own subsequent treatment of
the deposit becomes new source material B2's NEXT scan can read, because FD-03's lineage is itself part
of the estate's stated content the scanner indexes (Part IV §4.2).

14.5 Worked trace: an illustrative deposit from insight to FD-03 lineage entry. Take the bounded
insight from Part XIII §13.7's worked lifecycle trace — MODERATE-tier, two-domain, boundary-conditioned.
Its consumer route, per §14.3's rule, includes FD-03 (it is a genuine knowledge-shaped delta) and IAS-B1
(it bears on leverage, since a pattern about authority-and-visibility plausibly generalizes to future
system design decisions). The FD-03 handoff supplies the deposit's required fields directly from the
insight record: the delta ("advisory authority speeds downstream correction, bounded by whether a
downstream reader actually consumes the output"), the lineage (the two illustrative source datasets and
specific sections), and the epistemic marker (DERIVED, carried forward unchanged from Part III §3.5's
requirement). FD-03's own process then applies its own distillation discipline to decide the deposit's
place in its lineage — a decision this dataset does not narrate further, because it belongs entirely to
FD-03's own authority and its own dataset's mechanism.

14.6 What happens when FD-03 rejects a routed insight. Rejection is a legal, expected outcome, not a
failure of B2's own gating — FD-03 may reject a deposit for reasons entirely internal to its own
lineage discipline (redundant with an existing deposit, insufficiently novel by FD-03's own delta
standard even though it cleared B2's cross-dataset novelty bar, or simply out of scope for FD-03's
current distillation focus). B2's lifecycle records the rejection as a distinct outcome from CONSUMED
(a REJECTED-BY-CONSUMER sub-state, still logged, still provenance-preserved per Part XIII §13.6) and
does NOT automatically resubmit the same record — resubmission without new material would be exactly
the abstention-under-pressure failure Part XII §12.5 forbids, applied here to a downstream rejection
rather than an internal resource constraint. A rejected insight remains available for OTHER consumer
routes it may carry (IAS-B1 in the worked example above is unaffected by an FD-03 rejection) and remains
available for future re-routing if FD-03's own scope or lineage changes later.

14.7 Why B2 does not attempt to pre-filter for FD-03's specific taste. A tempting but rejected design
alternative would have B2's own gating (Part XII) tuned specifically to predict FD-03's acceptance
criteria, minimizing rejection rate. This is rejected as a design choice because it would make B2's
publication threshold DEPENDENT on one consumer's current preferences, coupling a general-purpose
synthesis engine's own evidence discipline to a single downstream system's evolving taste — exactly the
kind of tight coupling the ensemble-object boundary (IAS-F1 Article II) warns against, since it would
make B2 a de facto subsystem of FD-03 rather than an independent, multiply-consumed system in its own
right. B2's gates (Part XII) are set by its OWN evidence discipline; FD-03's acceptance criteria are
FD-03's own, and the two are deliberately allowed to diverge, with rejection as the visible, honest
signal of that divergence rather than a hidden coupling that would blur the boundary between the two
systems' distinct authorities.

14.8 A metric for the FD-03 handoff's own health, and the countermetric that keeps it honest. The
natural first metric for this pipeline, DEFINED, is the DEPOSIT ACCEPTANCE RATE (DAR): the
fraction of insight records routed to FD-03 (per §14.3's rule) that FD-03 actually accepts into
its own lineage, tracked over B2's lifecycle record (§14.4). A rising DAR looks, on its face, like
evidence B2's gating (Part XII) is well-calibrated to FD-03's own standard — but §14.7 already
establishes that B2 must never TUNE its gates to chase FD-03's taste, which means a DAR pursued
directly, as a target to maximize, would create exactly the coupling §14.7 forbids: an engine
could raise its own DAR simply by routing ONLY the most conservative, least novel candidates to
FD-03, the ones least likely to be rejected precisely because they are least likely to carry a
genuine delta — inflating acceptance by starving FD-03 of its own hardest, most valuable cases.
The ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRIC, DEFINED: DAR is reported ALONGSIDE the mean CORROBORATION TIER
(Part IV §4.4) of the routed population, never alone, so a rising acceptance rate achieved by
routing only floor-tier, uncontroversial candidates shows up immediately as a falling mean tier in
the same report — the two numbers moving in opposite directions is itself the detection signal
that DAR is being gamed rather than earned. (NORMATIVE: this is the same pairing discipline Part I
§1.7 applies to the estate-wide CDIR metric, applied here at the single-pipeline level, because a
metric introduced without its countermetric in the same breath is, per this dataset's own
standard, not yet a metric this file is willing to publish.)

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. An insight record is built, from its first field, to arrive at FD-03's own
delta-and-lineage requirement without translation — but acceptance remains FD-03's own judgment, never
B2's to predict or pre-decide, and a rejection is logged as honest divergence between two systems'
independent evidence standards, not corrected away by tuning B2's own gates to one consumer's taste.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — THE PRINCIPLE-TO-ACIS-LAW PIPELINE
===============================================================================================

15.1 Why this handoff carries more weight than the FD-03 handoff. Where Part XIV's insight-to-FD-03
handoff can occur at any point once an insight clears Part XII's four gates, the principle-to-ACIS
handoff is gated behind the FULL extraction procedure of Part IX — domain breadth, survived
disconfirmation, mechanism stability — because ACIS's own ladder terminates, at its upper end, in LAW
status, the estate's highest epistemic commitment short of direct observation, and submitting
under-evidenced material to that ladder would either waste ACIS's falsification effort (§9.4) or,
worse, risk a weakly-evidenced candidate being promoted on the strength of ACIS's own process trusting
B2's extraction more than it has earned.

15.2 What ACIS needs from a principle candidate, and what B2 supplies. Per the project MEMORY's ACIS
Generation Zero entry, ACIS's epistemic ladder runs E0 through E7, with deposit-to-prove-to-UKDL-to-
hard-rule as a sealed promotion chain, and a No-Autopromotion discipline that DERIVES a candidate's
level from actual evidence rather than allowing self-assertion — critically, the same entry notes that
promotion to E4 (UKDL-level) specifically requires more than a single deposit's own internal
argument. B2's extracted principle is built to enter this ladder at a level ABOVE a raw single-domain
deposit precisely because it already carries what a single deposit cannot: multi-domain corroboration
(Part IX §9.2's breadth condition) and a documented, survived disconfirmation search (§9.2's second
condition) — the exact kind of cross-domain evidence ACIS's own No-Autopromotion discipline is
designed to require before granting a level the raw deposit could not earn alone. B2 does not assign
the ACIS level itself; it supplies the evidence package — statement, provenance across N>=3 domains,
disconfirmation log, mechanism-stability argument — that lets ACIS's own ladder assign the level under
ACIS's sole authority.

15.3 The FALSIFIER gap, named explicitly. ACIS's schema requires a stated falsifier before a claim can
be graded on its ladder at all; B2's extraction procedure (Part IX) produces a disconfirmation LOG —
what was searched and not found — which is evidentially adjacent to but not identical with a falsifier
in ACIS's specific schema sense (a falsifier states what future observation WOULD disprove the claim
going forward; a disconfirmation log states what past search did NOT find a counterexample). B2's
handoff package therefore includes the disconfirmation log as supporting evidence but explicitly does
NOT claim to have satisfied ACIS's falsifier requirement on ACIS's behalf — stating a proper falsifier
in ACIS's schema format is left to ACIS's own submission process, because falsifier construction
requires domain judgment about what a FUTURE test would look like, a forward-looking design choice this
dataset's own disconfirmation search (backward-looking, over existing estate content) does not
automatically supply. (NORMATIVE: this is a deliberate, named gap rather than an oversight, stated here
so a future implementer does not assume B2's disconfirmation log is a drop-in falsifier.)

15.4 Worked trace: the advisory-authority principle, from Part IX §9.5, entering ACIS's ladder.
Continuing the illustrative principle extracted in Part IX (three domains, one boundary condition,
mechanism-stable): the handoff package to ACIS includes the principle's statement with its boundary
condition attached ("advisory authority speeds downstream correction when a downstream reader consumes
the output"), its three-domain provenance set, and its full disconfirmation log (including the one
domain where the mechanism appeared present but the effect did not occur, and the boundary-condition
explanation that resolved it). ACIS's own process, under its own authority, would then need to (a)
construct a proper forward-looking falsifier — for instance, "if a fourth domain with an actively-
consuming downstream reader and advisory authority shows SLOWER correction than a comparable
final-authority system, the principle is falsified" — and (b) run its own No-Autopromotion check before
any level assignment. This dataset does not narrate ACIS's own decision further, because doing so would
be exactly the authority overreach Part III §3.8 and Part IX §9.4 both forbid: B2's trace ends at
delivering a well-formed, well-evidenced package, illustrated here only to show the package's shape, not
to predict or narrate ACIS's verdict.

15.5 Why B2 tracks ACIS submission outcomes without holding any authority over them. Symmetrically with
Part XIV §14.4's FD-03 feedback loop, B2's lifecycle tracking (Part XIII) records whether a submitted
principle was accepted onto ACIS's ladder, at what initial level, or declined, and — where declined —
whether the decline reason is itself informative for B2's own extraction procedure (for instance, if
ACIS repeatedly declines principles whose disconfirmation search covered fewer than four domains despite
clearing B2's own three-domain floor, that is a signal B2's Part IX §9.2 breadth threshold may be set
too low RELATIVE TO ACIS's own bar, prompting a candidate revision to B2's own gate calibration — a
governed-self-evolution proposal per Article IX, never a silent, unreviewed threshold change).

15.6 The asymmetry between the FD-03 and ACIS pipelines, stated plainly. The FD-03 pipeline (Part XIV)
accepts any insight clearing Part XII's four gates, a comparatively low and frequently-cleared bar,
producing a HIGH-VOLUME, lower-individual-stakes flow of deposits. The ACIS pipeline accepts only
extracted principles clearing Part IX's three-condition test, a comparatively rare and demanding bar,
producing a LOW-VOLUME, higher-individual-stakes flow of law candidates. (DERIVED: this asymmetry is
intentional and mirrors the estate's own general shape — many observations feed distillation, few
distillations become laws — and B2's dual pipeline is built to match that shape rather than to force
every candidate through the same gate regardless of its actual evidentiary weight.)

15.7 What B2 explicitly refuses to become: a second law-grading authority. The single clearest boundary
this Part exists to police, restated once more because it is the boundary most likely to erode under
convenience pressure over the corpus's life: B2 must never begin assigning its own epistemic levels in
ACIS's E0-E7 vocabulary, must never state that a principle "is" a law, and must never bypass ACIS's
submission process by routing a principle directly to a hard-rule proposal or a UKDL entry on the
strength of B2's own extraction alone. Every principle B2 produces remains, in B2's own vocabulary, a
PRINCIPLE — the taxonomy's fifth level (Part II §2.6) — until and unless ACIS's own ladder, under its
own authority, promotes it further; B2's own file never states otherwise about any real candidate it
might someday produce, and this dataset's own illustrative examples throughout are marked as
illustrative precisely so no future reader mistakes them for an actual ACIS submission this dataset
pre-empted.

15.8 Timing failure modes specific to the handoff, distinguished from the extraction-stage failures of
Part IX §9.7. Two recur even when the underlying principle itself is sound. PREMATURE SUBMISSION: a
principle is routed to ACIS the moment it clears Part IX's three conditions, without checking whether
ACIS's own ladder is mid-review on a closely related candidate — submitting a second, overlapping
principle while a first is still pending invites ACIS to do duplicate falsifier-construction work on
what may turn out to be the same underlying law from two angles, a waste of ACIS's own scarce
adjudication effort that B2's lifecycle tracker (Part XIII) is positioned to catch by checking open
submissions before routing a new one. STALE PACKAGE SUBMISSION: the inverse failure — a principle
clears extraction, sits in a submission queue for an extended period while the estate continues to
grow, and is submitted to ACIS with a disconfirmation log that no longer reflects the estate's current
content, because new systems built since the log was compiled were never checked against the
principle before submission; the guard is that B2's submission step re-runs a LIGHTWEIGHT
disconfirmation pass against any system sealed since the original extraction date, rather than
submitting a package whose "full estate" disconfirmation claim is, by the time ACIS reads it, already
out of date. 15.9 Why the asymmetry of §15.6 makes the ACIS pipeline more sensitive to both failures
than the FD-03 pipeline. Because the ACIS pipeline is low-volume and each candidate carries higher
individual stakes, a single premature or stale submission does proportionally more damage to ACIS's
trust in B2's packages than an equivalent lapse would do to FD-03's higher-volume, lower-stakes flow —
FD-03 can absorb an occasional weak deposit without much cost because volume dilutes any one deposit's
weight, but ACIS's ladder, evaluating far fewer candidates, would notice a pattern of stale or
duplicate submissions quickly, and per §15.5's feedback-loop discipline, a rising decline rate from
ACIS attributable to timing rather than evidentiary weakness is itself a signal that B2's submission
queue discipline, not its extraction procedure, needs the governed-self-evolution review.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. B2 delivers ACIS a principle candidate carrying multi-domain breadth, a survived
disconfirmation search, and a mechanism-stability argument no single deposit could supply alone — but
it does not construct ACIS's falsifier, does not assign an ACIS-ladder level, and does not narrate
ACIS's verdict, because the single most important thing B2 can do for the estate's highest-stakes
knowledge tier is hand it a stronger candidate, never a second authority over the same decision class.

PART XVI — EDGE CASES: NEAR-DUPLICATE INSIGHTS, PROVENANCE AMBIGUITY, CIRCULAR ATTRIBUTION, STALE DRIFT
===============================================================================================

16.1 Purpose: the recurring shapes that are neither clean successes nor clean failures. The preceding
Parts specified the engine's main paths; this Part catalogues four EDGE CASES — situations the engine
will genuinely encounter that do not resolve cleanly to publish-or-abstain under Part XII's gates alone,
each requiring a specific, named handling rule rather than being left to ad hoc judgment at
implementation time.

16.2 Edge case one: the near-duplicate insight. Two candidates, scanned in different passes (perhaps
separated by several dataset-sealing cycles), turn out to state substantially the same proposition in
different words, drawn from overlapping but not identical provenance sets. The handling rule, DEFINED:
before either candidate reaches PUBLISHED state, the engine runs a NEAR-DUPLICATE CHECK against its own
prior published insight records — not against the estate's raw claims (that is the scanner's job,
Part IV §4.2) but specifically against B2's OWN output history — using the same mechanism-level
comparison Part IV §4.3 applies to source claims. If the new candidate's mechanism matches an existing
published insight's mechanism, the new candidate does not publish as a separate record; instead, its
ADDITIONAL provenance (the new domain or claim it draws from that the original record did not) is
MERGED into the existing record's provenance set, strengthening the existing record's corroboration
tier (Part IV §4.4) rather than creating a redundant second record. (NORMATIVE: this is the specific
mechanism that keeps B2's own output from suffering the exact identity-versus-complementarity confusion
Part VI diagnoses in CO-05 and GK-02's domain — B2 applies its own boundary discipline to its own
output, not only to the estate's raw material, which is the reflexive consistency check a system
governing provenance-preservation owes to its own provenance records.)

16.3 Edge case two: provenance ambiguity when a source claim has itself been revised. A published
insight's provenance set names a specific dataset and Part; if that source dataset is later revised
(a Part rewritten, a claim's epistemic marker changed) after the insight already published, the
insight's provenance now points to content that no longer reads the way it did when the insight was
derived. The handling rule: the insight record's provenance entries are TIMESTAMPED and VERSIONED at
publication (an extension of Part III §3.5's provenance set, specified fully here), so a later revision
to the source does not silently invalidate the insight — instead, a scheduled or triggered
RE-VALIDATION check (using the same disconfirmation-search machinery of Part III §3.4, re-run against
the CURRENT source text) determines whether the insight still holds against the revised source. If it
does, the provenance entry is updated to the new version with a note that re-validation occurred and
passed; if it does not, the insight moves to a CONTESTED state (borrowing Article V's vocabulary
directly) pending resolution, never silently remaining PUBLISHED against a source that no longer
supports it. (DERIVED from IAS-F1's staleness discipline, Part IV §4.3 of the ontology, applied here to
derived claims rather than to system properties.)

16.4 Edge case three: circular attribution. A pattern is scanned across domains D1 and D2 and
published as insight I1. Later, a dataset revision to D2 CITES I1 (perhaps a domain author, aware of
B2's published insight, incorporates it into their own dataset's argument). A subsequent scan now finds
the pattern apparently corroborated a THIRD time — in D2's revised text — but this "third" corroboration
is not independent; it is D2 citing B2's own earlier synthesis of D1 and D2, which would, if uncaught,
let the engine inflate its own corroboration count by citing its own prior output as if it were new,
independent evidence — a specific, mechanical form of the SOURCE INDEPENDENCE failure Part IV §4.4
already names in general terms. The handling rule: every scan checks whether a candidate corroborating
claim's own provenance traces back, at any remove, to a PUBLISHED B2 insight record; if it does, that
claim is EXCLUDED from the corroboration count (it is not independent evidence, however textually
distinct it now appears) and is instead logged as DOWNSTREAM PROPAGATION of the original insight — a
genuinely useful signal (it shows the insight is being adopted) but a categorically different signal
from a corroboration, and the two must never be conflated in the corroboration scorer's tier assignment.

16.5 Edge case four: stale drift, where a once-valid pattern's mechanism erodes over time. A pattern
confirmed across two domains at extraction time may cease to hold in one of those domains months later
— not because the original extraction was wrong, but because the DOMAIN itself changed (a system was
refactored, a mechanism replaced) after the insight published. The handling rule: this is the SAME
re-validation mechanism as edge case two (§16.3), but triggered by a different signal — not a direct
revision to the cited Part, but the target domain's OWN independent aging, detected when a later,
unrelated scan happens to re-index the domain and finds its current claim shape no longer matches what
the insight's provenance recorded. Because this trigger is opportunistic (it fires only when some other
scan happens to touch the domain) rather than scheduled, B2's lifecycle explicitly does NOT promise
that every published insight is re-validated on any fixed cadence — a promise the engine's own token-
austerity boundary (Part III §3.6) cannot honestly make — and instead marks every published insight
with a "last re-validated" timestamp (mirroring IAS-F1's own property-staleness field, Part IV §4.3 of
the ontology) so a consumer can see, honestly, how long it has been since the insight's continued truth
was actually checked, rather than assuming an unchanged PUBLISHED status means recently-verified.

16.6 Why these four edge cases, and not a longer or shorter list. The four were selected because each
maps to a DISTINCT failure mode this dataset commits, in Part XVIII, to tracking end-to-end — near-
duplicates and circular attribution both feed spurious-synthesis risk (inflated apparent corroboration
from non-independent sources), while provenance ambiguity and stale drift both feed provenance-loss risk
(a record whose provenance no longer accurately describes its current support). A shorter list would
leave one of the two named failure modes without a concrete handling mechanism; a longer list, at this
dataset's current maturity, would risk enumerating hypothetical edge cases never actually observed
against real estate behavior, which the epistemic-honesty discipline (Article V) marks as HYPOTHESIZED
scope creep rather than DERIVED necessity — four is the set actually traceable to the two failure modes
this dataset is built to guard against, and Part XX names expanding this catalogue from real operating
history as an explicit open question for the engine's own evolution.

16.7 A consolidated detection signal across all four edge cases, because each was specified
independently but a single scan can trigger more than one simultaneously. The four handling rules
(§16.2-§16.5) were each defined against its own trigger in isolation; this subsection closes the
resulting gap by specifying what happens when a single candidate exhibits TWO of the four
conditions in the same pass — a case the preceding four subsections do not individually
anticipate. Illustratively: a candidate could simultaneously be a NEAR-DUPLICATE of an existing
published insight (§16.2) AND carry CIRCULAR ATTRIBUTION (§16.4), if the existing insight it
duplicates was itself the source a revised domain now cites. The ORDERING RULE, DEFINED: the
circular-attribution check (§16.4) always runs BEFORE the near-duplicate check (§16.2), because a
candidate whose apparent corroboration traces back to B2's own prior output must have that
non-independent evidence EXCLUDED first — otherwise the near-duplicate check would compare the
candidate against a published record using corroboration counts that already, silently, include
the candidate's own circular contribution, which would make the near-duplicate determination
itself unreliable. (DERIVED: ordering matters here specifically because §16.2's mechanism-
comparison and §16.4's exclusion both operate on the SAME candidate's provenance set, and running
them in the wrong order lets one check's output feed the other's input incorrectly.) The
consolidated MONITORING SIGNAL this ordering produces, useful independent of any single edge case:
a rising rate of candidates triggering TWO OR MORE edge-case conditions in the same scan is itself
evidence the estate's own dataset-revision cadence (feeding §16.3 and §16.5) is outpacing B2's
re-validation cadence, a resourcing signal distinct from any individual edge case's own handling
rule, and one this dataset flags for the governed-self-evolution review Part XV §15.5 already
establishes as the correct channel for adjusting B2's own operating parameters.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. A synthesis engine that never checks its own output for near-duplication, never
re-validates against a revised source, never excludes its own prior output from its own corroboration
count, and never marks a published insight's true validation age is an engine whose provenance
discipline applies to everyone's claims except its own — and that asymmetry is itself a provenance-loss
failure mode, not an exemption from one.

PART XVII — ADVERSARIAL CASES: INSIGHT LAUNDERING, FABRICATED SYNTHESIS, CONFIRMATION-BIASED TRANSFER
===============================================================================================

17.1 Why adversarial cases are distinct from edge cases. Part XVI's edge cases arise from the honest
operation of a correctly-built engine encountering genuinely ambiguous situations. This Part catalogues
ADVERSARIAL cases — situations where a candidate, a source, or a consumer's incentive could actively
exploit the engine's mechanism to produce a false or misleadingly-framed output, whether through
deliberate gaming or through an unintentional but systematic bias in how candidates are proposed or
read. Distinguishing the two matters because the remedy differs: an edge case needs a handling rule
(Part XVI); an adversarial case needs a DESIGNED RESISTANCE built into the engine's gates so the
exploit fails by construction, not merely by vigilance.

17.2 Adversarial case one: INSIGHT LAUNDERING. A single, weakly-evidenced claim, unable to clear its own
source dataset's local evidence bar, is deliberately phrased in two SEPARATE datasets in slightly
different vocabulary, specifically so that B2's scanner (Part IV §4.2) flags the pair as an independent
cross-domain corroboration — manufacturing apparent multi-domain support for a claim that was, in truth,
asserted once and then merely restated. This is the single most dangerous adversarial case named in this
dataset, because it directly exploits the exact mechanism (domain breadth, Part I's own definition) that
gives a cross-dataset insight its epistemic weight in the first place — it launders a single unverified
assertion into an apparently-independent, apparently-stronger claim purely through restatement. The
designed resistance: the SOURCE INDEPENDENCE check (Part IV §4.4, sharpened here) must verify not only
that the two datasets have different nominal authorship or sealing dates, but that the underlying
EVIDENCE each claim cites is independent — if both claims, traced to their own root justification, cite
the SAME underlying observation or the SAME original claim, they are not independent regardless of how
differently worded the restatements are, and the corroboration scorer must trace to that root rather than
stopping at the immediate claim text. (NORMATIVE: this is the single hardest-to-automate check this
dataset specifies, EXPERIMENTAL as a procedure, because tracing "underlying evidence" across arbitrarily
deep citation chains is itself a research problem; Part XX names improving this specific check as a
priority open question rather than claiming it is solved.)

17.3 Adversarial case two: FABRICATED SYNTHESIS. A candidate is proposed — whether by a malfunctioning
scanner, a corrupted summary in the audit-cache layer, or an adversarially-crafted dataset entry — whose
claimed source provenance does not actually support the stated mechanism when the cited Part is read in
full context, exploiting a scanner that matches on SHAPE (Part III §3.3) without a human or high-fidelity
check ever reading the cited source in full. The designed resistance is the blind-recognition check
already specified for pattern matching (Part IV §4.3) and abstraction validation (Part X §10.5),
generalized here as a MANDATORY gate before any PUBLISHED transition (Part XIII §13.2): no candidate
reaches PUBLISHED status without at least one verification pass that reads the cited source Part in full
(not merely its cached summary, invoking the bounded escalation Part III §3.6 permits specifically for
this purpose) and confirms the claim as actually stated, in context, rather than merely pattern-matched
by the summary layer. (DERIVED: this full-text confirmation step is the one place in the entire pipeline
where the token-austerity default of reading summaries is deliberately overridden, because the cost of a
fabricated, fully-published insight — which would then propagate to FD-03 or ACIS carrying B2's own
provenance-backed credibility — is categorically higher than the cost of one bounded raw read per
candidate that survives to publication, a small, late-pipeline set rather than the estate's full corpus.)

17.4 Adversarial case three: CONFIRMATION-BIASED TRANSFER. The transfer operator (Part VII) is asked, by
a downstream consumer or an Owner-facing request, to find support for a SPECIFIC, pre-desired conclusion
— "find a pattern that transfers to justify decision X" — rather than being run in its normal,
undirected mode across the estate's confirmed-pattern inventory. Under such a directed request, the
disconfirmation-first discipline (Part III §3.4, Part VII §7.2) is most at risk of being quietly
skipped or under-resourced, because the requester's own motivation creates pressure to find a
CONFIRMING transfer quickly rather than to search hard for the disconfirming case the discipline
requires. The designed resistance: the engine's decision model (Part XII) applies IDENTICALLY
regardless of whether a candidate arose from the engine's own undirected scan or from a directed
request, and — the specific structural safeguard — a directed request is logged, at CANDIDATE state
(Part XIII §13.2), with its REQUESTING PARTY and STATED PURPOSE as part of the record's own permanent
provenance, so that any published output from a directed request carries, visibly, the fact that it was
solicited rather than discovered, letting a downstream consumer weight that context appropriately even
though the gates themselves are not relaxed or tightened based on request origin. (NORMATIVE: origin-
transparency, not origin-based gate adjustment, is the correct resistance here, because adjusting gates
based on WHO asked would itself be a new, harder-to-audit bias.)

17.5 Adversarial case four: the PROVENANCE-STRIPPING ATTACK. A consumer, or a later editing pass on a
dataset the estate maintains, copies an insight's STATEMENT without its provenance set — presenting a
B2-derived claim as if it were a first-order, directly-observed fact in a NEW dataset, with no citation
back to B2 or to the original cross-dataset derivation. This does not corrupt B2's own records (which
remain intact per Part XIII §13.6's append-only discipline), but it corrupts the ESTATE's understanding
of the claim's true epistemic status, because a claim that was DERIVED (Part I §1.2's condition (b): not
stated in this form by any single dataset) now appears, in its new, provenance-stripped location, to be
DEFINED or OBSERVED — a downstream reader with no way to know otherwise. The resistance here is
necessarily weaker than the other three, because it defends against an action taken OUTSIDE B2's own
pipeline, in a dataset B2 does not control (UNRESOLVED as a fully-closed risk): the best available
mitigation, DEFINED here, is that B2's own periodic re-scan (Part XIII §13.5) checks whether a claim's
exact statement text appears, verbatim or near-verbatim, in a dataset NOT in the original provenance set
and without a citation back to the insight record's own stable ID (IAS-F1's addressing scheme, Part IX
of the ontology, applied here to insight records); when found, this is logged as a PROVENANCE-STRIPPING
FLAG, routed to the Owner-facing review layer rather than resolved autonomously (per Article IX's
governed-self-evolution boundary — B2 does not have the authority to edit another dataset's text to
restore the citation), because correcting another system's file is outside B2's own object entirely.

17.6 Why the four adversarial cases share a common lesson. Each of the four exploits the SAME underlying
property that gives cross-dataset synthesis its value — multi-domain independence, full-context grounding,
disconfirmation rigor, and traceable provenance, respectively — by attacking exactly the mechanism that
makes that property trustworthy rather than by attacking the engine's output directly. (DERIVED: this is
the general shape of adversarial risk against any evidence-aggregating system — the attack surface is
never the conclusion, it is the PROCESS that earns the conclusion its credibility — and it is why each of
this Part's four resistances targets a process step, never a downstream output filter.)

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. Every mechanism that gives a cross-dataset insight its epistemic weight — domain
independence, grounded provenance, honest disconfirmation, traceable citation — is also the exact surface
an adversarial actor or an unintentional systemic bias would need to exploit to produce a false or
misleadingly-credentialed claim, and a synthesis engine that has not named its own attack surface has not
actually earned the trust its outputs ask a downstream consumer to extend.

PART XVIII — FAILURE MODES: SPURIOUS SYNTHESIS AND PROVENANCE LOSS, CATALOGUED IN FULL
===============================================================================================

18.1 Why this Part exists separately from Parts XVI and XVII. Edge cases (Part XVI) are honest
ambiguities the engine handles correctly by design; adversarial cases (Part XVII) are exploits the
engine resists by design. FAILURE MODES, this Part's object, are what happens when a handling rule or a
resistance is ABSENT, INCOMPLETE, or FAILS IN PRACTICE — the two named failure modes the task brief for
this dataset commits to tracking, spurious synthesis and provenance loss, are catalogued here to their
root causes and consequences, consolidating threads from every preceding Part into one diagnostic
reference a future implementer or auditor can consult directly rather than re-deriving from scattered
mentions.

18.2 SPURIOUS SYNTHESIS, root causes catalogued. Root cause one: MECHANISM-MISMATCH LEAKAGE — the
matcher (Part IV §4.3) fails to catch a surface-shape match with no shared underlying mechanism, and a
candidate publishes on vocabulary resemblance alone (the analogical-overreach risk named directly in
Part VII §7.7, and the failure Part VIII §8.3's scenario two exists specifically to demonstrate the
engine correctly AVOIDING). Root cause two: INSUFFICIENT DISCONFIRMATION SEARCH — the adversarial search
of Part III §3.4 is run too narrowly (checking only domains where the pattern was already expected to
hold, rather than searching hardest where it is least expected), producing an apparently-clean
disconfirmation log that in fact never tested the pattern's real boundary. Root cause three: CORRELATED-
SOURCE INFLATION — the insight-laundering adversarial case (Part XVII §17.2) or the more benign circular-
attribution edge case (Part XVI §16.4) inflate apparent domain breadth without genuine independence.
Root cause four: PREMATURE EXTRACTION — a pattern is promoted to principle status (Part IX) on domain
breadth and mechanism stability alone, without the disconfirmation condition being independently
satisfied for the THIRD domain specifically (rather than inherited from the first two), a subtle failure
because §9.2's three conditions can each individually look satisfied while the disconfirmation search was
never actually re-run against the specific new domain that pushed breadth from two to three.

18.3 SPURIOUS SYNTHESIS, consequences and blast radius. A spurious insight that reaches PUBLISHED status
and is CONSUMED by FD-03 enters the estate's distillation lineage carrying an unearned epistemic marker,
and — the compounding risk this dataset's own Compounding Graph position makes especially severe — a
spurious PRINCIPLE that reaches ACIS's ladder risks corrupting the estate's HIGHEST epistemic tier, since
ACIS's own No-Autopromotion discipline is built to catch weak individual deposits, not necessarily a
principle arriving already dressed in multi-domain corroboration it did not actually earn (the specific
risk Part XV §15.1 names as "worse than wasting effort"). (DERIVED: this is precisely why Part XII's four
gates are conjunctive and non-negotiable rather than a scored average, and why Part IX's extraction test
requires the disconfirmation search to be re-run FRESH at three domains rather than inherited from the
two-domain stage — both design choices, traced back here, exist specifically to suppress this failure
mode's most severe consequence.)

18.4 PROVENANCE LOSS, root causes catalogued. Root cause one: FUSION WITHOUT A PROVENANCE MAP — the
exact failure Part V §5.4 names directly, where a fusion record's OBSERVED and HYPOTHESIZED fragments are
merged without preserving which came from which, manufacturing false confidence purely from combination.
Root cause two: SILENT OVERWRITE — a lifecycle violation of Part XIII §13.3's append-only discipline,
where a re-scored or re-validated record replaces its prior version rather than superseding it with both
retained, destroying the audit trail a later dispute or re-validation (Part XVI §16.3) would need. Root
cause three: DOWNSTREAM STRIPPING — the provenance-stripping adversarial case (Part XVII §17.5), where a
consumer or an external editing pass detaches a claim from its derivation, external to B2's own pipeline
but attributable, in part, to B2 not having supplied a sufficiently prominent or hard-to-drop citation
convention in the first place (an implementation lesson for the citation format's own design, named as an
open question in Part XX). Root cause four: STALE PROVENANCE MASQUERADING AS CURRENT — the edge case Part
XVI §16.5 names, where a published insight's "last re-validated" timestamp is either missing or ignored
by a downstream consumer, letting a since-eroded pattern continue to be treated as current support.

18.5 PROVENANCE LOSS, consequences and blast radius. Provenance loss is, in one sense, a quieter failure
than spurious synthesis — it does not necessarily make a FALSE claim, since the underlying content may
remain true — but it converts a CHECKABLE claim into an UNCHECKABLE one, which under Article V's own
terms is nearly as severe: a claim whose evidence trail has been lost cannot be doubted, re-validated, or
correctly weighted by any downstream consumer, and an estate that accumulates provenance-lost claims
over time degrades its own capacity for the exact epistemic honesty discipline the Constitution makes
non-negotiable, even if no single lost-provenance claim is individually false. (DERIVED: this is why
Part V frames provenance preservation as "the whole point, not a compliance afterthought" — the harm of
provenance loss compounds silently, unlike spurious synthesis, which at least risks a detectable,
falsifiable error somewhere downstream.)

18.6 The shared root, and the single structural fix that addresses both. Tracing both failure modes to
their deepest common root: both arise, in every catalogued case, from a step in the pipeline being
SKIPPED or SHORTENED under some form of pressure — time pressure (17.4's directed-request case), resource
pressure (12.5's abstention-under-constraint case), convenience pressure (16.2's uncaught near-duplicate),
or simple implementation incompleteness (17.5's weaker external resistance). No catalogued failure
originates from a correctly-executed pipeline producing a wrong answer; every one originates from a
shortcut. (NORMATIVE, and the single design principle this Part distills for a future implementer: the
engine's reliability is not a property of any one gate's individual strictness, it is a property of
NEVER SKIPPING a gate under pressure — which is precisely why Part XII §12.5 states that resource
constraints must reduce SCAN VOLUME, never gate RIGOR, as the one acceptable lever.)

18.7 Detection signals for each root cause, so an auditor knows what to look for rather than only what
can go wrong. For MECHANISM-MISMATCH LEAKAGE: a published pattern whose mechanism-stability argument
(Part IX §9.2's third condition) describes the mechanism in vocabulary borrowed directly from the
SOURCE domain rather than in domain-neutral terms is a tell — a genuinely stable mechanism should be
statable without leaning on either domain's specific jargon. For INSUFFICIENT DISCONFIRMATION SEARCH:
a disconfirmation log whose searched-domain list correlates strongly with the domains that already
confirmed the pattern (per Part IX §9.7's narrow-search failure) rather than sampling structurally
dissimilar domains is directly auditable by comparing the log's domain list against a similarity
ranking. For CORRELATED-SOURCE INFLATION: an auditor cross-checks each cited domain's authorship and
build-lineage metadata, the same check §9.7 requires proactively, applied here retroactively to
already-published material. For PREMATURE EXTRACTION: an auditor checks the disconfirmation log's
TIMESTAMP against the third domain's own sealing date — a disconfirmation search dated BEFORE the
third domain existed cannot have actually tested it, a purely mechanical, non-judgment-call check that
should be automatable rather than left to manual audit. 18.8 Detection signals for provenance loss,
symmetrically. FUSION WITHOUT A PROVENANCE MAP is detectable by checking whether a fusion record's
markers (Part V) can be traced, fragment by fragment, back to a specific source citation — a record
where markers exist but no per-fragment citation does is the direct tell. SILENT OVERWRITE is
detectable by checking whether a record's revision history contains gaps — a jump in a record's
internal version counter with no corresponding archived prior version is the signature §18.4's root
cause two leaves behind. DOWNSTREAM STRIPPING is, by definition, invisible to B2's own internal audit
once it happens externally, which is precisely why §18.4's proposed fix is a citation format hard
enough to drop that stripping requires deliberate effort rather than accidental truncation — an
implementation detail Part XX's open questions returns to. STALE PROVENANCE MASQUERADING AS CURRENT is
detectable by comparing a claim's last-re-validated timestamp against the age of the youngest system
in its provenance set — a claim re-validated before its own newest cited system existed cannot
possibly reflect that system's current state. 18.9 Why cataloguing detection signals matters as much as
cataloguing root causes. A root-cause list without a matching detection signal is diagnostic only after
the fact, once a consumer has already acted on a spurious or unchecked claim; pairing each root cause
with a concrete, checkable signal is what converts this Part from a post-mortem reference into
something an automated or periodic audit pass can actually run against the estate's live insight and
fusion inventories, closing the loop between "this is what can go wrong" and "this is how you would
notice it went wrong before it compounds further downstream."

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. Spurious synthesis manufactures false generality from mechanism-mismatch,
under-searched disconfirmation, correlated sources, or premature extraction; provenance loss converts a
true, checkable claim into an unchecked one through a missing map, a silent overwrite, external stripping,
or ignored staleness — and both failure families trace to the same root cause, a gate skipped under
pressure, which is why the engine's whole reliability rests on treating every gate as non-negotiable
rather than any single gate as sufficient.

PART XIX — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

19.1 Why the metrics Part follows the failure-mode Part, not the other way around. A metric proposed
before a system's failure modes are catalogued tends to measure what is easy to count rather than what
would have caught the failures that actually matter; this dataset's metrics are therefore constructed
in direct reference to Part XVIII's two named failure families, so that each metric either detects a
specific failure mode or is deliberately PAIRED with a countermetric that prevents the primary metric
from being gamed into hiding that same failure mode — the anti-Goodhart discipline this Part exists to
apply, not merely to name.

19.2 Primary metric one: PUBLISHED-INSIGHT VOLUME, and its mandatory countermetric. The naive metric —
how many insight records the engine publishes per scan cycle — is the single most Goodhart-prone number
in this entire dataset, because Part XVIII §18.6 already establishes that every catalogued failure
traces to a gate skipped under pressure, and pressure to raise volume is the single most direct route to
skipping a gate. Volume is therefore NEVER reported alone; it is always paired with its countermetric,
ABSTENTION RATE (the fraction of candidates that correctly failed one of Part XII's four gates), and a
volume increase that is NOT accompanied by a proportional or larger abstention-rate increase is treated
as a WARNING SIGNAL requiring investigation, not a success — a healthy engine scanning a growing estate
should see BOTH numbers rise together, because a larger estate produces more candidates and a
correctly-strict engine rejects most of them; volume rising while abstention falls is the specific,
measurable signature of gate erosion.

19.3 Primary metric two: DOMAIN BREADTH DISTRIBUTION, and its countermetric. Rather than a single
average breadth number (which a small number of very-high-breadth insights could inflate while most
published insights sit at the bare two-domain floor), the engine reports the full DISTRIBUTION of
domain-breadth counts across all published records for a given period. The countermetric is SOURCE-
INDEPENDENCE VERIFICATION RATE — the fraction of multi-domain corroborations where the source-
independence check (Part IV §4.4, Part XVII §17.2) actually traced to distinct root evidence, as
opposed to breadth counts that were never independence-checked at all. A rising average breadth
unaccompanied by a rising or steady verification rate is the measurable signature of the insight-
laundering risk (Part XVII §17.2) going undetected rather than resisted.

19.4 Primary metric three: DOWNSTREAM ACCEPTANCE RATE (FD-03 and ACIS), and its countermetric. The
fraction of routed insights and principles that FD-03 or ACIS actually accepts (Part XIV §14.6, Part
XV §15.5) is a genuine external check on the engine's own gating quality — a system whose four gates
were too permissive would show a LOW downstream acceptance rate, since the receiving system's own
independent evidence bar would catch what B2's gates missed. The countermetric here runs the opposite
direction from the first two: a downstream acceptance rate near 100% is ALSO a warning signal, not a
success, because it would suggest B2's own gates have converged on FD-03's or ACIS's specific taste
rather than applying B2's own independent evidence discipline — the exact coupling risk Part XIV §14.7
names and explicitly rejects as a design choice. (NORMATIVE: the healthy target band for downstream
acceptance is stated qualitatively, not as a false-precision number, per this dataset's own FUSION
SCORE convention — a MODERATE-HIGH acceptance rate with a non-zero, non-trivial rejection rate is the
signature of two independently-calibrated evidence standards working correctly in tandem, not of
either system deferring to the other.)

19.5 Primary metric four: RE-VALIDATION COVERAGE, and its countermetric. The fraction of published
insights that have been re-validated (Part XVI §16.3, §16.5) within some observed window, versus the
fraction whose "last re-validated" timestamp has gone stale beyond any reasonable window, is the direct
measure of provenance-loss risk accumulating silently in the engine's own published corpus. Because
Part XVI §16.5 already establishes the engine cannot honestly promise scheduled re-validation on a fixed
cadence (the opportunistic trigger constraint), the countermetric here is not "why isn't coverage
100%" but STALENESS TRANSPARENCY RATE — the fraction of consumer-facing insight records where the
last-re-validated timestamp is actually surfaced to the consumer at read time, versus records where a
consumer could plausibly read the insight without ever seeing how old its last check was. A high
staleness-transparency rate with moderate re-validation coverage is honest and acceptable; low coverage
combined with low transparency is the specific, measurable signature of stale drift (Part XVI §16.5,
Part XVIII §18.4 root cause four) accumulating invisibly.

19.6 Primary metric five: CROSS-SYSTEM AMPLIFICATION COUNT, and its countermetric. Per the
Strengthening Matrix's own evidence requirement for this dataset's row ("one insight traceable to >=2
datasets, present in neither"), the most direct measure of B2 actually fulfilling its distinct object
is a running count of insights meeting that exact criterion, cross-checked against IAS-B1's own
leverage-ranking consumption (Part XIV §14.3's IAS-B1 route) to confirm the insights are not merely
counted but actually USED in a downstream ranking decision. The countermetric is UNCONSUMED-INSIGHT
AGE — the count and age distribution of PUBLISHED records that have never reached CONSUMED status
(Part XIII §13.2) by any of their routed consumers; a growing backlog of old, unconsumed insights is
the measurable signature of the record-level orphan condition Part XIV §14.1 names, and it is tracked
specifically so that "we published N insights" is never reported without the accompanying honesty of
"and M of them were never actually used."

19.7 Why no metric in this Part is a single scalar the engine optimizes directly. Consistent with Part
IV §4.4's rejection of false-precision scoring and Part XII §12.4's rejection of framing bounded
insights as lesser achievements, none of the five metric pairs in this Part is proposed as a target the
engine's own operation should be tuned to MAXIMIZE. (NORMATIVE: a synthesis engine tuned to maximize any
one of these numbers directly would, by the exact mechanism Goodhart's law describes and Part XVIII
§18.6 already demonstrates concretely for this domain, degrade the underlying quality the metric was
meant to proxy; the correct institutional use of this Part's metrics is DIAGNOSTIC — a periodic review
comparing the paired numbers against each other and against Part XVIII's failure-mode catalogue, run by
whatever governance layer oversees the engine, never an automated objective the engine itself is
scored against and rewarded for improving.)

19.8 Reporting cadence and who reads the five pairs. Consistent with §19.7's diagnostic-not-optimized
framing, the five metric pairs are reviewed on a periodic cadence by the same governance layer that
owns the engine's own gate calibration decisions (per Part IX §9.8 and Part XV §15.9's governed-
self-evolution discipline), never surfaced as a live, continuously-updating dashboard the engine's own
operators watch in real time — a live dashboard tuned for continuous improvement is precisely the
environment in which Goodhart pressure is strongest, because every individual reviewer, watching a
number move, faces a natural pull toward small adjustments that move it favorably on THIS cycle,
each individually defensible, that compound into exactly the gate erosion §19.2 warns against. A
periodic, batch review — comparing a quarter's worth of paired numbers against a quarter's worth of
Part XVIII's catalogued failure signatures at once — is deliberately slower and less responsive to any
single cycle's pressure, and that slowness is a feature of the review cadence, not a limitation of it.
19.9 What a genuinely healthy reading of the five pairs looks like, stated concretely rather than only
abstractly. A healthy period shows: volume and abstention rising together (§19.2); a domain-breadth
distribution with genuine mass above the two-domain floor, accompanied by a steady or rising
source-independence verification rate (§19.3); a downstream acceptance rate sitting in the moderate-
high band with a non-trivial, non-zero rejection count from BOTH FD-03 and ACIS (§19.4); a staleness-
transparency rate near total even where raw re-validation coverage is moderate (§19.5); and a
cross-system amplification count that grows alongside a FLAT or shrinking unconsumed-insight backlog,
rather than both growing together, which would indicate the engine is publishing faster than the
estate can absorb (§19.6). No single one of these five conditions, met alone, is sufficient evidence of
health, mirroring the conjunctive-gate discipline Part IX §9.3 already establishes for extraction
itself — a metrics review that reads only one favorable pair while ignoring an unfavorable one is
committing the same failure, at the reporting layer, that a single-condition extraction gate would
commit at the evidence layer.

FINAL LAW — PART XIX. No metric in this dataset stands alone; every primary number that could be
gamed toward apparent success is paired with a countermetric that would reveal the gaming, and the
five pairs together are read diagnostically against the failure-mode catalogue of Part XVIII, never
optimized directly, because a synthesis engine that learns to maximize its own metrics has learned to
produce exactly the failure modes those metrics exist to catch.

PART XX — EVALUATION PROTOCOL AND BENCHMARKS: HOW TO PROVE SYNTHESIS QUALITY
===============================================================================================

20.1 Why evaluation needs its own Part beyond the metrics of Part XIX. Part XIX specified WHAT to
measure on an ongoing basis; this Part specifies HOW to run a bounded, repeatable EVALUATION — a
deliberate test of the engine's mechanism against known inputs with known correct answers, the kind of
check that can be re-run after any change to the engine's own gates, thresholds, or subengines to
confirm the change did not silently degrade quality, which an ongoing metrics dashboard alone cannot
guarantee because live estate data does not come with ground truth attached.

20.2 The retrodiction protocol, imported by direct reference from IAS-F1. IAS-F1's own Part XVI §16.3
(referenced, not rebuilt, per this dataset's non-duplication discipline) establishes a RETRODICTION
protocol: applying a formal test to cases whose answer is already known by construction, to demonstrate
the test resolves them correctly before trusting it on genuinely unknown cases. IAS-B2 adopts the
identical discipline for its own mechanism: a held-out set of SYNTHETIC candidate pairs, constructed
with KNOWN ground truth (some genuinely sharing mechanism, per Part IV §4.3's test; some sharing only
surface shape, per Part VII §7.7's analogical-overreach risk; some genuinely independent; some
deliberately laundered per Part XVII §17.2's construction) is run through the full pipeline, and the
engine's own gate-by-gate verdicts are checked against the KNOWN construction — not against a live
estate where the correct answer is itself uncertain.

20.3 The four benchmark categories, each targeting one Part's core claim. Benchmark category one,
MECHANISM DISCRIMINATION: pairs constructed to share surface shape but differ in underlying mechanism
(as in Part VIII §8.3's scenario two), testing whether the matcher (Part IV §4.3) correctly rejects
them — the pass criterion is a rejection rate at or near 100% on this specifically-adversarial
construction, because any pass-through here is a direct instance of Part XVIII §18.2's root cause one.
Benchmark category two, DISCONFIRMATION THOROUGHNESS: patterns constructed with a KNOWN boundary
condition planted in a specific, non-obvious domain, testing whether the disconfirmation search (Part
III §3.4) actually finds the planted boundary rather than confirming cleanly in the domains where
confirmation was easy — the pass criterion is that the engine's own disconfirmation log correctly
identifies the planted domain as the one requiring a boundary condition, not merely that the final
verdict happens to be correct by coincidence. Benchmark category three, LAUNDERING RESISTANCE: pairs
constructed exactly as Part XVII §17.2 describes — restated, root-identical claims dressed as
independent — testing whether the source-independence check correctly traces to the shared root and
excludes the pair from corroboration counting. Benchmark category four, PROVENANCE INTEGRITY UNDER
MUTATION: a published insight's source is deliberately mutated (a cited claim's epistemic marker
changed, a cited Part rewritten) after publication, testing whether the re-validation mechanism (Part
XVI §16.3) correctly detects the mutation and transitions the record to CONTESTED rather than leaving
it silently PUBISHED against stale support.

20.4 Pass thresholds, stated qualitatively and honestly. Consistent with this dataset's own rejection of
false-precision scoring (Part IV §4.4, Part XIX §19.7), the four benchmark categories are not reported
as a single aggregate pass percentage; each category is reported separately, because a high score on
mechanism discrimination combined with a low score on laundering resistance is a specific, actionable
finding a blended average would hide — the exact aggregation-hides-the-failure risk this dataset's own
Part XVII §17.6 names as the general shape of adversarial risk, here applied reflexively to the
engine's own evaluation.

20.5 The blind-recognition benchmark, specifically, and why it requires an independent rater. Part IV
§4.3 and Part X §10.5 both specify a blind-recognition check as part of the matcher's and the
abstraction subengine's own validation mechanism, and this check, uniquely among this dataset's
benchmarks, requires a rater who did NOT construct the synthetic test pairs and who is not the engine
itself — because the check's entire purpose is confirming that a match is recognizable to an INDEPENDENT
reasoner, not merely self-consistent with the engine's own prior reasoning. (EXPERIMENTAL: the precise
operational form of this rater — a separate model instance, a human domain reviewer, or a held-out
version of the engine trained or configured differently — is left UNRESOLVED here as an implementation
choice for whoever operationalizes this dataset's specification, with the single hard constraint that
the rater's construction must be demonstrably independent of the candidate's own construction, or the
check degenerates into the engine grading its own homework.)

20.6 Regression testing as a standing discipline, not a one-time evaluation. Per this dataset's own
Reality Contract obligations (Article VIII), the four benchmark categories are not a one-time
certification the engine passes once and is then trusted indefinitely; they are a REGRESSION SUITE,
re-run after any change to the engine's gates, thresholds, subengine logic, or the taxonomy itself
(Part II), with a full historical record of every run's category-by-category results kept exactly as
Part XIII §13.6 requires for insight records — because a system whose own evaluation history is not
itself provenance-preserved would be exempting its own trustworthiness claims from the discipline it
enforces on every other claim in the estate.

20.7 The limits of synthetic benchmarking, stated honestly. Synthetic, known-ground-truth benchmarks
(20.2-20.5) can prove the engine's MECHANISM behaves correctly on constructed cases; they cannot, by
themselves, prove that the engine's real, live output against the actual estate is producing GENUINELY
valuable insights, because "valuable" is a judgment about real content the synthetic constructions
cannot simulate. The complementary check — Part XIX's ongoing metrics, especially downstream acceptance
rate (§19.4) and cross-system amplification count (§19.6) — is what closes this gap, and this Part
explicitly states the boundary: synthetic benchmarks certify the MACHINERY; live metrics certify the
OUTPUT; neither alone is sufficient evidence of the engine's real-world value, and a claim that the
engine "works" on the strength of synthetic benchmarks alone would be exactly the static-verification-
does-not-prove-runtime-works failure the estate's own Mistake #17 doctrine names for code, generalized
here to a knowledge-producing system's own claims about itself.

20.8 Constructing the synthetic corpus itself, and the risk of an unrepresentative benchmark. A
retrodiction benchmark is only as informative as its construction is representative of the failure
shapes the live engine will actually encounter, and a benchmark corpus built entirely by the same team
that built the engine risks encoding the team's own blind spots into the "known ground truth" — a
constructed laundering pair, for instance, might unconsciously use the SAME laundering technique the
engine's designers already thought to guard against, leaving genuinely novel laundering techniques
untested even though the benchmark reports a clean pass. The mitigation mirrors §20.5's independent-
rater requirement extended to corpus construction: the synthetic corpus is periodically refreshed with
new adversarial constructions authored by someone who did not design the specific gate being tested,
ideally drawing on real disconfirmed or disputed cases surfaced by Part XVI's edge-case handling and
Part XVIII's failure catalogue once the engine has some live operating history, so the benchmark corpus
itself compounds in adversarial sophistication over time rather than staying frozen at whatever the
original designers happened to anticipate. 20.9 Interpreting a benchmark failure honestly, distinguished
from interpreting a benchmark pass. A pass on all four categories is necessary but not sufficient
evidence of quality (per §20.7's stated limit); a FAILURE on any category, by contrast, is direct and
sufficient evidence that a specific, named mechanism is not working as specified — mechanism
discrimination failing means the matcher is not doing its job (Part IV §4.3), disconfirmation
thoroughness failing means the adversarial search is not doing its job (Part III §3.4), and so on for
each category's corresponding mechanism. This asymmetry — pass is weak evidence, fail is strong
evidence — is itself worth stating plainly, because a team under delivery pressure is more likely to
over-read a clean benchmark pass as full certification than to under-read a benchmark failure, and
§20.7's boundary exists specifically to correct the more common and more dangerous of the two
misreadings. 20.10 Where benchmark failures should route once found. Consistent with the governed-
self-evolution discipline already established for gate calibration (Part IX §9.8, Part XV §15.9), a
benchmark failure is not silently patched by adjusting the specific gate until the specific failing
test passes — a fix scoped only to the known failing case risks producing a gate that passes the
benchmark without actually correcting the underlying mechanism gap, the evaluation-layer analogue of
Part XVIII §18.6's shortcut-under-pressure root cause. The correct response traces the failure to its
mechanism (per §20.9), proposes a governed fix at the mechanism level, and re-runs the FULL regression
suite (§20.6), not only the previously-failing category, before considering the fix validated.

FINAL LAW — PART XX. Synthetic, known-ground-truth benchmarks across four adversarially-constructed
categories prove the machinery behaves correctly before it is trusted on real content, live downstream
metrics prove the output is actually valued once deployed, and neither is a substitute for the other —
an engine that only ever cites its synthetic pass rate, or only ever cites its live acceptance rate, is
citing half of the evidence its own evaluation discipline requires.

explicitly against IAS-F1's relation typology (F1 Part VI) so each edge below carries a named type, not

21.3 Outbound edges: what IAS-B2 produces and who consumes it. To FD-03 (FEEDS, Part XIV in full):

21.4 The graphify relationship, specified fully for the first time in this dataset (deferred from
earlier Parts because it required the scanner's full mechanism, Part IV, to state precisely). Graphify's

precisely on query. IAS-B2's scanner (Part III §3.3, Part IV §4.2) is a CONSUMER of graphify's location
function in a specific, narrow sense: when the scanner needs to escalate from a cached summary to a
specific cited Part (Part III §3.6's bounded escalation, Part XVII §17.3's mandatory pre-publication
verification), it uses graphify's own locate capability to find the exact section rather than
re-implementing a second location mechanism — a CONSUMES relation (F1 Part VI §6.7, transient, per-scan)
rather than a standing dependency, because the scanner's primary path (reading cached summaries) does
not require graphify's location function at all; only the escalation path does. This is the precise,
narrow sense in which B2 "strengthens" graphify per the Strengthening Matrix's row: not by locating
things graphify could not, but by being graphify's highest-value CONSUMER for exactly the escalation
case graphify's own location precision was built to serve — a synthesis engine reading across the whole
estate is, in effect, the stress test that most exercises graphify's precision, and graphify's own
observability (were it to track its own consumer patterns) would see B2 as a consumer whose escalation
requests are concentrated exactly on the cited-Part granularity graphify's location function is built to
resolve.

21.5 The IAS-F2 (Advantage Algebra) relationship, named for completeness though it is lighter than the
above. IAS-F2, per the Strengthening Matrix, supplies fusion/advantage/compounding formulas that five
named systems' scores plug into; IAS-B2 is not listed among F2's primary five, and this dataset does not
force a fit — the corroboration scorer (Part IV §4.4) and the FUSION SCORE convention at this file's own
close deliberately use QUALITATIVE tiers rather than F2's advantage-algebra units, a choice CONTESTED as
either (a) a genuine scope boundary (B2's outputs are knowledge claims, not resource-allocation
decisions, and F2's algebra is built for the latter) or (b) an area for future integration once F2's
formulas mature enough to express an insight's corroboration strength in comparable units to a system's
advantage score. This dataset states the CONTESTED status honestly rather than forcing a premature
formula-fit that Part IV §4.4 and Part XIX §19.7 have already argued against on independent grounds
(false precision).

21.6 The relationship to IAS-B1, made mechanically precise beyond the routing rule of Part XIV §14.3.
IAS-B1's own object (per the Non-Duplication Ledger, candidates 11-12, 15, 19-20) is ranking candidate
interventions by how many systems they amplify — it needs CANDIDATES to rank, and it is not itself built
to originate genuinely cross-dataset candidates, because its own mechanism (per the ledger's description,
"distills one delta" and "promotes one insight," extended to ranking) operates on interventions already
identified, not on discovering them fresh from a cross-dataset read. B2 is therefore not merely "a
source among several" for B1's ranking input; it is specifically the source for the SUBSET of candidates
that are cross-dataset in origin, a subset no other system in the estate is positioned to originate,
which is the precise, checkable form of the Strengthening Matrix's own claim for this relationship — a
change scored by systems-amplified and ranked above a local fix requires, as a precondition, that the
cross-system-amplifying change has been FOUND at all, and B2 is the system whose object is finding it.

21.7 What integration explicitly does not create: a merged pipeline. Despite the density of the FEEDS
and STRENGTHENS edges named above, B2 remains, per IAS-F1's systemhood tests (F1 Part II §2.1), a single
addressable system with its own object, not a subsystem absorbed into FD-03, ACIS, IAS-B1, or D2A. Every
edge in this Part is a PRODUCER-CONSUMER or STRENGTHENS relation between distinct systems retaining
their own authority (per IAS-F1's Invariant Three), never a merger, and the density of B2's integration
is precisely what the Compounding Graph's amplification spine predicts for a system positioned at B2's
point in the estate's knowledge flow — heavily connected is not the same as absorbed, and this dataset's
own non-duplication proof (Part I, Parts VI, VII, X, XI) stands independently of how many edges this
Part enumerates.

FINAL LAW — PART XXI. IAS-B2 receives the estate's stated content and F1's shared vocabulary, and
produces insight records, principle candidates, leverage inputs, and abstraction candidates consumed by
FD-03, ACIS, IAS-B1, and D2A respectively — a dense compounding position in the estate's knowledge flow
that strengthens four named systems without merging into any of them, because every edge is a typed
relation between systems that keep their own distinct authority intact.

PART XXII — EVOLUTION, OPEN QUESTIONS, AND THE CLOSING FRAME
===============================================================================================

22.1 How the engine is expected to change as the estate grows. The engine's own architecture (Part III)
is built incrementally, but its THRESHOLDS and CATALOGUES are not expected to be static across the
corpus's life. As the estate grows past its current eighteen-plus domains, three specific evolutionary
pressures are DERIVED as likely, each traced to a mechanism already specified: first, the disconfirmation
search's own cost (Part III §3.4) grows with the number of domains it must check, which will eventually
require the search itself to be prioritized or sampled rather than exhaustive — an evolution this dataset
does not pre-specify, because the correct sampling strategy depends on estate scale data this dataset
does not yet have, and a premature specification would violate the same OBSERVED-not-anticipated
discipline IAS-F1 itself commits to at its own founding (F1 Part I §1.5). Second, Part XVI's four-edge-
case catalogue is explicitly named (§16.6) as expected to grow from real operating history, not as a
closed, final list. Third, Part IX §9.2's domain-breadth threshold (three domains for extraction) may
itself warrant recalibration once real submission-outcome data from ACIS (Part XV §15.5's feedback loop)
accumulates.

22.2 Governed self-evolution, applied to B2's own thresholds. Any of the three evolutionary pressures
named in 22.1, if acted upon, is a proposal under Article IX's governed-self-evolution boundary — an
evidence threshold, a validation pass (most naturally, a re-run of Part XX's benchmark suite under the
proposed new threshold, checked against the SAME synthetic ground truth to confirm the change does not
degrade the benchmark's own pass rates), explicit versioning of the threshold change, and a stated
rollback path if the new threshold's live metrics (Part XIX) degrade rather than improve after
deployment. (NORMATIVE: this is not a hypothetical caveat; it is the specific, load-bearing mechanism
that keeps B2's own gates from drifting looser over time under the exact convenience pressure Part
XVIII §18.6 names as the shared root of both failure families — a threshold change proposed and
evaluated under this discipline is the ONLY legitimate path by which B2's gates may ever loosen, and a
threshold quietly relaxed without this discipline is, by this dataset's own terms, itself an instance of
the gate-skipped-under-pressure failure mode.)

22.3 Open question one: the citation-format design for provenance-stripping resistance. Part XVIII §18.4
root cause three names this as an open implementation lesson; it is restated here as a formal open
question, UNRESOLVED: what citation convention (a stable-ID-bearing header, an inline marker format, a
required-field in any dataset template that quotes prior content) would make provenance-stripping
(Part XVII §17.5) maximally difficult to do accidentally, since the adversarial case names deliberate
stripping but the more common real-world risk is likely ACCIDENTAL — an author genuinely unaware a
statement they are restating originated as a B2 insight, simply because the citation was not prominent
enough to notice. This dataset does not resolve the citation format's specific design; it names the
question as the single most actionable open item for whoever operationalizes this specification.

22.4 Open question two: automating the mechanism-versus-surface test at scale. Parts IV §4.3, VII §7.2,
and X §10.5 each specify a mechanism-level test requiring judgment beyond simple shape-matching, and Part

require, or whether it structurally requires a human-in-the-loop or an independently-configured second
reasoning pass indefinitely, is UNRESOLVED — and this dataset commits to stating that it does not know
the answer, rather than asserting confidently in either direction, per Article V's honesty discipline
applied to the dataset's own limits, not only to the estate's claims.

22.5 Open question three: the fusion pass's own scaling ceiling. Part V §5.6 notes the fusion pass runs
lighter validation than full synthesis, and Part V §5.5 notes property-record assembly (feeding IAS-F1's
downstream consumers directly) is likely its highest-volume real use case. Whether the fusion pass's
lighter-weight contradiction check remains SUFFICIENT as the volume of property-record fragments grows
with estate size, or whether it will itself need the heavier corroboration machinery of Part IV §4.4 once
contradiction-check volume reaches a scale where false negatives (undetected contradictions) become
material, is UNRESOLVED, named here as a scaling question this dataset's current specification does not
pre-answer.

22.6 The single sentence closing the frame. Returning to where Part I opened: a federation of
independently-deep datasets systematically under-produces the propositions that live in their
conjunction rather than in any single part, because depth-per-dataset is everyone's local incentive and
cross-dataset synthesis is nobody's job absent a system built specifically to hold that job — IAS-B2 is
that system, built as a standing, incremental, provenance-preserving, disconfirmation-first engine that
converts the estate's growing depth into a growing SURPLUS of propositions no single dataset ever stated,
feeding that surplus to FD-03, ACIS, IAS-B1, and D2A under strict, bounded, advisory authority that never
displaces any of their own, so that the more the estate is written, the more it also discovers about
itself in the gaps between what was written.

22.7 What would falsify the thesis this dataset opened with. Consistent with Article V's falsifiability
discipline applied reflexively to B2's own founding claim (§ FINAL LAW below), the thesis that a

genuinely-novel, multi-domain-traceable insights as a year WITH one, the founding thesis would be
substantially weakened, and this dataset's own existence would warrant re-examination under the

observable, in principle, once sufficient operating history exists on both sides, rather than
insulating its own founding premise from ever being checked. 22.8 What this dataset explicitly did not

DECISION LOG
===============================================================================================

DL-1. DECISION: Split the engine into three explicit pipeline stages (scan, validate, publish) rather
than a single end-to-end matching-and-scoring step. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) a monolithic classifier
that ingests two claims and outputs a match/no-match verdict; (b) a two-stage split (detect, then
publish) that folds mechanism-confirmation and corroboration-scoring into one step. WHY THIS: the
three-stage split is the only structure that lets each stage's intermediate output populate the insight
record's own auditable fields (Part III §3.5, Part IV §4.6), satisfying Article V's requirement that a
claim carry a reason a downstream consumer could dispute, not merely a verdict. TRADE-OFFS: more
architectural surface area, more named subengines (Part IV) to specify and maintain, higher documentation
burden. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if live operating history shows the three-way split adds coordination
overhead without a measurable increase in disconfirmation-search quality (Part XIX §19.3's countermetric),
collapsing stages two and three (validate-and-score) back into one step would be reconsidered, provided
the resulting step still populates a full disconfirmation log.

DL-2. DECISION: Require disconfirmation-first search (actively hunting for counterexamples before
counting confirmations) rather than a confirmation-first-then-spot-check design. ALTERNATIVES
CONSIDERED: (a) confirmation-first, spot-checking a small sample of domains for counterexamples only
after a pattern already looks strong; (b) no explicit disconfirmation step at all, relying on
downstream consumers (FD-03, ACIS) to catch weak patterns. WHY THIS: confirmation-first search
systematically overstates generality because confirming examples are easier to find in any large corpus
(Part III §3.4); relying on downstream consumers would defeat the entire purpose of supplying them a
pre-vetted candidate (Part XIV §14.2, Part XV §15.1). TRADE-OFFS: disconfirmation-first search is more
expensive per candidate and slows the pipeline, directly trading throughput for reliability. REVERSAL
CONDITIONS: none identified that would justify reverting to confirmation-first; this decision is treated
as closer to an invariant than an adjustable threshold, because Part XVIII §18.6 traces essentially all
of the spurious-synthesis failure family back to exactly this discipline being weakened.

DL-3. DECISION: Give fusion (Part V) a lighter validation path than synthesis (Parts III-IV, IX) rather
than routing every candidate through the full four-gate decision model uniformly. ALTERNATIVES
CONSIDERED: (a) uniform gating for all candidate types regardless of whether they claim generality; (b)
an even lighter path that skips the contradiction check entirely for fusion, publishing on shape-match
alone. WHY THIS: fusion makes no claim of generality (Part V §5.1), so the heavier gates — built
specifically to guard against overstated generality — are solving a problem fusion does not have; option
(b) was rejected because even a non-generalizing composite can silently combine a correct and an
incorrect fragment if no contradiction check runs at all (Part V §5.6). TRADE-OFFS: two validation paths
in one engine adds a branch point (the identity-versus-complementarity test, Part VI §6.4) that must
itself be correctly applied, introducing its own failure surface if mis-routed. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if
the fusion pass's lighter check proves insufficient at scale (Part XXII §22.5's open question), the
heavier corroboration machinery would be extended to fusion, at the cost of the throughput gain this
decision currently trades for.

DL-4. DECISION: Route principle candidates to ACIS as evidence packages without assigning an ACIS-ladder
level or constructing ACIS's own falsifier (Part XV). ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) B2 assigns a
provisional ACIS-compatible level itself, subject to ACIS's override; (b) B2 constructs a candidate
falsifier in ACIS's schema format as part of the handoff package. WHY THIS: IAS-F1's Invariant Three
(singular authority per decision class) forbids two systems holding final authority over the same
grading decision; option (a) would create exactly this collision even framed as "provisional," because a
provisional level tends to anchor a downstream reviewer's judgment. Option (b) was rejected as
overreach because falsifier construction requires forward-looking domain judgment about what a FUTURE
test would look like, a design choice this dataset's backward-looking disconfirmation search does not
naturally produce (Part XV §15.3). TRADE-OFFS: ACIS must still do falsifier-construction work on every
submitted principle, meaning B2's handoff, however well-evidenced, does not fully collapse ACIS's own
labor. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if ACIS's own submission-outcome feedback (Part XV §15.5) shows a stable,
learnable pattern in how falsifiers get constructed for B2-sourced candidates specifically, a future,
separately-authored proposal could extend B2's package to draft (not assign) a candidate falsifier for
ACIS's own review — but this remains a proposal for a LATER decision, not this dataset's own scope.

DL-5. DECISION: Mark every published insight, principle, and transfer prediction with an explicit
epistemic level per Article V's vocabulary, defaulting to DERIVED or HYPOTHESIZED and never OBSERVED,
regardless of how strong the underlying corroboration. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) allow an insight
extracted from multiple OBSERVED source claims to itself inherit OBSERVED status; (b) introduce a new,
B2-specific epistemic tier between DERIVED and OBSERVED to reflect strong multi-domain corroboration.
WHY THIS: an insight is, by Part I's own definition, a step of reasoning BEYOND what any source directly
measured — even if every source claim is itself OBSERVED, the conjunction that produces the insight has
not itself been directly observed, only derived, and inheriting OBSERVED status would misrepresent the
insight's true epistemic grounding (Part VII §7.3's identical reasoning for transfer predictions). Option
(b) was rejected because it would duplicate Article V's own vocabulary rather than working within it, a
naming-only-novelty risk at the epistemics-vocabulary level. TRADE-OFFS: even a very strongly corroborated
principle (three-plus domains, clean disconfirmation search) is capped at DERIVED under this dataset's
own marking, which may understate its actual reliability to a consumer unfamiliar with the corroboration
tier (Part IV §4.4) sitting alongside it. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: none within this dataset's own authority —
any change to Article V's vocabulary itself belongs to the Constitution, not to any single dataset
built under it.

ANTI-PATTERNS
===============================================================================================

ANTI-PATTERN 1 — THE AGGREGATOR MASQUERADE. Building a system that concatenates or indexes every
dataset's claims into a larger searchable corpus and presenting the result as "synthesis," when no
derivation step (Part I §1.6) has actually occurred — this is the AGGREGATOR near-miss IAS-F1 names at
Part VII §7.7, applied concretely to this dataset's own object. PREVENTION: every candidate that reaches
PUBLISHED status must pass through the full lifecycle of Part XIII, including the mechanism-confirmation
step of Part IV §4.3, which a pure aggregator by construction never runs; an implementer should treat
"can this candidate's provenance set be reduced to a single dataset's own restatement" as a standing
red flag question at every code review of the scanner's own output.

ANTI-PATTERN 2 — GATE EROSION UNDER VOLUME PRESSURE. Loosening the four abstention gates (Part XII), the
extraction procedure's three conditions (Part IX §9.2), or the disconfirmation-first discipline (Part
III §3.4) in response to a low output volume, on the reasoning that "the engine isn't finding enough
insights" — this is the single shared root Part XVIII §18.6 traces every catalogued failure back to.
PREVENTION: Part XII §12.5's structural rule (reduce scan volume or scan frequency under resource
pressure, never gate rigor) must be enforced as a hard constraint on any future modification to the
engine's thresholds, and any proposed threshold change must go through the governed-self-evolution
discipline of Part XXII §22.2 (benchmark re-run, versioning, rollback path) rather than being applied
directly to a live engine because output volume looked disappointing on a dashboard.

ANTI-PATTERN 3 — CONSUMER-TASTE COUPLING. Tuning the engine's own gates, thresholds, or taxonomy
definitions specifically to match one downstream consumer's current acceptance criteria (most likely
FD-03's or ACIS's), minimizing that consumer's rejection rate as if it were a success metric — Part XIV
§14.7 names and rejects this explicitly for the FD-03 relationship, and Part XIX §19.4's countermetric
(a near-100% downstream acceptance rate is itself a warning sign, not a success) is built specifically to
detect it. PREVENTION: any proposed gate or threshold change must be justified against B2's OWN evidence
discipline (Parts III, IV, IX, XII) independently of its predicted effect on any single consumer's
acceptance rate, and Part XIX §19.4's countermetric should be checked BEFORE, not after, any threshold
change that a consumer's feedback appears to motivate.
