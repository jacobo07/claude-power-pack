PART I — THE PORTFOLIO AS AN INSTITUTIONAL OBJECT NO EXISTING SYSTEM OWNS
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding gap, stated precisely. The Claude Power Pack estate, as OBSERVED against the
Non-Duplication Ledger and the Strengthening Matrix on 2026-07-12, contains more than seventy
systems and eighteen sealed dataset domains, each of which was funded, built, and is maintained by
some draw on the estate's finite cognitive capital — token budget, Owner review attention, context
budget, and the standing maintenance burden a system imposes simply by continuing to exist. No
existing system holds the question of whether that capital, taken as a whole, is well spent. CO-01
prices one session. D2A-5 scores one proposal at the moment it is proposed, against the systems it
would overlap, and then stops looking — its score is a gate at birth, not a standing valuation.
token_irr and recall_roi each measure one asset's return or reuse in isolation, without ever asking
whether that asset's draw on capital is proportionate to its yield relative to the other sixty-nine
assets competing for the same finite budget. (OBSERVED: the ledger's own Family C entries name this
gap seven separate times — candidates 21 through 30 all reach for some piece of "the estate as an
economy," and the ledger's verdict is that none of the seven, alone, is the economy; together, folded,
they are.) The founding gap is therefore not "the estate lacks a scorer" — it has several, all good
at their scope — it is that the estate lacks a PORTFOLIO: an object that holds every system's capital
position at once, compares them on one shared basis, and can say, truthfully, which holdings are
earning more than they cost and which are not.

1.2 Why "portfolio" and not "budget" or "backlog." The estate already has budget-like objects (CO's
per-session ceilings) and backlog-like objects (owner_queue's prioritized list of not-yet-built
work). A portfolio is neither. A budget governs spend going forward for one unit of work; a backlog
orders future work not yet funded; a PORTFOLIO, as DEFINED in this dataset, is the set of capital
positions the estate CURRENTLY HOLDS — every system already built, already running, already drawing
capital — considered together as a single object whose composition can be examined, rebalanced, and
changed. The distinction matters because a budget and a backlog both look forward; a portfolio looks
at what exists NOW and asks whether it should continue to exist in its current form, at its current
scale, with its current claim on capital. (DERIVED: this is the specific sense in which candidate 21,
"Capability Portfolio Optimizer," is not simply CO-01 with a longer time horizon — CO-01's object is
future spend; the portfolio's object is standing holdings.) A portfolio without this backward-looking,
holdings-level view cannot answer the question every mature institution eventually must ask about
itself: not "what should we build next" but "given what we have already built, what is it worth to
keep."

1.3 The economic frame, and why it is not decoration. This dataset uses the vocabulary of capital
allocation — positions, valuation, liquidity, a board, an investment thesis — deliberately and not as
a metaphor borrowed for color. Every one of those terms names a real structural problem the estate
faces once its holdings exceed roughly a dozen systems, past which no single agent's working memory
can hold every system's cost and yield simultaneously without an external representation. (DERIVED
from the estate's own OBSERVED scale — seventy-plus systems is roughly six times past that threshold.)
A capital-allocation frame is the correct frame, as opposed to, say, a purely architectural or purely
observability frame, because the estate's central scarcity is not information about what exists (that
is IAS-E1's object) and not the shape of how systems relate (that is IAS-G1's and IAS-D1's object); it
is the finite pool of cognitive capital — tokens, Owner attention, context budget, maintenance
cycles — that every system draws from and that cannot fund all seventy-plus holdings at their current
scale simultaneously without waste. Where there is a finite, fungible, competed-for resource and many
claimants, capital-allocation theory is the correct theory, not an analogy borrowed for rhetorical
weight. (NORMATIVE.)

1.4 What the portfolio is not, stated against each near-miss. Three near-misses recur when a
proposal reaches toward "govern the estate's spending," and IAS-C1 must be distinguished from each
explicitly, per the level test's own discipline (IAS-F1 Part VIII §8.6, on claims that game the
majority-object rule). It is not a SUPER-BUDGET that simply sums CO-01's per-session ceilings across
all sessions — a sum of session budgets is still a resource-level aggregate (the AGGREGATOR
NEAR-MISS of IAS-F1 §7.7), because its object remains individual sessions, not standing system
holdings. It is not a SECOND BACKLOG competing with owner_queue — the portfolio does not propose new
work; it values and reallocates capital across EXISTING holdings, and where it does surface a
candidate for new investment (Part XII), it routes the candidate to owner_queue rather than
maintaining a parallel list. It is not a REPLACEMENT for D2A-5's proposal-time fusion scoring — a
system's D2A-5 score at birth becomes one INPUT to its portfolio valuation (Part IV), never a
substitute for the portfolio's own standing, evidence-leveled record. Each of these boundaries is
load-bearing: cross either one and IAS-C1 stops being a distinct ensemble-level object and starts
being a resource-level duplicate wearing portfolio language, exactly the failure the ledger's Article
I supreme law exists to prevent.

1.5 The four questions only a portfolio can phrase. A capital-allocation frame earns its place by
making four questions askable that no resource-level system, however well-built, can phrase on its
own, because each requires comparing across the WHOLE holding set rather than examining one item.
First: "of everything we currently fund, what is under-earning its keep?" — unanswerable by any single
system's own telemetry, because a system cannot judge its own relative standing against sixty-nine
peers it has no visibility into. Second: "where would the next unit of scarce capital produce the most
return?" — unanswerable by owner_queue alone, because a backlog orders WISHES, not the marginal return
of funding one wish against harvesting capital from an underperforming holding. Third: "how much of
our total capital is concentrated in how few holdings, and is that concentration a strength or a
risk?" — a question with no resource-level analogue at all, because concentration is a property of the
SET, not of any member. Fourth: "if we needed to free capital quickly for an urgent mission, which
holdings could we liquidate or throttle without breaking a depends-on chain?" — a question that
presupposes the whole portfolio's relation graph, imported by reference from IAS-F1 Part VI rather
than rebuilt here. (DERIVED: these four questions are the operational content behind the abstract
claim "the estate lacks a portfolio"; a dataset that cannot phrase them has not actually built one.)

1.6 The scope boundary against sibling Family-C candidates now folded here. Candidates 22 (Cognitive
Resource Allocation) and 29 (Scarcity Manager) both reached toward "allocate capital across more than
one session," which is exactly this dataset's territory once generalized from sessions to systems;
they are MERGED here rather than kept separate because a resource-allocation mechanism and a
scarcity-management mechanism operating on the SAME capital pool as the portfolio's valuation and
board, but built as separate objects, would immediately violate IAS-F1's Invariant Two (no unresolved
duplicate) the moment both tried to claim final authority over "how much capital does System X get
this cycle." Candidates 25 (Capital Allocation Board) and 26 (Investment Thesis) and 30 (Capability
Liquidity) are each real, distinct MECHANISMS within the portfolio's operation — a board is not the
same object as the valuation it acts on, a thesis is not the same object as the decision it
justifies, and liquidity is not the same object as value — and the ledger's SUBENGINE verdict for each
reflects exactly that: they are subsystems of IAS-C1 in the Part II §2.3 sense of the Federation
Ontology, not independent systems, because their authority is delegated by, and their object is a
facet of, the portfolio's own object.

1.7 Why this dataset must exist before, not after, the systems it governs stop growing. The estate's
own history — OBSERVED in the density spread the Dataset First Protocol found across sibling PP
families before a shared standard existed — shows that an institution which grows its holdings first
and builds an allocation discipline second pays for the gap twice: once in capital spent on holdings
that would not have survived an honest valuation, and again in the retrofitting cost of building a
portfolio view over holdings that were never designed to be valued consistently. IAS-C1 cannot undo
capital already spent, but it can stop the compounding of that specific waste going forward, and its
timing — arriving while the estate's growth is still active rather than after it has plateaued — is
itself a DERIVED design choice: a portfolio discipline introduced early prices new holdings honestly
from birth; one introduced late must first reconstruct valuations for holdings that never carried
one, a strictly harder problem this dataset does not shy from (Part IV §4.6 names it directly) but
would rather have avoided by existing sooner.

FINAL LAW — PART I. An estate with seventy holdings and no shared view of what each costs relative to
what it earns is not managing capital, it is merely accumulating systems; the portfolio is the
missing object that makes "which of our seventy holdings is worth what it costs" answerable at all,
and it earns its place in the federation precisely because that question belongs to none of its
parents.

PART II — ONTOLOGY RESOLUTION: THE PORTFOLIO'S OBJECTS AGAINST F1 AND F2
===============================================================================================

2.1 The discipline this Part exists to enforce. Per Article V and the Federation Ontology's own
governing rule (IAS-F1 Part I §1.5), every later IAS dataset resolves its objects against F1's
primitives — SYSTEM, SUBSYSTEM, CAPABILITY, ENSEMBLE — and, where it introduces value or scoring
language, against F2's ADVANTAGE ALGEBRA rather than inventing a parallel unit. IAS-C1 introduces
exactly four new objects beyond F1 and F2's existing eleven, and this Part defines each strictly in
terms of what already exists, so that no later Part of this dataset, and no sibling dataset reading
this one, must guess what a "position" or a "capital pool" means.

2.2 CAPITAL, defined against F1's COST property. F1's property model (Part IV) already names COST as
one of six canonical fields every system record carries — token share, session overhead, maintenance
burden. COGNITIVE CAPITAL, as DEFINED here, is NOT a new measurement of cost; it is the FUNGIBLE POOL
that every system's cost draws from, considered at the ensemble level rather than per-system. Three
sub-pools compose it, each already measured elsewhere in the estate and imported by reference rather
than re-measured: TOKEN CAPITAL (the aggregate token share the estate's sessions consume, whose
per-session instance CO-01 already governs), ATTENTION CAPITAL (the Owner's finite review and
decision cycles, the same scarce resource owner_queue already prioritizes against), and MAINTENANCE
CAPITAL (the standing engineering and governance attention required to keep a system correct as its
dependencies drift, the same burden F1's Part IV names but does not itself total). IAS-C1's
contribution is not measuring any of the three — CO-01, owner_queue, and the estate's own maintenance
telemetry already do that — but TOTALING them into one comparable pool a portfolio can allocate
across, exactly the mechanism by which this dataset strengthens CO-01 and owner_queue rather than
replacing either (Part XXI details the interface).

2.3 POSITION, the portfolio's atomic unit. A POSITION, as DEFINED, is the pairing of one F1 SYSTEM
with its current capital draw (Part 2.2's three sub-pools, summed and weighted) and its current
valuation (Part IV). A position is not the system itself — the system is F1's object, addressable and
stable per F1 Part IX — a position is the portfolio's RECORD of that system's standing as a capital
holding at a moment in time, carrying, minimally: the system's stable ID (imported, never reissued),
its capital draw across the three sub-pools, its current valuation and the valuation's evidence
level, its ASSET CLASS (Part III's taxonomy), and a last-reconciled timestamp inheriting F1's
staleness discipline (Part IV §4.3 of the Federation Ontology). (DERIVED: a position is to a system
exactly what F1's PROPERTY RECORD is to a system's attributes generally — a portfolio-scoped view
over the same underlying object, not a second object competing with F1's own record.)

2.4 CAPITAL POOL, the ensemble-level resource. The CAPITAL POOL is DEFINED as the total cognitive
capital available to the ensemble at a given cycle, decomposed into the three sub-pools of §2.2, and
constrained by real ceilings the portfolio does not set but must respect: the estate's actual token
budget as governed by CO-00's session ceilings aggregated upward, the Owner's actual available review
time, and the actual engineering capacity available for maintenance. The pool is EXPLICITLY finite
and EXPLICITLY the object every position competes for; this is the formal statement of "scarcity" that
Part IX operationalizes into a management mechanism, and it is the reason candidate 29 (Scarcity
Manager) folds cleanly into IAS-C1 rather than standing alone — a scarcity manager without a pool to
manage, and a pool without a scarcity manager to allocate it, are two halves of one object, not two
objects.

2.5 THESIS, the portfolio's forward-looking claim. An INVESTMENT THESIS, DEFINED, is a structured,
falsifiable claim about why a particular position (or a proposed new one) merits a change in its
capital allocation, stated in terms of the F2 Advantage Algebra's factors (Part III of the Advantage
Algebra: white-space, systems-strengthened, reuse, cross-project, compounding, model-independence)
rather than in prose alone. A thesis is not a valuation (Part IV computes valuations from OBSERVED
data); a thesis is a CLAIM about how a valuation will move if capital is added or withdrawn, and it
therefore carries its own evidence level, most often HYPOTHESIZED at formation and only reaching
DERIVED once tested against a subsequent valuation cycle (Part XII details the lifecycle). Importing
F2's factor vocabulary here, rather than inventing a parallel "why we should invest" schema, is the
specific mechanism by which IAS-C1 avoids duplicating F2's own algebra while still needing a
forward-looking object F2 itself does not define (F2's algebra scores what IS; a thesis claims what
WOULD BE).

2.6 BOARD VERDICT, the portfolio's decision object. A BOARD VERDICT, DEFINED, is the Capital
Allocation Board's (Part X) recorded decision over one position, drawn from a closed vocabulary —
INVEST, MAINTAIN, HARVEST, EXTINGUISH, WATCH — each carrying the thesis or valuation evidence that
produced it, the date, and the reconciliation cadence before the verdict must be revisited. A verdict
is DEFINED as advisory unless and until the position's own F1 AUTHORITY field (Federation Ontology
Part IV §4.2) is portfolio-governed, a distinction Part X §10.5 treats carefully, because the
portfolio must never silently seize final authority that F1's Invariant Three (singular authority per
decision class) reserves to another system without an explicit, recorded transfer.

2.7 The relation the portfolio adds to F1's typed graph: ALLOCATES-TO. F1's six relation types (Part
VI of the Federation Ontology) do not include a capital-flow relation, because F1 is deliberately
minimal and capital allocation is exactly the kind of ensemble-level operation F1 reserves for its
consumers to define. IAS-C1 therefore DEFINES a seventh relation, scoped to this dataset's family
rather than added to F1's own closed set (a boundary respected deliberately, per F1 Part VI §6.1's own
discipline against inflating the typed graph beyond necessity): ALLOCATES-TO, a directional,
time-stamped edge from the Capital Pool to a Position, weighted by the capital amount and tagged with
the Board Verdict that authorized it. The allocates-to graph, read over time, IS the portfolio's
history — every rebalance, harvest, and extinction is one or more allocates-to edges added or zeroed,
and Part XV's lifecycle model is, formally, the state machine this edge type's values pass through.

2.8 Why four objects and not more, and the discipline this leaves for the Parts that follow. Capital,
Position, Thesis, and Board Verdict, plus the one added relation, are DEFINED as the minimum
sufficient set: a smaller set could not phrase the four questions of Part I §1.5 (valuation needs
Position, forward claims need Thesis, decisions need Board Verdict, the constrained resource needs
Capital Pool); a larger set would risk re-narrating objects F1 or F2 already own, the exact discipline
F1 Part II §2.5 warns against. Every mechanism in the Parts that follow — the valuation engine (Part
IV), the allocation engine (Parts V-VII), the board (Parts X-XI) — is an operation over these five
objects and nothing else, which is what lets this dataset's later worked examples (Part XVI) and
adversarial cases (Part XVIII) be checked against a fixed, small vocabulary rather than an
ever-expanding one.

FINAL LAW — PART II. A portfolio that invents its own vocabulary for cost, value, and decision is a
second private dialect layered onto a federation F1 built expressly to prevent; Capital, Position,
Thesis, and Board Verdict are the only four new nouns this dataset needs, and each is defined strictly
in terms of what F1 and F2 already own.

PART III — TAXONOMY OF PORTFOLIO POSITIONS: SIX ASSET CLASSES
===============================================================================================

3.1 Why an asset class taxonomy, and why six. A portfolio that treats all positions as interchangeable
cannot allocate honestly, because the RIGHT response to a low valuation differs by what kind of holding
is under-earning: a young experimental system reads as low-valued for a different reason, and merits a
different board verdict, than an old, once-central system whose consumers have quietly migrated
elsewhere. F1's property model (Cost, Evidence Level, Reuse Horizon, Model-Dependence, Consumers,
Authority) supplies the raw attributes; ASSET CLASS, DEFINED here, is the classification function over
those attributes plus the position's ALLOCATES-TO history that determines which of six classes a
position currently occupies. The six are not arbitrary; each is DERIVED from a distinct combination of
consumer-count trend, valuation trend, and evidence level that the estate's own OBSERVED holdings
already exhibit in some form, and each demands a structurally different treatment from the Capital
Allocation Board (Part X).

3.2 CORE INFRASTRUCTURE. A position is CORE when its Consumers count (F1 Part IV) is high and stable
or rising, its evidence level is OBSERVED, and its depends-on fan-in (F1 Part VI §6.3) is wide — many
other systems would fail, not merely degrade, without it. Core positions are the estate's load-bearing
holdings; a token router or a knowledge-lookup kernel that a dozen other systems depend on to function
at all is core regardless of its own individual valuation trend, because a core position's TRUE value
includes the depends-on chain it anchors, not merely its own directly attributable output. (DERIVED:
this is why valuation, Part IV, must weight depends-on fan-in explicitly rather than scoring a position
purely on its own output — a naive per-system score would systematically undervalue core infrastructure,
the exact FALSE-PRECISION-adjacent failure IAS-F2 Part XVI warns against when a formula ignores a
structural factor its own graph already contains.)

3.3 AMPLIFIER. A position is an AMPLIFIER when its own direct output is modest but its STRENGTHENS
edges (F1 Part VI §6.6) touch many other positions' valuations positively — the position makes other
holdings more valuable without itself being the thing missions consume directly. F2's own worked
example of System K, which strengthens both System P and System Q without appearing as either's
direct consumer-facing output, is the canonical amplifier shape, imported here by reference rather
than re-derived. Amplifiers are the class most at risk of undervaluation by a naive portfolio view,
because a valuation engine that reads only a position's own consumer count and cost, without walking
its strengthens edges, will price an amplifier as if it were a minor, thinly-consumed system — exactly
the error Part IV §4.4's worked example exists to prevent.

3.4 NICHE. A NICHE position serves a narrow, well-defined mission type with few but STABLE and
HIGH-VALUE consumers — not many missions need it, but the ones that do need it badly, and no
substitute exists. A niche position's low consumer count must not be read as low value, which is
precisely the confusion Part XVII's concentration failure mode names when a portfolio, optimizing
naively for consumer breadth, starves a niche holding that a small but critical mission class cannot
function without. Niche positions are the class where biodiversity language (imported from IAS-D1's
ecology object, referenced not rebuilt) matters most: an estate that prunes every niche holding in
favor of broadly-consumed generalists has traded resilience for average-case efficiency, a trade this
dataset does not make unilaterally (Part IX §9.5 names the guard).

3.5 EXPERIMENTAL. An EXPERIMENTAL position is young — its evidence level has not yet reached OBSERVED,
its consumer count and reuse horizon are UNRESOLVED or provisional, and its D2A-5 birth score is its
only data point so far. Experimental positions carry deliberately elevated valuation uncertainty, and
the portfolio's discipline (Part XV) is to fund them at a bounded, capped allocation — enough capital
to generate a second data point, never enough that a wrong bet on one experimental position starves
the rest of the portfolio — until they graduate into one of the other five classes or are extinguished
for lack of traction. (NORMATIVE: the cap exists because an experimental position's HYPOTHESIZED value
is, by definition, unverified, and capital allocated on an unverified claim at scale is exactly the
fiction F1 Part IV's FINAL LAW warns against.)

3.6 LEGACY. A LEGACY position is one whose consumer count and valuation trend are declining but
non-zero — it still earns its keep, but less than it once did, and its trajectory, if unaddressed,
points toward the zombie class. Legacy is deliberately a WATCH class rather than an action class: the
board's default verdict for a legacy position is not immediate harvest, because a declining trend can
reverse (a niche mission type can resurge) and because the transition cost of retiring a still-consumed
position (breaking its consumers' depends-on edges) can exceed the capital saved, at least until an
orderly migration path exists. (DERIVED: this is the specific place IAS-D1's succession mechanism
(ledger candidate 39, routed to IAS-D1) becomes the portfolio's natural handoff — IAS-C1 flags the
legacy trend and the funding gap; IAS-D1 designs the succession; the portfolio does not attempt
succession planning itself, which would duplicate IAS-D1's object.)

3.7 ZOMBIE. A ZOMBIE position is one whose consumer count has fallen to zero or near-zero, whose
valuation is negative or indistinguishable from zero within measurement noise, and whose continued
capital draw — even a small one — produces no offsetting return. Zombies are the clearest EXTINGUISH
candidates (Part X), but the taxonomy is careful to distinguish a true zombie from two look-alikes: a
DORMANT position (Part XIII's liquidity discussion — zero current consumers but rapid, proven
redeployability for a mission type that recurs seasonally or rarely) and an UNMEASURED position (F1
Part IV §4.6's UNRESOLVED-property case — genuinely low-consumer but never properly instrumented, so
its true value is unknown rather than known-zero). Classifying a dormant or unmeasured position as a
zombie is a specific, named valuation-gaming and measurement failure this dataset treats seriously
(Part XVIII §18.3), because extinguishing a merely-dormant capability destroys real, if currently
idle, optionality.

3.8 Class transitions are not free, and the transition graph itself carries information. A position
does not move between classes instantaneously or without cost: an experimental position graduating to
niche or core requires at least one full valuation cycle of OBSERVED evidence (Part XV); a legacy
position sliding toward zombie crosses a threshold the board should flag before, not after, the
crossing (Part XI's cadence discipline); and a niche position occasionally SURGES into core status
when its narrow mission type becomes suddenly load-bearing (a synthetic example: a security-adjacent
niche capability that becomes core the moment a cross-cutting immune-system mechanism, IAS-D2, begins
depending on it structurally). The transition graph — which classes a position has occupied over its
lifetime, and how long it dwelt in each — is itself a signal the valuation engine reads (Part IV
§4.5), because a position with a volatile transition history (repeatedly bouncing between experimental
and legacy without ever settling into core, amplifier, or niche) is exhibiting a distinct failure
pattern from a position with a stable, monotonic trajectory, even if their CURRENT class happens to
match.

3.9 Worked classification pass over a synthetic six-position slice. To make the taxonomy concrete
without re-narrating any real PP module, consider six illustrative, synthetic positions drawn only to
exercise the classes. Position Router-9: forty-one consumers, OBSERVED evidence, wide depends-on
fan-in → CORE. Position Enrich-K: three direct consumers but eleven strengthens edges into other
positions' own valuations, each independently confirmed → AMPLIFIER. Position Vault-Lookup-2: six
consumers, all from one specialized compliance-adjacent mission type, no substitute exists, stable
over eighteen months → NICHE. Position Synth-Draft-7: launched two cycles ago, one D2A-5 birth score,
zero OBSERVED reuse yet → EXPERIMENTAL. Position Summ-Legacy-1: nine consumers, down from
twenty-three eighteen months ago, cost unchanged → LEGACY, flagged for a succession conversation with
IAS-D1. Position Old-Formatter-3: zero consumers for the past four cycles, valuation indistinguishable
from zero, no depends-on fan-in, no recorded dormancy pattern → ZOMBIE, routed to Part X for an
EXTINGUISH verdict. This pass demonstrates the taxonomy's operational payoff directly: six positions
that a flat "score everything the same way" view would rank by a single number instead receive six
DIFFERENT prescriptions, because their class, not merely their score, determines what the board should
do next.

FINAL LAW — PART III. Not all under-earning positions are the same problem, and not all
well-consumed positions are the same asset; a portfolio that scores every holding on one number and
ranks them is doing arithmetic, not capital allocation — the six classes are what let the same low
score mean "prune this" for a zombie and "protect this" for a niche.

PART IV — THE CAPITAL VALUATION MODEL: PRICING A SYSTEM AS A HOLDING
===============================================================================================

4.1 The valuation engine's mandate and its one hard rule. The VALUATION ENGINE is the subengine that
computes each position's current value, and its single hard rule, stated before any formula, is that
it MEASURES, it does not INVENT: every input to a position's valuation is an OBSERVED or DERIVED
quantity already produced by an existing system, imported by reference, never a fresh measurement
IAS-C1 runs itself. (NORMATIVE, and DERIVED directly from the Strengthening Matrix's own definition of
"strengthen" — Part IV §4.4 of the Federation Ontology already showed why three independently-invented
"cost" measurements from three consuming datasets is itself a duplication the ledger would have to
catch retroactively; the valuation engine is the mechanism by which IAS-C1 avoids being the fourth.)
This hard rule is what makes the valuation engine strengthen, rather than compete with, token_irr,
recall_roi, F2's Advantage Algebra, and CO-01's cost telemetry: it is a CONSUMER of all four, combined
into one position-level record, never a fifth parallel measurement.

4.2 The valuation formula's three inputs. Position Value V(S) for system S at cycle i is DEFINED as a
function of three inputs, each imported rather than computed fresh. First, the F2 ADVANTAGE SCORE
A(S, E_i) — the Institutional Advantage Unit the Advantage Algebra already computes for S, bounded to
[-1, +1], carrying its own evidence level. Second, the STRUCTURAL WEIGHT W(S) — a multiplier DERIVED
from S's position in F1's typed relation graph, specifically its depends-on fan-in (systems that would
fail outright without S) and its strengthens fan-out (systems S makes measurably better), because a
raw advantage score, as Part III §3.2 and §3.3 already showed for the core and amplifier classes,
systematically underprices structural importance that does not show up in S's own direct consumer
count. Third, the CAPITAL DRAW D(S) — the sum of S's three cognitive-capital sub-pools from Part II
§2.2, imported from CO-01's telemetry, owner_queue's attention accounting, and the estate's
maintenance records. Formally: V(S, E_i) = [A(S, E_i) x W(S)] / D(S), a ratio of structurally-weighted
advantage to capital cost, expressed in IAU-per-capital-unit, directly comparable across every
position in the portfolio regardless of asset class. (DEFINED formula; DERIVED that a ratio, not a raw
product, is the correct form, because two positions with identical advantage but different capital
draws are NOT equally good portfolio holdings — the cheaper one is strictly preferable, the exact
insight F2's own IAU already encodes at the single-system level and V(S) inherits by construction.)

4.3 The structural weight in detail, and why it prevents systematic mispricing. W(S), DEFINED, is
computed as one plus a capped contribution from depends-on fan-in (weighted 0.15 per dependent system,
capped at ten dependents) plus a capped contribution from strengthens fan-out (weighted 0.10 per
strengthened system, also capped at ten) — two capped linear terms chosen so that structural
importance amplifies a position's raw advantage score without letting an unboundedly large fan-in or
fan-out dominate the formula entirely; the caps exist specifically so that W(S) cannot alone
manufacture a high valuation for a position whose own A(S, E_i) is negative or near-zero, preserving
the discipline that structural importance modifies genuine advantage rather than substituting for it.
(EXPERIMENTAL: the specific coefficients and the cap of ten are a starting calibration, explicitly
flagged for revision once a full valuation cycle across the real estate's positions produces enough
data to check whether the weighting correctly separates the worked core and amplifier examples of
Part III from a merely well-connected but otherwise unremarkable position — this is an open
calibration question carried forward to Part XXII.)

4.4 Worked valuation: pricing an amplifier correctly where a naive score would fail. Return to Part
III §3.3's amplifier position, Enrich-K: three direct consumers, an unremarkable raw A(S, E_i) of
0.08 (near the IAU midpoint, reflecting modest direct output), but eleven confirmed strengthens edges
into other positions' own valuations. A naive valuation using A(S, E_i) alone would rank Enrich-K near
the bottom of the portfolio — a position that "does almost nothing" by direct measurement. The
structural weight corrects this: with strengthens fan-out capped at ten (Enrich-K's eleven edges hit
the cap), W(Enrich-K) equals one plus zero plus one full unit (0.10 times ten), or 2.0, doubling its
effective advantage before the ratio against its (deliberately modest, because amplifiers are
typically cheap to run) capital draw is taken. The resulting V(Enrich-K) lands in the upper third of
the synthetic six-position slice from Part III §3.9, correctly reflecting that Enrich-K's TRUE
contribution is distributed across eleven OTHER positions' valuations rather than concentrated in its
own. This worked example is the concrete demonstration of why §4.1's hard rule — measure, never
invent, but combine structurally — matters: a valuation engine that skipped the structural weight step
would have priced Claude Power Pack's most valuable amplifiers as its least valuable holdings,
precisely inverted.

4.5 Reading the transition-history signal into valuation. Part III §3.8 named the transition graph — a
position's history of asset-class occupancy — as a signal the valuation engine reads. Concretely, a
VOLATILITY PENALTY term, DERIVED and EXPERIMENTAL, subtracts a small fraction from V(S) proportional to
the number of class transitions S has undergone in the trailing four cycles beyond the single expected
experimental-to-settled transition, on the reasoning that a position which cannot settle into a stable
class is exhibiting either genuine mission-type instability (a real cost, because its consumers cannot
plan around it) or a measurement problem in its own upstream telemetry (also a real cost, because a
decision based on unstable measurement is a decision based on noise). The penalty is deliberately
small and capped, because volatility during a position's first two cycles (settling from EXPERIMENTAL)
is expected and must not be punished as if it were a mature position's instability — the penalty
applies only past the expected settling window.

4.6 The retrofitting problem: valuing a position with no prior record. Part I §1.7 named the harder
case directly: a system built before IAS-C1 existed, with no F2 advantage-score history, no captured
depends-on graph, and no capital-draw telemetry ever assembled. For such a position, the valuation
engine does NOT default to a guessed midpoint value — per F1 Part IV §4.6's own UNRESOLVED discipline,
inherited here without modification — it marks V(S) as UNRESOLVED and schedules a BACKFILL cycle: a
one-time reconstruction pass that computes A(S, E_i) from whatever historical evidence exists (mission
logs, consumer counts reconstructed from the Control Bus's event history per IAS-A2), assembles W(S)
from the current relation graph even where a historical graph is unavailable, and pulls D(S) from
whatever capital telemetry CO-01 retained. A backfilled valuation carries a permanently lower evidence
ceiling than a position valued from birth under full instrumentation — DERIVED it may reach OBSERVED
at best, never the higher confidence a position measured continuously since creation earns — and this
asymmetry is recorded, not smoothed over, because pretending a backfilled valuation is equivalent to a
continuously-measured one is exactly the false-precision failure IAS-F2 Part XVI catalogues.

4.7 Evidence level propagation: a valuation is only as strong as its weakest input. V(S)'s own
evidence level, per Article V's discipline applied here, is DEFINED as the MINIMUM of the evidence
levels of its three inputs — A(S, E_i)'s evidence level, W(S)'s (which inherits the relation graph's
own confidence, per F1 Part IX's addressing discipline), and D(S)'s (which inherits CO-01's telemetry
confidence). This minimum-propagation rule, rather than an average or a weighted blend, is a
deliberate, NORMATIVE choice: an average would let two well-measured inputs mask one poorly-measured
input, producing a valuation that READS as more confident than its weakest link actually supports, the
precise mechanism by which a portfolio quietly funds theater (F1 Part I §1.2's own warning, applied
here at the valuation-formula level rather than the ontology level).

4.8 Reconciliation cadence and staleness. Per F1's property-honesty invariant (Invariant Five, Part V
§5.6 of the Federation Ontology), every position's valuation carries a last-reconciled timestamp, and
IAS-C1 sets a default reconciliation cadence — DERIVED, not mandated by F1, which explicitly leaves
cadence to the consuming system — of once per capital-allocation cycle (Part XI defines the cycle's
length) for CORE and AMPLIFIER positions, and once every two cycles for NICHE, LEGACY, and EXPERIMENTAL
positions, on the reasoning that core and amplifier holdings carry the largest capital draw and the
largest error cost if their valuation drifts stale, while niche and legacy positions change more
slowly and can tolerate a longer reconciliation interval without materially risking a wrong board
verdict.

FINAL LAW — PART IV. Value is not a number pulled from a position's own self-report; it is
structurally-weighted advantage per unit of capital drawn, measured from inputs the estate already
produces and never invented fresh — and a valuation whose weakest input is unmeasured must say so,
because a confident number built on an UNRESOLVED foundation is capital allocated on a guess wearing
a formula's clothing.

===============================================================================================
PART V — THE ALLOCATION ENGINE: ARCHITECTURE AND SUBENGINES
===============================================================================================

5.1 What the allocation engine does that the valuation engine does not. Valuation (Part IV) answers
"what is this position worth right now." ALLOCATION answers a different question: "given the finite
capital pool (Part II §2.4) and every position's current valuation, how should capital actually move
this cycle." The two are deliberately separate subengines within IAS-C1's architecture, because
conflating them — letting a high valuation automatically and mechanically pull capital toward itself
without a distinct allocation step — would remove the very discretion the Capital Allocation Board
(Part X) exists to exercise, collapsing a governed decision into an unaccountable formula. (NORMATIVE:
valuation informs allocation; it does not replace the decision.)

5.2 The allocation engine's three subengines. MISSION-LEVEL ALLOCATION (Part VI) answers "which
positions does THIS mission draw capital from, and how much" — a per-mission, transient computation
that produces CONSUMES edges (F1 Part VI §6.7) rather than standing capital moves. SYSTEM-LEVEL
REBALANCING (Part VII) answers "should the STANDING capital draw of position S change this cycle" — a
periodic, portfolio-wide computation that produces or revises ALLOCATES-TO edges (Part II §2.7). The
SCARCITY RESOLVER (Part IX) is invoked by both of the first two subengines whenever their combined
demand for capital, in a given cycle, exceeds the pool — it does not allocate itself, it constrains
what the other two subengines may allocate, functioning as a hard ceiling rather than a third
allocation authority, which is the specific design choice that keeps F1's Invariant Three (singular
authority per decision class) satisfied: exactly one subengine, mission-level or system-level, holds
final authority over any one allocation decision at a time, and the scarcity resolver's role is
strictly to bound the total, never to pick winners itself.

5.3 Why mission-level and system-level allocation must be architecturally separate. A single combined
allocator that tried to answer both "what does this mission need right now" and "what should this
position's standing draw be going forward" in one computation would conflate two different time
horizons and two different failure costs. A wrong mission-level allocation wastes capital for one
mission and is cheaply reversible on the next mission. A wrong system-level rebalance changes a
position's STANDING draw, propagates through every mission that touches that position going forward,
and is expensive to reverse because reversal itself consumes a board cycle (Part XI). (DERIVED: this
asymmetry in reversal cost is why the two subengines run on different cadences — mission-level
allocation runs per mission, essentially continuously, while system-level rebalancing runs on the
board's periodic cycle, deliberately throttled.) Architecturally separating them is also what lets
IAS-A1 (Capability Router) remain the system that actually EXECUTES mission-level routing decisions,
consuming the mission-level allocator's capital-availability signal as one input among several rather
than IAS-C1 reaching into IAS-A1's own routing authority — the interface is detailed in Part XXI.

5.4 The engine's inputs and outputs, stated as an interface contract. INPUTS: the current Position
record set (Part II §2.3) with valuations (Part IV); the Capital Pool's current ceilings across the
three sub-pools (Part II §2.2); any pending mission's stated capital need, arriving as a request from
IAS-A1; and any standing Board Verdict already in force for a position (Part X) that constrains or
floors its allocation regardless of what the formula alone would compute (an EXTINGUISH verdict, for
instance, floors a position's allocation at its wind-down minimum, overriding whatever the raw
valuation math would otherwise permit, because a board decision is a governance fact the engine must
respect, not merely an input it may weigh). OUTPUTS: for mission-level allocation, a set of transient
CONSUMES edges plus a capital-availability signal returned to the requesting mission's router; for
system-level rebalancing, a set of proposed ALLOCATES-TO edge changes, which are PROPOSALS until the
board ratifies them (Part X §10.6) — the engine computes, the board decides, and this separation is
itself a deliberate governance boundary, not an implementation detail, because an allocation engine
that could unilaterally move standing capital without board ratification would concentrate authority
the Constitution's Article IX (governed self-evolution) requires to remain distributed and reviewable.

5.5 Failure containment: what happens when the engine's inputs are incomplete. The engine must operate
even when some positions carry UNRESOLVED valuations (Part IV §4.6) or when the capital pool's own
ceiling estimate is itself stale. The NORMATIVE rule is conservative-by-default: a position with an
UNRESOLVED valuation is allocated no MORE than its current standing draw (never increased on an
unmeasured claim) and is flagged for priority backfill rather than silently treated as average-valued;
a stale pool ceiling triggers a narrowed allocation window (the engine allocates only against the
LAST-CONFIRMED ceiling, not an extrapolated one) until the ceiling is reconciled. This containment
discipline is what keeps a data-quality gap in one corner of the estate from silently distorting
capital decisions across the whole portfolio — the failure is contained to the affected positions,
never propagated as false confidence into the rest of the allocation.

5.6 The engine's relationship to the Digital Twin and to real-time execution. The allocation engine,
as specified here, computes PROPOSED allocations; it does not itself execute a capital move against
any live system (Article VIII's reality contract: this mission specifies mechanisms, it does not build
executable code). Where a proposed system-level rebalance is large enough to carry meaningful risk —
a substantial cut to a position many missions depend on, for instance — the specification NAMES,
without building, the natural pre-commitment step: routing the proposal through IAS-F3 (Digital Twin)
for a simulated before/after read on the affected depends-on chain, before the board ratifies it. This
is a design recommendation carried into Part XXI's integration section rather than a built mechanism,
consistent with the Constitution's science-before-implementation boundary.

5.7 Worked containment trace: an UNRESOLVED position meets a stale ceiling in the same cycle. Consider a
synthetic case combining both failure paths of §5.5 at once: Position Draft-Assist-4 (Part XII §12.6's
illustrative case) carries an UNRESOLVED valuation because its backfill cycle (Part IV §4.6) has not yet
completed, and in the same cycle the estate's own maintenance-capital ceiling estimate has not been
reconciled for two cycles running, making it stale. The engine's conservative-by-default rule applies
twice, not once: Draft-Assist-4's allocation is held at its current standing draw regardless of any
mission-level request that would otherwise argue for an increase, and the maintenance sub-pool's total
allocatable capacity that cycle is computed against the LAST-CONFIRMED ceiling rather than an
extrapolated trend line, even though the trend line would have permitted a larger allocation window. The
two containments compound rather than interact — an UNRESOLVED position gains nothing from a generous
stale-ceiling read, and a stale ceiling costs every position in that sub-pool the same narrowed window,
not merely the UNRESOLVED one — which is the specific discipline that keeps a data-quality gap from
being selectively exploited by whichever position happens to carry it.

5.8 Auditability as a structural property of the engine's output, not an afterthought. Every proposal the
allocation engine emits — a CONSUMES edge, a proposed ALLOCATES-TO delta — carries, as a mandatory field
rather than optional metadata, the specific inputs that produced it: which valuation cycle's V(S) was
read, which capital-pool ceiling snapshot was consulted, and which standing Board Verdict (if any)
constrained the output. (NORMATIVE.) This is not a logging convenience; it is what makes Part X's board
deliberation possible at all, because a board asked to ratify a proposal it cannot trace back to specific
evidence is being asked to trust the engine's arithmetic on faith, exactly the failure Part X §10.6's
five-step ratification protocol is built to foreclose. An engine implementation that emits a bare number
without this provenance trail has not implemented Part V's specification, regardless of whether the
number itself is correct.

5.9 The engine's relationship to IAS-A1's own capacity model, and where the boundary sits precisely.
IAS-A1 (Capability Router) maintains its own notion of a system's operational capacity — concurrent
request limits, latency budgets, the purely technical ceiling on how much traffic a system can serve
regardless of capital. This is a DIFFERENT ceiling from the capital-availability signal Part VI's
mission-level allocation supplies, and the two must never be conflated: a position can be capital-rich
(ample allocation remaining) while operationally saturated (at its technical capacity limit), or the
reverse, and IAS-A1 is expected to consult BOTH signals independently before routing a mission, treating
IAS-C1's capital-availability signal as one gate among several rather than the sole determinant of
routability. (DERIVED: this boundary is why §5.4's interface contract states the engine's output as an
availability SIGNAL, not a routing DECISION — collapsing the two ceilings into one combined signal would
hide from IAS-A1 exactly which constraint was binding, making a genuine capacity problem look like a
capital problem or vice versa, and misdiagnosing the constraint leads to the wrong remedy being pursued.)

FINAL LAW — PART V. Valuation says what a holding is worth; allocation says what should happen to it
this cycle — and keeping those two questions in separate subengines, with the board standing between
computation and commitment, is what keeps the portfolio a governed capital-allocation system rather
than an unaccountable formula that moves capital on its own authority.

===============================================================================================
PART VI — MISSION-LEVEL ALLOCATION: CAPITAL ACROSS A CONCURRENT MISSION SET
===============================================================================================

6.1 The mission-level question and its distinct time pressure. At any moment the estate is running
some number of concurrent missions, each drawing on some subset of the portfolio's positions, and each
arriving with its own stated capital need and urgency. MISSION-LEVEL ALLOCATION answers, per mission
and in near-real-time, which positions serve it and how much of the pool's currently-available capital
that service consumes. Unlike system-level rebalancing (Part VII), mission-level allocation cannot wait
for a periodic board cycle — a mission that needs Router-9's capacity now cannot be told to wait three
days for the next allocation cycle — and this time pressure is the specific reason mission-level
allocation is architecturally a fast, largely mechanical subengine bounded by standing Board Verdicts
(Part V §5.4) rather than itself a deliberative process.

6.2 The mechanism: capital-availability as a signal to the router, not a routing decision itself. When
IAS-A1 (Capability Router) evaluates which system should serve a mission, mission-level allocation
supplies ONE input among several: for each candidate position, a CAPITAL-AVAILABILITY SIGNAL stating
whether serving this mission from this position is within the position's currently allocated draw, at
its floor (a legacy or watch-flagged position nearing its allocation ceiling), or would require an
EXCEPTION (a request exceeding the position's standing allocation, which mission-level allocation may
grant up to a small emergency reserve but never silently expand beyond). (NORMATIVE: mission-level
allocation informs routing, it does not perform routing — IAS-A1 retains final authority over which
system serves a mission, and IAS-C1's signal is advisory input, exactly the boundary the Strengthening
Matrix's evidence-required row for IAS-A1 demands: "a mission trace where A1 selects a system the
parent routers could not see," where the parent router here is IAS-C1's own signal, not a competing
authority.)

6.3 The emergency reserve, its size, and why it is small on purpose. Each capital sub-pool (Part II
§2.2) carries a small EMERGENCY RESERVE — DERIVED, sized as a minority fraction of the pool's total,
never a majority — that mission-level allocation may draw against when a mission's need exceeds a
position's standing allocation and the mission's urgency (a signal imported from owner_queue's own
priority scoring, never re-derived) justifies an exception. The reserve is deliberately small because
a reserve sized to absorb frequent exceptions would, in practice, become the DEFAULT allocation
mechanism, quietly bypassing the system-level rebalancing discipline of Part VII every time a position
runs short — the exact failure mode where a "temporary" mechanism becomes the load-bearing one because
it is easier to invoke than the governed alternative. A reserve draw that recurs against the same
position more than a bounded number of times within one cycle is, by design, escalated to Part VII's
rebalancing subengine rather than repeatedly drawn against — recurrence is itself the signal that the
position's STANDING allocation, not merely this mission's exception, needs revision.

6.4 Concurrent mission contention over the same position. When two or more concurrent missions draw
on the same position within its available capacity, mission-level allocation applies a PRIORITY
ORDERING imported, again without re-derivation, from owner_queue's own priority scores, with one
addition specific to this dataset: a mission whose stated capital need is small relative to the
position's remaining capacity is served before a mission whose need would exhaust most of the
remaining capacity, WHEN both carry equal owner_queue priority — a tie-break favoring throughput over
a single large draw, on the DERIVED reasoning that serving several small missions from the same
remaining capacity produces more total mission completions than reserving that capacity for one large
one, all else equal. This tie-break is EXPERIMENTAL and explicitly named as a candidate for revision
once real contention data exists (Part XX).

6.5 Worked trace: three concurrent missions, one contended position. Consider a synthetic trace,
illustrative only. Mission M-A (Owner priority HIGH, small stated need) and Mission M-B (Owner
priority HIGH, large stated need) both request Router-9's capacity within the same allocation window;
Router-9's remaining capacity this cycle covers M-A fully and roughly sixty percent of M-B's stated
need. Mission M-C (Owner priority MEDIUM) requests a different position, Enrich-K, with ample
remaining capacity, and is served without contention. For the Router-9 contention: M-A is served first
under the small-need tie-break of §6.4, consuming its full request; M-B receives the remaining
capacity, is flagged PARTIAL, and mission-level allocation returns a capital-availability signal to
IAS-A1 indicating the shortfall, at which point IAS-A1 — not IAS-C1 — decides whether to route M-B's
remaining need to a substitute position, accept the partial service, or escalate to the emergency
reserve of §6.3 given HIGH priority. This trace demonstrates the boundary of §6.2 concretely: IAS-C1
never decided M-B's fate, it supplied the availability signal IAS-A1 needed to decide it.

6.6 Recording mission-level allocation without inflating the standing graph. Every mission-level
allocation produces a CONSUMES edge (F1 Part VI §6.7), explicitly the most volatile and highest-
cardinality of F1's relation types, and this dataset inherits F1's own discipline (Part VI §6.10 of
the Federation Ontology) that consumes edges are not retained indefinitely in the live relation graph —
they are logged to the mission history (consumed by IAS-E1's observability fabric per Part XXI) and
aggregated into rolling CONSUMERS counts (F1 Part IV's property) rather than kept as individual
standing edges, because a graph that retained every transient mission-level draw as a permanent edge
would grow unboundedly and would defeat the very distinction between standing and transient relations
F1's typing exists to preserve.

6.7 Edge case: a mission that spans multiple capital-allocation cycles. Most missions complete within
one allocation window, but some — particularly large, multi-phase missions — span several. Mission-
level allocation handles this by treating a long-running mission's capital draw as a STANDING
sub-allocation for its duration, re-evaluated at each cycle boundary alongside system-level
rebalancing rather than re-requested from scratch each cycle, on the reasoning that a mission
mid-flight should not be starved of a position it has already been granted merely because a new
cycle's priority ordering would, from scratch, have favored a different mission — REVERSAL of an
in-flight allocation carries its own cost (partial mission work already spent against the original
allocation) that the priority ordering of §6.4 must weigh, not ignore, when a long-running mission
competes against a newly-arrived one.

6.8 What mission-level allocation deliberately does not do. It does not evaluate mission QUALITY or
whether a mission should exist at all — that judgment belongs to whatever system approved the mission
in the first place, never to the capital-allocation layer. It does not retroactively judge whether a
completed mission's capital draw was WORTH it — that is Part VIII's ROI question, evaluated after the
fact, not during allocation. And it does not attempt to forecast future mission demand — that is
IAS-C2's (Demand Forecasting) distinct object, and mission-level allocation consumes IAS-C2's forecast
as an input to sizing the emergency reserve (§6.3) without duplicating the forecasting function itself.

FINAL LAW — PART VI. Mission-level allocation is a fast, bounded, advisory signal that lets missions
draw on standing capital without waiting for governance, and its entire discipline is contained in one
sentence: it may size and prioritize a request within what the board has already allocated, but it may
never, on its own authority, change what the board allocated.

===============================================================================================
PART VII — SYSTEM-LEVEL REBALANCING: CHANGING STANDING CAPITAL DRAW
===============================================================================================

7.1 The rebalancing question and why it is periodic, not continuous. Where mission-level allocation
(Part VI) answers "what does this one mission get," SYSTEM-LEVEL REBALANCING answers "should Position
S's STANDING allocation — the baseline every mission-level request is measured against — change this
cycle." Rebalancing is deliberately periodic (bound to the board's cadence, Part XI) rather than
continuous, because a standing allocation that changed every time a single mission's demand fluctuated
would produce the exact instability Part IV §4.5's volatility penalty warns against, this time
inflicted on the portfolio's own capital structure rather than merely on one position's valuation
trend — consumers of a position need to be able to plan around a STABLE baseline, and a rebalancing
cadence is what supplies that stability while still allowing genuine, sustained demand or valuation
shifts to be reflected.

7.2 The rebalancing proposal's inputs. Each cycle, for every position, the rebalancing subengine reads:
the position's current valuation and evidence level (Part IV); its rolling CONSUMERS trend and
mission-level allocation history from the prior cycle (Part VI §6.6's aggregated logs); its asset class
(Part III) and any class-transition signal; its capital draw relative to the pool's total (a
CONCENTRATION check, cross-referenced against Part IX's scarcity discipline); and any standing Board
Verdict already in force. From these, it computes a PROPOSED delta — an increase, decrease, or hold —
for the position's standing allocation, never executing the delta itself (Part V §5.4's proposal/
ratification boundary).

7.3 The rebalancing formula's shape, and its asymmetry between increases and decreases. Proposed
deltas are NOT symmetric between growing and shrinking a position's allocation, and this asymmetry is
NORMATIVE and deliberate: an INCREASE proposal requires evidence at DERIVED or above (a valuation
trend confirmed across at least two consecutive cycles, not a single-cycle spike), while a DECREASE
proposal may be triggered by a single cycle's clear signal (a position that abruptly loses its last
several consumers, for instance) because the cost of under-reacting to a genuine decline (continued
capital waste) is treated as smaller than the cost of over-reacting to a noisy uptick (destabilizing a
position's consumers on a false positive) is treated as larger than the cost of a slower, confirmed
increase. (DERIVED, from the general principle that capital committed is harder to reclaim than
capital withheld — an increase that turns out wrong has already been spent by the time the second
confirming cycle would have caught it, while a decrease that turns out wrong can be reversed the
following cycle at the cost of one cycle's underfunding, a materially cheaper error.)

7.4 Concentration checks within rebalancing. Before finalizing a proposed increase, the rebalancing
subengine checks whether granting it would push the position's share of the total capital pool past a
CONCENTRATION THRESHOLD — DERIVED, and detailed fully in Part XVII's failure-mode treatment — beyond
which a single position's failure or mispricing would create outsized portfolio-wide risk. A proposed
increase that would cross the threshold is not silently capped; it is flagged to the board as a
CONCENTRATION FLAG requiring explicit board discussion (Part X §10.4) rather than either automatic
approval or automatic denial, because concentration is sometimes the CORRECT answer (a genuinely
irreplaceable core position legitimately warranting a larger share) and sometimes a warning sign, and
only the board's deliberation, not a mechanical cap, can tell the two apart reliably.

7.5 Worked rebalance: a niche position's confirmed uptick. Consider Vault-Lookup-2 (Part III §3.9's
niche position), whose consumer count, stable at six for eighteen months, rises to nine across two
consecutive cycles following a new compliance-adjacent mission type's adoption. Its valuation V(S)
rises correspondingly across both cycles (DERIVED-level evidence, satisfying §7.3's increase bar), its
capital draw remains modest (niche positions are typically cheap, per Part III §3.4), and the proposed
increase does not approach the concentration threshold. The rebalancing subengine proposes a moderate
increase, routes it to the board as a routine (non-flagged) item, and — absent an unusual board
objection — it is ratified the same cycle, illustrating the ordinary, low-friction path most
rebalancing proposals take; the flagged, deliberative path of §7.4 is the exception, reserved for
genuinely concentration-relevant or contested proposals, not the default for every increase.

7.6 Interaction with the liquidity subengine on decreases. A proposed decrease is never finalized
without first consulting Part XIII's liquidity assessment for the affected position: a decrease to a
position with LOW liquidity (one that cannot be quickly redeployed or whose consumers cannot quickly
substitute) carries a materially higher transition risk than an identical decrease to a HIGH-liquidity
position, and the rebalancing subengine's proposal to the board carries the liquidity read alongside
the raw valuation trend, so the board's deliberation (Part X) is never asked to weigh a decrease
without also seeing how disruptive that decrease would actually be to execute.

7.7 What rebalancing does not do: it does not extinguish. A rebalancing proposal's decrease floor is
bounded — it may shrink a position toward its minimum viable operating allocation, but the decision to
extinguish a position entirely (Part X's EXTINGUISH verdict) is a distinct, higher-threshold board
decision requiring its own evidence standard (Part X §10.3), never an automatic terminus of successive
rebalancing decreases. This separation exists because extinguishing a position has consequences
rebalancing's own formula does not price — breaking consumer dependencies, forfeiting sunk
capability-building cost, foreclosing future optionality — each of which belongs properly to the
board's deliberative judgment (and, per Part III §3.6, often to a coordinated handoff with IAS-D1's
succession mechanism) rather than to a mechanical formula's threshold-crossing.

7.8 Worked rebalance: a decrease proposal correctly deferred by a liquidity read. Consider a synthetic
case where Summ-Legacy-1 (Part III §3.9's legacy position) shows a second consecutive cycle of
declining consumer count, which alone would clear §7.3's single-cycle decrease bar comfortably. Before
the rebalancing subengine finalizes the proposed decrease, §7.6's liquidity consultation returns a
LOW-liquidity read for Summ-Legacy-1 specifically because its remaining three consumers have no ready
substitute position and its documentation, while adequate, has not been updated to reflect a recent
internal refactor — a genuine transition-risk signal the raw valuation trend alone would not have
surfaced. The rebalancing subengine's proposal to the board therefore carries both readings side by
side: the valuation trend supporting a decrease, and the liquidity read counseling a SLOWER, staged
decrease rather than a single large cut, illustrating that §7.6's consultation is not a formality
appended to an already-decided proposal but a genuine input that can change the SHAPE, though not the
direction, of what the engine proposes.

7.9 Why the decrease floor, not merely the extinguish threshold, is itself evidence-gated. §7.7 already
established that rebalancing cannot extinguish a position outright, but the floor toward which a
decrease may shrink a position is not an arbitrary operational minimum either — it is DEFINED, per Part
IX §9.3's floor guarantee, as the minimum capital draw below which the position's capability stops
functioning at all, and the rebalancing subengine reads this floor from the SAME source the scarcity
resolver consults, never computing its own, looser floor under time pressure. (NORMATIVE: a rebalancing
subengine implementation that hard-codes its own floor estimate rather than reading the position's
registered floor risks two symmetric failures — a floor set too low starves a position below functional
capacity while still reading as "operational" on paper, and a floor set too high needlessly blocks a
legitimate decrease the position's genuine trend warrants, and both failures are avoided by treating the
floor as a single, shared, position-level fact rather than a value each consuming mechanism is free to
reinterpret.)

7.10 Interaction between successive small decreases and the concentration index's counterpart signal.
Where Part XVII's concentration index tracks capital ACCUMULATING unhealthily in a few positions,
rebalancing carries a symmetric, less-discussed risk on the decrease side: a position subjected to
several small, individually-justified decreases across consecutive cycles, none large enough to trigger
a flagged review, can drift toward its floor by accumulation exactly as concentration can drift upward
by accumulation (Part XVII §17.6's adversarial case, mirrored). The rebalancing subengine therefore
tracks, per position, a rolling DECREASE STREAK — the count of consecutive cycles a position has
received any decrease, however small — and a streak crossing a bounded length (DERIVED, EXPERIMENTAL)
escalates the NEXT proposed decrease to the flagged path regardless of that single cycle's own
magnitude, so that a "death by a thousand cuts" pattern on the decrease side receives the same standing
visibility the concentration index gives the increase side.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. Standing capital allocation changes on a governed cadence, grows only on
confirmed evidence across more than one cycle, may shrink on a single clear signal, and never crosses
into extinguishing a position by mechanical accumulation of small decreases — rebalancing adjusts the
baseline; it does not, on its own, ever zero it.

===============================================================================================
PART VIII — ROI AT FEDERATION SCALE: AGGREGATING PER-ASSET RETURN INTO PORTFOLIO RETURN
===============================================================================================

8.1 The gap between per-asset ROI and portfolio ROI. token_irr measures a per-asset internal rate of
return; recall_roi measures how efficiently a specific asset's reuse pays back its original build
cost. Both are real, both are OBSERVED against actual estate telemetry, and neither, alone or summed
naively, answers the portfolio-scale question: is the ESTATE, taken as a whole, earning more from its
total capital deployment than that capital costs to hold. Summing seventy per-asset ROI figures is not
the same operation as computing the portfolio's aggregate return, for the same reason a fund's total
return is not the arithmetic mean of its holdings' individual returns — POSITION SIZE matters, and a
naive average would let one abandoned, badly-valued zombie position with a wildly negative ROI carry
the same weight in the portfolio's headline figure as a heavily-capitalized core position most of the
estate's capital actually sits in.

8.2 FEDERATION ROI, defined as a capital-weighted aggregate. FEDERATION ROI (F-ROI) for cycle i is
DEFINED as the sum, across every position S in the portfolio, of S's own return (imported from
token_irr and recall_roi, never re-measured) multiplied by S's share of total capital drawn that
cycle, divided by the total capital pool committed — a capital-weighted average, not a simple mean.
This is the specific mechanism by which IAS-C1 strengthens token_irr and recall_roi rather than
replacing them: F-ROI is a CONSUMER of both, combined at the correct weighting the two parent metrics,
each scoped to one asset, cannot themselves compute, because neither token_irr nor recall_roi has
visibility into the OTHER sixty-nine positions' capital shares needed to weight correctly.

8.3 Why F-ROI must be read alongside, never in place of, the class-level breakdown. A single F-ROI
number for the whole portfolio is useful as a headline trend but dangerous as a sole decision input,
for the same false-precision reason F2's own Fusion Score deliberately stays qualitative rather than
collapsing to one figure (IAS-F2 Part V). IAS-C1 therefore always reports F-ROI DECOMPOSED by asset
class (Part III): CORE-ROI, AMPLIFIER-ROI, NICHE-ROI, and so on, because a healthy portfolio's classes
are EXPECTED to show different return profiles — core positions typically show stable, moderate
returns; experimental positions show high variance including some negative figures by design (Part
III §3.5's bounded-bet discipline); niche positions show high returns on small capital bases — and a
single blended number hides exactly the composition information the board needs to judge whether the
portfolio's overall shape is healthy or merely lucky in one class this cycle.

8.4 The window problem: over what period is ROI honestly measured. A position's return over one cycle
can be noisy — a temporary mission surge, a one-off evaluation win — and F-ROI computed over too short
a window inherits that noise. IAS-C1 therefore computes F-ROI over a TRAILING WINDOW (DERIVED, aligned
with F2's own Compounding factor's trailing-window discipline, Part VI of the Advantage Algebra, rather
than inventing a separate window convention) long enough to smooth single-cycle noise but short enough
to remain a leading indicator rather than a lagging one that only confirms what the board already knew
from other signals. The specific window length is EXPERIMENTAL, calibrated the same way F2's own
compounding window is calibrated (Part XXII names this an explicit joint calibration question with
F2), and is not asserted here as a settled figure.

8.5 Marginal ROI: the return on the NEXT unit of capital, not the average return on capital already
committed. The board's forward decisions (Part X) are better served by MARGINAL ROI — an estimate,
DERIVED from the valuation trend of Part IV §4.2-4.3, of what an INCREMENTAL unit of capital to
position S would likely return, distinct from S's AVERAGE ROI on capital already sunk. A position can
show strong average ROI (its historical capital has paid off well) while showing weak or even negative
MARGINAL ROI (its capacity is saturated; more capital would not proportionally increase its output) —
the classic diminishing-returns shape, and conflating the two is a specific, named error this dataset
flags because it would otherwise lead the board to keep pouring capital into an already-saturated core
position on the strength of a historical average that no longer reflects the position's current
marginal economics. (DERIVED, and CONTESTED at the calibration level: whether the estate's positions
generally exhibit diminishing returns, constant returns, or occasionally increasing returns to
additional capital — network-effect positions like core knowledge-lookup systems may show INCREASING
marginal returns as consumer count rises — is not asserted uniformly here; Part XX treats it as an open
empirical question requiring class-specific study.)

8.6 Worked federation-scale computation on the synthetic six-position slice. Extending Part III §3.9's
slice with illustrative return figures: Router-9 (core, large capital share, moderate steady return),
Enrich-K (amplifier, small capital share, return computed including its structural-weight-corrected
contribution per Part IV §4.4), Vault-Lookup-2 (niche, small capital share, high return relative to
its size), Synth-Draft-7 (experimental, small capped capital share, currently negative return —
expected and tolerated per Part III §3.5), Summ-Legacy-1 (legacy, moderate capital share, declining
but still-positive return), Old-Formatter-3 (zombie, near-zero remaining capital share after prior
cycles' decreases, deeply negative return on its remaining draw). The capital-weighted F-ROI over this
slice is dominated by Router-9's large weight and moderate return, with Old-Formatter-3's very
negative return contributing little to the aggregate precisely BECAUSE its capital share has already
been shrunk by prior rebalancing (Part VII) — demonstrating that F-ROI and the rebalancing/board
mechanisms are not independent: a well-functioning allocation process shrinks the weight of its own
worst performers over time, which is itself a signal the board should watch (a portfolio where F-ROI
improves ONLY because bad positions were shrunk, with no genuine improvement in remaining positions'
returns, is improving by amputation, not by growth, and Part XIV §14.5 names this distinction directly
as a countermetric).

8.7 F-ROI's relationship to Net Institutional Capability Gain. F2 defines NICG (Net Institutional
Capability Gain, Part VIII of the Advantage Algebra) as the ensemble-wide index of whether the SUM of
everyone's individual improvement translates into genuine estate-wide improvement, explicitly
distinguishing the two. F-ROI is a NARROWER, capital-specific instance of the same discipline applied
to return-on-capital rather than to advantage-gain broadly: F-ROI asks whether capital committed is
earning a return; NICG asks the broader question of whether the ensemble's total capability genuinely
grew. The two are DERIVED to move together in a healthy portfolio (capital well-allocated toward
genuine advantage should show both a healthy F-ROI and a healthy NICG) but are not guaranteed to
agree, and a DIVERGENCE between them — F-ROI rising while NICG stagnates, for instance — is itself
diagnostic, flagged in Part XIV as a signal that capital is being allocated to positions whose
individual returns look good in isolation without the estate's genuine capability actually compounding,
a CONTESTED-pending-evidence scenario this dataset names rather than resolves.

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. A portfolio's return is not the average of its holdings' returns; it is their
capital-weighted aggregate, decomposed by class, read on a trailing window long enough to smooth noise
and short enough to still lead, and always distinguished from marginal return, because the number that
justifies TODAY's spend and the number that explains YESTERDAY's spend are never the same figure.

===============================================================================================
PART IX — SCARCITY MANAGEMENT: ALLOCATING WHEN DEMAND EXCEEDS THE POOL
===============================================================================================

9.1 Scarcity as the estate's permanent condition, not an exceptional one. The capital pool (Part II
§2.4) is, as a matter of definition, finite; total demand across every position's ideal allocation and
every mission's stated need will, in the estate's ordinary operation, EXCEED that pool more often than
not, because a healthy federation is one that keeps generating more good ideas for capital deployment
than it can fund at once — the alternative, a pool that always exceeds demand, would indicate a
portfolio that has stopped generating investable opportunity, a warning sign in its own right (Part
XX's open questions revisit this). SCARCITY MANAGEMENT, as DEFINED, is therefore not an emergency
mode invoked occasionally; it is the STANDING discipline by which the allocation engine (Parts V-VII)
and the board (Part X) operate under a permanent, not episodic, constraint.

9.2 The scarcity resolver's mechanism: a constrained-optimization ceiling, not a rationing formula. Per
Part V §5.2, the SCARCITY RESOLVER does not itself choose which positions or missions receive capital —
that authority remains with mission-level allocation (Part VI, bounded by priority) and system-level
rebalancing (Part VII, bounded by evidence and the board). The resolver's role is narrower and more
mechanical: given the combined DEMAND from both subengines in a cycle, and the pool's actual ceiling,
it computes the SHORTFALL (if any) and applies a PROPORTIONAL THROTTLE across the lowest-priority tier
of pending requests, in reverse priority order, until the ceiling is respected — never touching
higher-priority requests while any lower-priority request remains unthrottled, and never touching a
position's FLOOR (its minimum-viable operating allocation, below which the position cannot function at
all, distinct from being merely reduced) except through the board's own EXTINGUISH pathway (Part X),
not through the resolver's throttle.

9.3 The floor guarantee, and why it exists. Every position with a live Board Verdict other than
EXTINGUISH carries a FLOOR, DEFINED as the minimum capital draw below which the position cannot
execute its capability at all — a hard lower bound the scarcity resolver may never cross unilaterally.
The floor guarantee exists because a resolver empowered to throttle a position below its floor is, in
effect, extinguishing it through scarcity pressure without the board's deliberate EXTINGUISH decision
(Part X §10.3) ever being made — an ungoverned death by a thousand cuts that would violate the
Constitution's Article IX governed-self-evolution requirement as surely as an explicit unilateral
extinguish would, merely by a different mechanical path. A position whose demand genuinely cannot be
met even at its floor, cycle after cycle, is not a scarcity-resolver problem; it is escalated to the
board as a genuine EXTINGUISH-or-invest-more candidate (Part X §10.7).

9.4 Cross-mission versus cross-system scarcity, and why the resolver treats them differently. Scarcity
within a single cycle's mission-level demand (many missions wanting the same position, Part VI §6.4-
6.5) is resolved by the priority-and-throughput tie-break already specified there — a FAST,
per-position mechanism. Scarcity ACROSS the whole portfolio's rebalancing proposals (many positions
each requesting an increase in the same cycle, collectively exceeding what the pool can fund even
after existing draws are accounted for) is a SLOWER, portfolio-wide problem, and here the resolver
applies a distinct rule: proposed increases are ranked by MARGINAL ROI (Part VIII §8.5), not merely by
asset class or by requesting position's seniority, on the DERIVED reasoning that when the pool cannot
fund every deserving increase, the increases most likely to produce the largest genuine return per
unit of additional capital should be funded first — the scarce resource should flow toward its highest
marginal use, the textbook capital-allocation principle this dataset imports deliberately rather than
inventing a portfolio-specific alternative.

9.5 The biodiversity guard against pure marginal-ROI optimization. A resolver that ranked PURELY by
marginal ROI, with no other constraint, would systematically starve niche positions (Part III §3.4) in
favor of already-broad, already-efficient core and amplifier positions, because a niche position's
absolute marginal return, even when high relative to its own tiny capital base, will often lose a
head-to-head ranking against a core position's marginal return on a much larger base. IAS-C1 therefore
carries a NORMATIVE guard, imported by cross-reference from IAS-D1's ecology object (never rebuilt
here): a minimum PROTECTED SHARE of the capital pool is reserved for niche and experimental positions
collectively, insulated from pure marginal-ROI competition against core and amplifier positions, on the
reasoning that a portfolio optimized purely for marginal return this cycle systematically underinvests
in the diversity and optionality that protect the estate's NEXT cycle — the same trade-off named, not
resolved unilaterally, in Part III §3.4, and resolved here by an explicit reserved share rather than by
hoping marginal-ROI ranking alone produces a biodiverse outcome.

9.6 Worked scarcity resolution across a contested cycle. Consider a synthetic cycle where five
positions each submit a rebalancing increase proposal (Part VII) whose combined ask exceeds the
pool's available increase capacity by roughly forty percent. Two of the five are core positions with
strong, confirmed marginal ROI; one is an amplifier with moderate marginal ROI; two are niche
positions with high per-unit marginal ROI but small absolute asks. The protected-share guard (§9.5)
first reserves capacity for the two niche proposals, funding both in full given their small absolute
size. The remaining capacity is insufficient for both core proposals and the amplifier proposal
combined; ranked by marginal ROI, the higher-marginal-ROI core proposal and the amplifier proposal are
funded, and the lower-marginal-ROI core proposal is PARTIALLY funded, with the shortfall flagged to the
board (§9.3's escalation path) rather than silently dropped, because a core position receiving less
than its confirmed-evidence increase warrants is itself a signal the board should see, not a routine
throttle outcome to bury in a log.

9.7 Scarcity as information, not merely as a constraint. Beyond its mechanical role, the pattern of
WHICH requests get throttled, cycle after cycle, is itself a signal IAS-C2 (Demand Forecasting)
consumes: a recurring shortfall in a particular class or mission type is evidence of genuine unmet
demand the estate should consider expanding its total capital pool to meet (a decision outside IAS-C1's
own authority — the pool's actual ceiling is set by real constraints, Part II §2.4 — but one the
portfolio's scarcity pattern can evidence and escalate). This is the specific mechanism by which the
scarcity resolver strengthens IAS-C2 rather than merely absorbing its own friction silently.

9.8 Worked cross-mission versus cross-system scarcity interaction, and why the resolver must check both
before either is finalized. A single cycle can present BOTH forms of scarcity §9.4 distinguishes,
simultaneously, and the resolver's protocol handles them in a fixed order rather than either in
isolation: mission-level contention (Part VI §6.4-6.5) is resolved first, within the cycle's already-
standing allocations, because it operates against capital already committed and does not itself change
any position's baseline; system-level rebalancing scarcity (§9.4's second case) is resolved second,
against whatever net-new capacity remains after mission-level draws for the cycle are accounted for,
because a rebalancing increase that is ratified without first knowing how much of the standing pool
mission-level demand has already consumed would risk double-committing the same capital. (DERIVED: this
ordering is not arbitrary — reversing it, resolving rebalancing scarcity before mission-level contention
is known, would require the mission-level subengine to operate against a capital baseline that could
shift underneath it mid-cycle, reintroducing exactly the instability Part VII §7.1 names as the reason
rebalancing is periodic rather than continuous.)

9.9 The resolver's own failure mode when the floor guarantee and the proportional throttle disagree. A
specific edge case merits its own treatment: a cycle where even throttling every lower-priority request
to its floor still leaves total demand exceeding the pool, because the SUM of every live position's floor
alone exceeds the pool's total ceiling — a genuine, if rare, structural scarcity condition rather than an
allocation-mechanics problem. The resolver does not attempt to solve this by breaching a floor (§9.3's
guarantee is absolute against unilateral resolver action); instead, a floor-sum-exceeds-pool condition is
an automatic, mandatory escalation to the board as a PORTFOLIO-WIDE capacity emergency, distinct from an
ordinary contested-cycle escalation (§9.6), because it signals the estate's total holdings, at their bare
functional minimums, no longer fit within its actual capital ceiling — a condition this dataset names as
a hard limit on how many positions the estate can hold live simultaneously, not merely how generously it
can fund them.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. Scarcity is not an exception the portfolio occasionally handles; it is the
estate's permanent condition, and the resolver's entire discipline is to throttle the lowest-priority
margin, never a position's floor, to rank contested increases by marginal return, and to protect a
reserved share for the diversity a pure marginal-return ranking would otherwise starve.

===============================================================================================
PART X — THE CAPITAL ALLOCATION BOARD: DECISION MODEL
===============================================================================================

10.1 Why a board, and not a formula, holds the final verdict. Every mechanism specified so far —
valuation (Part IV), mission-level and system-level allocation (Parts VI-VII), scarcity resolution
(Part IX) — computes proposals, signals, and flags. None of them COMMIT a standing change to a
position's allocation or existence. The CAPITAL ALLOCATION BOARD (CAB), absorbing ledger candidate 25
as a subengine of IAS-C1 rather than a standing separate system, is the deliberative body that converts
proposals into Board Verdicts (Part II §2.6), and its existence as a distinct decision layer — rather
than letting the highest-scoring proposal auto-execute — is the specific mechanism by which this
dataset satisfies the Constitution's Article IX (governed self-evolution): the portfolio proposes,
strengthened by the algebra and the engines that precede this Part; the board, standing in for the
Owner's own judgment or an Owner-delegated authority, promotes.

10.2 The five-verdict vocabulary, defined precisely. INVEST: increase a position's standing capital
allocation, requiring the confirmed-evidence bar of Part VII §7.3. MAINTAIN: hold a position's current
allocation unchanged, the DEFAULT verdict for any position whose valuation, class, and trend show no
material signal requiring action — maintain is not a null decision, it is an ACTIVE confirmation that
the position's current standing has been reviewed and found appropriate, distinct from a position never
reviewed at all (which would carry a stale reconciliation flag, Part IV §4.8, not a MAINTAIN verdict).
HARVEST: reduce a position's allocation toward its floor while keeping it operational, typically applied
to LEGACY positions per Part III §3.6's watch discipline, freeing capital for reallocation without
severing the position's remaining consumers. EXTINGUISH: retire a position entirely, requiring the
higher evidentiary bar of §10.3, coordinated where applicable with IAS-D1's succession mechanism.
WATCH: neither change allocation nor commit to a future action, but flag a position for closer
monitoring at a shortened reconciliation cadence — used for positions showing early ambiguous signals
(an emerging concentration flag, an unconfirmed valuation trend) that do not yet warrant one of the
other four verdicts but should not be silently left at a standard cadence either.

10.3 The evidentiary bar for EXTINGUISH, stated explicitly. Because extinguishing a position is the
board's least reversible verdict — reversing it means rebuilding a capability from nothing rather than
merely re-adjusting a standing allocation — it carries the highest evidentiary bar in the vocabulary:
OBSERVED zero-or-near-zero consumers sustained across at least two consecutive reconciliation cycles
(never a single-cycle reading, mirroring but exceeding the increase bar of Part VII §7.3), confirmed
ABSENCE of dormancy or unmeasured-property look-alikes (Part III §3.7's explicit guard against
misclassifying a dormant or unmeasured position as a true zombie), and a documented check against
IAS-D1's ecology map for any symbiosis the position participates in that would be lost — a check the
board consults, not one IAS-C1 computes itself, respecting IAS-D1's own distinct object. An EXTINGUISH
verdict that skips any of these three checks is, by this dataset's own discipline, not a valid verdict
regardless of who issues it.

10.4 How a proposal reaches the board, and what triggers deliberation versus routine ratification. Not
every rebalancing proposal (Part VII) requires the same depth of board attention. ROUTINE proposals —
small, unflagged increases or decreases within a position's normal variance, like Part VII §7.5's
worked niche-position uptick — are ratified through an expedited path requiring only a single board
reviewer's sign-off, logged but not deliberated at length. FLAGGED proposals — those crossing the
concentration threshold (Part VII §7.4), those proposing EXTINGUISH, those where the scarcity
resolver's escalation (Part IX §9.6) surfaced a shortfall against confirmed evidence, or those where
IAS-D1's ecology map flags a parasitism or symbiosis concern — require full board deliberation: a
recorded discussion, an explicit vote or consensus record, and a documented rationale referencing the
specific evidence (valuation trend, thesis, liquidity read) that produced the verdict. (NORMATIVE: the
two-tier structure exists so that board attention, itself a scarce ATTENTION-CAPITAL resource per Part
II §2.2, is spent on the decisions that actually warrant deliberation rather than exhausted on routine
ratification of proposals the engines have already adequately evidenced.)

10.5 The authority boundary: advisory by default, final only by explicit, recorded transfer. Per Part
II §2.6, a Board Verdict is DEFINED as advisory unless the affected position's F1 AUTHORITY field
(Federation Ontology Part IV §4.2) has been explicitly reassigned to portfolio governance. In practice,
this means: for the majority of positions, the board's INVEST/MAINTAIN/HARVEST verdict is a strong
recommendation the position's own governing system (whichever system holds that position's F1 final
authority — often the system's own maintainers, or a domain-specific governor) is expected to honor,
but the portfolio does not silently seize final decision rights merely by computing a verdict. EXTINGUISH
is the deliberate exception, treated as requiring EXPLICIT Owner or Owner-delegated ratification before
execution regardless of the position's existing authority assignment, because retirement is
irreversible enough that no advisory-only mechanism should be able to trigger it even indirectly — this
is the specific, NORMATIVE place IAS-C1 refuses to let its own engine's confidence substitute for
governed sign-off, consistent with Article IX's "IAS proposes; the Owner promotes."

10.6 The ratification step, stated as a protocol. A rebalancing proposal (Part VII) or scarcity
escalation (Part IX) becomes a Board Verdict through: (1) proposal arrival with its full evidentiary
package (valuation, trend, liquidity read, concentration check); (2) routine-versus-flagged
classification (§10.4); (3) for routine proposals, single-reviewer ratification within the current
cycle; for flagged proposals, scheduled deliberation, which may itself request additional evidence
(a Digital Twin simulation per Part V §5.6, for instance) before a verdict is reached; (4) the verdict
is recorded as a Board Verdict object (Part II §2.6) with its evidence, date, and reconciliation
cadence; (5) the verdict is published to the position's ALLOCATES-TO edge (Part II §2.7), updating the
standing allocation the next mission-level allocation cycle reads against. This protocol is
deliberately auditable at every step — a Board Verdict without a traceable path back through this
five-step sequence to its originating evidence is, per Article V's evidence discipline, not a valid
verdict but an unevidenced assertion wearing a verdict's form.

10.7 The escalation path for genuinely contested cases. Some cases resist the routine/flagged binary —
a position with strong marginal ROI (Part VIII §8.5) but also a strong concentration flag (Part VII
§7.4), or a niche position (Part III §3.4) whose protected-share guard (Part IX §9.5) conflicts with a
scarcity resolver's throttle recommendation. For these, the board's protocol names a THIRD path:
deferred deliberation with an explicit named tension — the board records BOTH competing readings (the
marginal-ROI case for funding, the concentration-risk case against) rather than forcing a single
verdict that hides the genuine disagreement, and the recorded verdict, whichever way it lands, carries
both readings in its evidence package. This is a direct, NORMATIVE application of Article V's demand
that CONTESTED evidence be stated as contested rather than resolved by fiat when the underlying
evidence genuinely points both ways.

FINAL LAW — PART X. A verdict is not a score crossing a threshold; it is a recorded, evidenced,
auditable decision by a deliberative body that reserves its heaviest scrutiny for its least reversible
choice, and a verdict that cannot trace back through proposal, evidence, and ratification to a
specific board action is not governance — it is a formula's output wearing governance's language.

===============================================================================================
PART XI — THE BOARD'S PROTOCOL AND CADENCE
===============================================================================================

11.1 The allocation cycle, defined. The ALLOCATION CYCLE is the periodic unit at which system-level
rebalancing (Part VII) and board deliberation (Part X) operate — DEFINED here as a fixed period whose
exact length is EXPERIMENTAL (Part XXII), chosen to be long enough that a position's trend across two
cycles genuinely reflects sustained change rather than noise (Part VII §7.3's confirmed-evidence bar
depends on this), and short enough that a genuinely urgent shift (a sudden zombie signal, a sudden
concentration risk) is not left unaddressed for an unreasonable span. Mission-level allocation (Part
VI) operates continuously WITHIN a cycle, drawing against whatever standing allocation the most recent
cycle's board verdicts set; the cycle boundary is where the standing allocation itself can change.

11.2 Board composition, stated as a role model rather than a headcount. The CAB, as specified, is not
DEFINED here as a fixed roster of named individuals — that is an operational detail outside this
dataset's science-before-implementation scope (Article VIII) — but as a ROLE MODEL: a PORTFOLIO
REVIEWER role (responsible for routine ratification, §10.4), a DELIBERATION QUORUM role (responsible
for flagged-proposal discussion, drawing on domain expertise relevant to the affected position's asset
class), and OWNER RATIFICATION authority (required for EXTINGUISH per §10.5, and available as an
escalation path for any deferred-tension case per §10.7). Whether these roles are filled by a human
Owner, an Owner-delegated review process, or a combination, is an implementation decision this dataset
deliberately leaves open, consistent with specifying mechanism rather than prescribing organizational
staffing.

11.3 Between-cycle exception handling. Not every capital event can wait for the next scheduled cycle.
A position whose consumers vanish abruptly mid-cycle (a sudden zombie signal, Part III §3.7), or a
scarcity shortfall against strong evidence (Part IX §9.6), can trigger an OUT-OF-CYCLE REVIEW — a
narrower, single-position deliberation that does not reopen the full portfolio's allocation but
addresses the specific urgent signal, logged and folded into the NEXT scheduled cycle's full record
rather than treated as a separate, parallel governance track. (NORMATIVE: out-of-cycle review exists
specifically so that the cadence discipline of §11.1, which exists to prevent noise-driven churn, does
not become an excuse to ignore a genuinely urgent signal until the next scheduled date.)

11.4 The cycle's own retrospective step. At the close of each cycle, before the next cycle's proposals
are generated, the board conducts a brief RETROSPECTIVE: comparing the PRIOR cycle's verdicts against
what actually happened (did an INVEST verdict's confirmed evidence hold up in the following cycle's
valuation; did a HARVEST verdict's freed capital actually get redeployed to a higher-marginal-ROI use;
did a MAINTAIN verdict on a position later flagged concentration turn out to have been premature). This
retrospective is the specific mechanism by which the board's OWN calibration — the coefficients of Part
IV §4.3, the window length of Part VIII §8.4, the cycle length itself — accumulates evidence toward
revision, feeding Part XXII's open calibration questions with real, cycle-over-cycle data rather than
leaving them permanently EXPERIMENTAL.

11.5 Appeal and reconsideration. A position's own maintainers, or the mission types most affected by a
verdict, may request RECONSIDERATION of a Board Verdict before its next scheduled reconciliation,
presenting evidence the original deliberation did not have access to — a newly-confirmed consumer
relationship, a corrected valuation input, a liquidity re-assessment. Reconsideration is not
automatically granted; it requires the requesting party to name SPECIFIC evidence the original verdict
lacked, not merely to disagree with the verdict's conclusion, preserving the evidentiary discipline of
Part X §10.6 against becoming a rubber-stamp override triggered by objection alone.

11.6 Interaction with owner_queue's own cadence. owner_queue operates on its own prioritization
cadence, distinct from the allocation cycle, and IAS-C1 does not force owner_queue onto its own
schedule — the two are RECONCILED, not merged: the board's INVEST verdicts, when they represent a
genuinely new capability rather than an increase to an existing position, are routed AS CANDIDATES into
owner_queue's own backlog (Part I §1.4's boundary, restated operationally) rather than executed
directly by the board, because building genuinely new capability is owner_queue's and the mission
composer's object, not the portfolio's — the portfolio values and reallocates EXISTING holdings; new
construction is proposed to, and prioritized by, the system that already owns that decision.

11.7 Why the protocol is deliberately heavier than a pure algorithm, and the cost this imposes
knowingly. Every deliberative step named in this Part — quorum discussion, retrospective, appeal,
reconciliation with owner_queue — consumes ATTENTION CAPITAL (Part II §2.2), the same scarce resource
the portfolio itself allocates. This is a knowing, NORMATIVE trade-off: a purely algorithmic allocator
would be cheaper to run but would concentrate irreversible authority in an unaccountable formula,
exactly the failure Part X §10.1 exists to prevent; the protocol's cost is priced explicitly as part of
the portfolio's own MAINTENANCE CAPITAL sub-pool (Part II §2.2), so that the governance overhead of
running the CAB is itself a visible line item the board can evaluate against its own value, rather than
a hidden cost external to the system it governs.

11.8 Worked out-of-cycle review, traced end to end. Consider a synthetic case where a position's
consumer count falls to zero abruptly, mid-cycle, following an unrelated upstream system's own
deprecation that silently removed the position's only remaining consumer. Under §11.3, this triggers an
OUT-OF-CYCLE REVIEW rather than waiting for the scheduled cycle boundary: the review is scoped narrowly
to this ONE position (never reopening the full portfolio's standing allocations), a single reviewer
confirms the zero-consumer signal against the Control Bus event log (never accepting the report at face
value, per Part XVIII §18.2's discipline applied here), and — because a zero-consumer signal alone does
not clear the EXTINGUISH evidentiary bar of Part X §10.3, which requires TWO consecutive confirmed
cycles — the out-of-cycle review's own action is limited to a WATCH verdict and an accelerated
reconciliation cadence for this one position, not an immediate extinguish. The position's fate is then
decided at the NEXT scheduled cycle, by which point either a second confirming reading has accumulated
(clearing the EXTINGUISH bar) or the consumer has been restored (reverting to MAINTAIN), demonstrating
that out-of-cycle review's actual function is to shorten the OBSERVATION window for an urgent signal,
not to shortcut the evidentiary bar itself.

11.9 What the retrospective step does NOT do, stated against a specific near-miss. §11.4's retrospective
compares prior verdicts against subsequent outcomes, and a specific implementation risk is treating the
retrospective as a second opportunity to RE-DECIDE prior verdicts retroactively — reversing a HARVEST
that, in hindsight, freed capital that then sat idle for a cycle, as if the original decision had been
wrong given information that was not available when it was made. The retrospective's actual function is
narrower and forward-looking only: it generates CALIBRATION evidence for Part XXII's open questions and
flags patterns for the NEXT cycle's deliberation, but it does not re-litigate a prior Board Verdict
itself, which would violate the same evidentiary discipline Part XVIII §18.6 names against retrospective
revisionism of a resolved thesis — a resolved verdict, like a resolved thesis, is a fixed historical fact
the estate learns from, not a decision perpetually reopened by hindsight.

11.10 The cost of the protocol's own governance overhead, tracked as its own metric rather than assumed
acceptable. Per §11.7's honest pricing of the CAB's attention-capital draw, this dataset names the
specific risk of a protocol that grows heavier over time without a corresponding check: as the estate's
holdings grow past seventy, and as the flagged-proposal rate (Part X §10.4) rises with them, the
DELIBERATION QUORUM role's own attention load can itself begin to approach the scale of a standing
operational burden rather than an occasional review function. Part XIX §19.9's PORTFOLIO REVIEWER
ATTENTION LOAD metric exists specifically to make this visible on the same instrument panel as every
other capital-consuming holding the board governs, closing the loop this Part's own FINAL LAW names: the
protocol is priced against the same capital pool it governs, and that price is expected to be reviewed,
not merely accepted as a permanent fixed cost of doing business.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. A cadence too tight produces churn from noise; a cadence too loose leaves urgent
signals unaddressed; and a board that never retrospects its own prior verdicts against what actually
happened is not learning — the protocol's discipline is cycle, exception, retrospective, and appeal,
each bounded, each evidenced, and each itself priced against the same capital pool it governs.

PART XII — INVESTMENT THESIS FORMATION AND FALSIFICATION
===============================================================================================

12.1 Why a thesis, distinct from a valuation, is needed at all. Part IV's valuation engine answers
"what is S worth NOW, given OBSERVED evidence." It cannot, by construction, answer "what would S be
worth IF capital were added" — that is a forward claim about a state that does not yet exist, and no
amount of OBSERVED data about the current state can settle a forward claim by itself. An INVESTMENT
THESIS (Part II §2.5), absorbing ledger candidate 26, is the object that carries this forward claim
explicitly, structured so it can later be checked against what actually happened rather than left as
an unfalsifiable justification retroactively fitted to whatever outcome occurs.

12.2 The thesis template: five required fields. Every thesis, whether attached to an existing
position's proposed INVEST verdict or to a candidate for entirely new investment routed to
owner_queue (Part XI §11.6), states, in a fixed structure DERIVED from F2's own advantage-factor
vocabulary (Federation Ontology Part III of the Advantage Algebra) rather than free prose: the CLAIM
(what specifically will improve — a valuation figure, a consumer count, a strengthens fan-out — and by
roughly how much); the MECHANISM (which of F2's factors — white-space, systems-strengthened, reuse,
cross-project, compounding, model-independence — the additional capital is expected to move, and
through what causal path); the FALSIFIER (the specific, measurable observation that would prove the
thesis WRONG, stated in advance, before the capital is committed, per the ACIS falsifiability
discipline this dataset imports by cross-reference rather than rebuilds); the TIME HORIZON (how many
cycles until the falsifier can be honestly checked, distinct from the reconciliation cadence of Part
IV §4.8, because a thesis may reasonably require more cycles to prove out than a routine valuation
reconciliation); and the OPPORTUNITY COST (what capital allocated to this thesis is NOT available for,
naming at minimum the next-best alternative use the scarcity resolver's ranking, Part IX §9.4,
identified).

12.3 Why the falsifier field is the thesis's load-bearing component. A thesis with a claim and a
mechanism but no stated falsifier is unfalsifiable by construction — any subsequent outcome can be
narrated as confirming it, the precise failure this dataset's epistemic discipline (Article V) exists
to prevent from infecting forward-looking capital claims specifically. (NORMATIVE, DERIVED from
Article V's own general rule applied to the thesis object: a claim without a stated falsifier defaults
to HYPOTHESIZED and CANNOT license an INVEST verdict on its own, per Part VII §7.3's confirmed-evidence
bar — a thesis is necessary but not sufficient for an increase; it must still be checked against actual
valuation movement across at least one subsequent cycle before the evidence reaches DERIVED.) A thesis
whose falsifier reads as "if the position does not improve" is too vague to be checked; a properly
formed falsifier names a specific, measurable threshold — "if Vault-Lookup-2's consumer count does not
rise above nine within two cycles, the thesis is falsified" — the same discipline that makes F2's own
Advantage Algebra checkable rather than rhetorical.

12.4 The thesis lifecycle, and its four states. A thesis passes through PROPOSED (formed, attached to
a rebalancing or new-investment candidate, not yet acted on); FUNDED (the board has ratified the
associated INVEST verdict; capital has moved); TESTING (the stated time horizon is running; the
position's valuation is being tracked against the claim); and RESOLVED — either CONFIRMED (the
falsifier did not trigger; the claimed movement occurred within a reasonable tolerance, and the
thesis's evidence level is promoted from HYPOTHESIZED to DERIVED) or FALSIFIED (the falsifier
triggered; the claimed movement did not occur). A FALSIFIED thesis is not deleted or quietly dropped —
it is retained in the position's record as a specific, named lesson, feeding the calibration questions
of Part XXII and, where the same mechanism-type thesis has failed repeatedly across different
positions, feeding IAS-B1's (Cognitive Multiplier) broader search for which intervention TYPES
reliably amplify versus which merely sound plausible.

12.5 Worked thesis: forming, testing, and resolving a claim on Vault-Lookup-2's uptick. Return to Part
VII §7.5's niche position. Before that increase was proposed, a thesis was formed: CLAIM — Vault-
Lookup-2's consumer count will exceed nine within two cycles as the new compliance-adjacent mission
type matures; MECHANISM — reuse (the new mission type will invoke Vault-Lookup-2's existing capability
without requiring redesign, an F2 Reuse-factor claim); FALSIFIER — if consumer count has not exceeded
nine by the close of the second subsequent cycle, or if the new mission type routes to a substitute
system instead, the thesis is falsified; TIME HORIZON — two cycles; OPPORTUNITY COST — the modest
capital increment is not available to Enrich-K's own pending (lower-priority, per §9.4's marginal-ROI
ranking that cycle) increase request. Across the two subsequent cycles, consumer count in fact reached
nine and then eleven, the mechanism's reuse claim held, and the thesis resolves CONFIRMED, promoting
its evidence level and strengthening the confidence of any FUTURE thesis citing a similar reuse
mechanism for a comparable niche position — this is the specific, concrete channel through which
thesis formation compounds institutional learning across cycles rather than restarting from zero each
time.

12.6 A worked FALSIFIED thesis, and what the record does with it. Consider a second, illustrative
synthetic thesis attached to a proposed increase for a different position, Draft-Assist-4: CLAIM — a
capital increase will raise cross-project reuse (the F2 Cross-Project factor) as the capability is
adopted by a second project beyond its origin; MECHANISM — cross-project; FALSIFIER — if no second
project adopts the capability within three cycles, the thesis is falsified. At the close of the third
cycle, no second-project adoption occurred; the thesis resolves FALSIFIED. The position's allocation is
NOT automatically reverted by the falsification alone (Part VII's rebalancing discipline still governs
any decrease, requiring its own cycle-over-cycle evidence per §7.3), but the falsified thesis is
recorded against Draft-Assist-4's history, and — this is the specific compounding value a falsified
thesis carries — any FUTURE cross-project-mechanism thesis for a structurally similar position now
starts from a baseline that includes at least one documented cross-project claim that did not pan out,
which is genuine evidence for calibrating how readily cross-project theses should be trusted going
forward, not merely a record of one position's disappointment.

12.7 The thesis's relationship to D2A-5's birth-time scoring. Every position begins its life with a
D2A-5 fusion score at proposal time (Federation Ontology's own worked reference), which is itself a
kind of forward claim about the proposal's expected value. A thesis, as this dataset defines it, is
NOT a duplicate of that birth-time score — D2A-5's score is a GATE decision (should this be built at
all, given what it would overlap), evaluated once, at birth; a thesis is a STANDING, re-formable claim
about incremental capital to an EXISTING holding, evaluated repeatedly across the position's life. A
position's very first thesis, at the moment of its graduation from EXPERIMENTAL (Part III §3.5), often
directly incorporates its D2A-5 birth score as one input to its mechanism claim, but every subsequent
thesis, proposed at later cycles as the position's own trajectory unfolds, is a fresh claim checked
against fresh evidence, never a static carry-forward of the birth score.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. A thesis that cannot be falsified is a story, not a claim, and a claim that is
never checked against what actually happened teaches the estate nothing; every thesis this dataset
funds names, in advance, the exact observation that would prove it wrong, and every thesis this
dataset resolves — confirmed or falsified — is retained as evidence for the next one.

PART XIII — CAPABILITY LIQUIDITY: HOW FAST A HOLDING REDEPLOYS
===============================================================================================

13.1 The question liquidity answers that valuation alone cannot. A position's valuation (Part IV)
states what it is worth as it currently stands. LIQUIDITY, absorbing ledger candidate 30 as a
subengine of IAS-C1, states something different: how QUICKLY that position's capability could be
redirected — redeployed to a new mission type, substituted by a consumer that needs it, or wound down
without breaking dependent systems — if circumstances required it. Two positions with identical
valuation can carry very different liquidity: a well-documented, loosely-coupled capability with many
potential substitute consumers is HIGH-liquidity; a narrowly-specialized capability deeply
depends-on'd by exactly one other system, with no ready substitute, is LOW-liquidity, regardless of how
highly either currently values.

13.2 Why liquidity matters to allocation decisions specifically. Part VII §7.6 already named the
concrete interaction: a proposed decrease to a LOW-liquidity position carries materially higher
transition risk than an identical decrease to a HIGH-liquidity one, because the low-liquidity
position's consumers cannot quickly substitute if the decrease turns out to be a mistake or if the
position's capacity genuinely becomes insufficient afterward. Liquidity is therefore not a separate
score competing with valuation for the board's attention; it is a RISK MODIFIER the board reads
alongside valuation whenever a decrease, harvest, or extinguish verdict is under deliberation (Part X
§10.4's flagged-proposal path always includes the liquidity read for exactly this reason).

13.3 The liquidity formula's components. LIQUIDITY(S), DEFINED, is derived from three OBSERVED or
DERIVED inputs, imported rather than freshly measured: SUBSTITUTABILITY (the count and readiness of
other positions that could serve S's consumers if S were reduced or removed, read from F1's
COMPETES-WITH relation, Federation Ontology Part VI §6.4 — a position with several genuine competitors
is more liquid than one with none); COUPLING DEPTH (the inverse of S's depends-on fan-in from Part IV
§4.3 — a position many others depend on for correct function, not merely convenience, is LESS liquid,
because unwinding it breaks things rather than merely inconveniencing them); and DOCUMENTATION AND
PORTABILITY (imported from the estate's model-independence and reuse-horizon properties, F1 Part IV
§4.2 — a well-documented, model-independent capability is easier to redeploy or hand off than one
tightly bound to undocumented, model-specific tuning). A position scoring high substitutability, low
coupling depth, and high portability is HIGH-liquidity; the inverse combination is LOW-liquidity, and
most real positions fall somewhere between, which is why liquidity is reported as a graded read rather
than a binary flag.

13.4 Liquidity is not the same as replaceability, and conflating them is a specific, named error. A
HIGH-liquidity position is not necessarily a position the estate SHOULD replace — liquidity measures
how EASILY it could be redeployed or substituted, not whether doing so would be a good idea. A core
position (Part III §3.2) can be simultaneously highly-valued, load-bearing, AND high-liquidity if
genuine substitutes exist and it is well-documented — liquidity in that case is a strength (the estate
has resilience, not fragility, in that capability) rather than a signal to act. Conflating "liquid"
with "dispensable" would lead a board to systematically harvest its BEST-documented, best-substituted
positions merely because their liquidity reads high, precisely backward from the intent — liquidity is
read as a modifier on the COST of a decrease decision, never as an independent argument FOR a
decrease.

13.5 Illiquid capability as a distinct portfolio risk, independent of valuation. A position can carry
strong current valuation and still represent a standing PORTFOLIO RISK if its liquidity is low and its
capital draw is large — not because it is performing poorly, but because the estate has, in effect, a
large, illiquid, single-point-of-failure holding that a sudden disruption (the position's own failure,
a dependency change, a maintainer's departure) could not be quickly worked around. This is a distinct
signal from concentration (Part VII §7.4, which measures SHARE of capital) — a position can be modest
in capital share and still be a liquidity risk if its depends-on fan-in is deep relative to its own
documentation and substitutability. IAS-C1 reports LIQUIDITY RISK as its own board-visible flag,
distinct from the concentration flag, precisely because the two point toward different mitigations:
concentration risk is addressed by diversifying capital share; liquidity risk is addressed by
investing in documentation, portability, or a deliberate substitute-building thesis (Part XII), not by
reducing the position's allocation at all.

13.6 Worked liquidity assessment across the synthetic slice. Router-9 (core): low substitutability
(few systems could take over model-routing at its scale on short notice), deep coupling (many depend
on it), but reasonably well-documented → LOW-to-MODERATE liquidity, flagged for a standing liquidity-
risk watch despite its strong valuation, precisely the case §13.5 names. Enrich-K (amplifier): moderate
substitutability (its enrichment function could, with effort, be approximated by two other positions),
shallow coupling (systems it strengthens are not strictly dependent on it, per its FEEDS-not-DEPENDS-ON
relation type, Federation Ontology Part VI §6.2 versus §6.3), well-documented → HIGH liquidity. Vault-
Lookup-2 (niche): NO substitutability (no other position serves its narrow mission type at all),
moderate coupling, adequately documented → LOW liquidity, which is precisely why Part III §3.4 and Part
IX §9.5's protected-share guard exist — a niche position's low liquidity is not a defect to correct, it
is the honest cost of genuine specialization, and the board's response to low niche liquidity is
protection, not pressure to become more substitutable.

13.7 Worked liquidity-risk escalation: a modest-capital position that is nonetheless a structural risk.
Return to §13.5's distinction between concentration risk and liquidity risk with a concrete, synthetic
worked case. Consider Coord-Base-5 (Part XVI §16.2's third core position): its capital share is
unremarkable, well below the concentration threshold of Part VII §7.4, yet its depends-on fan-in is
nearly as deep as Router-9's, and unlike Router-9 it has never been substituted or forked, so its
SUBSTITUTABILITY reads as low despite adequate documentation. Coord-Base-5 therefore surfaces a
LIQUIDITY RISK flag (§13.5) while showing NO concentration flag at all — a position the concentration
index alone would never surface, precisely because concentration measures capital SHARE and liquidity
risk measures redeployability, and a modestly-capitalized but deeply-coupled, poorly-substitutable
position can carry serious structural risk that a share-based metric is, by construction, blind to. The
board's response, per §13.5, is investment in documentation and a deliberate substitute-building thesis
(Part XII) for Coord-Base-5's function, never a reduction in its allocation, because reducing capital to
a low-liquidity, high-coupling position would increase, not decrease, the very fragility the flag exists
to surface.

13.8 The liquidity formula's own failure mode: mistaking recent documentation effort for genuine
portability. Because DOCUMENTATION AND PORTABILITY (§13.3) is one of three liquidity inputs, a specific
gaming-adjacent risk parallels Part XVIII's catalogue directly: a position's maintainer investing in a
documentation pass shortly before a reconciliation cycle can raise its measured liquidity without any
corresponding improvement in genuine substitutability or reduced coupling depth, because documentation
quality is comparatively easy to improve quickly while substitutability (requiring an actual alternative
position to exist) and coupling depth (requiring an actual change in the relation graph) are not.
COUNTERMETRIC, following the general principle of Part XVIII §18.8: the documentation-and-portability
term is weighted as the SMALLEST of the three liquidity inputs precisely because it is the most easily
and cheaply manipulated, and a HIGH-liquidity read requires genuine strength in substitutability or
coupling depth, not documentation alone — a well-documented but singly-depended-on, zero-substitute
position remains LOW-liquidity regardless of how thorough its documentation becomes, consistent with
§13.6's own worked reading of Vault-Lookup-2.

13.9 Liquidity's relationship to the lifecycle state machine, stated explicitly. A position's liquidity
is not itself a lifecycle state (Part XV §15.2), but it materially affects HOW a DECLINING-to-RETIRED
transition, once evidenced, should be executed rather than whether it should occur: a HIGH-liquidity
position moving toward EXTINGUISH can, in the general case, be wound down in a single cycle with its
consumers substituting cleanly, while a LOW-liquidity position clearing the same evidentiary bar (Part X
§10.3) still requires the board's EXTINGUISH deliberation to weigh a longer, staged wind-down or an
explicit IAS-D1 succession handoff (Part III §3.6) before capital is fully withdrawn, because an abrupt
withdrawal from a low-liquidity position risks breaking dependent systems in a way a high-liquidity
withdrawal does not. Liquidity therefore shapes the EXECUTION of an extinguish decision without ever
being permitted to shape whether the decision's own evidentiary bar has been cleared, preserving the
separation §13.4 insists on between "easy to change" and "should be changed."

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. Value says what a holding is worth; liquidity says how much it would cost the
estate to change its mind about that holding quickly — and a portfolio that only ever reads value
without reading liquidity will, sooner or later, discover its most valuable holding is also its most
dangerous one to have concentrated capital in, at the exact moment it can least afford to learn that.

PART XIV — MEASURED GOVERNANCE YIELD: THE COST OF GOVERNING, PRICED HONESTLY
===============================================================================================

14.1 The metric this Part folds, and why it folds here rather than standing alone. The Non-Duplication
Ledger routes Family O (Governance Automation, candidates 141-150 — rule_compiler, done_gate,
sweep_enforcer, governance-overlay, the spec_gate tier system) to REFERENCE, explicitly folding its
"measured governance yield" concept into IAS-C1 and IAS-E1 rather than standing it up as a fifteenth
IAS dataset. The reasoning, made explicit here rather than merely inherited: governance mechanisms
(gates, sweeps, rule compilers, overlays) are THEMSELVES capital-consuming holdings — they draw
maintenance capital and attention capital exactly as any other system does — and a portfolio that
values every OTHER system's return on capital while treating its OWN governance apparatus as a free,
unmeasured overhead is not honestly pricing the estate's full capital deployment. GOVERNANCE YIELD,
DEFINED here, is the value-to-cost ratio of the estate's governance mechanisms specifically, computed
by the SAME valuation discipline (Part IV) applied to a distinct class of positions: governance
holdings.

14.2 Why governance holdings need the taxonomy extended, not replaced. The six asset classes of Part
III apply to governance mechanisms exactly as to any other position — a widely-invoked gate that many
other systems' correctness depends on is CORE (rule_compiler, whose output every rule-consuming system
relies on, would classify core under Part III §3.2's fan-in test); a narrowly-scoped, rarely-triggered
sweep is more likely NICHE or, if its trigger conditions have not fired in a long window, drifting
toward LEGACY or ZOMBIE (Part III §3.6-3.7) — and this is precisely the honest discipline "measured
governance yield" was reaching for under Family O's original candidates: a governance mechanism does
not get a permanent pass from portfolio scrutiny merely because its FUNCTION is oversight rather than
mission execution; it is a holding like any other and is priced like one.

14.3 The governance yield formula, and its one deliberate asymmetry against pure ROI. GOVERNANCE
YIELD for a governance position G is computed identically to V(S) in Part IV §4.2 — structurally-
weighted advantage over capital draw — with one deliberate, NORMATIVE addition: the advantage term for
a governance holding includes a PREVENTED-HARM component, DERIVED from the count and severity of
violations the mechanism actually caught (an OBSERVED quantity, read from the mechanism's own audit
log — a gate's block count, a sweep's catch count) rather than from consumer count alone, because a
governance mechanism's primary value is often precisely that it prevents something from happening,
which shows up nowhere in a naive consumer-count or output-volume measure the way a mission-serving
system's value would. Excluding prevented-harm would systematically undervalue exactly the governance
mechanisms doing their job quietly and well — a gate that blocks nothing because bad inputs stopped
arriving once its reputation was established is, perversely, at risk of reading as low-value under a
pure activity count, the precise trap Part XVI names as one of the taxonomy's harder edge cases.

14.4 Worked governance-yield computation, illustrative and synthetic. Consider a synthetic governance
position, Gate-Q, whose direct "consumer" count (systems whose writes it screens) is high but whose
recent BLOCK count has fallen to near-zero over several cycles. A naive interpretation reads this as
Gate-Q "doing nothing" and drifting toward LEGACY. The prevented-harm-aware formula instead checks
whether the low block count reflects genuine absence of violations (in which case Gate-Q's low
recent activity is a SUCCESS signal, not a decline signal — the position remains CORE, its low
block-count trend explained rather than mistaken for irrelevance) or whether it reflects the gate
itself having drifted stale and simply failing to catch what it once caught (in which case the low
block count IS a decline signal, but the correct board response is a re-instrumentation thesis per
Part XII, not a harvest). Distinguishing the two readings requires a SEPARATE evidence check — a
periodic red-team or adversarial-probe pass confirming the gate still catches KNOWN violation
patterns — that this dataset names as a required input to governance-position valuation specifically
(a check most mission-serving positions do not need, because their value is measured by what they
DO, not by what they successfully PREVENT).

14.5 The countermetric: governance yield must not be gamed by reducing enforcement to inflate the
ratio. A governance mechanism's value-to-cost ratio can be trivially "improved" by weakening its own
enforcement — a gate that stops checking anything shows zero maintenance friction and, under a naive
formula ignoring the prevented-harm and stale-detection checks of §14.4, could read as newly efficient.
This dataset names this explicitly as the SPECIFIC adversarial case governance-yield measurement must
resist, and the resistance mechanism is exactly the red-team check of §14.4: a governance position's
yield may never be computed from its OWN self-reported activity log alone; it requires the independent
adversarial-probe confirmation before a rising yield trend for a governance holding is treated as
genuine improvement rather than as a hollowed-out mechanism reporting fewer catches because it is
catching less, not because there is less to catch.

14.6 Governance yield as an estate-wide health signal, not merely a per-mechanism score. Aggregated
across all governance holdings (using the same capital-weighted approach as Part VIII's F-ROI), a
FEDERATION GOVERNANCE YIELD figure tells the board something F-ROI over mission-serving positions
alone cannot: whether the estate's overall investment in oversight — gates, sweeps, rule compilation,
the review overlays every dev skill inherits — is proportionate to the harm it prevents and the
correctness it preserves, or whether the estate has accumulated GOVERNANCE OVERHEAD past the point of
proportionate return, a distinct and named failure mode (governance mechanisms multiplying because
adding one is always locally justifiable, while the AGGREGATE friction they impose on every write, every
commit, every deploy is never priced against their aggregate prevented-harm) that this dataset flags
as the specific reason Family O's yield concept belongs in the portfolio rather than being left
unmeasured.

14.7 The interface to IAS-E1's own observability role. Federation Governance Yield's raw activity
inputs (block counts, catch counts, audit-log volumes) are OBSERVED by IAS-E1 (Observability Fabric)
as part of its general cross-system telemetry; IAS-C1 does not re-instrument governance mechanisms
itself, it CONSUMES IAS-E1's activity telemetry exactly as it consumes token_irr and recall_roi for
mission-serving positions (Part VIII §8.2), applying the governance-specific valuation adjustment
(§14.3's prevented-harm term and §14.5's adversarial-probe requirement) on top of shared, imported
data. This is the concrete, checkable mechanism by which Family O's fold is honored: no new telemetry
system was built; an existing telemetry stream (IAS-E1's) is read through a governance-specific
valuation lens IAS-C1 supplies.

14.8 A failure mode distinct from self-weakening: the externally defeated gate, and its detection
signal. NORMATIVE, and distinct from §14.5's self-weakening case: a governance mechanism can be
rendered inert not by softening its OWN enforcement logic but by a higher-privilege configuration
change entirely outside the mechanism's own code path — a permission or execution mode that silently
routes around the gate rather than through it, leaving the gate's implementation, its tests, and its
audit-log schema all fully intact and code-review-clean, while its ACTUAL invocation count falls
toward zero. This is OBSERVED as a distinct failure class in the estate's own governance history: an
execution mode defaulting to bypass can defeat a deny-list gate while the gate itself reports nothing
wrong, because it was never called rather than because it caught nothing — the same zero-block
signature §14.4 examines can also mean genuine success, now shown to have a THIRD possible cause
requiring its own check. DETECTION SIGNAL, DERIVED: compare the mechanism's actual invocation count
(how often it was called, read from the mechanism's own execution trace, not its block count) against
the expected call volume implied by its declared consumer count from Part III §3.2's fan-in test. A
CORE-classified gate whose invocation count has fallen far below its fan-in-implied expectation while
consumer count itself remains high is not deterring successfully and is not merely under-enforcing —
it is not running, and the divergence between expected and observed invocation volume is the specific,
checkable signal that isolates this failure mode from both §14.4's healthy explanation and §14.5's
self-weakening case, each of which requires a different board response: re-instrumentation for
staleness (Part XII), an adversarial probe for self-weakening (§14.5), and a configuration-level audit
of what sits UPSTREAM of the gate's own code for the defeated-gate case specifically — a check this
dataset names as a required addition to §14.4's periodic red-team pass, not a replacement for it, since
a single probe pass cannot distinguish "the gate ran and correctly found nothing" from "the gate never
ran" without also logging invocation counts alongside catch counts.

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. Governance mechanisms are holdings, not exemptions; a gate priced only by what
it catches will always look worse the better it deters, and a gate priced without an independent
adversarial check can always improve its own ratio by quietly enforcing less — governance yield is
honest only when it prices prevented harm and verifies, adversarially, that the harm is still being
prevented rather than merely no longer measured.

PART XV — THE LIFECYCLE OF A PORTFOLIO POSITION

15.1 Why the lifecycle is a formal state machine, not a narrative. Parts III through XIV have each
touched pieces of a position's life — birth as EXPERIMENTAL (Part III §3.5), class transitions (Part
III §3.8), valuation reconciliation (Part IV §4.8), rebalancing (Part VII), board verdicts (Part X),
thesis testing (Part XII), extinction (Part X §10.3). This Part assembles those pieces into one formal
LIFECYCLE, DEFINED as the ordered set of states a position's ALLOCATES-TO edge (Part II §2.7) passes
through over its life, because a scattered set of per-Part rules, without a single state machine tying
them together, risks exactly the fragmentation Part I §1.2 warned a coordinator without shared
vocabulary produces — each mechanism operating on its own local notion of "where is this position in
its life" rather than one shared answer every mechanism reads.

15.2 The seven lifecycle states. PROPOSED: a candidate position exists only as a D2A-5 birth score and
an owner_queue entry; no capital has moved, no Position record (Part II §2.3) exists yet. FUNDED: the
board has ratified initial capital allocation; a Position record is created at the EXPERIMENTAL asset
class (Part III §3.5) with its capped allocation. TESTING: the position is operating under its initial,
capped allocation, accumulating its first cycles of OBSERVED evidence; no valuation has yet reached
DERIVED. SETTLED: the position has graduated from EXPERIMENTAL into one of the five mature classes
(CORE, AMPLIFIER, NICHE, LEGACY is reached only later, ZOMBIE likewise), its valuation now resting on
OBSERVED, cycle-confirmed evidence rather than a single birth score. MATURE: the position has held a
stable class and valuation trend across multiple cycles, is subject to the standard rebalancing
cadence (Part VII), and is the state most positions spend most of their life in. DECLINING: the
position's valuation or consumer trend has turned negative across at least one confirmed cycle,
triggering the LEGACY watch discipline (Part III §3.6) and, where the decline continues, eventual
HARVEST or EXTINGUISH board consideration. RETIRED: the position has received and executed an
EXTINGUISH verdict; its ALLOCATES-TO edge is zeroed, its stable ID (F1 Part IX) is preserved
permanently (never reused, per F1's own addressing discipline) as a historical record, and any
succession the extinction required has been handed off to IAS-D1.

15.3 Transitions are gated by evidence, never by elapsed time alone. Every transition in §15.2 requires
a SPECIFIC evidentiary trigger, not merely the passage of cycles: PROPOSED to FUNDED requires board
ratification (Part X §10.6); TESTING to SETTLED requires at minimum the confirmed-evidence bar of Part
VII §7.3 (evidence at DERIVED or above, not a single-cycle reading); MATURE to DECLINING requires the
single-cycle clear-signal bar of Part VII §7.3's decrease asymmetry; DECLINING to RETIRED requires the
full EXTINGUISH evidentiary bar of Part X §10.3 (sustained zero-or-near-zero consumers across at least
two cycles, dormancy and unmeasured-property ruled out). A position that has simply existed for many
cycles without triggering any of these evidentiary thresholds remains in its current state
indefinitely — MATURE is not a waypoint toward eventual DECLINING by mere aging, and a portfolio
mechanism that quietly aged positions toward retirement without fresh evidence would violate the
entire evidentiary discipline this dataset is built on.

15.4 The reverse transition: DECLINING positions that recover. Part III §3.6 already named this
possibility for the LEGACY class narratively; the lifecycle formalizes it as a legitimate transition,
DECLINING back to MATURE, triggered by the same confirmed-evidence bar as any other class-improving
move (Part VII §7.3) — a position whose consumer count, having fallen for several cycles, reverses and
recovers across two confirmed cycles exits DECLINING and re-enters MATURE at its (freshly reconciled)
current valuation, WITHOUT carrying a permanent stigma from its DECLINING history beyond what the
transition-volatility signal of Part IV §4.5 already prices. This reversibility is deliberate: a
lifecycle model that treated DECLINING as a one-way ratchet toward RETIRED would bias the board toward
premature extinction of positions capable of genuine recovery, exactly the harm the LEGACY-not-
immediate-harvest discipline of Part III §3.6 was built to prevent.

15.5 RETIRED is not deletion: the preserved record and its consumers. When a position transitions to
RETIRED, its historical valuation series, its thesis record (both confirmed and falsified, Part XII
§12.4-12.6), and its full lifecycle transition history are RETAINED, not deleted — consumed by IAS-B2
(Insight Synthesis) as raw material for cross-dataset pattern-finding (which mechanism-types of thesis
reliably succeed or fail, which asset-class transitions correlate with which outcomes) and by IAS-D1
(System Ecology) as part of the estate's succession and extinction history. A RETIRED position's
capital, freed by its zeroed ALLOCATES-TO edge, re-enters the capital pool (Part II §2.4) at the NEXT
allocation cycle, available for the scarcity resolver (Part IX) to allocate toward the highest
marginal-ROI pending proposal — this is the concrete mechanism by which extinction, rather than being
merely a loss, becomes new investable capacity, the portfolio's own version of capital recycling.

15.6 The lifecycle as the substrate for the portfolio's own aggregate health metrics. Reading the
CURRENT distribution of all positions across the seven states — how many are TESTING versus MATURE
versus DECLINING versus RETIRED in a given period — is itself one of Part XIX's headline metrics: a
portfolio with a healthy pipeline shows a steady flow of PROPOSED-to-FUNDED-to-SETTLED positions
alongside an occasional, evidenced flow of DECLINING-to-RETIRED positions; a portfolio with NO
DECLINING-to-RETIRED flow over a long window is not necessarily healthy — it may mean the board is
failing to act on genuine decline signals (Part XVII's concentration failure mode names one path to
this), a distinction the lifecycle distribution alone cannot resolve without also reading the
underlying evidence, but which it can, at minimum, flag as worth checking.

15.7 Worked lifecycle trace across a position's full seven-state history. Consider a fully synthetic
position, Probe-New-9 (Part XVI §16.2's second experimental position), followed across its complete life
to make the state machine concrete rather than abstract. PROPOSED: Probe-New-9 exists only as a D2A-5
birth score and an owner_queue entry for three cycles while awaiting board attention amid other
priorities. FUNDED: the board ratifies its initial capped allocation; a Position record is created at
EXPERIMENTAL. TESTING: across its first two cycles, its consumer count is still UNRESOLVED-adjacent (too
new for a confident read), and its D2A-5 score remains its primary evidence. SETTLED: by its third
cycle, a confirmed, DERIVED-level consumer trend emerges and it graduates into NICHE, per Part III §3.4's
narrow-but-stable pattern. MATURE: it holds NICHE status with a stable, modest valuation for several
subsequent cycles, subject to the standard two-cycle rebalancing cadence Part IV §4.8 sets for niche
positions. This trace demonstrates that most of a position's life, once past its earliest cycles, is
spent in ordinary, low-drama MATURE stability — the dramatic transitions (DECLINING, RETIRED) are the
exception the mechanism must handle correctly, not the typical case the lifecycle model is built around.

15.8 The lifecycle's treatment of a position that never leaves TESTING. A specific, named edge case: a
position whose EXPERIMENTAL evidence never reaches the DERIVED bar required for SETTLED (Part VII §7.3),
cycle after cycle, neither confirming enough consumer trend to graduate nor showing the clear decline
signal that would trigger an early EXTINGUISH consideration — a position stuck in an evidentiary limbo
rather than a decisive trajectory. Because Part III §3.5's capped experimental allocation already bounds
the capital risk such a position can pose, the lifecycle does not force a decision merely to resolve the
ambiguity; instead, a position remaining in TESTING beyond a bounded number of cycles (DERIVED,
EXPERIMENTAL) is automatically flagged to the board as a STALLED EXPERIMENTAL case, requiring an
explicit board judgment — extend the testing window with fresh justification, apply an accelerated
EXTINGUISH evaluation despite the absence of a clean decline signal, or leave it flagged for continued
observation — rather than either an automatic graduation or an automatic extinguishment, preserving the
discipline that a transition requires evidence, not merely the passage of time, per §15.3, while still
ensuring genuine ambiguity does not go unexamined indefinitely.

15.9 Why RETIRED positions' preserved IDs matter beyond historical record-keeping. §15.5 already
established that a RETIRED position's stable ID is never reused. This Part names the concrete failure a
reused ID would cause: a NEW position later built to serve a similar mission type, if assigned a
previously-RETIRED position's ID, would inherit that ID's entire historical valuation series, thesis
record, and lifecycle transition history in any system that queries by ID rather than by creation date —
silently conflating a genuinely new holding's fresh evidence with a prior, unrelated holding's closed
history, and specifically corrupting any retrospective (§11.4) or retrodiction (Part XX §20.3) analysis
that treats the ID's history as continuous. F1's own permanent-non-reuse discipline (Part IX of the
Federation Ontology) is therefore not merely an addressing convention IAS-C1 happens to inherit; it is a
load-bearing precondition for every historical analysis this dataset's evaluation protocol depends on.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. A position's life is not a story told after the fact; it is a state machine whose
every transition requires a named evidentiary trigger, reversible where the evidence reverses,
permanent only at retirement, and even retirement preserves the record rather than erasing it —
because a portfolio that forgets its own history cannot learn from either its confirmed theses or its
falsified ones.

PART XVI — WORKED EXAMPLE: A SYNTHETIC TWELVE-POSITION PORTFOLIO REBALANCE, END TO END
===============================================================================================

16.1 Purpose and scope of this worked example. Parts IV through XV each demonstrated one mechanism in
isolation — a single valuation, a single rebalance, a single thesis, a single liquidity read. This Part
runs the FULL cycle — valuation, rebalancing proposal, scarcity resolution, board deliberation, thesis
formation and ratification, lifecycle transition — across one synthetic, twelve-position slice of a
portfolio in a single allocation cycle, to demonstrate that the mechanisms compose into one coherent
process rather than remaining a set of independently-plausible but never-jointly-exercised parts. All
twelve positions are illustrative and synthetic, constructed to exercise the mechanisms, not to assert
real measurements of any actual PP module.

16.2 The starting portfolio state. Twelve positions, spanning all six asset classes: three CORE
(Router-9, Ledger-Core-1, Coord-Base-5), two AMPLIFIER (Enrich-K, Weight-Boost-2), two NICHE
(Vault-Lookup-2, Compliance-Scan-6), two EXPERIMENTAL (Synth-Draft-7, Probe-New-9), two LEGACY
(Summ-Legacy-1, Old-Sched-3), one ZOMBIE (Old-Formatter-3), and one GOVERNANCE holding (Gate-Q, from
Part XIV). The capital pool this cycle has roughly eight percent of its total available for net new
allocation across all proposals combined (the remainder is already committed as standing draw to the
twelve positions' current allocations) — a tight but not exceptional scarcity condition, chosen
specifically to force genuine trade-offs rather than a trivial cycle where every proposal is fundable.

16.3 Valuation pass (Part IV). Each position's V(S) is recomputed. Router-9's structural weight
(depends-on fan-in near the cap) keeps its valuation strong despite unremarkable direct output growth.
Enrich-K's strengthens-fan-out-corrected valuation (per Part IV §4.4's worked mechanism) again lands
higher than its direct consumer count alone would suggest. Vault-Lookup-2 shows the confirmed uptick
from Part VII §7.5 and Part XII §12.5, now in its second confirming cycle. Synth-Draft-7 shows a
negative but bounded return, within its capped experimental allocation, per Part III §3.5's expected
pattern. Old-Formatter-3 confirms zero consumers for a fourth consecutive cycle. Gate-Q's governance
yield (Part XIV §14.4) is checked against a scheduled adversarial-probe pass, which confirms it still
catches known violation patterns despite a falling raw block count — the probe result is what keeps
Gate-Q's valuation from being mistakenly read as declining.

16.4 Proposals generated (Parts VI-VII, IX). Rebalancing generates five proposals this cycle: an
INVEST proposal for Vault-Lookup-2 (confirmed-evidence bar met, small ask); an INVEST proposal for
Router-9 (confirmed-evidence bar met, LARGE ask, crossing the concentration threshold per Part VII
§7.4 — FLAGGED); a HARVEST proposal for Summ-Legacy-1 (single-cycle clear decline signal, per §7.3's
decrease asymmetry); an EXTINGUISH candidate for Old-Formatter-3 (fourth consecutive zero-consumer
cycle, dormancy and unmeasured-property checks both ruled out per Part X §10.3 — FLAGGED); and a MAINTAIN
confirmation for the remaining seven positions, including Gate-Q, whose adversarial-probe-confirmed
valuation supports holding its allocation steady. Combined, the Router-9 and Vault-Lookup-2 INVEST asks
exceed the cycle's eight-percent net-new capacity.

16.5 Scarcity resolution (Part IX). The protected-share guard (§9.5) first reserves Vault-Lookup-2's
small niche ask in full. The remaining capacity is insufficient for Router-9's full large ask; ranked
by marginal ROI (§9.4), Router-9's proposal is PARTIALLY funded, with the shortfall escalated to the
board per §9.3 rather than silently throttled, because Router-9 is a CORE position with confirmed
evidence — exactly the case §9.6's worked scenario anticipated.

16.6 Board deliberation (Part X). The routine path ratifies Vault-Lookup-2's INVEST and Summ-Legacy-1's
HARVEST within the cycle without extended discussion. The flagged path convenes for Router-9's partial
funding (weighing the concentration-threshold crossing against the confirmed marginal-ROI evidence —
resolved, in this illustrative pass, as a partial INVEST up to the concentration threshold rather than
either a full grant or a full denial, with the remainder logged as unmet demand feeding IAS-C2) and for
Old-Formatter-3's EXTINGUISH (all three evidentiary checks confirmed; the ecology check against IAS-D1
finds no active symbiosis; Owner ratification is obtained per §10.5's irreversibility rule; the position
transitions to RETIRED).

16.7 Thesis and lifecycle outcomes. Vault-Lookup-2's original thesis (Part XII §12.5) formally resolves
CONFIRMED this cycle, its evidence promoted to DERIVED, and its lifecycle state moves from MATURE
(already settled) with an upward valuation reconciliation — it does not change LIFECYCLE state further,
since it was already SETTLED and MATURE, but its class-internal standing strengthens. Old-Formatter-3
transitions DECLINING (it had already been there) to RETIRED, its capital recycled into next cycle's
pool (Part XV §15.5). A fresh thesis is formed and attached to Router-9's partial-funding decision,
with a falsifier specifying the marginal-ROI threshold the additional capital must clear within two
cycles, entering TESTING.

16.8 The retrospective, and what this cycle would teach the next one (Part XI §11.4). At the following
cycle's retrospective, the board would check: did Router-9's partial-funding thesis's falsifier trigger
or hold; did Old-Formatter-3's recycled capital, now available in the pool, actually flow to a
higher-marginal-ROI use in the next cycle's allocation, or sit idle; did Gate-Q's adversarial-probe-
confirmed valuation remain stable, validating that the probe check was the right instrument for
governance-position valuation. Each of these three checks is a concrete, checkable retrospective item
this single worked cycle generates for the NEXT cycle to evaluate — demonstrating that the mechanisms
compound cycle-over-cycle rather than resetting fresh each time.

16.9 A second contested worked pass: what changes if the cycle's scarcity is more severe. To stress-test
the mechanisms beyond §16.4-16.6's roughly-forty-percent shortfall, consider a variant of the same
twelve-position cycle where net-new capacity is only three percent of the pool rather than eight — a
genuinely tight cycle. The protected-share guard (§9.5) still reserves Vault-Lookup-2's small niche ask
in full, because the guard's reservation is sized against the total pool, not against the cycle's net-new
capacity specifically, and a niche ask small enough to clear even a tight cycle's remaining room is
exactly the kind of low-absolute-cost, high-marginal-return request the guard is designed to protect
first. Router-9's large ask, however, receives a MUCH smaller partial allocation than in the base case,
and the shortfall escalated to the board (§9.3) is correspondingly larger and more urgent — illustrating
that the mechanisms' qualitative behavior (protect niche first, rank the rest by marginal ROI, escalate
rather than silently drop unmet core demand) holds across scarcity severity, while the QUANTITATIVE
outcome scales with how tight the cycle actually is, a distinction the board's deliberation (Part X
§10.7) must read from the evidence package each time rather than assuming a fixed severity.

16.10 What the worked example deliberately does not demonstrate, named honestly. This twelve-position
pass exercises ONE cycle's mechanisms end to end; it does not, and cannot by construction, demonstrate
multi-cycle compounding — whether Router-9's partial-funding thesis (§16.7) actually confirms or is
falsified requires a SECOND cycle this Part does not run, and whether the concentration index's trend
(Part XVII §17.4) genuinely rises or falls across many cycles requires a longer synthetic series than one
worked pass can honestly provide. Naming this limitation explicitly, rather than implying a single worked
cycle proves the mechanisms' multi-cycle claims, is itself consistent with Part XX's evaluation
discipline: a worked example demonstrates INTERNAL COHERENCE (the mechanisms hand off to one another
correctly within a cycle), which is a necessary but not sufficient condition for the EFFICACY claim Part
XX §20.2 states as HYPOTHESIZED and reserves for retrodiction and forward comparison to actually test.

16.11 The role of a worked example in this dataset's own epistemic discipline. A worked example, however
carefully constructed, is a DEMONSTRATION of a specification's internal logic, not EVIDENCE of the
specification's real-world efficacy — the twelve positions and their figures are synthetic and
illustrative throughout, chosen to exercise every mechanism at least once in interaction with the others,
never to assert a measurement about any actual PP module. This distinction matters specifically because a
worked example that reads as concrete and detailed can be mistaken, by a careless reader, for OBSERVED
evidence rather than the DERIVED illustration it actually is; this Part's own epistemic tagging
throughout (Part XVI's narrative carries no OBSERVED tags on any of its twelve positions' figures,
deliberately) is the safeguard against that specific misreading.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. Run end to end across twelve synthetic positions and one contested cycle, the
mechanisms of Parts IV through XV do not merely coexist on paper — they hand off to one another in a
single coherent sequence: valuation feeds proposals, proposals feed scarcity resolution, scarcity
resolution feeds board deliberation, deliberation feeds thesis and lifecycle state, and the cycle's own
outcomes become the next cycle's retrospective evidence.

PART XVII — FAILURE MODE: PORTFOLIO CONCENTRATION
===============================================================================================

17.1 Naming the failure mode precisely. PORTFOLIO CONCENTRATION, as DEFINED for this dataset, is the
condition in which a disproportionate share of the estate's total cognitive capital accumulates in a
small number of positions, such that the failure, mispricing, or sudden decline of any ONE of those
positions would inflict outsized, estate-wide damage disproportionate to that single position's
apparent share of the portfolio's headline metrics. Concentration is not, by itself, always a mistake
(§7.4 already named the legitimate case of a genuinely irreplaceable core position warranting a large
share); the failure mode is UNEXAMINED or UNGOVERNED concentration — capital that has drifted toward a
few positions through the accumulated effect of many individually-reasonable rebalancing decisions,
never once evaluated as a portfolio-level pattern.

17.2 The mechanism by which concentration accumulates silently. No single rebalancing decision (Part
VII) is, in isolation, unreasonable: each confirmed-evidence increase to an already-large core position
looks, examined alone, like a well-evidenced INVEST verdict. Concentration is an EMERGENT property of
many such individually-sound decisions compounding over many cycles, exactly the kind of property Part
III §3.1 of the Federation Ontology names as visible only at the ensemble level — no single-position
valuation ever "sees" the portfolio's aggregate concentration, which is precisely why the concentration
THRESHOLD check (Part VII §7.4) must be a PORTFOLIO-LEVEL gate applied at every proposed increase,
never left to individual position-level valuation to self-regulate.

17.3 The three concrete harms concentration causes. FRAGILITY: a heavily concentrated portfolio's
aggregate outcome becomes hostage to a small number of positions' continued correct function, and F1's
own boundary-invariant discipline (Federation Ontology Part V §5.4) offers no protection here, because
concentration is a legal, invariant-compliant state — it is a portfolio-quality failure, not an
ontology violation, and requires this dataset's own governance rather than F1's floor. STARVED
OPTIONALITY: capital concentrated in a few large positions is capital NOT available to fund the
niche, experimental, and amplifier positions whose long-run value (Part IX §9.5's protected-share
reasoning) the estate depends on for its future diversity, even when each individual concentration-
increasing decision looked marginally correct at the time it was made. FEEDBACK AMPLIFICATION: a
concentrated position that IS an amplifier or a core dependency, once it begins to decline, will pull
down every position that depends on or is strengthened by it FASTER and MORE SEVERELY than an
equivalent decline in a less-connected position would, because its structural weight (Part IV §4.3),
the very property that justified its large allocation, is the same property that transmits its decline
outward through the relation graph.

17.4 Detecting concentration before it becomes a crisis. IAS-C1 tracks, as a standing metric (Part XIX
details the full metric set), the HERFINDAHL-STYLE CONCENTRATION INDEX — DERIVED, the sum of squared
capital shares across all positions, a standard capital-allocation instrument imported here rather than
invented, chosen because it is sensitive to genuine concentration (a portfolio with capital spread
across many small shares scores low; a portfolio with most capital in two or three positions scores
high) without requiring an arbitrary share-count threshold. A RISING trend in this index over several
cycles, even while no SINGLE proposed increase individually crosses the per-position concentration
threshold of Part VII §7.4, is itself a board-visible signal — this is the specific mechanism by which
the failure mode of §17.2 (many small, individually-reasonable decisions accumulating into a
portfolio-level problem) becomes detectable, because the index aggregates what no single decision's
own review would ever surface.

17.5 The remedy, and why it is diversification pressure, not a hard cap. IAS-C1 does not impose a hard
ceiling on any position's capital share (a hard cap would itself risk starving a genuinely irreplaceable
core position of capital its confirmed evidence warrants, the exact opposite error). Instead, a rising
concentration index triggers DIVERSIFICATION PRESSURE: the scarcity resolver's marginal-ROI ranking
(Part IX §9.4) is temporarily adjusted with a concentration PENALTY term applied to further increases
for the already-large positions driving the index up, making it progressively harder, not impossible,
for concentration to keep compounding, while leaving the board's own deliberative judgment (Part X)
as the final word on any specific case where a large position's evidence genuinely still warrants
further investment despite the pressure.

17.6 Worked adversarial case: gradual concentration disguised as a sequence of routine proposals. A
CONTESTED, illustrative scenario: over six consecutive cycles, a single core position (Router-9,
again) receives six successive confirmed-evidence INVEST proposals, each individually below the
per-proposal concentration threshold (Part VII §7.4) and each individually ratified through the
ROUTINE path (Part X §10.4) without triggering deliberation, because no single proposal ever crossed
the flag. By the sixth cycle, Router-9's cumulative share has crossed the portfolio-level concentration
threshold, but because each contributing decision was individually routine, none of them individually
triggered board deliberation. This is the precise adversarial shape §17.4's concentration index exists
to catch: the index is computed and reviewed EVERY cycle, independent of whether any single proposal
that cycle was flagged, specifically so that a slow accumulation across many routine decisions cannot
evade board visibility the way a single large jump would be caught immediately by the per-proposal
threshold. Without the standing index check, this six-cycle drift would be, by construction, invisible
to every mechanism specified before this Part.

17.7 A second concentration vector the index alone does not catch: correlated, not merely large,
positions. §17.4's Herfindahl-style index catches capital concentrated in few LARGE positions, but a
distinct concentration risk exists when capital is spread across MANY positions that are, in practice,
correlated — sharing a common upstream dependency, a common maintainer, or a common failure mode — such
that a single event could impair several positions simultaneously despite none of them individually
crossing the concentration threshold or dominating the index. (DERIVED, and named as a genuine gap
rather than resolved here: this dataset's Herfindahl-style index measures capital SHARE distribution,
not capital CORRELATION, and a full treatment of correlated-risk concentration would require reading
F1's relation graph for shared-dependency clusters, a mechanism this Part names as a candidate extension
rather than specifies fully, consistent with Part XXII's own discipline of naming open questions rather
than silently assuming the index's current form is complete.) The board's own qualitative review of
flagged positions (§17.5) is, in the meantime, the only defense against this correlated-risk vector,
because it can read the relation graph directly even where the standing index cannot yet quantify it.

17.8 Worked example of the starved-optionality harm, made concrete rather than asserted abstractly.
§17.3 named STARVED OPTIONALITY as one of concentration's three harms; consider a synthetic
illustration: across several cycles of steadily increasing allocation to two already-large core
positions (each individually evidenced, each individually reasonable per §17.2), the estate's total
experimental-position funding — capped per position under Part III §3.5 but aggregately shrinking as the
protected share (Part IX §9.5) is squeezed by the concentration trend — falls to a level insufficient to
fund more than one new experimental bet per cycle, down from three or four in earlier cycles. No single
decision caused this; the AGGREGATE drift did, and its cost is not visible in any position's own
valuation — it shows up only as a declining EXPERIMENTAL-to-SETTLED graduation rate in the lifecycle
distribution (Part XV §15.6, Part XIX §19.8), which is precisely why §17.4's concentration index and
§19.8's lifecycle distribution are read TOGETHER by a diligent board, neither sufficient alone to surface
this specific harm.

17.9 The remedy's own limits, stated honestly. §17.5's diversification pressure mechanism makes further
concentration progressively harder, never impossible, and this dataset does not claim the penalty term
is strong enough, at its current EXPERIMENTAL calibration, to reliably prevent every concentration
episode before it becomes board-visible — the six-cycle worked case of §17.6 is itself an illustration
of a drift the standing index catches only AFTER a meaningful share has already accumulated, not before
the first contributing decision was made. The honest claim this Part licenses is narrower: the index
makes accumulating concentration detectable within a bounded number of cycles rather than invisible
indefinitely, converting an otherwise-silent failure mode into a periodically-reviewed one, which is a
real improvement over having no aggregate check at all but is not the same claim as preventing
concentration from ever occurring.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. No single reasonable decision causes concentration; six reasonable decisions in
a row can, and a portfolio that only checks each proposal against its own threshold, never the
portfolio's aggregate shape, will watch concentration accumulate in plain sight, one routine
ratification at a time.

PART XVIII — FAILURE MODE: VALUATION GAMING AND ADVERSARIAL CASES
===============================================================================================

18.1 The general shape of valuation gaming. Every formula this dataset defines — V(S)'s structural
weight (Part IV), F-ROI's capital weighting (Part VIII), marginal-ROI ranking (Part IX §9.4),
governance yield's prevented-harm term (Part XIV) — creates an INCENTIVE for whoever controls a
position's inputs to shape those inputs favorably rather than to genuinely improve the position's
actual value. VALUATION GAMING, DEFINED, is any pattern by which a position's reported valuation rises
without a corresponding genuine rise in the advantage the position actually provides to missions or to
other systems. This Part catalogues the specific gaming shapes this dataset's own formulas are most
exposed to, following F2's own precedent (Advantage Algebra Part XVII) of naming adversarial cases
against a formula explicitly rather than assuming good-faith inputs.

18.2 CONSUMER INFLATION: manufacturing or double-counting consumers to raise structural weight. Because
W(S) (Part IV §4.3) rewards depends-on fan-in, a position's apparent value can be inflated by
recording DEPENDS-ON edges that are, on inspection, merely FEEDS relations mislabeled to the stronger
type, or by the DOUBLE-BOOKED CONSUMER pattern F1's own property model already named (Federation
Ontology Part IV §4.6) — two positions each claiming the same downstream mission as "their" dependent.
COUNTERMETRIC: consumer and dependency counts are never accepted from a position's own self-report;
they are read from the Control Bus's OBSERVED event log (IAS-A2), exactly as F1's own resolution of
the double-booked-consumer edge case already specifies, and a depends-on classification (versus a
weaker feeds classification) requires evidence of actual failure propagation, not merely a claimed
relationship — imported directly from Federation Ontology Part VI §6.3's own definitional discipline.

18.3 DORMANCY MASKING: hiding a true zombie behind a false dormancy claim, and its inverse. Part III
§3.7 already named the risk of misclassifying a genuinely dormant position as a zombie; the adversarial
INVERSE is a position's maintainer claiming dormancy (a seasonal, rare-but-real mission type) to avoid
an EXTINGUISH verdict for a position that is, in fact, simply no longer needed. COUNTERMETRIC: a
dormancy claim requires a documented, checkable RECURRENCE PATTERN — a specific mission type, with at
least one prior OBSERVED invocation cycle, not merely an assertion that "it might be needed again" —
and a dormancy claim that is never actually confirmed by a real recurrence within a bounded number of
cycles converts automatically to a standard DECLINING-toward-EXTINGUISH evaluation, closing the
loophole a permanently-renewable dormancy claim would otherwise open.

18.4 THESIS SHOPPING: proposing a favorable thesis mechanism after observing which mechanism types
have historically been approved. Because Part XII's thesis template requires selecting a MECHANISM
from F2's factor vocabulary, a position's advocate could learn, over successive cycles, which
mechanism claims tend to be approved and default to citing that mechanism regardless of whether it
genuinely describes the position's situation. COUNTERMETRIC: the falsifier requirement (Part XII §12.3)
is the primary defense — a thesis's mechanism claim is checked against its STATED falsifier at
resolution, not merely accepted at proposal time, so a mechanism cited opportunistically rather than
genuinely will simply fail its falsifier at a higher rate over time, and this dataset tracks
PER-MECHANISM-TYPE confirmation rates (an extension of Part XII §12.4's lifecycle tracking) specifically
so that a mechanism type with a suspiciously high proposal rate but a low confirmation rate becomes
visible as a pattern, flagged for the board's scrutiny of future theses citing it.

18.5 GOVERNANCE SELF-EXEMPTION: a governance holding avoiding its own scrutiny by classifying itself
outside the portfolio. Because Part XIV establishes that governance mechanisms ARE positions subject
to valuation, the specific adversarial risk is a governance mechanism's maintainers arguing it should
be exempt from portfolio scrutiny (as, say, "infrastructure" rather than a valued holding), which would
recreate exactly the ungoverned-governance-overhead problem §14.6 names. COUNTERMETRIC: NORMATIVE and
absolute — no mechanism drawing standing cognitive capital is exempt from Part III's taxonomy and Part
IV's valuation by virtue of its FUNCTION; the only exemptions this dataset recognizes are scope
exemptions (a mechanism entirely outside the ensemble boundary, F1 Part III §3.4) which governance
mechanisms, by definition, are not, since they act ON the estate's own systems.

18.6 RETROSPECTIVE REVISIONISM: reframing a falsified thesis's mechanism after the fact to avoid the
falsification record. Because Part XII §12.6 records falsified theses as a standing part of a
position's history, an adversarial pattern is a maintainer arguing, after a falsifier has triggered,
that the ORIGINAL mechanism claim was actually different from what was recorded, effectively erasing
the falsification. COUNTERMETRIC: the thesis template (Part XII §12.2) is recorded at PROPOSAL time,
before the outcome is known, specifically so that its claim, mechanism, and falsifier are fixed and
immutable once FUNDED — any later reframing is itself flagged as a distinct, named event (a THESIS
AMENDMENT, permitted only prospectively for a NEW thesis, never retroactively rewriting a resolved
one) rather than silently accepted, preserving the falsifiability discipline Part XII §12.3 exists to
protect.

18.7 SCARCITY EXPLOITATION: manufacturing artificial urgency to bypass the deliberative path. Because
mission-level allocation's emergency reserve (Part VI §6.3) exists to serve genuinely urgent requests
without full board deliberation, an adversarial pattern is repeatedly framing routine capital needs as
urgent to draw against the reserve rather than going through the slower, more scrutinized rebalancing
path. COUNTERMETRIC: Part VI §6.3 already names the core defense — a reserve draw recurring against
the same position beyond a bounded count within one cycle is automatically escalated to Part VII's
rebalancing subengine rather than repeatedly granted, converting the exploit's own repetition into the
signal that exposes it.

18.8 A seventh adversarial case, named for completeness: LIQUIDITY MASKING. Because §13.3's liquidity
formula draws partly on documentation and portability, a position's maintainer facing a proposed
decrease could, in principle, inflate the SUBSTITUTABILITY input by pointing to a nominally-competing
position that has never actually served the position's consumers and would require substantial
adaptation to do so, making the position read as more liquid (and thus a lower-transition-risk decrease
target, ironically arguing FOR rather than against action) or, in the inverse framing, understating
substitutability to argue a proposed decrease is riskier than it genuinely is. COUNTERMETRIC:
substitutability, per §13.3, is read from F1's COMPETES-WITH relation, which itself requires OBSERVED
evidence of genuine overlap (Federation Ontology Part VI §6.4's own definitional bar), not a claimed or
hypothetical competing capability — a position asserted as a "competitor" that has never actually served
an overlapping consumer set does not qualify for the relation type at all, closing the loophole at the
relation-typing layer rather than at the liquidity-formula layer, consistent with §18.2's own
consumer-inflation countermetric applying the same discipline to a different relation type.

18.9 The general countermetric principle underlying all seven cases. Every countermetric named in this
Part shares one structure, DERIVED and stated once here rather than seven times: no self-reported input
is trusted at face value when an independent, OBSERVED alternative exists (§18.2, §18.5, §18.8), every
forward claim is checked against its own pre-committed falsifier rather than against a post-hoc
narrative (§18.3, §18.4, §18.6), and every mechanism designed for a genuine exception is instrumented to
detect its own repeated invocation as a signal of misuse (§18.7). This is the same general discipline
F2's own
Part X (Anti-Goodhart Countermetrics) establishes for the Advantage Algebra, applied here specifically
to the portfolio's own formulas rather than re-derived from scratch, which is itself the concrete
evidence that IAS-C1's gaming-resistance is not an afterthought but an inherited, consistent estate-wide
discipline.

18.10 Why the catalogue is closed at seven for this sealing, and what would justify an eighth entry. This
dataset does not claim the seven cases of §18.2-§18.8 are exhaustive of every way a valuation-hungry
maintainer could shape a formula's inputs — Part XXII §22.2 already names an adversarial red-team pass
against precisely this catalogue as an open benchmark this dataset does not yet possess. The bar for
adding an eighth case is stated here so the catalogue does not grow by anecdote: a candidate gaming
pattern earns entry only once it names a SPECIFIC formula this dataset defines, a SPECIFIC input that
pattern would distort, and a SPECIFIC countermetric distinguishable from the seven already catalogued —
a pattern that merely restates an existing case under a new name (for instance, a variant of consumer
inflation applied to a different relation type than §18.2 already covers) is a sub-case of an existing
entry, not a new one, and this dataset's own discipline (Article V's honesty requirement, applied
reflexively here) forbids inflating the catalogue's count for the appearance of thoroughness.

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. Every formula in this dataset creates an incentive to shape its own inputs, and
a portfolio that trusts self-reported inputs at face value is not measuring value, it is measuring
persuasiveness — the seven named cases exist because a countermetric written after the gaming pattern is
discovered is a countermetric written too late.

PART XIX — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

19.1 The metric set, assembled rather than invented fresh. This Part consolidates the headline metrics
named across Parts IV through XVIII into one reference set, each paired with the countermetric that
prevents it from being gamed in isolation — following F2's own discipline (Advantage Algebra Part X)
that a formula unaccompanied by a countermetric is an invitation to be gamed, applied here to the
portfolio's own instrument panel rather than restated abstractly.

19.2 FEDERATION ROI (F-ROI, Part VIII §8.2). Primary metric: capital-weighted aggregate return across
the portfolio, decomposed by asset class. Countermetric: F-ROI IMPROVEMENT-BY-AMPUTATION check (Part
VIII §8.6) — an F-ROI rise driven primarily by shrinking the capital share of poor performers, rather
than by genuine improvement in remaining positions' own returns, is flagged separately and never
reported as equivalent to organic improvement; the board sees BOTH the headline F-ROI and its
decomposition into "improvement from growth" versus "improvement from pruning."

19.3 CONCENTRATION INDEX (Part XVII §17.4). Primary metric: the Herfindahl-style sum of squared capital
shares, tracked cycle over cycle. Countermetric: the index alone cannot distinguish HEALTHY
concentration (a genuinely irreplaceable core position) from UNGOVERNED concentration (Part XVII
§17.1's distinction); it is always read alongside the per-position evidence trail for whichever
positions are driving the index, never treated as sufficient justification for action on its own.

19.4 GOVERNANCE YIELD (Part XIV §14.3, §14.6). Primary metric: value-to-cost ratio for governance
holdings, including the prevented-harm term. Countermetric: the mandatory adversarial-probe requirement
(Part XIV §14.4-14.5) — a governance yield reading computed from self-reported activity logs alone,
without independent confirmation that the mechanism still catches known violation patterns, is marked
UNVERIFIED and excluded from the board-visible headline figure until probed.

19.5 LIQUIDITY RISK FLAG (Part XIII §13.5). Primary metric: positions combining large capital share
with low substitutability and deep coupling. Countermetric: liquidity risk is never allowed to trigger
an automatic allocation change on its own (§13.4's explicit warning against conflating liquidity with
dispensability) — it is a board-visible flag requiring deliberate response (documentation investment,
substitute-building thesis), never a mechanical trigger for reducing a position's allocation.

19.6 THESIS CONFIRMATION RATE, per mechanism type (Part XII §12.4, Part XVIII §18.4). Primary metric:
the fraction of theses citing a given F2 mechanism factor (white-space, reuse, cross-project,
compounding, and so on) that resolve CONFIRMED versus FALSIFIED, tracked separately per mechanism type.
Countermetric: a LOW confirmation rate for a mechanism type is not automatically a reason to stop
funding theses citing it — some genuine mechanisms (compounding, in particular, per F2's own
Compounding Formula, Part VI of the Advantage Algebra) are expected to show delayed, harder-to-confirm
effects; the rate is read alongside the mechanism's typical time horizon before being treated as
evidence of thesis-shopping (Part XVIII §18.4) versus a genuinely slow-maturing mechanism type.

19.7 SCARCITY THROTTLE PATTERN (Part IX §9.7). Primary metric: which classes and mission types are
recurrently throttled cycle over cycle. Countermetric: a recurring throttle is not automatically
evidence the pool should expand (Part IX §9.7 names this as a signal to IAS-C2, not a self-executing
trigger) — it must be checked against whether the throttled proposals were themselves well-evidenced
(a recurring throttle of consistently weak, low-marginal-ROI proposals is the scarcity resolver working
correctly, not a sign of genuine unmet demand).

19.8 LIFECYCLE DISTRIBUTION (Part XV §15.6). Primary metric: the count of positions in each of the
seven lifecycle states at a given time. Countermetric: neither a HIGH nor a LOW rate of DECLINING-to-
RETIRED transitions is, by itself, good or bad — a zero rate over a long window could mean a genuinely
healthy, low-churn portfolio or could mean the board is failing to act on decline signals (Part XV
§15.6's own caveat); the distribution is a prompt to investigate the underlying evidence, never a
verdict in itself.

19.9 PORTFOLIO REVIEWER ATTENTION LOAD (Part XI §11.7). Primary metric: the attention capital consumed
by the CAB's own deliberative process per cycle, tracked as its own line item. Countermetric: a
LOW attention load is not automatically efficient — it could mean genuinely few flagged proposals
arose (good) or that the routine/flagged classification (Part X §10.4) has drifted too permissive,
routing proposals that should be deliberated through the expedited path instead (bad); periodic
sampling of routine-path ratifications against what a full deliberation would have concluded is the
check this dataset names, without asserting a specific sampling rate here (left as an operational
detail).

19.10 Why the metric set is deliberately incomplete, and stays that way. This set names the metrics
this dataset can currently justify with a stated formula, an input source, and a countermetric; it does
NOT claim to be exhaustive. A metric proposed later without a paired countermetric is, per this Part's
own discipline applied reflexively to itself, not yet ready for board-visible reporting — it may be
tracked experimentally, but is not promoted to the headline instrument panel until its own gaming
surface has been examined with the same rigor Part XVIII applied to the seven mechanisms preceding it.

19.11 A tenth metric, named to close a gap the original nine leave open: THESIS OPPORTUNITY-COST
ACCURACY. Primary metric: for every RESOLVED thesis (Part XII §12.4), whether the OPPORTUNITY COST field
named at proposal time (§12.2) correctly identified what capital was actually foregone, checked
retrospectively against what the named next-best alternative's own valuation trajectory did across the
same window. Countermetric: an opportunity-cost field that is never checked against what actually
happened to the foregone alternative degrades, over time, into decorative prose attached to a thesis
template rather than a genuine accounting of trade-offs — this metric exists specifically to keep the
opportunity-cost field honest by giving it its own retrospective check, mirroring the falsifier's own
confirmed/falsified discipline (Part XII §12.4) but applied to the COST side of a thesis rather than the
CLAIM side, which the original eight metrics of §19.2-19.9 do not separately track.

19.12 Reading the metric set as a system, not as ten independent dials. No single metric in this Part's
instrument panel is designed to be read in isolation, and this Part closes by naming the specific
COMBINATIONS a diligent board reads together rather than separately, because several of the harms this
dataset catalogues are visible only at the intersection of two metrics, never in either alone. F-ROI
(§19.2) read against the lifecycle distribution (§19.8) reveals whether rising returns reflect genuine
growth or amputation-driven pruning. The concentration index (§19.3) read against the liquidity risk
flag (§19.5) distinguishes a large-but-liquid core position (a strength) from a large-and-illiquid one (a
structural risk, per Part XIII §13.5). Governance yield (§19.4) read against the scarcity throttle
pattern (§19.7) reveals whether governance holdings are themselves ever competing for, and losing, scarce
capital to mission-serving positions in a way that would eventually degrade oversight quality. Thesis
confirmation rate (§19.6) read against the new opportunity-cost accuracy metric (§19.11) reveals whether
a mechanism type that reliably confirms its claims is also reliably naming its true costs, or merely its
true benefits. None of these four cross-readings is itself a new formula; each is a specific, named
discipline for how the board's own attention (Part XI §11.7) should move across an instrument panel
that, read one dial at a time, would miss exactly the patterns this dataset's own failure-mode Parts
(XVII, XVIII) were built to catch.

19.13 The instrument panel's own reporting cadence, and why it does not match the allocation cycle
one-to-one. Not every metric in this Part is recomputed at the same cadence Part XI §11.1 sets for the
allocation cycle itself. F-ROI (§19.2) and the concentration index (§19.3), being capital-weighted and
share-based respectively, are recomputed EVERY cycle regardless of whether any position's own
reconciliation is due that cycle, because both depend on the full portfolio's current allocation state
rather than any single position's fresh valuation. Governance yield (§19.4), by contrast, is gated on the
adversarial-probe cadence (Part XIV §14.5), which this dataset does not assume aligns with the allocation
cycle length, and a governance-yield figure reported between probe cycles is explicitly marked
UNVERIFIED rather than silently carried forward at its last-probed value. The thesis confirmation rate
(§19.6) and the new opportunity-cost accuracy metric (§19.11) update only as underlying theses actually
resolve, which is itself irregular relative to the cycle boundary, since a thesis's stated time horizon
(Part XII §12.2) is set independently per thesis. (NORMATIVE: reporting every metric on a single, uniform
cadence regardless of what each metric's own underlying evidence actually supports would manufacture a
false uniformity — a board reading a same-cadence dashboard could reasonably assume every number on it
is equally fresh, when in fact freshness varies by metric, and this Part's own instrument panel therefore
carries a per-metric staleness indicator alongside its value, inheriting F1's property-honesty invariant
at the metric-reporting layer rather than only at the position-valuation layer Part IV §4.8 already
covers.)

FINAL LAW — PART XIX. A metric without a countermetric is a target waiting to be gamed, and this
dataset's own instrument panel is disciplined by the same rule it imposes on every position it
measures: no number is trusted until the way it could be gamed has been named, and named first.

PART XX — EVALUATION PROTOCOL: HOW TO PROVE THE PORTFOLIO WORKS
===============================================================================================

20.1 The evaluation question, stated precisely. It is not enough for this dataset to specify mechanisms
that are internally coherent (Part XVI's worked example demonstrated coherence); a portfolio discipline
must be evaluated against the counterfactual it claims to improve on — an estate ALLOCATING capital
WITHOUT this dataset's mechanisms, relying instead on ad hoc, arrival-order, or purely local decisions.
This Part specifies the PROTOCOL by which that comparison would be run, per Article VIII's
science-before-implementation boundary: a specification of the evaluation, not an executed evaluation
(which requires the runtime translation, Part XXI, and a later implementation mission, both outside
this dataset's own scope).

20.2 The primary evaluation claim and its falsifier. The claim this dataset licenses, stated as
HYPOTHESIZED pending the evaluation protocol below: positions allocated under the mechanisms of Parts
IV-XV will show, over a multi-cycle window, a HIGHER capital-weighted F-ROI (Part VIII) than an
otherwise-identical set of positions allocated by arrival order or local self-report alone. The
FALSIFIER: if a controlled comparison (§20.3) shows no statistically meaningful F-ROI difference, or
shows the portfolio-governed allocation underperforming, the central efficacy claim of this dataset is
FALSIFIED and the mechanisms require revision before further claims of institutional advantage are
made on their behalf.

20.3 The retrodiction protocol. Following F2's own precedent (Advantage Algebra Part VIII §8.7's
worked retrodiction), the primary evaluation instrument is RETRODICTION: applying the valuation and
allocation mechanisms of Parts IV-IX RETROSPECTIVELY to the estate's own historical capital-deployment
record (where such a record exists — CO-01's session telemetry, token_irr and recall_roi's historical
series) and checking whether the mechanisms' own INVEST/HARVEST/EXTINGUISH verdicts, had they been
followed at the time, would have outperformed the allocation decisions actually made. This is a WEAKER
form of evidence than a genuine forward controlled trial (§20.4) — a retrodiction cannot fully separate
the mechanism's genuine foresight from hindsight bias in how its own coefficients were calibrated
against the very history being retrodicted against — but it is available immediately, without waiting
multiple forward cycles, and is named here as the FIRST evaluation step, explicitly bounded in its
evidentiary weight.

20.4 The forward controlled comparison. The stronger evaluation instrument, requiring multiple forward
allocation cycles once the mechanisms are live: a HELD-OUT set of positions allocated under the
portfolio's own board-verdict discipline, compared against a comparable set allocated by the estate's
prior, pre-IAS-C1 process (or, where a full held-out comparison is impractical given the estate's
single instance, a BEFORE/AFTER comparison of the SAME positions' F-ROI trend across the transition).
This forward comparison is EXPERIMENTAL by construction — it has not been run, cannot be run before
this dataset's mechanisms are implemented, and its outcome, whichever direction it points, is the
actual test of §20.2's claim, not merely confirmatory color.

20.5 Auxiliary evaluation checks beyond F-ROI. Beyond the primary F-ROI claim, this dataset's specific
sub-claims each carry their own narrower evaluation check: the structural-weight correction (Part IV
§4.3) is evaluated by checking whether positions the correction newly upweighted (amplifiers, core
infrastructure) show, in retrodiction, a genuinely higher realized contribution than a naive
consumer-count-only valuation would have predicted; the concentration index (Part XVII §17.4) is
evaluated by checking whether historical episodes the estate's own retrospective record shows as
"a position became too central and its decline caused disproportionate downstream harm" would have
been flagged EARLIER by the index than they were actually noticed; the governance-yield prevented-harm
term (Part XIV §14.3) is evaluated by checking whether governance mechanisms the adversarial-probe
check confirms as still-effective show materially different valuation trajectories than mechanisms
that fail the probe, validating that the probe genuinely discriminates rather than passing everything.

20.6 What would count as genuine disconfirmation, stated in advance. Consistent with §20.2's falsifier
discipline applied to the dataset as a whole rather than only to its headline claim, this Part names,
in advance, the specific patterns that would count as this dataset's mechanisms having FAILED rather
than merely needing calibration adjustment: if retrodiction (§20.3) shows the portfolio's verdicts
would have systematically underperformed arrival-order allocation across MULTIPLE independent
historical windows (not one noisy sample); if the concentration index (§17.4) shows no earlier-warning
value over the estate's own prior, informal noticing of concentration episodes; if thesis confirmation
rates (Part XIX §19.6) across ALL mechanism types remain persistently low even after accounting for
delayed-effect mechanisms, suggesting the thesis template itself fails to distinguish genuine from
opportunistic claims. Any of these three would constitute EVIDENCE AGAINST the dataset's central
claims, not merely a tuning note, and this dataset commits, per Article V's honesty discipline, to
treating such evidence as disconfirming rather than explaining it away post hoc.

20.7 Evaluation cadence and who runs it. Consistent with Part XI §11.4's retrospective discipline, the
evaluation protocol is not a one-time gate passed at launch and never revisited; it runs at an
EXPANDING cadence — an early retrodiction pass before mechanisms go live, a first forward check after a
handful of cycles (sufficient for the confirmed-evidence bar of Part VII §7.3 to have been exercised at
least once across a meaningful sample of positions), and periodic re-evaluation thereafter as part of
the standing retrospective, so that the dataset's own efficacy claim remains a live, re-checked
hypothesis rather than a claim asserted once and never revisited.

20.8 The retrodiction's own data-quality precondition, named as a gating check before the pass is
trusted. §20.3 already names retrodiction as available immediately but evidentiarily weaker than a
forward trial; this Part adds the specific precondition a retrodiction pass must satisfy before its
output is treated as meaningful at all: the historical CO-01, token_irr, and recall_roi records it reads
must themselves cover a window with enough position-cycles of OBSERVED (not merely UNRESOLVED-and-
backfilled, per Part IV §4.6) data that the retrodicted verdicts are not dominated by positions whose
historical record is itself a low-confidence reconstruction. A retrodiction pass run predominantly
against backfilled histories would be testing the mechanisms against the estate's OWN reconstruction
uncertainty as much as against genuine historical signal, and this dataset names that specific confound
explicitly rather than letting a retrodiction result be read as cleaner evidence than its own inputs
actually support — the retrodiction's evidence level, like any DERIVED quantity in this dataset (Part IV
§4.7's minimum-propagation rule, applied here at the evaluation-protocol level), is bounded by the
weakest link in the historical data it retrodicts against.

20.9 What a MIXED retrodiction result would mean, and why this dataset does not pre-commit to a single
interpretation of partial success. §20.6 names the patterns that would count as clean disconfirmation;
this Part names the more likely real-world outcome explicitly rather than leaving it implicit: a
retrodiction pass that shows the mechanisms outperforming arrival-order allocation for SOME asset classes
(say, correctly upweighting amplifiers per Part IV §4.4's worked logic) while showing no meaningful
difference, or even mild underperformance, for OTHERS (say, niche positions, where the protected-share
guard's calibration, Part XXII §22.5, is itself named as an open question). A mixed result of this shape
is neither a clean confirmation nor a clean falsification of §20.2's headline claim; it is evidence for
CLASS-SPECIFIC calibration revision (consistent with §22.3's open question on structural-weight
generalization) rather than either a wholesale adoption or a wholesale rejection of the mechanisms, and
this dataset commits in advance to treating a mixed result as exactly that — a calibration signal, not
evidence to be forced into either the confirming or disconfirming bucket for the sake of a cleaner
headline finding.

FINAL LAW — PART XX. A mechanism this dataset cannot specify a way to disconfirm is a mechanism this
dataset has not actually subjected to evidence; retrodiction first, a forward comparison second, and a
named list of patterns that would count as failure, stated before the evaluation runs rather than
interpreted charitably after — that is the difference between a claim and a hypothesis wearing a
claim's confidence.

PART XXI — INTEGRATION WITH IAS-E1, IAS-C2, IAS-D1, AND THE WIDER FAMILY
===============================================================================================

21.1 Why integration is specified as a Part, not left implicit in the Compounding Graph. The
Compounding Graph names IAS-C1's inbound and outbound edges in one paragraph each; this Part expands
those edges into the specific, checkable INTERFACE each sibling dataset reads or writes, because a
compounding claim without a specified interface is exactly the kind of unevidenced strengthening claim
the Strengthening Matrix's own law (its closing paragraph) forbids stating as fact.

21.2 IAS-E1 (Observability Fabric): the primary loop's upstream edge, in full. The Compounding Graph's
primary loop names "IAS-E1 reveals which systems are used / dead / dark → IAS-C1 uses usage+ROI data
to allocate." Concretely: IAS-E1's usage telemetry (consumer counts, invocation frequency, dead-
capability flags) is the SOURCE data Part IV §4.2's valuation formula reads for its consumer-count and
depends-on inputs, and IAS-E1's dark-matter findings (capabilities present but never invoked) are the
DIRECT trigger for a position's classification review toward ZOMBIE (Part III §3.7) or DORMANT
(§3.7's look-alike distinction) status. The evidence artifact the Strengthening Matrix requires
(IAS-C1's row: "a portfolio ranking that reorders parent per-item scores") is concretely: a position
that IAS-E1's OWN per-system usage score would rank LOW (because usage alone undercounts amplifiers
and core infrastructure, Part IV §4.4) but that IAS-C1's structurally-weighted valuation ranks
substantially HIGHER, demonstrating the reordering the matrix requires as checkable evidence.

21.3 IAS-E1: the reverse edge, IAS-C1 strengthening IAS-E1. The relationship is not one-directional.
IAS-C1's valuation engine, in computing structural weight (Part IV §4.3), constructs a richer read of
the relation graph's depends-on and strengthens subgraphs than IAS-E1's own general-purpose
observability sweep is optimized to prioritize (IAS-E1's own scope, per the Strengthening Matrix, is
broad cross-system visibility, not deep per-position economic analysis) — IAS-C1 therefore returns to
IAS-E1 a REFINED signal: which positions' usage telemetry most urgently needs backfilling (Part IV
§4.6's UNRESOLVED valuations name exactly the positions IAS-E1's own sweep should prioritize
instrumenting next), turning IAS-C1's own valuation gaps into a prioritized worklist for IAS-E1's
telemetry coverage rather than a dead end.

21.4 IAS-C2 (Demand Forecasting): the foresight loop's full round-trip. The Compounding Graph's
foresight loop names IAS-C2 predicting next-needed capabilities and IAS-C1 pre-allocating to the
winners. Concretely, in the direction IAS-C1 CONSUMES: IAS-C2's demand forecast is read as one input to
sizing the emergency reserve (Part VI §6.3) and to informing which EXPERIMENTAL positions (Part III
§3.5) merit their capped initial allocation versus being deprioritized in owner_queue. In the direction
IAS-C1 PRODUCES for IAS-C2: the scarcity resolver's recurring-throttle pattern (Part IX §9.7, Part XIX
§19.7) is the SPECIFIC signal IAS-C2 consumes as evidence of genuine unmet demand — a mission type or
asset class throttled repeatedly across cycles, its throttled proposals independently confirmed
well-evidenced (not merely weak proposals correctly rejected, per §19.7's countermetric), is exactly
the retrodiction-checkable signal ("a forecast that matched a later real demand") the Strengthening
Matrix's evidence requirement for IAS-C2 names.

21.5 IAS-D1 (System Ecology): the primary loop's downstream edge and the succession handoff. The
Compounding Graph names IAS-D1 reading mission outcomes to update fitness, symbiosis, and parasitism,
feeding back into IAS-C1's allocation via IAS-G1. Concretely: Part III §3.6 already specified the
succession handoff explicitly — a LEGACY position's declining trend is FLAGGED by IAS-C1 to IAS-D1
rather than IAS-C1 attempting succession planning itself, because succession (which new capability
should replace an aging one, and how the transition is sequenced) is IAS-D1's distinct object, not
IAS-C1's. In the reverse direction, IAS-D1's PARASITISM findings (a position consuming more capital
than it returns in value, judged from the ecology's own fitness lens) are a DIRECT input to Part X's
board deliberation — a parasitism flag from IAS-D1 is treated as evidence comparable in weight to a
zombie signal from IAS-C1's own valuation engine, cross-checked rather than either dataset's finding
silently overriding the other's, consistent with F1's Invariant Three requiring a single, not
competing, final authority per decision — here, the BOARD remains the single authority, consuming both
signals as inputs.

21.6 CO-01, token_irr, recall_roi: the direct capital-and-return interface, restated as a data
contract. Per Part II §2.2 and Part VIII §8.2, IAS-C1 reads CO-01's session-cost telemetry as the
CAPITAL sub-pool input, and token_irr and recall_roi as the per-asset return components of F-ROI's
capital-weighted aggregate. The data contract is deliberately narrow: IAS-C1 requires from CO-01 a
per-position rolled-up cost figure (not raw per-session logs, which remain CO-01's own scope), and from
token_irr/recall_roi a per-position return figure at the SAME reconciliation cadence Part IV §4.8
specifies — where a parent's native reporting cadence differs, IAS-C1's valuation engine interpolates
conservatively (never extrapolating optimistically) rather than forcing the parent to change its own
reporting schedule to match the portfolio's needs, preserving the strengthen-don't-modify discipline of
Article III.

21.7 D2A-5 and owner_queue: the birth-time and new-investment interfaces. D2A-5's proposal-time fusion
score is read once, at a position's FUNDED lifecycle transition (Part XV §15.2), as the position's
initial thesis input (Part XII §12.7) — never re-read or re-weighted afterward, because D2A-5's score
answers a birth-time question the portfolio's own subsequent, repeated valuation cycles supersede.
owner_queue receives, per Part XI §11.6, any INVEST verdict representing genuinely new capability
(rather than an increase to an existing position) as a prioritized CANDIDATE, carrying its
supporting thesis, never executed directly by the board — preserving owner_queue's own final authority
over what gets BUILT, while IAS-C1 retains final authority only over what gets FUNDED among things
already built.

21.8 FD (distillation): the capital-light, high-optionality asset sub-class. FD deposits — distilled
knowledge artifacts rather than executable systems — are, per Part III's taxonomy, a distinct
sub-pattern within the portfolio: typically NICHE or AMPLIFIER in character (Part III §3.3-3.4), with
unusually LOW capital draw (a deposit, once distilled, costs little to maintain relative to an
executable system) and unusually HIGH liquidity (Part XIII) because a well-formed distillation is, by
FD's own portability discipline, readily redeployed across contexts. IAS-C1 does not build a separate
valuation track for FD deposits; it applies Part IV's standard formula, noting that FD deposits are
EXPECTED, as a class, to show a favorable value-to-capital ratio precisely because of their structural
cost profile — an expectation this dataset states as DERIVED-pending-retrodiction (§20.3) rather than
asserted as already confirmed.

21.9 IAS-B1 and IAS-B2: the two integration edges this Part had not yet made explicit as data contracts.
Part XII §12.4 already named that a FALSIFIED thesis, where the same mechanism-type has failed repeatedly
across different positions, feeds IAS-B1's (Cognitive Multiplier) broader search for which intervention
types reliably amplify; concretely, the interface is IAS-C1 exporting its PER-MECHANISM-TYPE
confirmation-rate table (Part XIX §19.6) at each cycle boundary, which IAS-B1 consumes as one input among
several intervention-type signals it aggregates, never as IAS-B1's sole evidence source. Part XV §15.5
similarly named IAS-B2 (Insight Synthesis) consuming a RETIRED position's full historical record;
concretely, this is a one-time export at the moment of RETIRED transition — the position's valuation
series, thesis record (both confirmed and falsified), and lifecycle transition history, packaged as a
single historical artifact IAS-B2 reads for cross-dataset pattern-finding, never a live, continuously-
polled feed, because a retired position's history is, by definition, closed and does not change further
after export.

21.10 The IAS-F3 (Digital Twin) interface, named as a conditional rather than standing data contract.
Part V §5.6 already specified that a large proposed rebalance MAY be routed through IAS-F3 for a
simulated before/after read before board ratification; the interface, stated as a data contract rather
than a design recommendation restated, is CONDITIONAL rather than standing: IAS-C1 does not export a
continuous feed to IAS-F3, it issues a single, on-demand simulation REQUEST — the affected position's
current depends-on subgraph and the proposed delta — only when the flagged-proposal path (Part X §10.4)
determines a simulation is warranted, and IAS-F3 returns a single before/after read consumed as one
additional piece of evidence in that proposal's board deliberation package, never as an automatic gate
every proposal must clear.

FINAL LAW — PART XXI. Every edge the Compounding Graph names in one line, this dataset specifies as a
checkable data contract: what is read, in what form, at what cadence, and what is produced in return —
because a compounding claim that cannot be traced to a specific field one system reads from another is
a diagram, not an integration.

PART XXII — EVOLUTION, BENCHMARKS, AND OPEN QUESTIONS
===============================================================================================

22.1 How this dataset's own mechanisms are expected to evolve. Per the Constitution's Article IX
(governed self-evolution), IAS-C1's own formulas — the structural-weight coefficients (Part IV §4.3),
the F-ROI trailing window (Part VIII §8.4), the allocation cycle length (Part XI §11.1), the
concentration threshold (Part VII §7.4) — are EXPERIMENTAL by explicit declaration, not settled
constants, and their evolution path is the retrospective mechanism (Part XI §11.4) itself: each cycle's
outcomes are evidence for whether the current calibration is correctly separating genuine signal from
noise, and a calibration change is itself proposed, evidenced, and ratified through the SAME board
protocol (Part X) that governs any other portfolio decision — the mechanisms that govern the estate's
capital are themselves governed by the estate's capital-allocation discipline, not exempted from it, a
deliberate, NORMATIVE closing of the loop.

22.2 Benchmarks this dataset names but does not yet possess. Three benchmarks would materially
strengthen this dataset's calibration once available, named here as explicit gaps rather than silently
assumed away: a REAL retrodiction pass (Part XX §20.3) across the actual estate's historical CO-01,
token_irr, and recall_roi records, currently UNRESOLVED because it requires implementation (Article
VIII's boundary) this mission does not build; a cross-CYCLE sample large enough to distinguish genuine
diminishing-versus-increasing marginal returns by asset class (Part VIII §8.5's CONTESTED open
question), requiring multiple real allocation cycles under the mechanisms described here; and an
adversarial RED-TEAM pass against the seven gaming cases of Part XVIII, run by a party genuinely
incentivized to find a gap the countermetrics miss, rather than by this dataset's own authors, whose
blind spots are, by construction, the ones least likely to be caught by self-review.

22.3 Open question one: does the structural-weight formula generalize, or does it require per-class
recalibration. Part IV §4.3's coefficients were chosen to correctly separate the ILLUSTRATIVE core and
amplifier examples of Part III and Part IV; whether a SINGLE set of coefficients correctly weights
structural importance across all six asset classes, or whether NICHE and GOVERNANCE positions (whose
structural importance manifests differently — narrow-but-critical rather than broadly-connected)
require class-specific weighting functions, is UNRESOLVED and named as the first calibration priority
for the retrospective mechanism of §22.1 to address with real data.

22.4 Open question two: the correct relationship between F-ROI and NICG divergence. Part VIII §8.7
named the possibility that F-ROI and F2's NICG could diverge — capital-return improving while genuine
ensemble-wide capability gain stagnates — and flagged this as CONTESTED-pending-evidence. Whether such
divergence, if OBSERVED, indicates a portfolio allocation problem (capital flowing to positions with
good individual returns but limited genuine ensemble impact) or indicates a measurement problem in
either F-ROI or NICG's own formula, is not resolved here and requires joint calibration work with
IAS-F2 once both metrics have enough cycles of real data to compare.

22.5 Open question three: whether the protected-share guard's size is correctly calibrated. Part IX
§9.5's biodiversity guard reserves a share of capital for niche and experimental positions, insulated
from pure marginal-ROI competition. The SIZE of that reserved share is asserted here only as
"a minority fraction," deliberately unspecified precisely, because too small a share fails to protect
genuine diversity (the guard becomes symbolic) while too large a share systematically underfunds
genuinely high-marginal-ROI core and amplifier proposals (the guard becomes its own concentration-in-
reverse). This is EXPERIMENTAL and named as a joint calibration question with IAS-D1, whose ecology
object is better positioned to judge what level of biodiversity the estate's actual mission mix
requires.

22.6 Open question four: whether governance yield's prevented-harm term is measurable at scale.
Part XIV §14.3's prevented-harm component depends on a governance mechanism's audit log recording
severity, not merely count, of caught violations — a data quality requirement not every existing
governance mechanism in the estate currently satisfies. Whether this term can be computed honestly
across ALL governance holdings, or only for the subset with sufficiently rich audit logs (leaving the
remainder's governance yield UNRESOLVED rather than computed), is a genuine, near-term implementation
question this dataset flags rather than assumes away.

22.7 Open question five: the estate's own appetite for this level of formalization. This dataset
specifies a five-object model, three subengines, a five-verdict board vocabulary, a six-class taxonomy,
and a seven-case adversarial catalogue — a substantial governance apparatus in its own right, whose own
MAINTENANCE CAPITAL cost (Part XI §11.7) is real and priced. Whether the estate's actual scale, at
seventy-plus systems, genuinely warrants this level of formal apparatus, or whether a lighter-weight
version would capture most of the value at a fraction of the governance overhead, is CONTESTED and
UNRESOLVED — this dataset asserts only that the apparatus specified here is the minimum sufficient to
answer Part I §1.5's four founding questions, not that a simpler apparatus could not answer three of the
four adequately at lower cost, a genuine trade-off the estate's own retrospective evidence, not this
dataset's own confidence, must ultimately settle.

22.8 Open question six: whether the estate's own capital-pool ceilings are themselves correctly measured.
Every mechanism in this dataset treats the three capital sub-pool ceilings (Part II §2.4) as
externally-given constraints the portfolio respects but does not set. This Part names, as a genuine and
UNRESOLVED gap rather than a settled input, that the ceilings THEMSELVES — the estate's actual token
budget, the Owner's actual available review time, the actual engineering capacity for maintenance — are
measured by systems outside this dataset's own scope (CO-00's session ceilings, the Owner's own calendar
and attention, informal engineering-capacity estimates), and whether those upstream measurements are
themselves as rigorously evidenced as this dataset requires of every position's valuation is a question
this dataset cannot answer from within its own object model. A portfolio built on a mismeasured ceiling
would allocate correctly RELATIVE to a wrong total, a distinct failure mode from any this dataset's own
Parts IV through XXI address, and named here specifically so it is not silently assumed away by the
apparent rigor of everything built on top of it.

22.9 Open question seven: whether the six-class taxonomy itself needs a seventh class as the estate's
holdings diversify further. Part III's six classes were DERIVED from the estate's OBSERVED holdings as
they existed at this dataset's own sealing. As the estate's mission mix continues to diversify — new
categories of holding that do not cleanly map to CORE, AMPLIFIER, NICHE, EXPERIMENTAL, LEGACY, or
ZOMBIE, for instance a genuinely HYBRID position that behaves as core infrastructure for one mission
family while remaining niche for every other — the taxonomy's own completeness is not guaranteed to hold
indefinitely, and this dataset does not assert it will. Whether a seventh class, or a multi-class tagging
model replacing the current single-class assignment, becomes necessary is left as an explicit,
UNRESOLVED question for the retrospective mechanism (§22.1) to surface from real classification
friction, rather than pre-empted by adding speculative classes now that the estate's current holdings do
not yet demonstrably need.

22.10 The closing synthesis. IAS-C1 exists because seventy-plus systems drawing on one finite pool of
cognitive capital, with no shared valuation, no shared board, and no shared discipline for deciding what
to fund, maintain, or retire, cannot be said to be ALLOCATED at all — merely accumulated. Every
mechanism in this dataset, from the structural-weight correction that prices amplifiers honestly, to the
falsifiable thesis that prevents forward claims from becoming unfalsifiable narratives, to the
concentration index that catches six reasonable decisions compounding into an unreasonable pattern, to
the governance-yield discipline that prices the estate's own oversight apparatus rather than exempting
it, serves one object: making the seventy-plus systems answerable, as a whole, to the same evidentiary
discipline the Constitution demands of every claim made about any one of them individually.

FINAL LAW — PART XXII. A portfolio discipline that cannot name its own open questions has not actually
examined itself; seven are named here — structural-weight generalization, F-ROI/NICG divergence, the
protected-share calibration, governance-yield's data requirements, the estate's own appetite for this
apparatus's cost, the ceiling-measurement precondition every allocation ultimately rests on, and the
taxonomy's own completeness as the estate's holdings diversify further — and each remains open not as a
gap in this dataset's confidence but as the honest boundary of what evidence, at the moment of sealing,
this dataset actually has.

DECISION LOG
===============================================================================================

D-1. DECISION: Value positions by a ratio (structurally-weighted advantage over capital draw, Part IV
§4.2) rather than by a raw advantage score or a raw cost figure alone. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a)
rank positions purely by F2's IAU, ignoring cost; (b) rank purely by capital efficiency (return per
token), ignoring structural importance. WHY THIS: a raw IAU ranking would favor expensive, high-output
positions over cheap, equally-valuable ones (violating the basic capital-allocation principle that cost
matters); a pure efficiency ranking would systematically undervalue core infrastructure and amplifiers
whose direct output understates their true contribution (Part III §3.2-3.3). TRADE-OFFS: the ratio form
requires a structural-weight term that is itself EXPERIMENTAL and adds a layer of calibration risk
neither simpler alternative would carry. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if retrodiction (Part XX §20.5) shows the
structural-weight correction does NOT improve on a naive ratio across a real historical sample, revert
to the simpler A(S)/D(S) form without the W(S) term.

D-2. DECISION: Separate mission-level allocation from system-level rebalancing into two distinct
subengines (Part V §5.2-5.3) rather than one combined allocator. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: a single
allocator handling both per-mission draws and standing rebalancing in one pass, re-evaluated
continuously. WHY THIS: the two operations carry different reversal costs and different appropriate
time horizons (Part V §5.3); combining them risks either making mission-level allocation too slow
(waiting for a full rebalancing pass) or making system-level rebalancing too reactive to single-mission
noise. TRADE-OFFS: two subengines require an explicit interface and reconciliation logic (the scarcity
resolver, Part IX) that a single allocator would not need. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if the two subengines'
outputs conflict often enough that the scarcity resolver becomes a bottleneck rather than an occasional
arbiter, reconsider a tighter coupling, though not a full merge (which would reintroduce the
conflation risk this decision exists to avoid).

D-3. DECISION: Make the Capital Allocation Board's authority ADVISORY by default, requiring explicit
transfer of F1's final-authority field before a verdict binds (Part X §10.5), with EXTINGUISH as the
sole mandatory-ratification exception. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) grant the board final authority
over all position allocations by default; (b) make every verdict, including EXTINGUISH, purely
advisory. WHY THIS: default final authority would risk violating F1's Invariant Three across every
position whose own governing system already holds final authority over some facet of its operation;
purely advisory EXTINGUISH would let an irreversible action proceed without the explicit sign-off
Article IX requires for governed self-evolution. TRADE-OFFS: advisory-by-default reduces the board's
practical teeth for non-extinguish verdicts, relying on the affected system's own governors to honor a
recommendation rather than being compelled. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if a pattern emerges of INVEST or
HARVEST verdicts being routinely ignored by position governors despite strong evidence, escalate to
Owner-authorized final authority for specific decision classes, on a case-by-case basis, never a
blanket transfer.

D-4. DECISION: Require a stated, pre-committed falsifier as a mandatory field of every Investment
Thesis (Part XII §12.2-12.3), rather than accepting a thesis with only a claim and a mechanism.
ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: a lighter-weight thesis template without a formal falsifier, relying on the
board's general judgment at the next reconciliation cycle to assess whether a funded position "worked
out." WHY THIS: an unfalsifiable claim can always be narrated as confirmed after the fact (Article V's
own epistemic discipline, applied specifically to forward capital claims); the falsifier is what
converts a thesis from persuasive prose into checkable evidence. TRADE-OFFS: the mandatory-falsifier
requirement adds friction to thesis formation and will, in some genuine cases, reject well-intentioned
proposals whose advocates cannot yet articulate a crisp falsifier for a genuinely fuzzy, early-stage
opportunity. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if the falsifier requirement is OBSERVED to be systematically
rejecting genuinely valuable early-stage theses (a false-negative rate problem), introduce a narrower
"provisional thesis" category with a relaxed falsifier bar but a correspondingly smaller capital cap —
not by abandoning the falsifier requirement itself.

D-5. DECISION: Fold "measured governance yield" (Family O) into IAS-C1 as a governance-specific
valuation lens (Part XIV) rather than routing it to IAS-E1 alone or building it as a standalone
mechanism. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) route governance yield entirely to IAS-E1, treating it as pure
observability; (b) leave governance mechanisms unpriced, outside the portfolio's scope entirely. WHY
THIS: governance mechanisms are capital-consuming positions like any other (Part XIV §14.1), and pricing
them requires the SAME valuation formula (Part IV) IAS-C1 already applies elsewhere — routing to IAS-E1
alone would duplicate the valuation formula a second time for one asset sub-class; leaving them unpriced
would create exactly the ungoverned-governance-overhead blind spot §14.6 names. TRADE-OFFS: governance
positions require an ADDITIONAL valuation input (the prevented-harm term and adversarial-probe
requirement, Part XIV §14.3-14.5) that mission-serving positions do not need, adding a genuine
special-case branch to an otherwise-uniform valuation engine. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if the
adversarial-probe requirement proves impractical to run at the necessary cadence across all governance
holdings (Part XXII §22.6's open question), narrow the prevented-harm term to only the subset of
governance mechanisms with sufficiently rich audit logs, marking the remainder's yield UNRESOLVED
rather than abandoning the fold entirely.

D-6. DECISION: Track a portfolio-level Concentration Index every cycle (Part XVII §17.4), independent
of whether any single proposal that cycle crossed the per-position concentration threshold.
ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: rely solely on the per-proposal threshold check (Part VII §7.4), trusting that
individually-flagged large proposals would surface concentration risk without a separate aggregate
metric. WHY THIS: Part XVII §17.6's worked adversarial case demonstrates concretely that a slow,
six-cycle drift built from individually-routine decisions is invisible to a per-proposal-only check;
only a standing, cycle-independent aggregate metric catches gradual accumulation. TRADE-OFFS: the index
adds a metric to track and interpret correctly (§19.3's countermetric — the index alone cannot
distinguish healthy from unhealthy concentration) that a simpler, threshold-only design would not
require. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: none currently anticipated — this mechanism directly closes a
demonstrated detection gap and its removal would reopen that gap without an equally effective
substitute currently specified.

ANTI-PATTERNS
===============================================================================================

ANTI-PATTERN 1: TREATING VALUATION AS A SUBSTITUTE FOR THE BOARD'S JUDGMENT. The most likely
implementation error is collapsing Part IV's valuation formula and Part X's board deliberation into one
step — letting a computed V(S) auto-trigger an allocation change without the routine/flagged
classification (Part X §10.4) or the board's own deliberative record. PREVENTION: the proposal/
ratification boundary (Part V §5.4) must be enforced structurally, not merely documented — a rebalancing
proposal is not a Board Verdict until it has passed through the protocol of Part X §10.6, and any
implementation that lets a valuation number write directly to a position's standing ALLOCATES-TO edge
without an intervening verdict record has reintroduced the unaccountable-formula failure Part V §5.1
and Part X §10.1 both exist to prevent.

ANTI-PATTERN 2: MEASURING CONCENTRATION OR GOVERNANCE YIELD FROM SELF-REPORTED DATA. The second most
likely error is implementing the concentration index (Part XVII §17.4) or governance yield (Part XIV
§14.3) from whatever data is most conveniently already logged by the position itself, rather than from
the independent, OBSERVED sources this dataset specifies (the Control Bus event log for consumer
counts, Part XVIII §18.2; the adversarial-probe pass for governance yield, Part XIV §14.4-14.5).
PREVENTION: every metric in Part XIX's instrument panel names its required input source explicitly;
an implementation that substitutes a self-reported log for an independently-observed one, even as a
"temporary" measure during early rollout, has reopened exactly the gaming surface Part XVIII catalogues,
and the substitution should be flagged UNRESOLVED rather than silently accepted as equivalent.

ANTI-PATTERN 3: LETTING THE SIX-CLASS TAXONOMY CALCIFY INTO A ONE-TIME LABEL. The third likely error is
treating a position's asset class (Part III) as assigned once at creation and never revisited, rather
than as a live classification recomputed each valuation cycle from current evidence (consumer trend,
transition history, structural weight). A position mislabeled CORE at launch and never reclassified,
even as its consumer count declines toward LEGACY over many cycles, will continue to receive the
CORE-appropriate treatment (routine, less-scrutinized rebalancing) long after its actual trajectory
warrants the LEGACY watch discipline (Part III §3.6) and the board's closer attention. PREVENTION: asset
class is DEFINED as a function of current attributes (Part III §3.1), recomputed at the same cadence as
valuation itself (Part IV §4.8), never cached indefinitely from a position's founding classification —
an implementation that treats class as a static field set once at FUNDED and never recomputed has
broken this dataset's own central mechanism for distinguishing what kind of low score means what kind
of problem.
