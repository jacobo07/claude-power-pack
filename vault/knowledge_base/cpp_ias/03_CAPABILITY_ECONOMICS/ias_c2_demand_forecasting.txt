===============================================================================================
PART I — THE DEMAND OBJECT: WHAT "CAPABILITY DEMAND" MEANS AT THE ENSEMBLE LEVEL
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding gap. The estate, as OBSERVED across its 70-plus systems, is thoroughly instrumented
for what has already happened and adequately instrumented for what is happening now: the Cognitive OS
meters a session's live spend, CO-12's gap radar flags a capability that is missing THIS turn,
backlog_autopilot lists items already filed, and owner_queue surfaces decisions already waiting on
the Owner. None of these systems is instrumented for what will be needed NEXT — not next in the
sense of "the next line in an existing queue," but next in the sense of "a capability class no
queue yet contains, because the mission that will require it has not yet been dispatched." This is
the founding gap C2 exists to close: the estate reacts well and observes adequately, but it does not
anticipate. (OBSERVED: every parent named in the Matrix row is a REACTIVE system — it processes an
input that already exists; DERIVED: a system that predicts the input before it arrives is a distinct
temporal register, not a faster version of the reactive systems.)

1.2 CAPABILITY DEMAND, defined. CAPABILITY DEMAND, as DEFINED in this dataset, is the ensemble-level
claim that a named capability (IAS-F1 §2.4) will be required by one or more future missions within a
stated horizon, expressed with a magnitude (how many missions, or how much load), a confidence
(how strong the evidence for the claim is), and a horizon (how far out the claim reaches before it
expires or must be recomputed). A demand record is therefore a quadruple: capability name, magnitude,
confidence, horizon. This is deliberately a claim ABOUT THE FUTURE, epistemically distinct from an F1
property (Part IV of F1, which describes a system's CURRENT state) and from an F2 score (which rates a
system's PAST-TO-PRESENT advantage). Demand is the family's first genuinely prospective object, and it
inherits F1's ensemble-state discipline: a demand claim is HYPOTHESIZED until the forecasting engine
(Part III) has run its evidence pipeline over it, and remains HYPOTHESIZED — never OBSERVED — until the
horizon has elapsed and retrodiction (Part XI) confirms or refutes it. No demand record may be treated
as OBSERVED before its horizon closes; that premature promotion is the single most common failure this
dataset must guard against (Part XV §15.2).

1.3 Demand is a property of the ENSEMBLE, not of any one mission. A single mission's stated requirement
("this session needs a knowledge lookup") is a resource-level fact, already owned by whichever system
serves that mission. Demand, in C2's sense, is the AGGREGATE and FORWARD-PROJECTED shape of many such
requirements across the estate's mission flow — the pattern, not the instance. (DERIVED, by the
majority-object rule of F1 §8.4: C2 reads resource-level mission data as input, but its produced object
is an ensemble-level projection over the capability space, which is what keeps C2 ensemble-level rather
than a duplicate of whatever system serves any one mission.) A demand record naming "knowledge-grounded
summarization capacity" as HIGH magnitude over the next quarter is not a claim about any session; it is
a claim about the shape of the mission flow the estate should expect to serve.

1.4 The three demand shapes. Three shapes of demand recur, and C2 names them because the forecasting
engine, capacity planner, and portfolio allocator each treat them differently. STEADY DEMAND is a
capability whose need recurs at roughly constant rate across historical windows — a baseline the
estate can staff once and maintain. SPIKE DEMAND is a capability whose need concentrates sharply around
a specific triggering condition (a new project's onboarding, a seasonal governance sweep, a sealed
mission's aftermath) and falls away once the condition passes. EMERGENT DEMAND is a capability with no
historical precedent in the estate's mission history at all — a genuinely new need signalled only by
early, thin evidence (a novel mission type appearing for the first time, an Owner statement of future
intent, a sibling IAS dataset's own forward reference). (DERIVED: the three shapes differ in the
evidence they can offer — steady demand is provable by trend, spike demand by trigger-correlation,
emergent demand only by weak leading indicators — so the forecasting engine's confidence model in
Part III must be shape-aware rather than applying one uniform method to all three.)

1.5 Demand versus backlog, precisely. It is tempting to read "capability demand" as a fancier name for
"the backlog," and this temptation must be resolved explicitly because it is the exact overlap the
Non-Duplication Ledger flagged for Candidate 27. A BACKLOG item, as backlog_autopilot already owns it,
is a KNOWN, FILED, discrete unit of work with an owner and a priority. A DEMAND record is a projected,
often UNFILED, aggregate claim about a capability CLASS, not a work item. The relation between them is
temporal and directional: healthy demand forecasting REDUCES the rate at which future backlog items
arrive as surprises, because a capability anticipated by C2 and pre-positioned (via the foresight loop)
either never becomes a backlog item at all (the mission is served immediately, because the capability
was already ready) or arrives as a backlog item the Owner recognizes rather than one that blindsides
the queue. (NORMATIVE: C2's success condition is partly measured by a DECLINE in backlog_autopilot's
surprise-arrival rate, specified as a shared evaluation in Part XVII.) C2 does not process backlog
items; it predicts the capability-shaped hole a class of future backlog items will fall into.

1.6 Demand versus a gap. CO-12's gap radar, as OBSERVED in the estate, flags an absence that is TRUE
NOW: a mission arrived, a capability was needed, and it either did not exist or could not be located in
time. C2's demand object is prospective: it flags an absence that is projected to become true LATER,
before any mission has yet failed to find the capability. The relation is again directional and
compounding rather than overlapping: every CO-12 gap that recurs across multiple missions is exactly
the evidence a demand forecast should have caught in advance, and C2's retrodiction discipline (Part
XI) explicitly re-plays CO-12's historical gap log as one of its primary evidence sources (Part II
§2.3). A demand forecast that never gets validated against CO-12's actual gap history is an unfalsifiable
forecast, which Part XI treats as a defect, not an acceptable variant.

1.7 The demand record's lifecycle, previewed. A demand record is not static once produced; it moves
through states — PROJECTED (freshly forecast, HYPOTHESIZED), PRE-POSITIONED (a portfolio or capacity
decision has acted on it), CONFIRMED (a real mission arrived needing exactly this capability within the
stated horizon), PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED (the capability was needed but at different magnitude or
timing than forecast), or REFUTED (the horizon elapsed with no matching mission). This lifecycle,
specified fully in Part X, is what lets C2's forecasts be audited rather than merely asserted, and it
is the mechanism by which the dataset satisfies Article V's epistemic-honesty requirement for every
demand claim it produces: a forecast is never allowed to sit in PROJECTED state indefinitely without
its horizon eventually forcing a verdict.

1.8 Why demand must be forecast for CAPABILITIES, not for MISSIONS. A tempting simplification would
have C2 forecast future MISSIONS directly — predict the next mission the estate will run. This is
rejected on two grounds. First, mission content is combinatorially unbounded and largely determined by
the Owner's intent, which C2 has no authority to predict or presume (a boundary violation of F1's
Invariant Four, Part V §5.5, applied here as the estate's authority does not extend into forecasting
Owner intent as if it were a governed object). Second, and more decisively, missions are not the unit
the downstream loop consumes: IAS-F3 simulates whether BUILDING A CAPABILITY pays off, and IAS-C1
allocates capital TO CAPABILITIES, not to predicted missions. Forecasting at the capability level is
therefore the correct altitude — coarse enough to be estimable from pattern, fine enough to be
actionable by the systems that consume the forecast. (NORMATIVE + DERIVED from the foresight loop's own
consumer contract.)

FINAL LAW — PART I. Capability demand is a claim about the SHAPE of future mission flow projected onto
the capability space — magnitude, confidence, and horizon over a named capability — and it is neither a
backlog (which is filed and known) nor a gap (which is present and true now); it is the estate's first
object that reasons forward, and every claim it produces remains HYPOTHESIZED until its horizon closes
and retrodiction says otherwise.

===============================================================================================
PART II — THE DEMAND SIGNAL TAXONOMY: WHERE FORECASTS COME FROM
===============================================================================================

2.1 Why a forecast needs a named signal taxonomy before it needs a method. A forecasting engine that
reads "whatever data is available" produces forecasts whose evidence base cannot be audited, because no
two forecasts will have consumed the same inputs in the same way. C2 therefore DEFINES a closed,
named set of SIGNAL SOURCES the forecasting engine (Part III) is permitted to read, each with a stated
reliability class, so that every demand record can cite exactly which signals produced it and at what
strength. (NORMATIVE: an unaudited forecast is not a forecast, it is a guess wearing a forecast's
vocabulary — the exact failure this dataset's Part XV names as forecast theater.)

2.2 Signal One — MISSION HISTORY. The estate's own record of missions run, capabilities invoked, and
outcomes observed (fed, per F1's typed relation graph, by every system's own consumes and feeds edges
accumulated over time) is the richest and most reliable signal source: it is OBSERVED data about what
actually happened, not a projection. Mission history is read in WINDOWS — trailing periods over which
capability-invocation frequency is counted — and the choice of window length is itself a forecasting
parameter with consequences specified in Part III §3.4. Mission history is strongest for STEADY demand
(Part I §1.4) and weakest for EMERGENT demand, because a capability with zero historical invocations by
definition contributes nothing to a history-based count.

2.3 Signal Two — ESTATE FLOW. Distinct from mission history's backward-looking count, ESTATE FLOW is
the CURRENT rate and direction of capability invocation, dependency formation, and relation-graph change
across the ensemble — effectively a live derivative of the mission-history signal, read from the
Control Bus's event stream (IAS-A2) and the Observability Fabric's usage telemetry (IAS-E1) where those
sibling systems are live, and from the estate's own audit-cache change log where they are not yet wired.
Estate flow is the signal that catches a demand curve BENDING before mission history's slower-moving
average would show it — a capability whose invocation rate is accelerating this month, even if its
trailing-year total is unremarkable, is a flow signal C2 must weight more heavily than history alone
would justify. (DERIVED: flow is a leading indicator relative to history's lagging indicator; the
forecasting engine's confidence model combines both because either alone underperforms.)

2.4 Signal Three — THE EXISTING QUEUES. backlog_autopilot's filed items, owner_queue's pending
decisions, and CO-12's logged gaps are, individually, resource-level or session-level facts, but taken
together across a trailing window they form a signal about the CLASS of capability the estate has
repeatedly needed and repeatedly had to file, escalate, or flag as absent. This is the signal source
that most directly operationalizes the Matrix's evidence requirement, because a demand forecast built
partly from these three queues' own history is, by construction, checkable against those same queues'
future behavior — the retrodiction loop of Part XI closes most tightly on this signal source precisely
because producer and validator share a vocabulary. (NORMATIVE: this is also the signal source most prone
to the self-fulfilling-prophecy failure mode of Part XV §15.3, addressed there directly, because a
forecast that only ever re-reads the queues it will later be checked against risks confirming itself by
construction rather than by genuine predictive power.)

2.5 Signal Four — SIBLING DATASET FORWARD REFERENCES. Several IAS datasets, in the course of their own
work, name a capability the estate does not yet have as a precondition for something they need — IAS-D1
naming a niche (Ledger candidate 35) no system currently fills, IAS-G1 flagging an architectural
decision that will require a capability not yet built, IAS-B1 ranking an intervention that presumes a
capability's existence. These forward references are WEAK signals individually (a single sibling's
passing mention is not strong evidence) but are the estate's only access to EMERGENT demand (Part I
§1.4), because by definition an emergent capability has no mission history and thin estate flow. C2
treats sibling forward references as a distinct, explicitly LOW-reliability-class signal, never silently
merged with the higher-reliability history and flow signals, so that a demand record's confidence
correctly reflects when it rests substantially on this thinner evidence base.

2.6 Signal Five — OWNER STATEMENT OF INTENT. Where the Owner states a future direction explicitly (a
stated intent to build in a new domain, expand into a new project type, or retire a class of mission),
this is the single highest-reliability signal available to the forecasting engine, because it is not an
inference from pattern but a direct declaration from the one authority the estate's boundary invariant
(F1 §5.5) recognizes as external to the systems it governs. C2 never infers Owner intent from behavior
alone (that would cross into presuming authority it does not hold, per Part I §1.8); it only ingests
intent the Owner has stated, and marks any demand record built substantially on this signal as
NORMATIVE-sourced rather than pattern-derived, a distinction Part IV's confidence model carries through
to the record itself.

2.7 Reliability classes and why they must be visible per record, not averaged away. F2's evidence-level
discipline (imported by reference) already establishes that a composite score must not collapse its
component evidence levels into one number without preserving the weakest link. C2 applies the identical
discipline to signals: a demand record's confidence field (Part IV) is DERIVED as a function that is
capped, not averaged, by its weakest contributing signal class when that signal is load-bearing rather
than merely corroborating — a demand record resting primarily on a single LOW-reliability forward
reference may not report HIGH confidence merely because mission history happens to show a loosely
related trend, because the loosely related trend did not actually predict the specific capability in
question. (NORMATIVE, DERIVED from F2 §12.3's composite ceiling rule, applied to signals rather than to
score factors.)

2.8 Worked example: five signals disagreeing on one capability, and how the taxonomy resolves it.
Consider a synthetic capability, "cross-project failure fingerprint correlation," illustrative only.
Mission history over the trailing year shows near-zero invocation (this exact capability has not
existed to be invoked). Estate flow shows a sharply rising rate of cross-project references in recent
sibling work (IAS-D2's own drafting activity touching multiple projects). The existing queues show two
owner_queue items in the last quarter asking, in different words, "why didn't we catch this failure in
project B when it was already fixed in project A" — the same underlying need, filed twice, independently,
because no capability yet exists to answer it directly. A sibling forward reference (IAS-D2's own text)
explicitly names the gap. No Owner statement of intent exists yet. Read individually, the mission-history
signal alone would forecast near-zero future demand (the naive trap of Part XV §15.2); read together, the
taxonomy correctly classifies this as EMERGENT demand (Part I §1.4) with MODERATE confidence — not HIGH,
because it rests on flow, queue-recurrence, and forward reference rather than direct history or Owner
statement, but not LOW either, because three independent signal sources converge on the same specific
capability rather than one thin reference standing alone. This worked trace is the taxonomy's payoff:
five signal classes, read separately and combined by the capped-not-averaged rule, produce a defensible,
citable confidence level that a single signal source could not have produced alone.

FINAL LAW — PART II. A forecast is only as auditable as its named signal sources; C2 reads five, ranked
by reliability class, capped rather than averaged when they disagree, and a demand record that cannot
name which of the five produced it — and at what strength — has not been forecast, it has been guessed.

===============================================================================================
PART III — THE FORECASTING ENGINE: ARCHITECTURE AND SUBENGINES
===============================================================================================

3.1 The engine's mandate, stated precisely. The FORECASTING ENGINE, as DEFINED, is the second-order
capability (F1 §7.2) that consumes the five signal classes of Part II and produces demand records (Part
I §1.2) at a stated cadence, over a stated set of tracked capabilities, with confidence and horizon
attached. Its direct object is not any mission or any signal in isolation; it is the projection of the
capability space itself — which is why it passes F1's second-order test and is admissible as the load-
bearing engine of an ensemble-level dataset rather than a duplicate of any single-signal parent (mission
history alone belongs to no one system; estate flow alone is IAS-A2/E1's telemetry; the engine's object
is the SYNTHESIS across all five, which none of the five signal-owning systems performs on its own).

3.2 Subengine One — the TREND SUBENGINE. Reads mission-history windows (Part II §2.2) and computes,
per tracked capability, a trailing invocation-rate trajectory: rising, flat, falling, or cyclical. The
trend subengine is the workhorse for STEADY demand and the first pass over SPIKE demand (a spike shows
as a sharp, short-lived rise against an otherwise flat trend). It is DELIBERATELY the simplest subengine
— a moving-window rate calculation, not a sophisticated statistical model — because Part XV's over-
fitting failure mode is specifically a risk of adding model sophistication faster than the estate's
mission-history volume can support; a simple trend read honestly outperforms a complex model fit to too
few historical points. (NORMATIVE: model complexity is bounded by data volume, not by what is
technically available; this is the engine's own anti-Goodhart discipline applied at the architecture
level, ahead of the metric-level countermetrics of Part XVI.)

3.3 Subengine Two — the FLOW SUBENGINE. Reads estate flow (Part II §2.3) and computes the SECOND
DERIVATIVE the trend subengine cannot see: not the rate, but the rate of change of the rate — is this
capability's invocation accelerating faster than its trailing-year trend would predict. The flow
subengine exists specifically to catch capabilities the trend subengine would under-forecast, because a
trend is by construction backward-looking and a genuinely bending curve looks, for several windows,
identical to noise around a flat trend until enough points accumulate. The flow subengine trades this
earlier detection for a HIGHER false-positive rate (short-lived spikes in flow that never sustain into
real demand), and Part IV's confidence model prices this trade-off explicitly by discounting flow-only
signals relative to trend-confirmed ones.

3.4 Subengine Three — the QUEUE-RECURRENCE SUBENGINE. Reads the existing queues (Part II §2.4) and
detects when the SAME underlying capability-shaped need recurs across multiple, nominally distinct,
backlog items, owner_queue decisions, or CO-12 gaps — the pattern demonstrated in the worked example of
Part II §2.8. This subengine's core operation is a similarity match over the SEMANTIC CONTENT of queue
items, not their surface wording (two owner_queue items phrased entirely differently can name the same
underlying capability gap), which makes it the most methodologically demanding of the four subengines
and the one most dependent on the estate's own knowledge-location capability (referenced, not rebuilt,
from GK) to establish that two queue items are actually about the same capability rather than
superficially similar ones.

3.5 Subengine Four — the FORWARD-REFERENCE AND INTENT SUBENGINE. Reads sibling forward references and
Owner statements of intent (Part II §2.5-2.6) and is the only subengine capable of surfacing genuinely
EMERGENT demand with no historical footprint at all. Its output is deliberately treated as a SEED rather
than a full forecast: a forward reference alone produces a demand record capped at MODERATE confidence
(per the capped-not-averaged rule of Part II §2.7) regardless of how many sibling datasets independently
name the same gap, because independent naming is still evidence of shared awareness, not evidence of
actual future mission demand — the two are correlated but not identical, and conflating them is a
specific instance of the self-fulfilling-prophecy risk named in Part XV §15.3.

3.6 The combination layer: how four subengine outputs become one demand record. The four subengines run
independently and produce, per tracked capability, up to four partial signals: a trend reading, a flow
reading, a queue-recurrence reading, and a forward-reference reading. The COMBINATION LAYER, DEFINED,
merges these into one demand record per capability per forecasting cycle, applying three rules in order.
First, the CORROBORATION RULE: a capability flagged by two or more subengines independently receives a
confidence boost proportional to the number and diversity of corroborating subengines, never proportional
to the number of raw signals within one subengine (ten mission-history data points corroborate each
other far less than one trend reading plus one independent flow reading, because the ten points share a
common source and the two readings do not). Second, the CEILING RULE of Part II §2.7: the combined
confidence may not exceed what the STRONGEST single subengine's reliability class licenses, even after
corroboration boosts. Third, the HORIZON-RECONCILIATION RULE: where subengines disagree on horizon (the
trend subengine implying a multi-quarter horizon, the flow subengine implying a near-term spike), the
combination layer reports BOTH, as a demand record may carry a near-term component and a longer-term
component simultaneously rather than being forced into one collapsed horizon.

3.7 Why the engine forecasts capabilities, never systems, as its unit of output. A capability, per F1
§2.4, is provider-independent; a system is one provider. The forecasting engine's output is deliberately
scoped to the capability, not to "which system should provide it," because that latter judgment belongs
to IAS-A1 (routing) and IAS-C1 (portfolio allocation) downstream, and C2 forecasting at the system level
would pre-judge a build-or-route decision that is not C2's object to decide. (DERIVED from the ensemble-
object boundary of Constitution Article II: C2's distinct object is demand for CAPABILITIES, and
collapsing that into a system-level recommendation would drift C2's object toward IAS-C1's territory,
the exact kind of scope creep the ledger's D2A verdicts exist to prevent.)

3.8 Cadence and staleness. The engine runs on a stated CADENCE — a forecasting cycle length — chosen so
that it is frequent enough to catch flow-subengine signals while their evidence is still fresh, and
infrequent enough that trend-subengine windows have accumulated meaningfully new data between runs; a
cadence shorter than the trend window's own resolution produces forecasts that differ from their
predecessor only in noise, wasting the downstream consumers' attention on non-signal. Every demand record
carries the F1 property-model staleness marker (F1 §4.3) by direct inheritance: a demand record not
reconciled within its own horizon is stale, and Part X's lifecycle state machine forces a verdict — moving
it to CONFIRMED, PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED, or REFUTED — rather than letting it persist indefinitely as an
unresolved PROJECTED claim.

3.9 Worked trace: one forecasting cycle end to end. A cycle begins by reading the tracked-capability
registry (Part IX names the mechanism that populates it). For each tracked capability, the four
subengines run in parallel over their respective signal sources, each producing a partial reading or an
explicit null (a subengine may have no signal for a given capability, which is itself informative — a
capability with zero flow and zero queue recurrence but a strong trend reading is a stable, unremarkable
STEADY case, correctly the least interesting output of the cycle). The combination layer applies its
three rules and emits demand records, each stamped with the cycle's timestamp, the contributing subengine
set, and the resulting confidence and horizon. Records are then handed to Part IX's decision model, which
is the first consumer, and separately logged for Part XI's later retrodiction pass, which is the engine's
own accountability mechanism.

FINAL LAW — PART III. The forecasting engine is four narrow, individually simple subengines — trend,
flow, queue-recurrence, forward-reference — combined by a corroboration-then-ceiling rule that never lets
volume of same-source evidence masquerade as diversity of evidence; sophistication belongs to the
combination layer, not to any one subengine, because a complex model fit to thin history is a forecast
that has learned to describe its own noise.

===============================================================================================
PART IV — THE FORECAST OBJECT: STRUCTURE, CONFIDENCE, HORIZON
===============================================================================================

4.1 Why the record's schema is its own Part. Part I introduced the demand record informally as a
quadruple; this Part fixes its full schema precisely, because every downstream consumer — the
opportunity-cost ledger (Part VI), the capacity planner (Part VII), the decision model (Part IX), and
the sibling datasets IAS-F3 and IAS-C1 — reads this exact structure, and a schema that drifts between
consumers reintroduces the fragmentation F1's property model (F1 Part IV) was built to prevent at the
system-property level, now at the demand-record level.

4.2 The full record. A DEMAND RECORD, DEFINED, carries: capability name (resolved against F1's
capability namespace, never a free-text label); magnitude (an ordinal estimate — LOW, MODERATE, HIGH,
or CRITICAL load, never a fabricated precise count where the evidence does not support one, per F2
§11.3's precision-matching discipline imported by reference); confidence (the capped-not-averaged
composite of Part III §3.6, itself carrying its own evidence-level marker per F2's score-is-a-claim
discipline, F2 Part XII); horizon (NEAR — within one forecasting cycle, MID — within several cycles, or
LONG — beyond the engine's reliable trend-window resolution); contributing-signal set (which of the five
signal classes and which subengine readings produced this record, satisfying Part II's auditability
requirement); lifecycle state (Part X's state machine — PROJECTED / PRE-POSITIONED / CONFIRMED /
PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED / REFUTED); and cycle timestamp (when it was produced, for staleness tracking).

4.3 Why magnitude is ordinal, not numeric, by design. A forecasting engine reading noisy, partial,
five-source signals cannot honestly produce "this capability will be invoked 47 times next quarter" —
that precision is not supported by the evidence and would violate the measurement-discipline rule C2
imports from F2 §11.3 (report no more precision than the evidence supports). Ordinal magnitude
(LOW/MODERATE/HIGH/CRITICAL) is the honest resolution: coarse enough to be defensible from five weak-to-
moderate signal sources, fine enough to distinguish "watch this" from "act on this now," which is the
only distinction the downstream capacity planner (Part VII) and decision model (Part IX) actually need
to act. (NORMATIVE: a forecast that reports false numeric precision is not more useful than an honest
ordinal one — it is LESS useful, because it invites downstream consumers to over-trust a number the
engine itself cannot defend.)

4.4 The confidence field's internal structure. Confidence is not a single scalar either; it is itself a
small record: a LEVEL (per F2 Part XII's evidence-level-for-a-score vocabulary, restricted here to the
subset meaningful for a forward claim — HYPOTHESIZED as the default floor, EXPERIMENTAL where the
forecast explicitly carries its own falsification test per Article V, and never OBSERVED before horizon
closure per Part I §1.2) and a NUMERIC BAND (a coarse range, not a point estimate, reflecting the
combination layer's corroboration math) used only for RELATIVE ranking among demand records within one
cycle, never asserted as an absolute probability the estate has validated. (DERIVED: the numeric band
exists solely so the decision model of Part IX can rank ten demand records against each other without
adjudicating each one's philosophy of probability; it is a ranking aid, not a forecast-accuracy claim in
itself — that claim is Part XVI's job, computed only after the fact.)

4.5 Horizon as a discipline against false urgency. A demand record without an explicit horizon invites
the downstream consumer to treat every forecast as equally urgent, which collapses the capacity planner's
prioritization into the exact "everything is a P0" failure the estate's Owner-queue governance already
guards against at the decision level (HR-BACKLOG-003 in spirit, generalized here to forecasts). C2's
horizon field is therefore mandatory, never optional, and a demand record produced without a defensible
horizon estimate — the engine genuinely cannot say whether this is near-term or long-term — is marked
horizon UNRESOLVED rather than defaulted to NEAR, mirroring F1 §4.6's UNRESOLVED-over-guessed discipline
for properties, applied here to horizon specifically because a guessed NEAR horizon is the single most
expensive guess this dataset could make: it would misallocate scarce near-term capacity (Part VII) against
a claim that was never actually urgent.

4.6 Record identity and versioning across cycles. Because the engine runs on a cadence (Part III §3.8),
the SAME capability is forecast repeatedly, cycle over cycle, and each cycle's record for that capability
is a new VERSION of an ongoing forecast, not an unrelated new claim. C2 assigns a stable FORECAST LINE
identifier per capability (distinct from, but structurally analogous to, F1 §9.2's stable system ID),
under which successive cycle records are chained, so that a capability's confidence and magnitude can be
read as a TRAJECTORY across cycles — rising confidence over several cycles is itself informative (the
forward-reference subengine's seed is being corroborated by an emerging trend), and this trajectory
reading is exactly what feeds Part XI's retrodiction validation and Part XVI's forecast-accuracy metric.

4.7 Worked example: a demand record's full lifecycle expressed in the schema. Returning to the
cross-project failure fingerprint correlation capability of Part II §2.8: cycle 1 produces a record with
magnitude MODERATE, confidence level HYPOTHESIZED (numeric band low-moderate), horizon MID, contributing
signals {flow, queue-recurrence, forward-reference}, lifecycle PROJECTED. Cycle 4 (three cycles later),
having observed the flow signal sustain and a fourth queue item recur, produces a new version on the same
forecast line: magnitude raised to HIGH, confidence level still HYPOTHESIZED but numeric band raised,
horizon tightened from MID to NEAR, contributing signals now {trend (early), flow, queue-recurrence
(x4), forward-reference}, lifecycle still PROJECTED. When a real mission subsequently invokes a newly
built cross-project fingerprinting capability within the NEAR horizon, the forecast line transitions to
CONFIRMED, and this exact trajectory — rising confidence, tightening horizon, eventual confirmation — is
the shape Part XVII's evaluation protocol looks for as evidence the engine is functioning as designed,
distinct from a forecast that simply asserted HIGH confidence from cycle one with no trajectory to show
for it, which Part XV treats as a red flag regardless of whether that single-shot forecast happened to be
right.

4.8 Schema validation at production time, and the rejection discipline. A demand record's six fields (Part
IV §4.2) are not advisory formatting guidance; they are a validation contract. C2 DEFINES a MALFORMED
RECORD as any candidate record missing a required field, carrying a magnitude value outside the four-rung
ordinal scale, or carrying a confidence level marked OBSERVED before its horizon has closed (a violation
Part I §1.2 already forbids at the epistemic level, restated here as a mechanical validation rule).
Downstream consumers — the decision model (Part IX), the capacity planner (Part VII), and any future
IAS-C1 or IAS-F3 implementation — are specified to REJECT a malformed record outright rather than
attempting a best-effort repair (silently defaulting a missing horizon to NEAR, for instance, is exactly
the false-urgency failure Part IV §4.5 already names), and a rejected record is logged as a validation
failure against the forecasting engine's own cycle, not silently dropped. (NORMATIVE: a validation layer
that repairs rather than rejects hides the engine's own defects from the metrics of Part XVI, because a
repaired record looks, from the consumer's side, indistinguishable from a well-formed one.)

4.9 Schema versioning across a governed revision. Article IX permits the Owner to approve a proposed
revision to the demand record schema itself (adding a field, tightening the magnitude scale, or similar) as
the estate's own forecasting practice matures. When such a revision is approved, C2 does not retroactively
rewrite records already produced under the prior schema version; each record carries an implicit SCHEMA
VERSION tag (derived from its cycle timestamp relative to the revision's effective date, Part IV §4.2), and
a consumer reading a mixed population of old- and new-version records — as the retrodiction protocol (Part
XI) necessarily must, spanning a long historical window — is specified to interpret each record against its
OWN version's field definitions rather than coercing older records into the newer schema's shape. (DERIVED:
this is the identical discipline F1 applies to a system's own property model across a governed capability
change — history is read as it was recorded, not retrofitted to a later standard, because retrofitting
would silently alter what the retrodiction population of Part XI is actually measuring.)

FINAL LAW — PART IV. A demand record is capability, ordinal magnitude, capped-composite confidence,
explicit horizon, named signal provenance, and lifecycle state — six fields, none of them a free-text
guess dressed as data — and a forecast whose confidence does not move across cycles as evidence
accumulates has not been forecasting, it has been asserting.

===============================================================================================
PART V — OPPORTUNITY COST: THE VALUE SACRIFICED BY A CHOICE
===============================================================================================

5.1 The second half of the object, and why it cannot be built without the first half. Candidate 24 of
the Non-Duplication Ledger names Opportunity Cost with NO closest parent and NO overlap — it is white
space in the truest sense, an object the estate has never named at all. But opportunity cost is not
computable in isolation: the value sacrificed by choosing capability A over capability B is only
meaningful relative to a claim about what B would have been WORTH, and the only place in the estate that
produces a forward-looking worth claim over not-yet-built capabilities is the demand object of Parts
I-IV. This is why Candidates 24 and 27 were routed to the SAME dataset rather than sealed as siblings:
opportunity cost is demand's necessary complement, not an independent white-space object that happens to
share a family folder.

5.2 OPPORTUNITY COST, defined. OPPORTUNITY COST, as DEFINED in this corpus, is the forecast-weighted
value of the highest-ranked capability demand that an allocation decision did NOT serve, measured at the
moment the decision was made and re-assessed as the forecast's horizon closes. Where the estate spends
cognitive capital — Owner attention, agent-hours, session budget, a build slot in IAS-C1's portfolio — on
capability X, the opportunity cost of that choice is not "zero, because X was worth building"; it is the
value the NEXT-BEST forecast demand record (Part IV) was carrying at the moment of choice, whether or
not that next-best demand was ever acted upon. (DERIVED from standard economic opportunity-cost
reasoning, formalized here specifically over C2's own demand-record schema rather than imported wholesale
from outside the corpus, per Article VII's domain-purity requirement — no external economic framework's
terminology is imported; only the concept, expressed entirely in F1/C2-native objects.)

5.3 Why opportunity cost requires a RANKED set of alternatives, not a single counterfactual. A common
error is to compute opportunity cost against one imagined alternative ("what if we had built Y instead").
This understates the true cost whenever a THIRD option, Z, was actually the better foregone choice, and
overstates it whenever Y was never a serious contender at all. C2 instead requires the decision model
(Part IX) to rank ALL live demand records at the moment of an allocation decision, and opportunity cost is
computed against the HIGHEST-ranked record among those NOT chosen — never against an arbitrarily selected
single alternative. (NORMATIVE: this ranking requirement is what makes opportunity cost auditable rather
than rhetorical; a claimed opportunity cost that cannot name which specific, ranked demand record it was
computed against is not a measurement, per Part XVI's countermetric for this exact gaming risk.)

5.4 The opportunity cost is provisional at the moment of choice, and settles as the horizon closes. At
the instant an allocation decision is made, the opportunity cost figure is necessarily built from
HYPOTHESIZED demand records — the same epistemic floor every demand record carries per Part I §1.2. This
provisional figure is useful for decision-making (Part IX consumes it precisely because a decision cannot
wait for certainty), but it is not the FINAL opportunity cost. As the foregone demand record's own horizon
closes and Part XI's retrodiction resolves it to CONFIRMED, PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED, or REFUTED, the
opportunity cost figure SETTLES: a foregone demand that turns out REFUTED means the true opportunity cost
was lower than estimated (nothing of value was actually sacrificed, because the alternative would not have
paid off); a foregone demand that turns out CONFIRMED at higher magnitude than forecast means the true
opportunity cost was higher than estimated. This settlement mechanic is what keeps opportunity cost
honest across time rather than frozen at a single, necessarily uncertain, moment.

5.5 Opportunity cost is not regret, and the distinction matters operationally. A settled opportunity cost
figure showing that a foregone alternative would have paid off well is INFORMATION for the next allocation
cycle, never a verdict that the original decision was wrong. The decision was made against the confidence
and evidence available at the time (Part IV §4.4's numeric band, not hindsight), and Article V's
epistemic-honesty discipline requires that a decision be judged against the information available when it
was made, not against information that only existed later. C2 explicitly frames settled opportunity cost
as a RECALIBRATION input to the forecasting engine (closing the foresight loop's own final edge, per the
Compounding Graph: "outcome confirms/refutes the forecast → IAS-C2 recalibrates") rather than as an
accountability instrument for blaming a past allocator. (NORMATIVE: conflating the two — using settled
opportunity cost to assign blame rather than to recalibrate — is named explicitly as an anti-pattern in
the dataset's closing sections, because it would create an incentive to never rank alternatives honestly
in the first place, defeating Part V §5.3's whole purpose.)

5.6 The opportunity cost RECORD, structurally. An OPPORTUNITY COST RECORD, DEFINED, is produced
alongside every allocation decision the decision model (Part IX) governs, and carries: the chosen
capability/system, the foregone demand record (by forecast line identifier, Part IV §4.6), the
provisional cost estimate at decision time (derived from the foregone record's magnitude and confidence
band), the decision timestamp, and — filled in only later — the settled cost once the foregone record's
horizon closes. This record is the atomic unit Part VI's opportunity-cost ledger accumulates, and its
two-phase structure (provisional then settled) is the mechanism by which C2 avoids ever asserting a final
opportunity-cost number before the evidence exists to support one, honoring the same discipline Part IV
applied to demand records now applied to their derivative cost claims.

5.7 Worked example: two live demand records, one allocation, one opportunity cost. Suppose, at a given
decision point, the decision model has two live, ranked demand records: Record Alpha (capability "batch
governance-sweep automation," magnitude HIGH, confidence numeric band moderate-high, horizon NEAR) and
Record Beta (the cross-project fingerprint correlation record of Part II §2.8's worked example, magnitude
MODERATE rising, confidence band low-moderate, horizon MID). The estate allocates its available build
slot to Alpha. The opportunity cost record names Beta as the foregone alternative (the highest-ranked
among those not chosen — here, the only other live record, but in a richer cycle it would be whichever
surviving record ranked second), computes a provisional cost proportional to Beta's magnitude and
confidence at that moment, and is logged open. Two cycles later, Beta's flow and queue-recurrence signals
both strengthen further (per Part IV §4.7's trajectory), and by the time Beta's MID horizon closes, a real
mission does arrive needing exactly this capability, unserved because it was never built — Beta resolves
CONFIRMED at magnitude HIGH, higher than its forecast at decision time. The opportunity cost record
settles UPWARD from its provisional estimate, and this settled, upward-revised figure becomes the specific
evidence the next forecasting cycle uses to raise Beta's (or its successor line's) priority in the
following allocation round — the recalibration Part V §5.5 describes, made concrete.

FINAL LAW — PART V. Opportunity cost is the forecast-weighted value of the best alternative an allocation
did not choose, computed against a ranked set of live demand records and settled only once the foregone
record's own horizon closes; it recalibrates the next forecast, and it is never evidence that the original
choice was a mistake.

===============================================================================================
PART VI — THE OPPORTUNITY COST LEDGER: RECORDING AND ATTRIBUTING SACRIFICED VALUE
===============================================================================================

6.1 Why individual records need a ledger. Part V defines one opportunity cost record for one allocation
decision. Read individually, a stream of such records answers "what did this specific choice cost" but
cannot answer estate-wide questions: is the estate systematically under-serving one class of demand
(a recurring pattern of high-magnitude foregone records concentrated in one capability domain), is a
particular allocator's decision pattern consistently more or less costly than another's, or is total
sacrificed value across the estate trending up or down over time. The OPPORTUNITY COST LEDGER, DEFINED,
is the accumulated, queryable store of every opportunity cost record produced by the decision model,
together with the aggregation and attribution operations specified in this Part.

6.2 Aggregation by capability domain. The ledger's first operation groups opportunity cost records by
the DOMAIN of their foregone demand record's capability (a coarse category above the individual
capability name — for instance, "cross-project reliability," "knowledge synthesis," "governance
automation" — drawn from the same capability namespace F1 resolves system objects against). A domain
whose foregone-and-later-CONFIRMED records accumulate disproportionately relative to other domains is a
DERIVED signal that the estate's allocation pattern is systematically under-serving that domain — not
because any single decision was wrong (Part V §5.5), but because the pattern across many decisions
reveals a structural bias the individual decisions could not see. (DERIVED: this is the ledger's
distinctive value over the individual record — pattern visible only in aggregate, exactly the emergent-
property discipline F1 Part III established for the ensemble object generally, applied here to a
derived economic quantity rather than to system properties directly.)

6.3 Attribution without blame: the allocator-neutral framing. The ledger tracks WHICH decision produced
each opportunity cost record (the decision model instance, the allocation context, the cycle) because
attribution is necessary for the recalibration Part V §5.5 requires — the forecasting engine needs to
know which forecast line was foregone and how its magnitude evolved, which requires knowing which
decision foreclosed it. This tracking is explicitly NOT designed to produce a per-decision-maker score;
C2 has no object called "allocator performance," and building one from the ledger's attribution data
would be constructing a resource-level judgment (about a specific decision instance) the ledger's own
object — estate-wide sacrificed value pattern — does not license. (NORMATIVE: this boundary is stated
explicitly because the ledger's raw data COULD be misused this way, and Part XIII names the misuse as an
adversarial case precisely because the temptation is real and the data would technically support it.)

6.4 The RUNNING TOTAL and its honest limits. The ledger can sum settled opportunity cost figures into a
running total — an estate-wide figure for "value forecast to have been sacrificed by allocation choices
over a trailing period." This total is useful directionally (is it rising or falling cycle over cycle)
but C2 explicitly refuses to present it as a precise, comparable-across-periods dollar-equivalent or
token-equivalent figure, because the underlying magnitude field (Part IV §4.3) is ordinal by design, and
summing ordinals into a false-precision total would violate the exact measurement discipline Part IV
§4.3 established for individual records. (DERIVED: a ledger total is legitimately expressible as "the
count and domain-distribution of HIGH/CRITICAL-magnitude foregone-and-confirmed records this period,"
never as a single scalar cost figure; the former is honest and actionable, the latter is false precision
compounded across many already-uncertain inputs.)

6.5 The ledger as IAS-C1's and IAS-G1's input, not their replacement. The Compounding Graph names IAS-C1
(Portfolio) as a consumer of C2's forecast for pre-allocation purposes; the ledger extends this by giving
IAS-C1 a SECOND input — not just "what is demanded" but "what has historically been sacrificed when this
domain lost an allocation contest" — which sharpens portfolio prioritization without IAS-C1 needing its
own parallel mechanism to derive the same pattern. Similarly, IAS-G1's complexity-budget and topology
decisions benefit from knowing which capability domains carry a persistent sacrificed-value pattern,
because a domain that keeps losing allocation contests despite recurring confirmed demand may indicate
an architectural gap the estate's current topology cannot easily serve, not merely an allocation-priority
gap C1 alone can fix. (DERIVED: this is the specific mechanism by which C2's ledger strengthens systems
beyond the four named in the Matrix row without claiming those additional strengthenings as the dataset's
OFFICIAL evidence-bearing relationships — they are consequences of the ledger's existence, honestly
noted, not claims requiring their own Matrix row.)

6.6 Ledger hygiene: expiry and the forgetting discipline. An opportunity cost ledger that never forgets
becomes, over a long enough estate history, dominated by stale entries whose domains, capability
namespace, or even underlying mission-type relevance have shifted so far that aggregating them with
recent entries produces noise rather than signal. The ledger applies an EXPIRY WINDOW, analogous to F1's
staleness marker but specified here at the ledger-aggregate level: entries older than the window remain
individually queryable (for historical audit, retrodiction validation in Part XI, and the accountability
that Part VI §6.3 explicitly does NOT want misused, but which honest historical record-keeping still
requires) but are excluded from the RUNNING TOTAL and domain-aggregation views that drive live decisions,
so that a five-year-old sacrificed-value pattern in a since-retired capability domain does not silently
bias today's allocation recommendations.

6.7 Worked example: a domain-level pattern the ledger surfaces that no single decision could show. Over
six successive allocation cycles, the ledger accumulates five opportunity cost records whose foregone
demand records all fall in the "cross-project reliability" domain, four of which later settle CONFIRMED
at HIGH or CRITICAL magnitude (the fifth REFUTED). No single one of the six original allocation decisions
was obviously wrong in isolation — each chose a reasonably-ranked alternative given what was known at the
time. But the domain-aggregation view (Part VI §6.2) surfaces a pattern invisible to any single decision:
cross-project reliability capability is being systematically outranked at decision time and then
systematically confirmed as real demand afterward, five times running. This pattern, and only this
pattern — not any individual record — is the actionable signal the ledger passes to IAS-C1: the domain's
RANKING WEIGHT in the decision model (Part IX) itself may be miscalibrated, a structural finding no
single opportunity cost record, however large, could have supported alone.

6.8 The ledger's query surface, and why it is read-only by construction. Every consumer named in this Part
— IAS-C1, IAS-G1, the decision model's own domain-weighting step (Part IX §9.2) — reads the ledger; none of
them, per this Part's own design, WRITES to it directly. The ledger's only writers are the decision model
(opening a new opportunity cost record at the moment of allocation, Part IX §9.6) and the retrodiction-
equivalent terminal-state mechanism (settling a record's foregone-value field, Part V §5.4). This
read-many, write-few structure is a deliberate mirror of F1's authority-field discipline (F1 Part IV §4.2)
applied to a ledger rather than to a system property: a query surface open to many consumers but a write
surface narrow enough to audit completely is what keeps the ledger's own provenance trustworthy, because a
ledger writable from arbitrary points in the estate could not support Part XI's leakage-resistant validation
discipline, which depends on knowing exactly which mechanism produced every entry.

6.9 A second worked example, illustrating the ledger's LOW-TO-MODERATE redundancy risk directly. Consider a
mature estate segment where the ledger has accumulated forty opportunity cost records across six capability
domains over an extended trailing period. Read individually, each record is unremarkable — a provisional
estimate, a settlement, nothing anomalous. The domain-aggregation view (Part VI §6.2), however, surfaces
that thirty-one of the forty records cluster in exactly two domains, while the remaining four domains
combined carry only nine records total. This is not, by itself, evidence of a defect — it may simply reflect
that those two domains are genuinely where the estate's real contested allocation decisions concentrate.
But it IS the exact signature Part XVI §16.5's DOMAIN CONCENTRATION countermetric exists to catch: a
settlement-accuracy figure computed over this ledger, if reported as one pooled estate-wide number, would be
overwhelmingly a statement about those two domains alone, dressed as a claim about the whole estate. The
ledger's own per-domain reporting discipline (Part VI §6.4) is what prevents this specific worked pattern
from silently becoming a false generalization, and it is the concrete reason the domain-level view, not the
running total, is the ledger's primary consumer-facing artifact.

FINAL LAW — PART VI. The ledger's value is not the sum of its individual records; it is the domain-level
pattern that only accumulation reveals — and it names patterns, tracks attribution for recalibration, and
refuses both false-precision totals and per-allocator blame, because an economic instrument that invites
either has stopped measuring and started punishing.

===============================================================================================
PART VII — CAPACITY PLANNING: FUTURE COGNITIVE CAPACITY AS A GOVERNED OBJECT
===============================================================================================

7.1 From CO-08's envelope to C2's horizon. CO-08, as OBSERVED in the estate, governs the PARALLEL
ENVELOPE — how many concurrent sessions, panes, or subagent dispatches the estate's current host and
governance rules permit RIGHT NOW (the small integer caps named across the Windows-bridge and subagent
doctrine are exactly this envelope, instantiated for one operational constraint). This is a resource-
level, present-tense object, correctly owned by CO-08 and not redefined here. CAPACITY PLANNING, as C2
DEFINES it, is the ensemble-level, future-tense complement: given the demand records the forecasting
engine has produced, will the estate's PROJECTED future capacity — across hosts, across the systems that
provide tracked capabilities, across the Owner's own available attention — be SUFFICIENT to serve the
demand as it arrives, and if not, where is the projected shortfall.

7.2 Why capacity planning cannot be computed from demand alone. A demand record states what will be
needed; it says nothing about what the estate will be ABLE to supply. Capacity planning therefore
requires a second input alongside the demand records: a CAPACITY PROJECTION, DEFINED as a forward
estimate of the estate's own serving capability along the same horizon bands (NEAR/MID/LONG) the demand
records use, built from the CURRENT envelope (read from CO-08 by reference, never re-measured) extended
forward by any KNOWN future changes to that envelope the Owner has stated or the estate's own governance
has scheduled (a planned reduction in parallel sessions during a maintenance window, a planned expansion
after new tooling lands). Where no such known change exists, the capacity projection defaults to "current
envelope held constant," explicitly marked as an ASSUMPTION rather than a forecast, because unlike
demand, capacity has no independent forecasting engine of its own in this dataset — C2 forecasts demand;
it only PROJECTS capacity from what CO-08 and the Owner already state.

7.3 The SHORTFALL, defined and computed. A SHORTFALL, DEFINED, exists for a horizon band when the sum of
CRITICAL- and HIGH-magnitude demand records projected to land in that band exceeds what the capacity
projection for the same band can plausibly serve, given the estate's own historical relationship between
envelope size and mission throughput (read, where available, from IAS-E1's flow telemetry; where
unavailable, estimated conservatively rather than optimistically, per the anti-overreach discipline of
Part XIV). A shortfall is not a single number; it is a RECORD naming the horizon band, the demand records
contributing to it, the capacity projection it exceeds, and a magnitude of its own (the same ordinal
scale as demand magnitude, for consistency). Shortfall records are the capacity planner's primary output
and the input the decision model (Part IX) weighs most heavily when a genuine scarcity, rather than a
mere allocation preference, is in play.

7.4 Capacity planning's relationship to scarcity foresight (Part VIII), stated to avoid overlap. It is
tempting to treat capacity planning and scarcity foresight as the same mechanism under two names, and
this Part draws the boundary explicitly before Part VIII builds on it: capacity planning answers "will
supply meet demand" as an AGGREGATE, estate-wide question over tracked capabilities; scarcity foresight
(Part VIII) answers the narrower, sharper question of WHICH SPECIFIC scarce resource — a particular
skill, a particular sibling system's attention, a particular narrow expertise — is projected to become
the estate's binding constraint before any aggregate shortfall would show it. Capacity planning is the
estate's macro balance sheet; scarcity foresight is its early-warning system for the single tightest
link. Both consume the same demand records; they answer different questions from them, and Part VIII
inherits capacity planning's shortfall records as one of its own inputs rather than recomputing them.

7.5 The three capacity postures. Given a shortfall record (or its absence), C2 characterizes the
estate's capacity POSTURE for a horizon band as SLACK (capacity projection comfortably exceeds demand;
the estate has room to absorb emergent demand without a shortfall), TIGHT (capacity projection roughly
matches aggregate high-and-critical demand; further demand growth in that band would produce a
shortfall), or CONSTRAINED (a shortfall record already exists for that band). This three-way posture is
deliberately coarse — the same ordinal-over-numeric discipline of Part IV §4.3 — and exists specifically
to give the decision model (Part IX) and IAS-C1 a fast-readable summary without requiring either to
re-derive it from the underlying demand and capacity records each time.

7.6 Capacity planning does not recommend HOW to add capacity. C2's object, per its own founding
boundary (Part I §1.8, generalized here), is the projection and the shortfall — it identifies WHERE and
WHEN a shortfall is projected, never HOW the estate should resolve it (hire, retrain, retire a competing
capability, renegotiate the envelope with the host). That prescriptive judgment belongs to IAS-C1's
portfolio allocation and, where an architectural response is implicated, to IAS-G1 — C2 hands both
systems a shortfall record as input, exactly as the Compounding Graph specifies ("IAS-C1 Portfolio
pre-allocates to the winners"), and does not itself decide the allocation. (NORMATIVE: this restraint is
what keeps capacity planning from drifting into IAS-C1's territory, the identical discipline Part III §3.7
applied to keep the forecasting engine from drifting into system-selection.)

7.7 Worked example: a projected TIGHT posture tightening into CONSTRAINED across three cycles. At cycle
N, the "governance automation" domain (Part VI §6.7's aggregation category) shows capacity posture TIGHT
for the MID horizon: two HIGH-magnitude demand records exist, and the capacity projection (current
envelope held constant, per Part VII §7.2's default assumption) can plausibly absorb them but with no
slack for anything further. At cycle N+2, a third HIGH-magnitude demand record enters the same domain and
horizon band (the forecasting engine's own trend subengine catching a genuinely accelerating pattern), and
the aggregate now exceeds the capacity projection: posture moves to CONSTRAINED, and a shortfall record is
produced naming all three contributing demand records, the domain, the horizon, and a magnitude of HIGH.
This shortfall record is handed to IAS-C1 as a pre-allocation input and to IAS-F3 as a candidate for
simulation (Part XVIII) — should the estate expand its envelope, retire a lower-priority competing
capability in the same domain, or accept the shortfall and let the third demand record likely resolve
PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED or REFUTED at horizon close. C2's own role ends at producing and handing off the
shortfall record; it does not adjudicate which of those three responses the estate takes.

7.8 Capacity projection across a growing multi-host estate. Part VII §7.2 grounds the capacity projection in
CO-08's single-host envelope by reference, held constant absent a stated change. As the estate's own
footprint grows to span multiple hosts (a condition this dataset does not assume but must not silently
break under), the capacity projection's INPUT widens to the SUM of each host's own CO-08-governed envelope,
read individually and combined additively rather than averaged — because two hosts each TIGHT on their own
envelope do not average to one SLACK estate; they remain two TIGHT constraints that happen to sit side by
side, and a capacity planner that pools them into one comfortable-looking aggregate would hide exactly the
per-host shortfall Part VII §7.3 is built to surface. (DERIVED: this is the same aggregation discipline Part
VI §6.4 applies to the opportunity cost ledger — sum only what is honestly summable, and per-host envelope
figures are summable in raw count but their TIGHT/SLACK/CONSTRAINED posture is not, because posture is a
ratio-derived category, not a quantity.)

7.9 Failure mode specific to capacity planning: a stated future envelope change that does not materialize.
Part VII §7.2 permits the capacity projection to extend beyond "current envelope held constant" when the
Owner has stated a future change (a planned expansion after new tooling lands, for instance). Where that
stated change is later delayed, scaled back, or cancelled — a legitimate, ordinary occurrence, not an
adversarial one — a capacity projection built on the ORIGINAL stated change becomes silently stale in
exactly the way Part III §3.8's staleness marker already guards against for demand records, but capacity
projections carry no independent forecasting engine of their own to re-derive them each cycle (Part VII
§7.2's own boundary). C2's specified response is procedural, not mechanical: a capacity projection built on
a stated future change carries that statement's own source and date as part of the projection record, so
that a downstream consumer can independently verify whether the stated change is still current before
trusting a projection more than one or two cycles old, rather than the capacity planner silently continuing
to assume a change that the Owner has since revised.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. Capacity planning projects the estate's future ability to serve the demand it has
forecast, in the same horizon bands and the same ordinal discipline as demand itself, and it stops at
naming the shortfall — never at prescribing the fix, which belongs to the allocation and architecture
systems the shortfall record is handed to.

===============================================================================================
PART VIII — SCARCITY FORESIGHT: ANTICIPATING CONTENTION BEFORE IT BITES
===============================================================================================

8.1 The narrower question capacity planning cannot answer alone. Part VII's capacity planner answers an
aggregate question across a whole domain and horizon band. But an estate can show a comfortably SLACK
aggregate posture while one specific, narrow resource — a particular sibling system's limited attention,
a specific rare-expertise capability with exactly one provider, the Owner's own decision bandwidth for a
specific decision class — is already the estate's binding constraint, invisible in an aggregate sum
because it is a single thin thread rather than a broad shortage. SCARCITY FORESIGHT, DEFINED, is the
mechanism that tracks these THIN, NARROW, SINGLE-POINT constraints specifically, distinct from and
complementary to capacity planning's aggregate view (Part VII §7.4 already drew this boundary; this Part
builds the mechanism on the correct side of it).

8.2 The SCARCE RESOURCE object. A SCARCE RESOURCE, DEFINED, is any capability, system, or decision
class whose CONSUMER COUNT (F1 Part IV's property, read by reference) is high relative to its PROVIDER
COUNT — in the degenerate and most consequential case, a capability with many consumers and exactly one
provider, meaning any degradation, retirement, or contention on that provider propagates directly to
every consumer with no redundancy to absorb it. This is, precisely, F1's DEPENDS-ON relation (F1 §6.3)
read in aggregate across many consumers rather than for one pair: a scarce resource is a system that many
others DEPEND-ON, not merely FEED-FROM, because a feeds-edge failure degrades gracefully while a
depends-on-edge failure at a single-provider chokepoint does not.

8.3 Scarcity foresight's forward-looking contribution: projected consumer growth against static supply.
The estate's Observability Fabric (IAS-E1, where live) can report a scarce resource's CURRENT consumer
count. Scarcity foresight's distinct contribution is combining that current count with C2's own demand
records: if a HIGH-magnitude demand record names a capability that a scarce resource is a load-bearing
dependency for, and that scarce resource's provider count is not itself independently forecast to grow,
the scarcity is PROJECTED TO TIGHTEN before the aggregate capacity posture (Part VII) would show any
shortfall at all — because the aggregate sum across the whole domain may remain comfortably SLACK even
as this one narrow dependency chain becomes the actual limiting factor for every mission that touches it.

8.4 The SCARCITY WARNING record. A SCARCITY WARNING, DEFINED, is produced when a scarce resource's
projected consumer growth (derived from demand records naming capabilities that depend on it) outpaces
its provider count's own trajectory, and it names: the scarce resource, its current and projected
consumer count, its provider count and that count's own trajectory (growing, flat, or itself at risk —
a scarce resource whose sole provider is ALSO the subject of a demand-driven strain elsewhere compounds
the warning), and a projected TIGHTENING HORIZON (when the estate should expect this specific
constraint to bind, distinct from any individual demand record's own horizon). Scarcity warnings are
handed to the same consumers as shortfall records (IAS-C1, IAS-F3) but carry a materially different
recommendation shape: a shortfall suggests "more capacity may be needed here," while a scarcity warning
suggests "this single point should be diversified or protected before it becomes the estate's binding
constraint," a distinction Part IX's decision model treats differently when weighing responses.

8.5 The relationship to F1's ecology-adjacent concepts, referenced not rebuilt. IAS-D1's System Ecology
(per the Ledger and Matrix) already governs symbiosis, parasitism, and monoculture across the estate as
its own distinct object — a broader, present-and-structural view of the ensemble's health. Scarcity
foresight does not duplicate this; it supplies IAS-D1 with a FORWARD-LOOKING input (a specific,
demand-driven projection of where a single-provider dependency will tighten) that IAS-D1's own,
more general ecology-health assessment can incorporate, exactly as the Compounding Graph's cross-dataset
feedback table specifies for demand-forecast outputs generally. Scarcity foresight is C2's narrow,
demand-conditioned lens on a phenomenon IAS-D1 studies more broadly; it is not a second monoculture
detector built independently.

8.6 Why scarcity foresight must distinguish TRUE single-provider scarcity from MERELY-CONCENTRATED
demand. A capability with ten consumers and three providers is not scarce in the sense this Part cares
about, even if all ten consumers' demand is projected to grow, because the three-provider redundancy
means no single point of failure exists — growth here is a capacity-planning concern (Part VII), not a
scarcity concern. Scarcity foresight is reserved specifically for the LOW-PROVIDER-COUNT case, and a
mechanism that flagged every capability with rising demand as a scarcity warning would flood the
decision model with false positives, diluting the signal precisely where it matters most: the genuinely
irreplaceable chokepoints. (NORMATIVE: the provider-count threshold below which a resource counts as
scarce is a tunable parameter, but the PRINCIPLE — scarcity is about REDUNDANCY, not about DEMAND
MAGNITUDE alone — is fixed and non-negotiable, because conflating the two is the specific failure mode
that would make this mechanism indistinguishable from, and therefore duplicative of, capacity planning.)

8.7 Worked example: a scarcity warning capacity planning alone would have missed. Suppose a narrow
capability — "reconciling a hook-dispatcher canonical-versus-live drift," referencing the estate's own
documented single-point pattern where exactly one mechanism performs this reconciliation — shows LOW
aggregate demand in absolute terms (few missions directly invoke it) but is a DEPENDS-ON prerequisite for
three separately-forecast HIGH-magnitude demand records in unrelated domains, each of which silently
assumes this reconciliation capability will remain available. Capacity planning's domain-aggregation view
(Part VI §6.2, Part VII §7.5) would show each of those three domains individually at a comfortable SLACK
or TIGHT posture, because the reconciliation capability's own load is thin relative to any one domain's
total. Scarcity foresight, reading the DEPENDS-ON relation across all three domains simultaneously, is the
only mechanism that surfaces the aggregate truth: this one narrow, single-provider capability is a hidden
chokepoint for three otherwise-unrelated forecasts at once, and a scarcity warning is produced naming all
three dependent demand records, the single provider, and a tightening horizon set by whichever of the
three dependent forecasts has the nearest horizon of its own.

8.8 Scarcity foresight does not recommend HOW to diversify, mirroring Part VII §7.6's restraint exactly. A
scarcity warning names the scarce resource, its consumer and provider trajectory, and a tightening horizon
— it does not recommend adding a second provider, retiring a dependent capability, or renegotiating the
provider's own load, because each of those responses is a build-or-architecture decision belonging to
IAS-C1 or IAS-G1, not to C2 (the identical boundary Part VII §7.6 draws for shortfall records, restated here
because the temptation to prescribe is, if anything, stronger for a scarcity warning than for a shortfall —
a single named chokepoint invites an obvious-seeming fix in a way an aggregate shortfall does not, and
that obviousness is precisely why the restraint must be stated explicitly rather than assumed self-evident).

8.9 Worked example: a scarcity warning that resolves as a false alarm, and what that teaches. Suppose a
scarcity warning is issued naming a narrow "cross-repository governance-sync reconciliation" capability as a
single-provider dependency for two forecast demand records, with a tightening horizon of NEAR. Two cycles
later, one of the two dependent demand records resolves REFUTED at horizon close (the real mission volume
never materialized in that domain), and the second, while CONFIRMED, resolves at a magnitude low enough that
the reconciliation capability's actual load never approaches strain. The scarcity warning, in retrospect,
did not bind — this is a FALSE ALARM in the precise sense Part XVI §16.6's countermetric names, and C2's
honest handling is to log the warning's own resolution (bound / did-not-bind) exactly as a demand record's
lifecycle resolves (Part X), feeding the false-alarm rate rather than being quietly forgotten because it
did not turn out to matter. A scarcity mechanism that only remembers its correct warnings and forgets its
false ones would show an artificially strong lead-time metric while hiding the exact over-eager-flagging
risk Part VIII §8.6 already named as scarcity foresight's own besetting risk.

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. Scarcity foresight watches the redundancy of a resource against its projected
consumer growth, not the raw magnitude of demand for it, because the estate's most dangerous future
constraint is rarely the biggest number — it is the single thread three unrelated forecasts are all,
unknowingly, standing on.

===============================================================================================
PART IX — DECISION MODELS: FROM FORECAST AND OPPORTUNITY COST TO AN ALLOCATION RECOMMENDATION
===============================================================================================

9.1 The consumer-facing surface of the whole dataset. Parts I-VIII build the objects — demand, forecast,
opportunity cost, capacity, scarcity. This Part specifies the DECISION MODEL: the mechanism that reads
all of them together, at a given allocation moment, and produces a RANKED RECOMMENDATION for where the
estate's next unit of build-or-serve capacity should go, which the ledger of Part VI then records against
as the moment an opportunity cost record is opened. The decision model is C2's output surface toward
IAS-C1 (Portfolio) and, indirectly through IAS-F3, toward architecture decisions — it does not itself
allocate (Part VII §7.6's restraint applies identically here); it RANKS and RECOMMENDS, and the systems
with actual allocation authority per F1's Invariant Three (F1 §5.4, singular authority per decision class)
decide.

9.2 The ranking inputs, named exhaustively. For each live, tracked capability with an open demand record
(Part IV), the decision model reads: the demand record's magnitude and confidence (Part IV); the
capability's horizon (near-term demand is weighted for urgency, but not unconditionally above longer-term
CRITICAL demand — Part IX §9.3 specifies the trade-off); the capacity posture for its domain and horizon
(Part VII §7.5 — a capability in a CONSTRAINED domain is weighted differently than an identical one in a
SLACK domain, because the marginal value of serving it is higher where supply is already tight); any
scarcity warning naming it as a dependent (Part VIII §8.4 — a capability that is itself a load-bearing
dependency for several other forecasts is weighted upward, because its foregone value compounds across
every dependent); and the ledger's domain-level historical pattern (Part VI §6.2 — a domain with a
persistent pattern of confirmed-but-foregone demand is weighted upward, the recalibration mechanism made
operational).

9.3 The weighting rule, and why it is deliberately not a single collapsed formula. Unlike F2's Advantage
Score (F2 Part III-IV), which DOES collapse its factors into one formula because portfolio ranking across
already-built systems tolerates that compression, C2's decision model deliberately PRESERVES its five
ranking inputs as a visible, ordered table rather than collapsing them into one number, for a reason
specific to this dataset's object: an allocation decision made under genuine uncertainty about the
FUTURE benefits from a human-legible trade-off table more than from a single score that hides which input
is actually driving the recommendation. (NORMATIVE: this is a deliberate divergence from F2's precedent,
justified by the difference between scoring what EXISTS, which F2 does, and ranking claims about what
MIGHT BE NEEDED, which C2 does — collapsing genuine forward uncertainty into false single-number
confidence is precisely the false-precision failure Part IV §4.3 and Part VI §6.4 already guard against
elsewhere in this dataset, and the decision model inherits the same discipline at the ranking layer.)

9.4 The recommendation record. A RECOMMENDATION, DEFINED, is the decision model's output: a ranked list
of live demand records, each annotated with its five contributing inputs (Part IX §9.2), a stated
TOP RECOMMENDATION (the highest-ranked record), and — critically — the OPPORTUNITY COST PREVIEW: for the
top recommendation, a statement of what the SECOND-ranked record represents as the provisional foregone
value should the top recommendation be chosen (directly feeding Part V §5.6's opportunity cost record the
moment an actual allocation decision is made against this recommendation). The recommendation is handed
to IAS-C1 as an input to portfolio allocation and, per the foresight loop, to IAS-F3 as a candidate for
simulation before the recommendation is acted upon.

9.5 Overrides, and why the decision model must accept them gracefully. IAS-C1, holding actual portfolio
allocation authority, may choose NOT to follow the top recommendation — for reasons entirely outside C2's
own visibility (an Owner directive, a resource constraint C2's capacity projection did not capture, a
strategic bet on a lower-ranked but strategically important capability). This is not a failure of the
decision model; C2's recommendation is advisory, per F1's authority-field discipline (F1 Part IV §4.2's
AUTHORITY property, applied here: C2 holds advisory, not final, authority over allocation). When an
override occurs, the decision model logs it (which recommendation was made, which choice was actually
taken) without penalty or flag, because an override pattern is itself useful RECALIBRATION information —
a decision model whose top recommendation is overridden frequently in a PREDICTABLE direction (always
deprioritized in favor of one particular domain, say) is a signal that the ranking weights (Part IX §9.2)
may be systematically under-valuing something the Owner and IAS-C1 consistently value more, which Part
XVII's evaluation protocol treats as a legitimate recalibration signal rather than as the decision model
simply being wrong.

9.6 The recommendation's relationship to the opportunity cost ledger, closing the loop within this Part.
Every time an allocation decision is actually made — whether it follows the top recommendation or
overrides it — the CHOSEN capability and the recommendation's own ranking (specifically, the highest-
ranked record among those NOT chosen, per Part V §5.3's ranking requirement) together open a new
opportunity cost record in the ledger. This is the precise mechanical link between Part IX's decision
model and Part VI's ledger: the decision model does not merely recommend, it SUPPLIES the ranked
alternative set the opportunity cost record's foregone-value field requires, and without this supply the
ledger could not compute opportunity cost at all — the ledger has no independent ranking mechanism of its
own; it depends (an F1 DEPENDS-ON relation, stated explicitly) on the decision model for every record it
opens.

9.7 Worked example: a full ranking table and the recommendation it produces. At one allocation moment,
three live demand records compete: the "batch governance-sweep automation" record from Part V §5.7
(magnitude HIGH, NEAR horizon, domain posture TIGHT, no scarcity dependents, domain ledger pattern
neutral); the cross-project fingerprint correlation record (magnitude MODERATE rising, MID horizon,
domain posture SLACK, flagged as a scarcity dependent by Part VIII §8.7's worked warning, domain ledger
pattern shows one prior REFUTED entry); and a third, "governance automation" record from Part VII §7.7's
worked shortfall (magnitude HIGH, MID horizon, domain posture CONSTRAINED, no scarcity flag, domain
ledger pattern shows the five-record pattern of Part VI §6.7). The decision model's table surfaces all
three side by side rather than collapsing them: the governance-automation record's CONSTRAINED posture
and strong ledger pattern push it to the TOP despite its MID rather than NEAR horizon, because the
weighting rule (Part IX §9.3) treats a domain's persistent historical under-service as comparably urgent
to raw horizon proximity; the fingerprint-correlation record ranks second, elevated above its raw
magnitude alone would suggest specifically because of its scarcity-dependent flag; the batch-sweep record,
despite its NEAR horizon and HIGH magnitude, ranks third because its domain shows no persistent
under-service pattern and no scarcity dependency. The recommendation names governance-automation as top,
with the fingerprint-correlation record as the opportunity cost preview — precisely because the ranking
table made the trade-off visible rather than asserting a single score that would have hidden why the
NEAR-horizon batch-sweep record was outranked.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. The decision model ranks, previews the opportunity cost of its own top
recommendation, and defers final authority to the systems the Constitution assigns it to — because a
forecast that also claimed the power to allocate would have crossed, silently, from advisory into
authority it was never granted.

===============================================================================================
PART X — PROTOCOL: THE DEMAND-TO-PRE-POSITION LIFECYCLE
===============================================================================================

10.1 Why the lifecycle needs its own Part, separate from the schema. Part IV §1.7 previewed the demand
record's lifecycle states informally. This Part specifies the PROTOCOL — the state machine's transitions,
who or what triggers each one, and what evidence each transition requires — because a lifecycle named but
not operationalized is exactly the kind of ontology-without-enforcement gap F1 Part V §5.1 warned against
at the ensemble level, now addressed at the demand-record level specifically.

10.2 The five states, and the transitions between them. PROJECTED is the entry state, produced by the
forecasting engine (Part III) at the close of a cycle; every demand record begins here. PRE-POSITIONED
is entered when a downstream consumer — most often IAS-C1 acting on the decision model's recommendation
(Part IX), sometimes IAS-F3 having simulated the capability's build (Part XVIII) — takes a concrete action
in response to the record: allocating a build slot, reserving capacity, or otherwise committing estate
resources against the forecast before the horizon closes. A record may remain in PROJECTED past its
horizon without ever being pre-positioned, which is not a defect in the record itself but a legitimate
allocation choice the Owner or IAS-C1 made. CONFIRMED, PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED, or REFUTED are TERMINAL
states, entered only at horizon close, via the retrodiction mechanism of Part XI, based on whether a real
mission's actual invocation pattern matched the forecast's magnitude within its stated horizon.

10.3 The trigger discipline: no state transition without a named cause. Every transition in the state
machine is logged with a trigger — the specific event, decision, or retrodiction pass that caused it —
because an audit trail that shows a record moved from PROJECTED to CONFIRMED with no stated cause is
unfalsifiable in exactly the way Part II §2.1 already rejected for signal sourcing, now applied to the
lifecycle itself. (NORMATIVE: this is not bureaucratic overhead; it is the specific mechanism that lets
Part XVII's evaluation protocol distinguish a genuinely predictive engine from one that got lucky, because
"lucky" and "predictive" produce identical terminal states but very different trigger-and-trajectory
histories, per Part IV §4.7's trajectory discussion.)

10.4 PRE-POSITIONING as the loop's action point. The transition into PRE-POSITIONED is the single most
consequential event in the lifecycle, because it is the moment C2's purely advisory forecast becomes a
real commitment of estate resources — the exact point the Compounding Graph's foresight loop names as
"IAS-C1 Portfolio pre-allocates to the winners." C2 tracks this transition specifically so that the
LATENCY between a demand record first reaching a given confidence-and-magnitude threshold and its actual
pre-positioning can be measured (Part XVII's evaluation protocol uses this latency as one of its
benchmarks: a forecasting-and-decision pipeline that consistently pre-positions capacity only AFTER a
demand record has already reached CRITICAL magnitude is not meaningfully ahead of a purely reactive
system, even if its forecasts are individually accurate).

10.5 The forced-verdict rule at horizon close. Part III §3.8 already established that a demand record's
staleness forces a lifecycle verdict rather than allowing indefinite PROJECTED persistence. This Part
specifies the mechanism precisely: at horizon close, the retrodiction pass (Part XI) MUST assign one of
the three terminal states — there is no fourth "still uncertain" terminal state, because Article V's
epistemic-honesty discipline requires every claim to eventually be evaluated against evidence, and a
demand record that is allowed to expire into permanent ambiguity is a claim that was never actually held
accountable. Where evidence is genuinely thin at horizon close (neither clearly matching nor clearly
absent), the record resolves PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED with an explicit note naming the ambiguity, rather than
being silently dropped from the ledger — an ambiguous outcome is still an outcome, and Part XI's
retrodiction validator (its own accountability mechanism, Part XI §11.6) is itself evaluated partly on
whether it produces a defensibly LOW rate of such ambiguous resolutions over time, because a validator
that resolves everything as "partially confirmed" is dodging its own job.

10.6 Cross-cycle chaining and the forecast line's cumulative record. Because a forecast line (Part IV
§4.6) may carry many successive PROJECTED versions before a single terminal verdict is reached, the
protocol specifies that the terminal state applies to the LINE's most recent, most-evidenced version, not
to some averaged or earliest version — a forecast line whose confidence rose across four cycles before
horizon close is evaluated against its FINAL, most-informed prediction, because evaluating it against its
thin, early-cycle prediction would unfairly penalize (or unfairly credit) the engine for evidence it had
not yet accumulated at that earlier point. This is the specific procedural safeguard that makes Part XVII's
forecast-accuracy metric meaningful rather than an artifact of when in a forecast line's life the
evaluation happened to be taken.

10.7 Who consumes each state, operationally. PROJECTED records are visible to IAS-C1 and IAS-F3 for
consideration (per the foresight loop) but carry no committed resource. PRE-POSITIONED records are
visible to IAS-A1 (routing), because a capability pre-positioned against a forecast is a capability that
should be preferentially discoverable by a mission actually needing it, closing the loop the Compounding
Graph names ("IAS-A1 Router finds them ready when the real mission arrives"). Terminal-state records
(CONFIRMED, PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED, REFUTED) are consumed primarily by the forecasting engine itself, for
recalibration (Part V §5.5, Part XI), and by the ledger (Part VI), for the domain-pattern aggregation that
depends on knowing which foregone records actually paid off.

10.8 Worked example: one forecast line's complete transcript. Cycle 1: capability "batch governance-sweep
automation" enters PROJECTED at MODERATE magnitude, MID horizon, triggered by the trend subengine alone.
Cycle 3: magnitude rises to HIGH, horizon tightens to NEAR, triggered by a corroborating flow-subengine
reading — still PROJECTED. Cycle 4: the decision model's recommendation (Part IX §9.7's worked example)
ranks this record top; IAS-C1 allocates a build slot — transition to PRE-POSITIONED, trigger logged as
"IAS-C1 allocation, cycle 4, following C2 recommendation." Cycle 7 (within the NEAR horizon window from
cycle 3's tightening): a real mission invokes the newly-built capability at a magnitude consistent with
the HIGH forecast — retrodiction pass at horizon close assigns CONFIRMED, trigger logged as "retrodiction
pass, cycle 7, matched real invocation within horizon." This six-cycle transcript — PROJECTED, rising
evidence, PRE-POSITIONED, CONFIRMED, each transition named and triggered — is exactly the auditable shape
Part XVII's evaluation protocol is built to find, and its absence (a record that jumps straight from a
thin cycle-1 PROJECTED to a CONFIRMED with no intervening trajectory or trigger) is the specific pattern
Part XV treats as a red flag.

10.9 Manual lifecycle overrides, and why none are specified. Nowhere in this Part's five-state protocol is a
mechanism for a human or an agent to manually SET a demand record's lifecycle state outside the triggers
named in Part X §10.2-10.3. This is a deliberate omission, not an oversight: a manual override capability,
however well-intentioned ("I know this one will confirm, let's mark it PRE-POSITIONED early to unblock
routing"), would puncture the exact trigger-discipline (Part X §10.3) that makes the lifecycle auditable, and
would be functionally indistinguishable from the horizon-shopping and leakage risks Part XIV §14.6 and Part
XI §11.3 already name as adversarial patterns. Where a genuine need exists to accelerate a capability's
availability ahead of its forecast's own terminal verdict, the correct mechanism is for IAS-C1 to make an
ordinary allocation decision through the decision model (Part IX) — which DOES legitimately transition a
record to PRE-POSITIONED via a named, logged trigger — rather than for any mechanism to reach into the
lifecycle state machine directly and relabel a record's own history.

10.10 Interaction with Part XIII's deduplication edge case. Because two forecast lines can be found, after
the fact, to describe one underlying capability (Part XIII §13.4), the lifecycle protocol must specify what
happens to EACH line's own transition history when a merge occurs. The rule: the surviving, merged line
inherits the UNION of both original lines' logged transitions, each retaining its own original trigger and
timestamp, rather than either history being discarded — a merge is an identity correction, not an erasure,
and Part XVII's evaluation protocol, reading a merged line's full transcript, must be able to see that the
line was originally two separately-seeded forecasts before it can correctly interpret an unusually rich
early trajectory (two subengines corroborating from cycle one is a different signal than one subengine
corroborating with itself, and only the preserved pre-merge history lets a later auditor tell the two apart).

FINAL LAW — PART X. A lifecycle state is not a label applied at will; it is a transition with a named
trigger, forced to a terminal verdict at horizon close, chained across a forecast line's full evidence
trajectory — and a demand record with no transcript to show for its journey from PROJECTED to terminal is
a claim that was never actually held accountable.

===============================================================================================
PART XI — RETRODICTION VALIDATION: FORECAST VERSUS LATER REAL DEMAND, ON HISTORY
===============================================================================================

11.1 Why retrodiction is the dataset's own evidence requirement, not an optional add-on. The Matrix's
evidence-required column for IAS-C2 states, exactly: "a forecast that matched a later real demand
(retrodiction on history)." This is not a suggestion for how C2 might someday be validated; it is the
SPECIFIC artifact the Strengthening Matrix requires before C2's claim to strengthen CO-12, backlog_
autopilot, owner_queue, and liveness may be stated as fact rather than marked HYPOTHESIZED (Constitution
Article III). RETRODICTION VALIDATION, DEFINED, is the mechanism that produces this exact artifact: running
the forecasting engine (Part III) as if positioned at a PAST point in the estate's mission history, letting
it forecast forward using only signals available AT that past point, and then checking its output against
what ACTUALLY happened afterward, which the estate's own historical record already contains.

11.2 The retrodiction protocol, stepwise. First, select a HISTORICAL VANTAGE POINT — a past cycle boundary
in the estate's mission history, chosen far enough back that a full horizon's worth of subsequent history
exists to check against. Second, RECONSTRUCT the signal state as of that vantage point: mission history
truncated to only what preceded it, estate flow as it stood then, the existing queues' contents at that
moment, and any forward references or Owner statements that were actually made by that point (never
signals from after the vantage point — a retrodiction that accidentally leaks future information is not a
retrodiction, it is a rehearsed answer, and this leakage is the single most important procedural error this
Part must prevent). Third, RUN the forecasting engine exactly as specified in Part III against this
reconstructed, truncated signal state, producing demand records dated as if freshly forecast at the
vantage point. Fourth, ADVANCE to the present (or to a later point at least one horizon beyond the vantage
point) and read the estate's ACTUAL subsequent history — which capabilities were actually invoked, at what
real magnitude, within the forecast's stated horizon. Fifth, SCORE each retrodicted record against the
actual outcome using the identical terminal-state logic of Part X §10.5 (CONFIRMED, PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED,
REFUTED), exactly as if the vantage point had been a live forecasting cycle all along.

11.3 The leakage discipline, elaborated. Because retrodiction is run AFTER the fact, with full knowledge
of what actually happened available to whoever runs the validation, the single greatest risk to the
protocol's integrity is inadvertent information leakage — a human or process running the retrodiction
who already knows the outcome subtly tuning the reconstructed signal state (Part XI §11.2, step two) to
make the forecast look better than a genuinely blind forecast would have been. C2 guards against this with
a STRUCTURAL rather than a purely procedural safeguard: the signal reconstruction step is performed by
literally filtering the estate's own timestamped records to the vantage point's cutoff — a mechanical
truncation, not a human recollection of "what we knew then" — so that the reconstruction is bounded by the
SAME timestamp discipline every demand record already carries (Part IV §4.2's cycle timestamp), making
leakage a verifiable-absent property of the reconstruction step rather than a matter of the validator's
good faith.

11.4 Multiple vantage points, and why a single retrodiction proves little. One successful retrodiction —
a single vantage point whose forecast matched later reality — is compatible with genuine predictive power
and with pure chance; Article V's epistemic-honesty discipline does not let C2 claim OBSERVED strengthening
evidence from one lucky trace any more than F2's evidence-level discipline lets a single successful
measurement promote a system's whole property record to OBSERVED (F1 §4.3's staleness and reconciliation
logic implies exactly this caution). C2 therefore runs retrodiction across MULTIPLE vantage points spanning
the estate's available history, and the Matrix's evidence requirement is satisfied only by a POPULATION of
retrodiction results — a stated count of vantage points, the fraction that resolved CONFIRMED or
PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED versus REFUTED, and the fraction where the forecast's horizon and magnitude were
directionally correct even when not exactly matched — never by a single cherry-picked success.

11.5 Reading the retrodiction population honestly: three possible verdicts. A retrodiction population can
support one of three honest readings, and C2 commits to naming which one applies rather than defaulting to
the most favorable. VALIDATED: retrodiction across multiple vantage points shows a CONFIRMED-or-partially-
confirmed rate meaningfully above what a naive baseline (Part XI §11.7) would achieve, licensing the
Matrix's strengthening claim as DERIVED-from-evidence rather than HYPOTHESIZED. INCONCLUSIVE: the
retrodiction rate is not distinguishable from the naive baseline given the available history's volume,
which is an honest, nameable outcome — not a failure of the engine necessarily, but a statement that the
estate's history is not yet rich enough to validate the claim either way, and the Matrix's strengthening
claim remains HYPOTHESIZED until more history accumulates. REFUTED: the retrodiction rate is at or below
the naive baseline, meaning the engine's added sophistication over simple pattern-matching is not earning
its complexity cost, and Part XV's over-fitting failure mode (Part XV §15.2) is the diagnostic path this
verdict triggers.

11.6 The validator's own accountability. Retrodiction validation is itself a mechanism, and per this
dataset's own discipline (Part X §10.5's closing note), a mechanism that produces only favorable verdicts
is suspect. C2 specifies that the retrodiction protocol must be run against AT LEAST ONE vantage point
DELIBERATELY chosen where the estate's actual subsequent history is already known to show a capability
that FAILED to materialize despite apparent early signal (a genuine REFUTED case in the historical record,
not merely constructed) — including a known-negative case in the validation population is the mechanism's
own test that it is capable of correctly reporting REFUTED, rather than one whose scoring logic has been
implicitly tuned to always find a match.

11.7 The naive baseline, named explicitly. Every retrodiction population is compared against a NAIVE
BASELINE, DEFINED as the retrodiction rate a trivial forecasting rule would achieve — for instance, "the
capability domain with the single highest raw historical invocation count will remain the highest-demand
domain next horizon," a rule requiring none of the four subengines or the combination layer. C2's
forecasting engine's added value is only claimed relative to this baseline's own retrodiction rate, never
in absolute terms, because an absolute CONFIRMED rate of, say, sixty percent sounds impressive in isolation
but is worthless evidence of the engine's own contribution if the naive baseline achieves the same sixty
percent by doing far less work. (NORMATIVE: this baseline comparison is the specific countermetric that
prevents Part XVI's forecast-accuracy metric from being gamed by an engine that has simply learned to
restate the recent past, addressed fully in Part XVI §16.3.)

11.8 Worked example: a small retrodiction population and its honest reading. Suppose the estate's
available mission history supports five vantage points spaced across its recorded life. Running the full
protocol produces: three vantage points where the top-ranked forecasted demand record resolved CONFIRMED
or PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED within horizon; one where it resolved REFUTED (the deliberately-included known-
negative case of Part XI §11.6); and one INCONCLUSIVE (evidence at horizon close was too thin to score
either way, itself logged honestly rather than forced). The naive baseline, run across the same five
vantage points, achieves two CONFIRMED-or-partial resolutions. The engine's three-of-five against the
baseline's two-of-five, on a population this small, does not yet support a VALIDATED verdict with
confidence — five vantage points is too thin a sample for the margin to be meaningful — and C2's honest
reading, per Part XI §11.5, is INCONCLUSIVE-LEANING-POSITIVE, explicitly logged as such, with the Matrix's
strengthening claim remaining marked HYPOTHESIZED pending a larger population as the estate's own mission
history continues to accumulate. This worked example is deliberately NOT a triumphant validation, because
the dataset's own epistemic-honesty discipline (Article V) forbids overstating a five-point sample as
proof, and a corpus that only ever shows itself succeeding has not demonstrated the discipline it claims
to practice.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. Retrodiction is the dataset's own evidence engine, run blind against reconstructed
past signal states, scored against a naive baseline across multiple vantage points including at least one
deliberately known-negative case — and a forecast validated against only its own successes has not been
validated at all.

===============================================================================================
PART XII — WORKED EXAMPLES ON SYNTHETIC MISSION DATA
===============================================================================================

12.1 Purpose and scope of this Part. Parts I-XI each carried one or two worked examples embedded in their
own argument, sufficient to illustrate a single mechanism at a time. This Part assembles ONE larger,
continuous synthetic scenario — illustrative only, built from patterns generic to the estate's own
documented history rather than any single real project's specifics, per Article VII's domain-purity
requirement — that exercises the forecasting engine, opportunity cost ledger, capacity planner, scarcity
foresight, and decision model TOGETHER across several cycles, so the dataset's mechanisms can be seen
composing rather than only in isolation.

12.2 Scenario setup. A synthetic estate segment tracks four capability domains: "governance automation,"
"cross-project reliability," "knowledge synthesis," and "session observability." At cycle 0 (the
scenario's start), mission history shows governance automation as the estate's steadiest, highest-volume
domain; cross-project reliability shows near-zero history (an emergent domain, per Part I §1.4); knowledge
synthesis shows a mature, flat trend; session observability shows a recently-declining trend (a domain the
estate has been investing in less).

12.3 Cycles 1-3: forecasting engine output. The trend subengine (Part III §3.2) reports governance
automation STEADY-HIGH, knowledge synthesis STEADY-MODERATE, session observability FALLING, and near-zero
signal for cross-project reliability (consistent with its thin history). The flow subengine (Part III
§3.3) reports accelerating estate-flow in cross-project reliability specifically (sibling datasets'
drafting activity referencing it, per the Part II §2.8 pattern) despite the trend subengine's near-zero
reading — the exact divergence the flow subengine exists to catch early. The queue-recurrence subengine
(Part III §3.4) confirms two independently-worded owner_queue items both resolving to the same
cross-project reliability gap. By cycle 3, the combination layer (Part III §3.6) produces: governance
automation demand record at HIGH magnitude, NEAR horizon, high confidence (trend-corroborated); cross-
project reliability demand record at MODERATE-rising magnitude, MID horizon, moderate confidence
(flow-plus-queue corroborated, per the corroboration rule); knowledge synthesis at MODERATE-steady,
LONG horizon, high confidence; session observability at LOW-and-falling, no urgency flagged.

12.4 Cycles 1-3: capacity and scarcity read alongside the forecasts. The capacity planner (Part VII)
reads the current envelope (from CO-08, by reference) and finds governance automation's domain posture
TIGHT (its HIGH demand comfortably matched by current capacity but with no slack remaining) and
cross-project reliability's domain posture SLACK (low current investment, plenty of room). Scarcity
foresight (Part VIII), scanning the depends-on relations across all four domains, discovers that the
narrow "cross-project failure fingerprint correlation" capability underlying the cross-project reliability
demand is itself a dependency for a nascent knowledge-synthesis initiative as well — two domains, one
thin single-provider dependency — and issues a scarcity warning naming both.

12.5 Cycle 4: the decision model's recommendation and the allocation. With one available build slot, the
decision model's ranking table (Part IX §9.2) weighs: governance automation (HIGH magnitude, NEAR horizon,
TIGHT posture, no scarcity flag, neutral ledger history) against cross-project reliability (MODERATE-
rising magnitude, MID horizon, SLACK posture, POSITIVE scarcity flag — it is a dependency for two domains,
not one — and a ledger history showing, per this scenario's backstory, one prior REFUTED entry in this
exact domain eighteen months earlier). The scarcity flag's cross-domain weight pushes cross-project
reliability's rank up despite its lower raw magnitude and looser horizon; governance automation's TIGHT
posture and NEAR horizon keep it competitive. The table is presented, not collapsed (Part IX §9.3), and
in this worked scenario the recommendation names governance automation as top (its NEAR horizon and
already-tight posture are judged, in the visible table, to outweigh cross-project reliability's scarcity
premium this cycle) — IAS-C1 follows the recommendation. An opportunity cost record opens naming
cross-project reliability as the foregone demand.

12.6 Cycles 5-9: the foregone record's trajectory and eventual settlement. Across the next several cycles,
cross-project reliability's flow and queue-recurrence signals continue strengthening (consistent with Part
IV §4.7's worked trajectory), and by cycle 8 a third, unrelated domain also surfaces a forward reference
naming the same underlying fingerprinting gap — now THREE domains implicated, strengthening the scarcity
warning further. At cycle 9, within the original MID horizon window, a real mission in the knowledge-
synthesis domain is blocked precisely because the fingerprinting capability was never built — the
opportunity cost record's foregone value settles UPWARD, and the underlying forecast line resolves
CONFIRMED at HIGH magnitude, higher than its cycle-3 MODERATE-rising estimate.

12.7 Cycle 10: recalibration closes the loop. The ledger's domain-aggregation view (Part VI §6.2) now
shows cross-project reliability with one historical REFUTED entry (eighteen months prior) and one newly
CONFIRMED, higher-than-forecast entry — not yet the five-record pattern of Part VI §6.7's standalone
worked example, but a visible upward trend the decision model's ranking weights (Part IX §9.2) incorporate
for the next allocation cycle, where cross-project reliability's domain-history weight is now
meaningfully higher than it was at cycle 4. Meanwhile the forecasting engine's own recalibration (Part V
§5.5) registers that its cycle-3 MID horizon on this forecast line was slightly too conservative — the
demand fully materialized closer to the horizon's near edge than its center — a small, honestly-logged
calibration note feeding Part XVI's forecast-accuracy metric rather than a dramatic correction.

12.8 What this composed scenario demonstrates that the isolated examples could not. Read end to end, the
scenario shows the foresight loop's own closing edge in miniature: a forecast (cycle 3) informed an
allocation decision (cycle 4) that knowingly accepted a real, previewed opportunity cost (cycle 4's
preview, Part IX §9.4) against a scarcity-flagged alternative (Part VIII); that alternative's later
confirmation (cycle 9) settled the opportunity cost record and fed a domain-level ledger pattern (Part VI);
and that pattern, in turn, measurably shifted the next cycle's ranking weights (cycle 10) — forecast to
allocation to outcome to recalibration to sharper forecast, the exact loop the Compounding Graph names,
demonstrated over one continuous synthetic trace rather than asserted as an abstract diagram.

12.9 A second, contrasting trace: a forecast that is confirmed as WRONG. The scenario of Part XII §12.2-12.8
is deliberately a case where the engine's early, thin signal on cross-project reliability eventually pays
off. Article V's epistemic-honesty discipline requires this dataset to show the opposite trace with equal
honesty, so this section runs a second, independent four-cycle synthetic scenario against the same estate
segment's "session observability" domain, which cycle 0 already showed as recently-declining (Part XII
§12.2). At cycle 2, the forward-reference subengine (Part III §3.5) picks up a single sibling dataset's
passing mention of a future observability need, and — despite the trend subengine's own FALLING reading and
the flow subengine reporting no acceleration — the combination layer produces a demand record at LOW-to-
MODERATE magnitude, capped confidence per the forward-reference-only ceiling (Part II §2.7), horizon LONG.
No allocation decision is made against it (the decision model's ranking table, Part IX §9.2, correctly ranks
it below the cycle's other live records given its thin, uncorroborated signal). At horizon close, six cycles
later, no mission has invoked the capability the forward reference named, and the trend has continued
falling rather than reversing: the record resolves REFUTED, exactly as the naive baseline (Part XI §11.7)
would have predicted from the trend alone. This is the correct, unremarkable outcome for a thin, single-
signal, uncorroborated forecast, and its inclusion here — alongside the cross-project reliability trace's
eventual success — is deliberate: a worked-examples section that shows only successful forecasts would
itself be an instance of the retrodiction-population bias Part XI §11.6 requires at least one deliberately
included known-negative case to guard against, now demonstrated at the illustrative-scenario level as well
as at the retrodiction-protocol level.

12.10 Reading the two traces side by side. The cross-project reliability trace (Part XII §12.2-12.8) and
the session-observability trace (Part XII §12.9) share an identical starting shape — a single forward-
reference subengine reading, unconfirmed by trend or flow — and diverge only in what the estate's later,
real mission history actually did. Neither trace was privileged by the combination layer at the moment of
forecast; each received exactly the confidence its corroboration and signal diversity earned, capped
identically per Part II §2.7's ceiling rule. The estate could not have known in advance which trace would
confirm and which would refute, and it should not have: a forecasting engine's honesty is not measured by
guessing right every time, it is measured by assigning confidence proportional to genuine evidence and
letting the world settle the rest. This is the composed scenario's second, quieter lesson, beyond Part XII
§12.8's loop-closing demonstration — the dataset's own epistemic discipline is not a claim that C2 always
succeeds, it is a claim that C2's stated confidence tracks its actual hit rate, which only a mixed
population of confirmed and refuted traces can demonstrate.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. A mechanism proven only in isolated fragments has not been proven to compose; this
scenario's value is not any single cycle's correctness but the visible closing of the loop — forecast,
allocation, foregone cost, confirmation, recalibration — across ten continuous cycles of one synthetic
trace.

===============================================================================================
PART XIII — EDGE CASES
===============================================================================================

13.1 The purpose of naming edge cases separately from failure modes. An edge case, as this dataset treats
the category, is a LEGITIMATE input configuration the mechanisms of Parts I-X must handle correctly
without producing a wrong answer — distinct from a FAILURE MODE (Part XV), which is a way the mechanism
itself goes wrong even on well-formed input. Naming edge cases here, ahead of adversarial cases (Part XIV)
and failure modes (Part XV), establishes the boundary of normal operation before the boundary of misuse
and the boundary of internal defect are addressed in turn.

13.2 Edge case: zero live demand records. At some allocation moment, the tracked-capability registry may
show no demand record above the threshold the decision model considers actionable — every tracked
capability sits at LOW magnitude or UNRESOLVED horizon. The decision model's correct behavior here is to
produce an EMPTY recommendation explicitly, not a forced ranking of low-value records dressed as
meaningful choices; a decision model that always produces a "top recommendation" regardless of whether
any candidate clears a minimum bar manufactures urgency where none exists, which Part XV §15.3 names as a
variant of the self-fulfilling-prophecy risk. The correct signal in this case is "no forecast-driven
allocation pressure this cycle," which is itself informative to IAS-C1 and should be logged as such.

13.3 Edge case: a demand record whose confidence never rises across its whole horizon. A forecast line may
persist at PROJECTED, low confidence, unchanged, across every cycle of its horizon, with no corroborating
subengine ever strengthening the initial forward-reference seed (Part III §3.5). This is not a defect in
the engine; a genuinely thin, uncorroborated signal that never gathers supporting evidence should indeed
remain low-confidence, and at horizon close it correctly resolves toward REFUTED or PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED
rather than being artificially promoted. The edge case to guard against is the opposite temptation: do not
let a stagnant-confidence record be quietly dropped from tracking before its horizon closes merely because
it looks unpromising, because dropping it early would remove it from the retrodiction population (Part XI
§11.4) and bias that population toward only the forecasts that looked good along the way.

13.4 Edge case: two demand records for the SAME underlying capability produced via different forecast
lines. Because the forward-reference subengine and the queue-recurrence subengine can each independently
seed a demand record for what is, on closer inspection, the same capability described in different
vocabulary by different sources, the combination layer (Part III §3.6) must include a DEDUPLICATION step
against the capability namespace before finalizing records for a cycle — resolved via the same knowledge-
location capability referenced in Part III §3.4, never rebuilt. Where deduplication is genuinely
ambiguous (two candidate records MIGHT be the same capability or might be two related-but-distinct ones),
C2 does not force a merge; it flags the pair as a CONTESTED capability-identity case and carries both
records forward separately, marked as contested, rather than either falsely merging two genuinely distinct
demands or falsely doubling the magnitude of one demand counted twice.

13.5 Edge case: a capacity projection with no historical throughput relationship to draw on. Part VII
§7.3 specified that shortfall computation estimates conservatively where the estate's own envelope-to-
throughput relationship is unavailable. The edge case this addresses directly is a BRAND NEW domain with
no operating history at all — the capacity planner cannot yet say how much mission volume a given envelope
size can actually serve in this domain, because no domain-specific throughput data exists yet. The correct
behavior is to mark the capacity projection for that domain UNRESOLVED (per F1 §4.6's UNRESOLVED-over-
guessed discipline, inherited throughout this dataset) rather than borrowing another domain's throughput
ratio as a stand-in, because domains differ enough in per-mission cost that a borrowed ratio would be a
guess wearing a number's authority.

13.6 Edge case: an opportunity cost record whose foregone demand record's forecast line is itself later
deduplicated or merged (Part XIII §13.4) after the opportunity cost record was opened. Because deduplication
can, in rare cases, resolve a previously-contested pair into a single merged forecast line after an
allocation decision has already opened an opportunity cost record against one of the two original lines,
C2 specifies that the opportunity cost record's foregone-value reference is updated to point at the merged
line's identity, carrying forward the pre-merge provisional estimate rather than recomputing it from
scratch, so that the ledger's aggregation (Part VI) does not silently lose the record's history at the
exact moment two forecast lines are found to have described one underlying reality.

13.7 Edge case: a scarcity warning where the sole provider is itself a demand-forecast-driven build
candidate, not yet an existing system. Part VIII's scarcity mechanism assumes, in its base case, a scarce
resource with an EXISTING sole provider. The edge case arises when the "provider" a set of demand records
depend on has not been built yet at all — the dependency is on a capability that is ITSELF still only a
PROJECTED demand record. Here, scarcity foresight correctly treats this as a compounded finding rather
than a standard scarcity warning: it is reported as a BUILD-PRIORITY AMPLIFIER attached to the not-yet-
built capability's own demand record (raising ITS priority in the decision model, per Part IX §9.2's
scarcity-dependent weighting) rather than as an independent scarcity warning about a resource that does
not yet exist to be scarce, since a resource cannot be scarce in the redundancy sense (Part VIII §8.6) if
it has zero providers rather than one — that is simply unmet demand, correctly handled by the ordinary
demand-and-shortfall mechanisms of Parts I and VII rather than by the scarcity mechanism's specific
provider-count logic.

13.8 Edge case: horizon close falling during an estate outage or extended pause. Where the estate's own
operational continuity is interrupted (a hosting outage, an extended Owner-driven pause across all missions)
spanning a demand record's horizon close, the retrodiction pass (Part XI) cannot run on schedule because the
signal state it would read is itself frozen mid-window rather than genuinely absent. The correct behavior is
to EXTEND the record's horizon by the outage's own measured duration before applying the forced-verdict rule
of Part X §10.5, rather than either forcing a premature terminal verdict against an artificially truncated
observation window or silently dropping the record from tracking — an outage-extended horizon is logged with
its own trigger (Part X §10.3: "horizon extended, cause: estate continuity interruption, duration: N cycles")
so the extension is itself auditable and cannot be confused with the discretionary horizon-widening Part XIV
§14.6 treats as an adversarial gaming pattern, because this widening names a verifiable external cause rather
than an unexplained one.

13.9 Edge case: a tracked capability renamed mid-cycle within the capability namespace. Where F1's own
capability namespace (governed outside C2, per Part XVIII's registry candidate) renames or re-scopes a
capability that already carries open forecast lines under its prior name, C2 does not treat this as a new,
unrelated capability entering EMERGENT demand from zero — doing so would sever the forecast line's own
trajectory (Part IV §4.6) and falsely reset a well-corroborated, multi-cycle forecast back to a thin,
single-cycle appearance. Instead, the forecast line's identifier is re-pointed to the renamed capability
while its full prior trajectory, contributing-signal history, and any open opportunity cost records
referencing it are carried forward unchanged, exactly mirroring the deduplication-merge carry-forward rule
of Part XIII §13.6 — a namespace rename is a relabeling of the SAME object, not the arrival of a different
one, and the forecasting engine's own signal counts (Part II) must be re-indexed against the new name rather
than restarted.

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. An edge case is not an excuse to guess; every boundary condition named here resolves
by falling back to the dataset's own already-stated disciplines — UNRESOLVED over guessed, contested over
falsely merged, logged-empty over manufactured urgency — because a mechanism that only behaves correctly on
the easy inputs has not actually been specified.

===============================================================================================
PART XIV — ADVERSARIAL CASES
===============================================================================================

14.1 The distinction from edge cases, restated for this Part's purpose. Where Part XIII addressed
legitimate inputs the mechanism must handle correctly, this Part addresses inputs or usage patterns that
are ADVERSARIAL — either a deliberate attempt to game the dataset's outputs for a narrow benefit, or an
unintentional but structurally exploitative usage pattern that the mechanism must be resistant to even
without malicious intent, because a system's design must survive both.

14.2 Adversarial case: gaming a forecast to justify a pre-existing allocation preference. Because a demand
record's forward-reference subengine (Part III §3.5) can be seeded by a sibling dataset's own forward
reference, a system or agent that WANTS a particular capability built could, in principle, engineer a
forward reference specifically to inflate that capability's forecast confidence, then point to the
resulting demand record as independent justification for an allocation it had already decided to make.
C2's defense is structural, not behavioral: the corroboration rule (Part III §3.6) caps confidence from a
forward-reference-only signal at MODERATE regardless of how many times the same underlying reference is
restated, and the retrodiction protocol (Part XI) evaluates whether forward-reference-seeded forecasts
actually resolve CONFIRMED at a rate above the naive baseline over time — a pattern of forward-reference-
seeded forecasts that consistently fail to confirm would itself surface, in the retrodiction population,
as evidence that this signal source (or a specific frequent source of forward references) is being gamed,
which is exactly the accountability mechanism Part XI §11.6 built for this purpose.

14.3 Adversarial case: gaming the opportunity cost ledger to inflate a domain's apparent priority. Because
the ledger's domain-aggregation view (Part VI §6.2) upweights domains with a persistent pattern of
foregone-then-confirmed demand, a party wishing to elevate a favored domain's future ranking could, in
principle, deliberately choose NOT to allocate to that domain in several consecutive cycles specifically
to manufacture a foregone-then-confirmed pattern, engineering the ledger's own upweighting mechanism
against itself. C2's defense is the settlement discipline of Part V §5.4: the pattern only accumulates
where the FOREGONE demand genuinely resolves CONFIRMED at horizon close via retrodiction-equivalent,
leakage-resistant evaluation (Part X §10.5's forced, evidence-based terminal state) — a domain
deliberately starved of allocation with no genuine underlying mission demand behind it will resolve those
records REFUTED, not CONFIRMED, because REFUTED versus CONFIRMED is determined by whether a REAL mission
actually arrived needing the capability, an outcome outside the gaming party's control. The gaming strategy
only "succeeds" if the underlying demand was genuine all along, in which case the resulting upweighting is
not gaming at all — it is the ledger correctly working, and Part VI §6.3's allocator-neutral framing
already removes the incentive to manufacture false scarcity for reputational gain, since the ledger
attributes no credit or blame to whoever made the original allocation choice.

14.4 Adversarial case: the imperial-boundary excursion, applied to demand forecasting specifically. F1
Part V §5.5 names the general imperial-boundary failure — a system claiming authority past the estate's
edge. C2's specific version of this risk is a decision model or capacity planner that begins forecasting
or "planning for" demand on the HOST MACHINE, the MODEL PROVIDER, or a PROJECT the estate does not govern,
reasoning by extension that "the estate's future capacity depends on the host's future capacity, so we
should forecast host capacity too." C2 explicitly refuses this extension: Part VII §7.2 grounds the
capacity projection in CO-08's own stated envelope and the Owner's own stated future changes to it,
NEVER in an independently-forecast prediction of host or provider behavior, because forecasting a resource
outside the estate's boundary is not capacity planning, it is presuming authority over an object F1's
Invariant Four (F1 §5.5) places explicitly out of bounds.

14.5 Adversarial case: weaponizing the "no per-allocator blame" discipline to avoid accountability for
consistently poor decisions. Part VI §6.3 deliberately builds the ledger to avoid attributing blame to
individual allocation decisions, for the legitimate reason stated there — decisions are judged against
information available at the time, not hindsight. An adversarial reading of this discipline would treat
it as a shield: "the ledger cannot judge me, so my allocation pattern is unreviewable." C2's response is
that the ledger's DOMAIN-level pattern (Part VI §6.2) remains fully visible and auditable even though its
PER-DECISION attribution is deliberately not converted into a blame score — a consistent pattern of
poorly-calibrated decisions is visible in the domain aggregation regardless of whose hand made each
individual choice, and that visibility is sufficient for the Owner or IAS-C1 to notice and act on a
pattern without C2 itself needing to build the per-allocator scoring mechanism that Part VI §6.3
deliberately declined to build. The discipline protects against misuse of raw attribution data; it does
not protect against the patterns the ledger is specifically designed to surface.

14.6 Adversarial case: horizon-shopping to keep a favored forecast perpetually PROJECTED. Because a
demand record's horizon can legitimately widen across cycles as new signal reshapes the combination layer's
horizon-reconciliation (Part III §3.6's third rule), a party could attempt to keep a favored forecast
line's horizon perpetually extending outward each cycle specifically to avoid ever reaching the forced-
verdict rule of Part X §10.5 — a forecast that is never allowed to be checked against reality. C2's
defense is that horizon changes are themselves logged as lifecycle transitions with named triggers (Part
X §10.3), and a forecast line whose horizon widens repeatedly WITHOUT new corroborating subengine evidence
justifying the widening (Part III §3.6's rule requires the widening to be evidence-driven, not
discretionary) is itself a detectable pattern — a widening trigger that names no new corroborating signal
is, by the trigger-discipline of Part X §10.3, an invalid transition, and Part XVI's forecast-accuracy
metric explicitly tracks horizon-widening-without-corroboration as a named gaming signature to watch for.

14.7 Adversarial case: coordinated multi-sibling forward-reference collusion. Where several sibling IAS
datasets, in the course of ordinary cross-referencing, might independently cite the same capability gap
(the legitimate pattern the corroboration rule, Part III §3.6, is built to reward), an adversarial variant
would have those references coordinated deliberately — not independently arrived at — specifically to
simulate the appearance of diverse corroboration and push a demand record's confidence upward through
Part III §3.6's corroboration boost. C2's structural defense is that the corroboration rule counts DIVERSE
SIGNAL CLASSES (Part III §3.6, restated in Part XV §15.4's anti-monoculture discipline), not diverse
citation COUNT within the single forward-reference class — multiple sibling citations, however many,
remain ONE signal class (Signal Four, Part II §2.5) and are capped at the same MODERATE ceiling regardless
of how many datasets echo the same underlying reference, so coordinated citation volume within one class
produces no confidence gain a single honest citation would not already produce. The only way to genuinely
raise such a record's confidence is for an INDEPENDENT signal class — trend, flow, or queue-recurrence — to
corroborate it, and none of those three is under any sibling dataset's authorial control.

14.8 Adversarial case: exploiting the null-foregone rule to suppress an inconvenient opportunity cost
record. Part XV §15.6 specified that a genuinely uncontested allocation (no second live demand record)
correctly produces a NULL foregone-value field rather than a fabricated one. An adversarial variant would
have a party artificially suppress or delay OTHER live demand records from entering the decision model's
ranking table at the moment of an allocation decision — not by gaming a forecast's confidence upward, as in
Part XIV §14.2, but by preventing a genuine competing record from being considered at all — specifically to
manufacture the appearance of an uncontested allocation and avoid an opportunity cost record being opened
against a real, inconvenient alternative. C2's defense is that the decision model's ranking table (Part IX
§9.2) is required to read ALL live demand records above the actionable threshold at the moment of decision,
not a curated subset, and the recommendation record (Part IX §9.4) itself becomes the audit artifact: a
recommendation that lists only one candidate where the tracked-capability registry (Part III §3.9) and the
forecasting engine's own most recent cycle output show other live, actionable records is a detectable
mismatch between the registry's own state and the recommendation's stated input, exactly the kind of
cross-referenced audit trail Part X §10.3's trigger discipline exists to make checkable.

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. Every adversarial case this Part names resolves the same way: not by adding a new
rule to catch the specific gaming attempt, but by observing that the dataset's existing evidence,
leakage, and attribution disciplines already remove the payoff from gaming, because a forecast can only be
gamed toward a resolution the real, later mission history does not actually control.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — FAILURE MODES
===============================================================================================

15.1 The distinction from edge and adversarial cases, once more, for precision. A failure mode, as this
Part treats the category, is a way the mechanism goes wrong WITHOUT any adversarial intent and WITHOUT any
unusual edge-case input — a defect that can arise from ordinary, well-intentioned operation of the engine
exactly as specified, if a specific discipline named earlier in this dataset is allowed to lapse. Naming
these explicitly, and naming which earlier Part's discipline prevents each one, is this dataset's own
internal accountability mechanism.

15.2 Failure mode: OVER-FITTING PAST DEMAND. The forecasting engine, run over a long enough mission
history, can begin to fit its trend subengine ever more tightly to historical noise rather than to genuine
signal — treating a random cluster of invocations in one trailing window as a meaningful trend rather than
statistical variance, especially in domains with low absolute invocation counts where noise and signal are
hardest to distinguish. This is the precise failure Part III §3.2 named preventively ("model complexity is
bounded by data volume, not by what is technically available") and Part XI §11.7's naive-baseline
comparison is the empirical check: an engine that has over-fit past demand will show a retrodiction rate
that does NOT meaningfully exceed the naive baseline, because over-fitting produces confident-looking
forecasts that do not actually out-predict a simple trailing average — they merely describe the past more
elaborately. The diagnostic signature is a forecasting engine whose CONFIDENCE bands are consistently
tight and high even in domains with thin absolute history (Part II §2.2's observation that mission history
is weakest precisely where it has the least data), which is itself a symptom worth monitoring
independently of the retrodiction population, because it can surface before enough vantage points have
accumulated to run a full retrodiction check.

15.3 Failure mode: FORECAST AS SELF-FULFILLING PROPHECY. Because a PRE-POSITIONED demand record
(Part X §10.2) triggers real resource commitment, and because IAS-A1's routing (Part X §10.7) preferentially
surfaces pre-positioned capabilities to missions, a demand forecast that leads to a capability being built
can itself CAUSE the very demand it predicted, simply by making the capability newly available and
therefore newly invocable — a mission that would never have specifically sought this capability may use it
opportunistically once it exists, inflating the apparent "confirmation" of the original forecast without
that confirmation reflecting genuine independent demand. This is the single most philosophically subtle
failure mode in the dataset, because it can make a forecast appear MORE validated than it actually is,
in exactly the direction the dataset's own incentives would prefer, making it the hardest failure mode to
self-detect. C2's partial defense is the retrodiction protocol's own structure (Part XI): retrodiction
vantage points chosen BEFORE a capability was pre-positioned test the engine's PURE predictive power, prior
to any self-fulfillment effect, because the capability did not yet exist to be opportunistically invoked at
that historical point — but retrodiction cannot fully solve the LIVE case, where a currently-PROJECTED
forecast might already be shaping present behavior in anticipation. C2 names this residual risk explicitly
as CONTESTED and UNRESOLVED rather than claiming a complete defense: the dataset's own forward-looking
validation is structurally weaker against self-fulfillment than its historical retrodiction is, and any
consumer of a demand record's confidence should discount it accordingly for capabilities already
pre-positioned versus those still purely PROJECTED and unbuilt.

15.4 Failure mode: SIGNAL MONOCULTURE. Where one signal source (most often mission history, the most
abundant and cheapest to compute) comes to dominate the combination layer's output simply because it is
easiest to compute at scale, the other four signal sources (Part II §2.3-2.6) atrophy from disuse rather
than from being genuinely less valuable — the flow subengine's earlier-detection advantage (Part III §3.3)
and the forward-reference subengine's emergent-demand coverage (Part III §3.5) are specifically the
capabilities lost when this atrophy occurs, because a monoculture forecasting engine reverts to trend-only
behavior and loses its ability to catch bending curves or genuinely novel demand early. The corroboration
rule's own structure (Part III §3.6) is the preventive discipline: because confidence is capped, not
raised, by a single dominant signal source acting alone, an engine drifting toward monoculture will show
FLAT, capped-MODERATE confidence across most of its output rather than the differentiated, trajectory-
rich confidence pattern of Part IV §4.7's worked example — a detectable signature distinct from genuine,
well-corroborated high confidence.

15.5 Failure mode: FORECAST THEATER. A forecasting cycle that runs, produces demand records, and is filed
without ever being consumed by the decision model, without ever informing an actual allocation, and
without ever being checked at retrodiction time, is FORECAST THEATER — activity that has the form of
demand forecasting without its function. This is the direct institutional-scale analogue of the estate's
own Scaffold Illusion discipline (Reality Contract, Mistake #16), applied to a forecasting system
specifically: a beautifully-structured demand record schema that nobody downstream ever reads is
indistinguishable, in its effect on the estate, from no forecasting engine at all, and considerably worse
than none in its effect on institutional trust once discovered. The defense is not a mechanism internal to
C2 but a CONSUMPTION AUDIT: Part XVII's evaluation protocol explicitly measures the RATE at which produced
demand records are actually read by the decision model and acted upon (pre-positioned, per Part X §10.4's
latency benchmark) versus the rate at which they simply expire unread, and a persistently low consumption
rate is itself the theater signature, regardless of how accurate the unconsumed forecasts individually
turn out to be.

15.6 Failure mode: OPPORTUNITY COST WITHOUT A GENUINE RANKED ALTERNATIVE. Part V §5.3 required opportunity
cost to be computed against the highest-ranked NOT-chosen live demand record. The failure mode arises when,
at a given allocation moment, there is genuinely only ONE live demand record above the actionable threshold
(Part XIII §13.2's edge case, in a degenerate form) and a naive implementation nonetheless manufactures an
opportunity cost figure against some arbitrarily-constructed "next-best" baseline rather than correctly
reporting that no genuine ranked alternative existed this cycle. C2's defense is definitional: Part V §5.6's
opportunity cost record schema requires naming the SPECIFIC foregone forecast line by identifier, and where
no second live record exists, the correct output is an opportunity cost record with an explicit NULL
foregone-value field, never an invented figure standing in for a genuine value — the same UNRESOLVED-over-
guessed discipline inherited once more from F1 §4.6, now applied to the specific case of a genuinely
uncontested allocation.

15.7 Failure mode: THRESHOLD FOSSILIZATION. The simulation threshold (Part XVIII §18.4), the reliability
classes (Part II §2.7), and the decision model's ranking weights (Part IX §9.2) are all specified as fixed
parameters in this dataset's own text, revisable only through Article IX's governed self-evolution path
(Part XVIII §18.5). The failure mode this invites, absent active discipline, is not a wrong-at-authoring
parameter but a RIGHT-AT-AUTHORING parameter that becomes wrong as the estate's own mission mix, history
volume, and capability namespace evolve, while nobody ever proposes the revision Article IX permits — the
threshold simply fossilizes, unreviewed, because no single failure it causes is dramatic enough on its own
to trigger a proposal, and gradual drift rarely produces the sharp signal that prompts a fix. This differs
from the other five failure modes in this Part in one respect: it is a failure of OMISSION rather than of an
active mechanism misbehaving, and its only defense is procedural rather than structural — Part XVII's
evaluation protocol, run on its own stated cadence (never skipped merely because recent results looked
adequate), is the mechanism that surfaces the drift by re-measuring the same benchmarks against the estate's
current state rather than assuming an old evaluation still holds, and a revision proposal citing a
benchmark's own DECLINING trend across successive evaluation runs — even where each individual run still
clears its threshold — is exactly the evidence Article IX requires and threshold fossilization otherwise
never generates on its own.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. Six failure modes, and every one of them fails the SAME way underneath its surface
difference: a discipline named earlier in this dataset — bounded complexity, blind retrodiction, corroboration
capping, consumption audit, the null-over-fabricated rule, or the evaluation cadence itself — was allowed to lapse, and a forecasting
system's honesty is measured not by whether these lapses can occur but by whether the system can detect its
own lapse before an allocation decision is made on the strength of it.

===============================================================================================
PART XVI — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

16.1 Why metrics come after failure modes in this dataset's ordering. Unlike a dataset that defines its
metrics first and hopes they resist gaming, C2 deliberately specifies its failure modes (Part XV) and
adversarial cases (Part XIV) BEFORE its metrics, so that every metric named here can be built with an
explicit answer to "how would this metric be gamed, and by which of the failure modes already catalogued."
This ordering is itself a NORMATIVE choice: a metric designed without first cataloguing its failure modes
is a metric designed to look good, not to measure well.

16.2 Metric One — FORECAST ACCURACY. FORECAST ACCURACY, DEFINED, is the fraction of terminal-state demand
records (Part X §10.5) that resolve CONFIRMED or PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED, computed over a trailing population
of forecast lines whose horizons have closed, reported ALWAYS alongside its naive-baseline comparator
(Part XI §11.7) and never as a bare percentage. Forecast accuracy is the dataset's headline metric because
it is the one the Matrix's evidence-required column names directly. Its anti-Goodhart countermetric is the
OVER-FITTING CHECK of Part XV §15.2: forecast accuracy reported without a simultaneous check on whether
confidence bands are appropriately WIDE in low-history domains is gameable by simply narrowing confidence
bands across the board (a narrower band is easier to "match" loosely) — the countermetric therefore pairs
raw accuracy with a CALIBRATION check: among records reported at a given confidence band, does the actual
CONFIRMED rate for that band roughly match the band's own stated strength, so that a MODERATE-confidence
record resolving CONFIRMED only as often as a LOW-confidence one would flag mis-calibration even where raw
accuracy looks acceptable.

16.3 Metric Two — CONSUMPTION RATE. CONSUMPTION RATE, DEFINED, is the fraction of produced demand records
that are actually read and acted upon by the decision model (entering PRE-POSITIONED, per Part X §10.4) or
explicitly overridden with a logged reason (Part IX §9.5), versus the fraction that simply expire unread —
the direct countermetric to forecast theater (Part XV §15.5). A HIGH forecast-accuracy figure paired with a
LOW consumption rate is not a success story; it means the engine forecasts well but the estate is not
listening, and C2 treats this combination as a distinct, nameable finding in its own evaluation output
(Part XVII), not as an accuracy achievement to celebrate in isolation.

16.4 Metric Three — LEAD TIME. LEAD TIME, DEFINED, is the latency (Part X §10.4) between a forecast line
first reaching actionable confidence and magnitude and the moment it is actually pre-positioned, measured
against the ALTERNATIVE latency a purely reactive system (one with no forecasting engine at all, that only
notices demand once CO-12 logs a gap or backlog_autopilot files an item) would have achieved. A positive
lead time — pre-positioning meaningfully BEFORE a reactive system would have noticed — is the concrete,
measurable form of the Matrix's expected gain ("pre-built capability before the mission needs it"). Its
countermetric is the SELF-FULFILLMENT DISCOUNT of Part XV §15.3: lead time computed without distinguishing
genuinely-anticipated demand from demand the pre-positioning itself induced overstates the engine's
foresight, so lead time is reported ALONGSIDE the fraction of its confirming missions that plausibly
pre-date any awareness the capability had been pre-positioned (a rough, honestly-labeled proxy, not a
precise measurement, per the false-precision discipline running throughout this dataset).

16.5 Metric Four — OPPORTUNITY COST SETTLEMENT ACCURACY. Measures how often a PROVISIONAL opportunity cost
estimate (Part V §5.4) at decision time turns out, at settlement, to have been directionally correct — did
a HIGH-provisional-cost foregone record indeed settle high, did a LOW-provisional-cost one indeed settle
low — distinct from forecast accuracy itself because a forecast can resolve correctly on magnitude while
the opportunity cost narrative built around it (which alternative was truly second-best, Part V §5.3) is
separately right or wrong. Its countermetric is DOMAIN CONCENTRATION: a settlement-accuracy figure that
looks strong only because it is drawn overwhelmingly from one or two domains (Part VI §6.2) is not
evidence the mechanism generalizes; the metric is reported per-domain, never pooled into one estate-wide
figure, precisely mirroring the ledger's own refusal (Part VI §6.4) to collapse ordinal magnitudes into a
false-precision total.

16.6 Metric Five — SHORTFALL AND SCARCITY WARNING LEAD TIME. Parallel to Metric Three but specific to
Parts VII-VIII: how far in advance of an actually-observed capacity strain (a real mission blocked or
degraded by insufficient capacity, or a real single-provider outage) did the capacity planner's shortfall
record or the scarcity warning correctly flag the coming constraint. Its countermetric is FALSE-ALARM
RATE: a shortfall or scarcity mechanism tuned to maximize lead time by flagging aggressively will also
flag constraints that never actually bind, and lead time reported without a paired false-alarm rate
rewards exactly the over-eager flagging the provider-count discipline of Part VIII §8.6 was built to
prevent.

16.7 Why no single composite score is defined across these six metrics. Consistent with Part IX §9.3's
deliberate refusal to collapse the decision model's ranking inputs into one number, this dataset likewise
refuses to define a single composite "C2 health score" across its six metrics. Each metric answers a
different operational question (is the engine accurate, is the estate listening, is it ahead of reactive
systems, is its economic narrative right, is its early-warning system trustworthy, is it drawing on diverse
evidence), and F2's own Fusion Score precedent (F2 Part V) — a deliberately qualitative, non-collapsed
composite for exactly this reason — is the pattern C2 follows rather than departs from: where F2 needed a
five-dimension Fusion Score because averaging would hide which dimension is weak, C2's six metrics remain
visibly separate for the identical reason, reported together but never summed.

16.8 Metric Six — SIGNAL-CLASS DIVERSITY INDEX. SIGNAL-CLASS DIVERSITY INDEX, DEFINED, is the fraction of a
trailing population of demand records whose confidence was corroborated by two or more of the five named
signal classes (Part II), as opposed to resting on a single class alone, computed per cycle and tracked
across cycles as its own trend line. This is the metric named but not separately specified in Part XV
§15.4's failure-mode discussion of signal monoculture, formalized here as a standing measurement rather than
only a diagnostic narrative. A diversity index that is HIGH and stable indicates the corroboration rule
(Part III §3.6) is functioning as designed; a diversity index that DECLINES over successive cycles, even
while forecast accuracy (Metric One) remains superficially acceptable, is the earliest available warning of
the monoculture failure mode, because accuracy computed against a population increasingly dominated by the
cheapest-to-compute signal (mission history) can remain stable for many cycles before the monoculture's cost
— lost early detection on bending curves, lost emergent-demand coverage — becomes visible in accuracy figures
at all. Its countermetric is DIVERSITY-WITHOUT-QUALITY: a diversity index can be gamed upward by an engine
that trivially seeks weak, low-value corroboration from additional signal classes regardless of whether that
corroboration is genuinely informative, so the diversity index is reported alongside the CEILING RULE's own
distribution (Part II §2.7) — a healthy diversity gain shows rising confidence bands moving proportionately
with genuine signal strength, not merely more signal classes checked as present without materially moving
the confidence figure.

16.9 Metric reporting cadence, and its relationship to the evaluation protocol. All six metrics of this Part
are computed on the SAME reporting cadence as the evaluation protocol (Part XVII §17.9), not on the
forecasting engine's own faster cycle cadence (Part III §3.8), for the identical reason: a metric
re-computed every forecasting cycle mostly reports noise between adjacent windows rather than a meaningful
trend. Each metric's reported value therefore always carries its own trailing-window boundaries alongside
the number itself, so that two metric reports from different cadence runs are never silently compared as if
drawn from directly adjacent evidence when in fact the intervening cycles supplied additional retrodiction
and consumption data neither report captured in isolation. (NORMATIVE: this is the same trajectory-over-
single-value discipline Part IV §4.6 and Part XVII §17.9 both already apply — a metric read once is a
snapshot; a metric read across successive reporting cadences is the trend line the estate's own governed
self-evolution (Part XVIII §18.5) actually needs before proposing a revision.)

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. Every metric in this Part is reported with its own named countermetric in the same
breath, because a metric published alone is a target waiting to be gamed by exactly the failure mode Part
XV already catalogued for it, and this dataset does not get to invoke that catalogue for everyone else's
metrics while leaving its own exposed.

PART XVII — EVALUATION: HOW TO PROVE IT WORKS, AND BENCHMARKS
===============================================================================================

17.1 The evaluation protocol's shape. Proving C2 works, in the sense Article XI requires ("SEALED only
when strengthening evidence exists"), is not a single test; it is a PROTOCOL combining the retrodiction
population (Part XI), the five metrics with their countermetrics (Part XVI), and a specific set of named
benchmarks this Part supplies, run together and reported as one evaluation artifact rather than as
scattered individual numbers.

17.2 Benchmark One — beat the naive baseline on forecast accuracy, across a population, not a single
trace. Already specified mechanically in Part XI §11.7; restated here as the evaluation protocol's FIRST
gate, because every other benchmark in this Part is conditional on clearing this one — a forecasting
engine that cannot beat a trivial trend-extrapolation baseline has not earned the complexity of the
remaining benchmarks, and the evaluation protocol reports a VALIDATED / INCONCLUSIVE / REFUTED verdict
(Part XI §11.5) as its own first line, before any other number.

17.3 Benchmark Two — positive lead time on at least one CONFIRMED forecast line, with the self-fulfillment
discount named. Where Benchmark One clears, the evaluation protocol reports at least one worked or
observed forecast line (structurally, the shape of Part X §10.8's transcript) showing pre-positioning that
preceded the confirming mission by a meaningfully positive lead time, with the self-fulfillment discount
of Part XVI §16.4 stated explicitly rather than omitted — a lead-time claim without this discount named is
not a complete benchmark result under this dataset's own discipline.

17.4 Benchmark Three — a shared evaluation with backlog_autopilot: declining surprise-arrival rate. Part
I §1.5 named this as C2's success condition relative to its most directly overlapping absorbed candidate.
The shared evaluation reads backlog_autopilot's own record of item-arrival "surprise" (an item filed with
no prior demand record naming its capability domain at any confidence above threshold) across a trailing
period, and compares the surprise rate BEFORE C2's forecasting engine was live against the rate AFTER —
a declining surprise-arrival rate, read directly from backlog_autopilot's own data rather than from C2's
internal records, is the specific cross-system evidence artifact this benchmark supplies, and it is
significant precisely because it is measured by the STRENGTHENED system's own instrumentation, not by C2
grading its own work.

17.5 Benchmark Four — a shared evaluation with CO-12: declining recurrence of the same gap. Similarly,
CO-12's gap radar logs gaps as they are found; a gap that recurs multiple times for the same underlying
capability across the estate's mission flow (Part I §1.6) is exactly the pattern a working demand forecast
should intercept before a third or fourth recurrence. The benchmark reads CO-12's own gap log for repeat-
gap incidents before and after C2's engine is live, again measured on the strengthened parent's own
instrumentation.

17.6 Benchmark Five — a shared evaluation with owner_queue: fewer CRITICAL-priority escalations
attributable to unanticipated capability absence. Where owner_queue's own priority-tagging identifies an
escalation as driven by "we needed X and it wasn't available," a declining rate of this specific
escalation cause, measured on owner_queue's own tagged data, is the third and final shared-instrumentation
benchmark, completing evidence against three of the four Matrix-named parents (CO-12, backlog_autopilot,
owner_queue) via each parent's own record rather than via C2's self-report.

17.7 Benchmark Six — liveness. The fourth Matrix-named parent, liveness, is strengthened differently in
kind rather than in the same before/after pattern as the other three: liveness's own job is distinguishing
genuinely active work from stalled or unbounded sessions (per the estate's own Process Hibernation
governance), and C2 strengthens it by supplying a legitimate EXPLANATION for a session that appears to be
running long or producing output without an obvious immediate mission driver — a forecasting-engine cycle
or a retrodiction pass legitimately runs on its own cadence, not tied to any single live mission, and
liveness's own advisory-only discipline (HR-STALLED-SESSION-ADVISORY-001, referenced by name because it is
a sealed estate rule C2 must not contradict) benefits from C2 declaring its own expected cadence
explicitly, so that a long-running forecasting cycle is correctly read as scheduled institutional work
rather than as an anomalous stall. This benchmark is evaluated qualitatively — does liveness's advisory
surface correctly distinguish a C2 cadence run from a genuine stall, checked by inspection of a handful of
logged C2 cycles against liveness's own advisory output — rather than by a numeric before/after comparison,
because the strengthening mechanism here is informational rather than volumetric.

17.8 Why no single "C2 works" verdict is issued, consistent with Part XVI §16.7. The evaluation protocol
reports six benchmark results — one internal (forecast accuracy vs. naive baseline) and five relative to
named parents' own instrumentation — as a TABLE, not collapsed into a pass/fail composite, for the
identical reason Part XVI declined a composite health score: a reader who wants to know specifically
whether C2 has actually reduced owner_queue's escalation burden should be able to read that one row
without it being diluted by a strong result on, say, liveness's qualitative benchmark. Article XI's
"SEALED only when strengthening evidence exists" is satisfied when the table shows, for EACH of the four
Matrix-named parents, at least one benchmark result at VALIDATED or the honest INCONCLUSIVE-with-stated-
sample-size reading of Part XI §11.8 — never a claim of blanket success across all six rows
simultaneously, which this dataset's own epistemic discipline would treat as itself suspicious.

17.9 Evaluation report cadence and cross-cycle comparability. The six-benchmark table (Part XVII §17.2-17.7)
is not a one-time artifact; it is produced on its own stated CADENCE — deliberately decoupled from the
forecasting engine's own, faster cadence (Part III §3.8), because a benchmark table re-run every forecasting
cycle would mostly show noise between adjacent evaluations, the same wasted-attention risk Part III §3.8
already names for an overly frequent forecasting cadence, now applied to evaluation itself. Each evaluation
report carries its own timestamp and the trailing window it was computed over, and successive reports are
read as a TRAJECTORY — a declining Benchmark Three surprise-arrival rate across three successive evaluation
reports is meaningfully stronger evidence than the same rate shown once, mirroring the exact trajectory
discipline Part IV §4.6 already established for individual demand records, now applied at the evaluation-
protocol level. A single evaluation report, read in isolation, supports only the same INCONCLUSIVE-LEANING
reading Part XI §11.8 modeled for a thin retrodiction population; the evaluation protocol's own claim to
strengthen the Matrix's four named parents (Article XI's SEALED bar) is only fully earned once a
multi-report trajectory exists to show the benchmarks holding or improving over successive, independently
computed evaluation cycles, not merely clearing their thresholds once.

17.10 Who reads the evaluation table, and what action each reader takes from it. The six-benchmark table
(Part XVII §17.2-17.7) has three distinct readers, each drawing a different conclusion from the same
artifact. The OWNER reads it to decide whether Article XI's "SEALED only when strengthening evidence
exists" bar has been cleared for each of the four Matrix-named parents, and to weigh any Article IX revision
proposal (Part XVIII §18.5) against the specific benchmark row that proposal cites as its evidence. IAS-C1
reads Benchmark One and Benchmark Two (forecast accuracy and lead time) as a standing input to how much
weight its own allocation process should place on C2's recommendation (Part IX §9.4) relative to its own
F2-based apparatus (Part XVIII §18.3) — a C2 whose accuracy benchmark is trending down should, correctly,
see its recommendation weighted down by IAS-C1 even absent any explicit governance action, because the
recommendation's own credibility is what IAS-C1 is entitled to judge for itself. The four strengthened
parents themselves (CO-12, backlog_autopilot, owner_queue, liveness) read only their OWN benchmark row —
Benchmark Four, Three, Five, and Six respectively — because each system's own legitimate interest is
whether C2 has actually reduced its burden, not whether C2's engine is accurate in the abstract, which is
precisely why Part XVII §17.8 refuses a collapsing composite: a single blended score would be unreadable to
a parent system that only needs to know its own row.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. Proof is a table of six benchmarks, four of them read from the STRENGTHENED
parents' own instrumentation rather than from C2's self-report, reported without a collapsing composite —
because a dataset that grades its own homework has not proven anything, and a dataset that only shows one
number has hidden which of its four parents it actually helped.

PART XVIII — INTEGRATION WITH THE FORESIGHT LOOP: F3 SIMULATE-BEFORE-BUILD, C1 PRE-ALLOCATE; EVOLUTION;
OPEN QUESTIONS
===============================================================================================

18.1 The foresight loop's two downstream edges, specified precisely. The Compounding Graph names two
consumers of C2's forecast output directly: "IAS-F3 Digital Twin simulates whether building them pays
off" and "IAS-C1 Portfolio pre-allocates to the winners." This Part specifies the INTERFACE each
integration crosses — what C2 hands across the edge, in what format, and what each downstream system is
expected to do with it — because the Compounding Graph names the relationship but, correctly, leaves each
dataset to specify its own side of the interface.

18.2 The F3 interface: what C2 hands the Digital Twin, and why simulation comes BEFORE pre-allocation in
the loop's stated order. A demand record reaching PROJECTED state with sufficient confidence (Part IV) and,
where relevant, an associated shortfall record (Part VII) or scarcity warning (Part VIII), is handed to
IAS-F3 as a CANDIDATE FOR SIMULATION — a request, not a command, for F3 to model the counterfactual
"if the estate builds this capability now, does the projected mission flow actually materialize enough to
justify the build cost, and what is the simulated blast radius of getting the forecast wrong." C2 does not
perform this simulation itself (that is explicitly F3's distinct object, per the Ledger's routing of
simulation/forecasting candidates 101-110 to IAS-F3, not to C2) — C2's contribution to the interface is
strictly the demand record and its associated economic context (the opportunity cost preview of Part IX
§9.4, the capacity posture of Part VII §7.5), all of which F3 needs as INPUT to its own simulation but
none of which C2 computes on F3's behalf. This ordering — forecast, then simulate, then allocate — is
DERIVED directly from the risk asymmetry involved: pre-allocating before simulating spends real capital on
an untested counterfactual, while simulating before pre-allocating is comparatively cheap, which is
precisely why the Compounding Graph places F3 between C2's forecast and C1's allocation rather than
routing C2's output directly to C1.

18.3 The C1 interface: what C2 hands the Portfolio, post-simulation. Once a candidate has been simulated
(or, for lower-stakes, lower-magnitude demand records where a full simulation is not warranted — Part
XVIII §18.4 addresses this threshold), C2's decision model's RECOMMENDATION (Part IX §9.4), carrying its
ranked table and opportunity cost preview, is the artifact handed to IAS-C1. IAS-C1 is expected to read
this recommendation alongside its own Advantage Score and Fusion Score apparatus (F2) for the SAME
candidate capabilities — C2 supplies the forward-looking DEMAND side of the portfolio equation; IAS-C1's
own F2-based apparatus supplies the backward-looking ADVANTAGE side for capabilities that already exist
in some form; the two are complementary inputs to one allocation decision that neither dataset makes
alone.

18.4 The simulation threshold: not every demand record warrants a full F3 pass. Running a full digital-
twin simulation (IAS-F3's own object, presumably itself resource-intensive) for every LOW-magnitude,
long-horizon demand record would be disproportionate to the decision at stake, and C2 specifies a
THRESHOLD, expressed in its own terms rather than presuming to specify F3's internal cost model: demand
records at HIGH or CRITICAL magnitude, or any record carrying a scarcity warning (Part VIII), are
candidates for full simulation before pre-allocation; MODERATE-and-below magnitude records without a
scarcity flag are handed directly to IAS-C1's ordinary portfolio process without a mandatory simulation
step, though F3 remains free to simulate any candidate it independently judges worth the cost. (NORMATIVE:
this threshold is a recommendation C2 makes about ITS OWN output's stakes, not a constraint on F3's own
authority to decide what it simulates.)

18.5 Governed self-evolution, per Article IX. This dataset's own forecasting engine, decision model
weights, and thresholds (the ranking weights of Part IX §9.2, the simulation threshold of Part XVIII §18.4,
the reliability classes of Part II §2.7) are themselves candidates for revision as the retrodiction
population (Part XI) and the evaluation benchmarks (Part XVII) accumulate more evidence over the estate's
life. Per Article IX, C2 does not self-modify these parameters autonomously; it PROPOSES a revision —
naming the specific weight or threshold, the evidence (a benchmark result or a retrodiction-population
shift) motivating the change, and the expected effect — and the Owner promotes or declines the proposal,
exactly as the Constitution requires for any IAS system's self-evolution. A revision proposal that cannot
name specific evidence from Part XI or Part XVII is not eligible for Owner consideration under this
discipline; it is treated as an unsubstantiated preference, not a proposal.

18.6 Open question, UNRESOLVED: how many vantage points are enough. Part XI §11.4 required MULTIPLE
vantage points before a retrodiction verdict is trusted, and Part XI §11.8's worked example showed five
vantage points producing only an INCONCLUSIVE-LEANING-POSITIVE reading. This dataset does not fix a
numeric threshold for "enough" vantage points, because the correct threshold depends on the estate's own
mission-history volume and variance, which will differ by domain and will grow over the estate's life;
fixing a number now would either be too permissive for a thin-history estate or unnecessarily conservative
for a mature one. This is left UNRESOLVED, to be addressed empirically as the retrodiction population
itself grows, rather than fixed by assertion.

18.7 Open question, UNRESOLVED: the residual self-fulfillment problem. Part XV §15.3 named the forecast-
as-self-fulfilling-prophecy failure mode and offered a partial defense (retrodiction on pre-existing
history is immune to it; live, currently-PROJECTED forecasts are not fully immune). This dataset does not
claim to have solved this residual risk; it is named honestly as the family's own open frontier, and any
future revision to C2 that claims to have closed it must do so with a specific, checkable mechanism, not
merely an assertion that the risk has been "considered."

18.8 Open question, CONTESTED: whether opportunity cost should ever inform F1's authority field. Part IX
§9.6's override logging (Part IX §9.5) raises, but does not resolve, whether a domain with a long,
consistent pattern of overridden top recommendations (Part IX §9.5) should eventually shift AUTHORITY
(F1 §4.2, §5.4) over that specific decision class away from advisory-C2-informed and toward some other
configuration. This is CONTESTED rather than resolved here because it touches F1's Invariant Three
(singular authority per decision class) and is, properly, a question for F1's own stewardship or for
IAS-A2's arbitration mechanism to adjudicate, not a decision C2 is positioned to make unilaterally about
its own authority relative to IAS-C1's.

18.9 Worked micro-example: the F3 handoff artifact, concretely. Returning to the governance-automation
shortfall record of Part VII §7.7: at the moment this record's HIGH magnitude and CONSTRAINED posture cross
the simulation threshold of Part XVIII §18.4, C2's handoff to IAS-F3 consists of exactly three named
artifacts, bundled but not merged — the demand record itself (Part IV §4.2, capability, magnitude, confidence,
horizon, signal provenance, lifecycle state), the shortfall record naming the specific contributing demand
records and the capacity projection it exceeds (Part VII §7.3), and the opportunity cost preview from the
decision model's most recent recommendation for this capability, where one exists (Part IX §9.4). F3 is
specified to treat all three as READ-ONLY inputs to its own simulation object — it does not edit or annotate
C2's records in place, and any simulation-derived finding F3 wishes to feed back toward C2 (for instance, a
simulated blast radius suggesting the shortfall's true magnitude is understated) travels back across the
interface as F3's OWN output, consumed by C2's next forecasting cycle as an additional signal candidate for
future taxonomy consideration (Part II), not as a retroactive edit to the original demand record's own
history — preserving, at the interface boundary, the identical read-then-append discipline Part IV §4.9 and
Part XIII §13.6 already apply within C2's own internal schema evolution.

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. C2 hands the foresight loop exactly two things — a demand record for F3 to
simulate and a ranked recommendation for C1 to weigh — and it evolves only by proposing evidenced
revisions to the Owner, because a forecasting system that quietly tunes its own weights in response to its
own retrodiction results has stopped being a forecast and started being a system that predicts whatever
makes its own last prediction look right.

D1. DECISION: Route Candidates 24 (Opportunity Cost) and 27 (Demand Forecasting) into ONE dataset rather
than sealing Opportunity Cost as an independent white-space dataset. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) seal
Opportunity Cost standalone, since the Ledger names it with no overlapping parent at all; (b) fold
Opportunity Cost as a subengine of IAS-C1 (Portfolio), since portfolio allocation is the most natural
consumer of an opportunity-cost figure. WHY THIS: opportunity cost is not computable without a
forward-looking claim about foregone value, and the only object in the corpus that produces such a claim
is the demand forecast this same dataset builds (Part V §5.1) — building it standalone would have forced
either a duplicate forecasting mechanism inside the "standalone" Opportunity Cost dataset or an
undeclared dependency on C2 from outside C2's own boundary. TRADE-OFFS: this dataset is now measurably
larger and covers two conceptually distinct mechanisms (forecasting and economic valuation) under one
roof, at some cost to focus. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if opportunity cost's usage patterns diverge sharply
from demand forecasting's own cadence and consumers (for instance, if IAS-C1 comes to need opportunity
cost computed far more frequently than forecasting cycles run), a future SPLIT into two datasets sharing
the demand-record schema (Part IV) as a common interface would be the corrective action, not a rebuild.

D2. DECISION: Fold Capacity Planning (Candidate 28) into C2 rather than leaving it as a pure extension of
CO-08. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) leave capacity planning entirely inside CO-08, only extending its
existing envelope logic forward in time; (b) build capacity planning as its own thin dataset, consuming
C2's demand output as an external input. WHY THIS: the Ledger's own verdict (MERGE → IAS-C2) already
settled this, and Part VII §7.2 demonstrates the mechanical reason — capacity planning is uncomputable
without demand records, and CO-08 has no demand-forecasting function of its own to supply them, so
capacity planning's natural home is beside the demand object it depends on. TRADE-OFFS: C2's own scope
now spans demand, opportunity cost, capacity, AND scarcity — four related but distinct sub-objects in one
dataset, increasing this dataset's own internal complexity relative to a narrower alternative. REVERSAL
CONDITIONS: if capacity planning's consumer set grows to need a cadence or authority distinct from
demand forecasting's own (for instance, real-time capacity alerts on a much faster cycle than forecasting
cadence), capacity planning could be SPUN OUT as its own dataset consuming C2's demand records as an
external interface, mirroring D1's reversal condition.

D3. DECISION: Make magnitude and confidence ORDINAL, never numeric-precise, in the demand record schema
(Part IV §4.2-4.3). ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) a numeric magnitude (an estimated invocation count) and
a numeric probability for confidence, matching more conventional forecasting practice; (b) a fully
qualitative, unstructured natural-language description with no fixed schema at all. WHY THIS: the five
signal sources (Part II) are individually weak-to-moderate reliability, and a numeric point estimate built
from their combination would assert precision the underlying evidence does not support, violating the
measurement-discipline principle imported from F2 §11.3. An unstructured natural-language alternative would
sacrifice the auditability and cross-cycle comparability (Part IV §4.6's trajectory tracking) the schema
needs. TRADE-OFFS: ordinal magnitude is less immediately actionable for a consumer wanting to size a build
precisely (how many agent-hours, specifically) — that sizing judgment is explicitly deferred to IAS-C1 and
IAS-F3, which C2 treats as correct scoping rather than as a gap. REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if a specific
domain's signal quality matures enough (through retrodiction evidence, Part XI) to support genuinely
numeric precision without false confidence, a domain-specific numeric extension to the ordinal schema
could be added without breaking the schema's cross-domain comparability, provided the ordinal fallback
remains available for domains that have not matured.

D4. DECISION: Give the decision model (Part IX) a visible, uncollapsed ranking table rather than a single
composite priority score, diverging from F2's Advantage Score precedent of collapsing factors into one
formula. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a) follow F2's precedent directly and define a single Demand Priority
Score formula analogous to the Advantage Score; (b) leave ranking entirely to IAS-C1's own judgment, with
C2 supplying only the raw demand records and no ranking at all. WHY THIS: F2's Advantage Score scores
EXISTING systems against PAST-TO-PRESENT evidence, which tolerates compression; C2 ranks CLAIMS ABOUT THE
FUTURE under genuine uncertainty, where a human-legible trade-off table is more useful and more honest
than a collapsed number that would hide exactly which uncertain input is driving a recommendation (Part
IX §9.3). Leaving ranking entirely to C1 would have made C2's own decision-model object hollow — a
demand-forecasting dataset that produces records nobody ranks is closer to forecast theater (Part XV
§15.5) than to a functioning second-order capability. TRADE-OFFS: a visible table is harder to consume
programmatically at scale than a single sortable number, imposing a small integration cost on IAS-C1.
REVERSAL CONDITIONS: if IAS-C1's own consumption pattern demonstrates that the visible table's five
dimensions collapse, in practice, to a stable, well-understood weighting that never actually needs
per-dimension inspection, a summary score COULD be layered on top of the table as a convenience view — but
the underlying table remains the source of truth, never replaced by the summary.

D5. DECISION: Explicitly refuse to build a per-allocator opportunity-cost score (Part VI §6.3), even
though the ledger's own attribution data would technically support one. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED: (a)
build the per-allocator score, on the reasoning that accountability improves allocation quality over
time; (b) omit attribution tracking entirely, avoiding the temptation altogether. WHY THIS: a
per-allocator score would create a direct incentive to never honestly rank a risky, high-upside
alternative as a genuine contender (Part V §5.3's ranking requirement), because a decision-maker penalized
for foregone-then-confirmed demand would be incentivized to suppress uncertain alternatives from the
ranking table altogether — precisely undermining the ranking discipline the ledger depends on to function
at all (Part IX §9.6). Omitting attribution entirely, alternative (b), would have made recalibration
(Part V §5.5) impossible, since recalibration needs to know which forecast line was foregone by which
decision. WHY the middle path (attribution kept, blame-scoring refused) is correct: it preserves exactly
the data recalibration needs while removing exactly the data a blame-score would need, a distinction made
possible because recalibration operates on FORECAST LINES, not on ALLOCATOR IDENTITY. TRADE-OFFS: the
estate loses a potential lever for identifying a specific allocator's decision quality, if such a lever
were ever legitimately wanted for other governance purposes outside C2's own scope. REVERSAL CONDITIONS:
Article IX's governed-self-evolution path is the only legitimate route to building a per-allocator score
in the future, and it would require an explicit Owner-authorized proposal citing evidence that the
ranking-suppression risk named here has not, in fact, materialized — a high bar deliberately.

===============================================================================================
GAP META-ANALYSIS
===============================================================================================

What gap this dataset closes. Before IAS-C2, the estate could observe present cost (Cognitive OS), flag
present absence (CO-12), record filed intent (backlog_autopilot and owner_queue), and judge present
liveness (liveness) — but held no object for FUTURE capability need and no object for the VALUE FORFEITED
by any allocation choice made against that future need. This is a genuine temporal gap: every parent named
in the Matrix row is built to react well to the present; none is built to anticipate. C2 closes this by
supplying, for the first time in the corpus, a forward-looking claim (the demand record) with its own
epistemic discipline (HYPOTHESIZED until horizon close, never asserted OBSERVED prematurely) and a
companion economic object (opportunity cost) that makes the cost of NOT anticipating visible and
recorded rather than silently absorbed.

Systems strengthened, and how, restated concisely. CO-12's gap radar is strengthened because a recurring
gap (Part I §1.6) is now interceptable by a forecast before its third or fourth occurrence, rather than
being independently rediscovered each time. backlog_autopilot is strengthened because a shrinking
surprise-arrival rate (Part XVII §17.4) means fewer of its filed items arrive with zero prior institutional
awareness. owner_queue is strengthened because fewer CRITICAL escalations should trace to unanticipated
capability absence (Part XVII §17.6). liveness is strengthened because C2's own declared cadence gives it
a legitimate, nameable explanation for a class of long-running, non-mission-tied session activity it would
otherwise have to treat as an undifferentiated stall signal (Part XVII §17.7).

Duplications avoided, restated from the Ledger's own verdicts. Candidate 24 (Opportunity Cost) was
white space with no parent to duplicate, and D1 in the Decision Log records why it was still folded into
C2 rather than sealed alone (it needed C2's forecast object to be computable at all). Candidate 27 (Demand
Forecasting) avoided duplicating backlog_autopilot specifically by forecasting UNFILED, aggregate,
capability-class demand rather than processing FILED, discrete, work-item backlog (Part I §1.5). Candidate
28 (Capacity Planning) avoided duplicating CO-08 specifically by projecting FUTURE, ensemble-wide capacity
against forecast demand rather than governing the PRESENT, single-host parallel envelope (Part VII §7.1).

New capabilities the ensemble now has that it did not have before this dataset. The estate can now: name a
capability's demand with a stated confidence and horizon before any mission has requested it; rank
competing future allocation candidates in a visible trade-off table rather than by arrival order or
recency; compute and settle an opportunity cost figure for what a chosen allocation forwent; project a
capacity shortfall or a single-provider scarcity constraint before either binds; and validate the entire
mechanism against its own historical record via retrodiction, rather than asserting its own competence.

Open risks, named honestly rather than smoothed over. The self-fulfilling-prophecy risk (Part XV §15.3)
is only partially defended and is explicitly left CONTESTED and UNRESOLVED (Part XVIII §18.7) rather than
claimed solved. The retrodiction population's required size (Part XVIII §18.6) is UNRESOLVED and will
depend on the estate's own history accumulating further. The dataset's five signal sources (Part II) are
of genuinely uneven reliability, and a forecasting engine over-reliant on the cheapest-to-compute source
(mission history) risks the signal-monoculture failure mode (Part XV §15.4) if the corroboration
discipline is not actively maintained. And the authority-shift open question (Part XVIII §18.8) is
explicitly deferred to F1/IAS-A2 rather than resolved unilaterally here, which is itself a risk in the
sense that the question remains open rather than answered.

===============================================================================================
ANTI-PATTERNS
===============================================================================================

Anti-pattern One: TREATING A HYPOTHESIZED DEMAND RECORD AS AN ACTIONABLE FACT BEFORE HORIZON CLOSE. The
most likely implementation error is a consumer (a future IAS-C1 build, or an Owner-facing dashboard) that
silently drops the confidence and lifecycle-state fields when displaying a demand record, presenting
"HIGH magnitude, cross-project reliability" as settled fact rather than as a PROJECTED claim carrying a
stated confidence band. PREVENTION: every consumer-facing surface of a demand record must render the
lifecycle state and confidence level alongside the magnitude, never the magnitude alone, and any downstream
system reading demand records programmatically should reject a record missing either field rather than
defaulting to an implicit "trust it" behavior — this is the schema discipline of Part IV §4.2 made into an
integration contract, not merely a display convention.

Anti-pattern Two: LETTING THE OPPORTUNITY COST LEDGER DRIFT INTO A BLAME INSTRUMENT. The second most
likely implementation error, and the one Part VI §6.3 and Part XIV §14.5 both address at length precisely
because it is tempting, is a future extension that adds a per-allocator dashboard or leaderboard built
from the ledger's attribution data, reasoning that "accountability improves decisions." PREVENTION: the
Decision Log's D5 entry stands as the standing prohibition; any future proposal to add per-allocator
scoring must go through Article IX's governed-self-evolution path explicitly, citing evidence that the
ranking-suppression risk has not materialized, rather than being added as an incremental dashboard feature
without that scrutiny — the prevention here is procedural (a required gate) rather than purely
architectural, because the ledger's raw data will always technically support the misuse; only the gate
prevents it from being built casually.

Anti-pattern Three: RUNNING THE FORECASTING ENGINE WITHOUT THE CORROBORATION-CEILING DISCIPLINE, LETTING
CONFIDENCE INFLATE FROM VOLUME RATHER THAN DIVERSITY OF SIGNAL. The third likely error is a well-intentioned
implementation that interprets "more corroborating cycles" as sufficient grounds for confidence growth,
without distinguishing genuinely diverse signal sources (Part III §3.6's corroboration rule) from repeated
readings of the SAME underlying signal source across successive cycles (ten mission-history windows in a
row are not ten independent pieces of evidence; they are one trend, sampled repeatedly). PREVENTION: the
combination layer's implementation must track corroboration by SIGNAL CLASS DIVERSITY (how many of the
five named classes contributed, per Part II) rather than by RAW CYCLE COUNT, and any confidence growth
attributable to volume-within-one-signal-class rather than diversity-across-classes should be flagged, in
the demand record's own contributing-signal-set field (Part IV §4.2), as a signal-monoculture risk
(Part XV §15.4) rather than silently absorbed into the confidence figure.

FUSION SCORE
===============================================================================================

Systems strengthened: four systems named in the Matrix row (CO-12, backlog_autopilot, owner_queue,
liveness), each with a distinct, independently-benchmarked mechanism (Part XVII §17.4-17.7) rather than
one generic "improves the estate" claim applied uniformly — the breadth is real but modest by the corpus's
own standard (the Matrix floor of four, not an outsized double-digit count some sibling datasets report),
appropriate to a dataset whose object (forward-looking economics) is narrower than, for instance, F1's
foundational or A2's cross-cutting scope.

Integration quality: HIGH where the interface is structurally forced (the demand-record schema, Part IV,
is the single artifact every downstream consumer reads, preventing the fragmentation F1's property model
was built to avoid at the system-property level); MODERATE-TO-UNRESOLVED where the interface depends on a
sibling dataset not yet built at the time of this authoring (IAS-C1 and IAS-F3 do not yet exist in this
corpus's build order — Part XVIII's interface specification is therefore this dataset's own COMMITMENT to
what it will hand across those edges, not yet a bidirectionally-validated integration, and this asymmetry
is named honestly rather than presented as already-proven).

Reuse: the demand-record schema (Part IV) and the five-signal taxonomy (Part II) are the dataset's most
reusable artifacts — both are designed to be read by any future sibling needing a forward-looking claim
about the estate, not merely by C1 and F3 specifically, and the ordinal-magnitude, capped-confidence
discipline (imported from F2, extended here) is itself a reusable PATTERN other forward-looking IAS work
could adopt without needing this dataset's specific forecasting engine.

Complexity cost: MODERATE-TO-HIGH. This dataset carries four related sub-objects (demand, opportunity
cost, capacity, scarcity) under one roof (Decision Log D1-D2's own acknowledged trade-off), a four-
subengine forecasting architecture (Part III), a five-state lifecycle with a forced-verdict rule (Part X),
and a six-benchmark evaluation protocol (Part XVII) — this is genuinely more machinery than a narrower
dataset would require, justified by the object's own genuine multi-part nature (Part V §5.1's argument
for why forecasting and opportunity cost could not be cleanly separated) rather than by scope creep, but
the complexity is real and should be weighed honestly against a leaner alternative architecture in any
future revision.

Redundancy risk: LOW-TO-MODERATE. The clearest redundancy risk is Part XV §15.4's signal monoculture
(the mechanism collapsing toward duplicating what mission-history-only trend-reading would already do)
and Part XV §15.5's forecast theater (the mechanism producing output redundant with simply not existing,
if unconsumed) — both are named, defended against structurally, and given dedicated metrics (Part XVI
§16.2-16.3) specifically because the risk is judged real enough to warrant standing measurement rather
than a one-time design assurance.

Original D2A verdict, restated from the Non-Duplication Ledger. Candidate 24 (Opportunity Cost): BUILD,

All three verdicts converge on one dataset, IAS-C2, per the Ledger's own routing.

Irreducible core demonstrated. Having authored the full eighteen-part specification, the irreducible core
this dataset actually depends on is narrower than its full surface: it is the DEMAND RECORD (Part IV) as
a forward-looking, evidence-leveled, capped-confidence claim over the capability namespace, combined with
the RANKED-ALTERNATIVE requirement (Part V §5.3) that makes opportunity cost computable from it. Every
other mechanism in this dataset — the four subengines, the capacity planner, the scarcity foresight, the
ledger's aggregation, the six-benchmark evaluation — is a CONSUMER or an EXTENSION of this core, not an
independent second object smuggled into the same file. This is the specific test Article II's
ensemble-object boundary requires, and this dataset passes it: strip away every subengine and every
benchmark and the demand record plus the opportunity-cost ranking rule would still constitute a coherent,
if minimal, second-order capability — the full eighteen-part apparatus is the DEVELOPED form of that core,
not padding around an otherwise-thin object.

Sovereignty test result. Per the ledger's own admission criterion (F1 §3.5, the ensemble-object test): does
C2's object recurse onto other capabilities rather than onto a resource? The demand record's object is the
future applicability of a capability across the estate's projected mission flow (ensemble-level, per F1
§8.2's formal test); the opportunity cost record's object is the relation between a chosen and a foregone
demand record (a relation between two capability-level claims, not a resource). Both pass the sovereignty
test cleanly. The test's harder edge, honestly named: capacity planning (Part VII) reads a single
system's (CO-08's) resource-level envelope as an input — but per the majority-object rule (F1 §8.4), its
produced object is a projected SHORTFALL across the ensemble's capability space, not a change to CO-08's
own envelope, so the sovereignty test is satisfied by what capacity planning DECIDES UPON (a shortfall
across capabilities) rather than by what it merely READS (one system's envelope). This dataset's
sovereignty verdict: PASSED, with the capacity-planning sub-object's dependency on CO-08 named explicitly
as the closest the dataset comes to a resource-level input, resolved correctly by the majority-object rule
rather than by asserting the dependency away.
