===============================================================================================
PART I — THE OBJECT ARCH-DECISION CANNOT SEE: ESTATE TOPOLOGY AS A GOVERNED WHOLE
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding gap, stated precisely. arch-decision, as OBSERVED in the estate's own registry, is
a mature, real capability: given one proposed change to one system or feature, it weighs
alternatives, records a rationale, and commits a decision. It is good at exactly this, and IAS-G1
does not propose to do it better, faster, or instead — Article III forbids replacement and this
dataset obeys it structurally, not merely rhetorically. What arch-decision cannot do, because its
object is one decision at one moment for one system, is ask the question that only becomes
askable once dozens of such decisions have accumulated: does the SUM of these locally-sound
choices cohere into a topology that is, considered as one thing, legible, appropriately layered,
and free of the kind of accidental complexity that no single decision introduced but that many
decisions, each individually defensible, jointly produced? (DERIVED: a question about the sum of
decisions is not answerable by any mechanism scoped to one decision, by the same logic F1 Part I
§1.4 uses to separate resource-level from ensemble-level questions.) This is the founding gap
IAS-G1 exists to close, and it is a gap in KIND, not a gap in QUALITY — arch-decision does not do
its job poorly; it does a different job than the one this dataset performs.

1.2 Why summing local optimality does not yield global optimality. This is not a novel claim
invented for this corpus; it is a structural property of any system built by many locally-rational
actors, OBSERVED across software architecture generally and DERIVED here for the specific case of
a seventy-plus-system federation. Each arch-decision, at the moment it is made, correctly weighs
the alternatives visible to it: the system under change, its immediate neighbors, the cost and
benefit of the proposed shape. What it structurally cannot weigh is the topology-wide cost of a
CLASS of choice repeated many times by many different decision-makers across many months — for
instance, the pattern of adding a bespoke point-to-point integration between two systems rather
than routing through an existing shared relation, a pattern each individual arch-decision might
correctly judge as the locally cheapest option, while the estate accumulates, invisibly, a dense
mesh of point-to-point edges that no single decision was wrong to add and that the topology, taken
as a whole, is nonetheless worse for having. (DERIVED: this is the architecture-specific instance
of Part III §3.2's general claim in F1 that ensemble value is a function of relation-graph
position, not of any single system's isolated properties — here restated as: ensemble
ARCHITECTURAL HEALTH is a function of the accumulated pattern of past decisions, not of any one
decision's local soundness.) IAS-G1 exists precisely to detect and price this class of emergent
cost, which by construction no resource-level decision mechanism can see.

1.3 The three sibling boundaries this dataset must respect, stated as refusals. IAS-G1 refuses
three temptations that would each collapse it back into a duplicate of a parent. First, it refuses
to render verdicts on individual proposed changes — that is arch-decision's decision right, and
Invariant Three of F1 Part V (authority is singular per decision class) forbids IAS-G1 from holding
final authority over "should system X adopt shape Y"; IAS-G1's authority, specified fully in Part
XIV, is advisory context feeding that decision, never a replacement for it. Second, it refuses to
dispose of duplication events itself — D2A's six verdicts (BUILD, MERGE-INTO-FAMILY, SUBENGINE,
EXTEND, COMPOSITION, REFERENCE) remain D2A's alone; IAS-G1 instead supplies D2A with topology-wide
evidence (is this a first duplication or the fifth of a recurring pattern?) that a single-event
view cannot generate. Third, it refuses to execute mutations directly on the live estate —
evolution_engine's execution role is untouched; IAS-G1 sequences and sandboxes (Part X, Part XI)
what evolution_engine later executes, but the act of executing remains evolution_engine's, per
Constitution Article X's prohibition on this mission mutating the estate at all. (NORMATIVE: these
three refusals are not modesty; they are the mechanism by which IAS-G1 satisfies Article III —
strengthen, never replace — at the level of concrete authority boundaries rather than as a slogan.)

1.4 The level test, applied to architecture. Running F1 Part VIII's formal test against IAS-G1's
own object confirms ensemble-level status independently of the ledger's verdict. Object(IAS-G1):
the topology graph over the estate's systems and its emergent properties (coupling density,
complexity-budget spend, drift magnitude, debt outstanding) — this set is not contained within any
single existing system's owned resource class (F1 §8.3), satisfying the level test on its first
clause. The second-order test (F1 Part VII §7.2) confirms independently: IAS-G1's direct object is
OTHER SYSTEMS' RELATIONS TO ONE ANOTHER (the FEEDS/DEPENDS-ON/COMPETES-WITH/DUPLICATES/STRENGTHENS/
CONSUMES graph of F1 Part VI) and the capabilities of arch-decision, D2A, and evolution_engine
themselves — not any one resource those systems govern. IAS-G1 does not decide which model serves
a session, which knowledge node is canonical, or which of two proposals wins a single duplication
dispute; it decides, and recommends, at the level of the graph those decisions collectively trace
across time. (DERIVED, using F1's own formal apparatus, not asserted by resemblance — this is the
retrodiction discipline F1 §8.7 models and this dataset applies to its own admission.)

1.5 Position in the build order and what this dataset inherits versus what it produces. IAS-G1 is
built in Tier 3, after the Foundations (F1, F2) and concurrently with the other systems fan-out,
and it inherits rather than re-derives three things: F1's object model and six relation types
(Part VI of this dataset reuses them directly, adding no new primitive graph), F2's advantage
algebra for expressing gain/cost/risk in comparable units (used throughout Parts III-XI wherever a
trade-off or a budget is priced), and — per the Compounding Graph's primary loop — IAS-D1's fitness
signal and IAS-E1's usage/dead-capability signal as inputs its own fitness evaluator (Part IV) and
drift detector (Part VI) consume rather than re-measure. What IAS-G1 itself PRODUCES, and what no
inherited input already contains, is the topology-specific machinery: the coupling and complexity
metrics of Part III, the fitness-to-shape translation of Part IV, the multi-criteria trade-off
resolution of Part V, the declared-versus-real divergence detection of Part VI, the connection-
level optimization of Part VII, the budget and debt instruments of Parts VIII-IX, the sandboxed
and sequenced mutation machinery of Parts X-XI, and the two folded capabilities — lock-in
detection (Part XII) and the dependency governor (Part XIII) — that the ledger routes here because
each is, on inspection, a facet of the same estate-wide topology object rather than a genuinely
separate concern.

1.6 Why the reader should expect density, not breadth, from what follows. A dataset governing an
object as broad as "the estate's own shape" could easily sprawl into a survey of every conceivable
architectural concern; this dataset deliberately does not. Every engine specified in Parts III-XIII
traces back to one of the ledger's ten Family-G candidates or one of the two folded candidates named
in §1.5, and no engine is introduced merely because it would be interesting architecture theory in the
abstract — the discipline Article VI names as padding-by-scope-creep is guarded against here at the
level of WHICH engines exist, not only at the level of how each is written once admitted. (NORMATIVE:
a reader who expects this dataset to also cover, for instance, code-style conventions, deployment
topology, or infrastructure provisioning will not find them here, because none of those objects
resolves to the estate's inter-system relation graph under the level test of Part VIII §8.2 — they are,
respectively, resource-level concerns already owned elsewhere or objects outside the ensemble boundary
F1 §3.4 draws.)

1.7 The reading order this dataset assumes, and why it matters for a document this dense. Parts II
through XIII build strictly on each other — the ontology of Part II is presupposed by every later
Part, and Parts III through XIII largely follow the dependency order in which one engine's output
becomes another's input (quality feeds fitness, fitness and quality feed the compiler, the compiler
and drift detection feed the optimizer, the optimizer feeds the budget and debt market, all of it
feeds the sandbox and planner). Parts XIV-XV then step back to specify authority and lifecycle across
the whole engine set, Part XVI demonstrates the composed pipeline on one trace, and Parts XVII-XXII
close with edge cases, adversarial cases, failure modes, metrics, evaluation, and integration, in that
order, because each of those closing categories depends on having the full engine set already defined
to reason about. A reader entering at Part X without Parts II-IX will find its vocabulary undefined;
this is a stated property of the document's structure, not an oversight.

FINAL LAW — PART I. arch-decision judges one choice, D2A disposes one duplication, evolution_
engine executes one mutation — three parents, each locally correct and each blind to the shape
their accumulated correctness produces; IAS-G1 is the governor of that shape, and it earns its
place in the family not by deciding better than its parents but by seeing what none of them, each
scoped to one event, was ever built to see.

===============================================================================================
PART II — THE ARCHITECTURE ONTOLOGY: TOPOLOGY, COUPLING, COHESION, SEAM, LAYER
===============================================================================================

2.1 Why architecture needs its own object vocabulary beyond F1's primitives. F1 supplies SYSTEM,
SUBSYSTEM, CAPABILITY, and the six typed relations, and IAS-G1 imports every one of them by
reference rather than redefining them (Constitution Article VI forbids re-narrating a parent's
object model). But F1's vocabulary describes WHAT exists and HOW it connects; it does not, on its
own, supply the vocabulary architecture specifically needs: how TIGHTLY two connected systems are
coupled, how COHERENTLY one system's internal capabilities belong together, where the estate's
natural SEAMS fall, and how those seams organize into LAYERS. This Part DEFINES the five
architecture-specific objects IAS-G1 adds on top of F1's foundation, each justified because at
least one later Part in this dataset cannot be written without it.

2.2 TOPOLOGY, defined precisely for this dataset. The TOPOLOGY is DEFINED as the F1 relation graph
(Part VI's six typed edges) restricted to the subset of edges that are STRUCTURAL rather than
transient — that is, every FEEDS, DEPENDS-ON, COMPETES-WITH, and STRENGTHENS edge that persists
across missions, explicitly excluding CONSUMES edges (F1 §6.7 defines these as the most volatile
type, created per-mission and not expected to persist) and treating DUPLICATES edges as a
transitional annotation on the topology rather than a stable structural feature of it (per F1
§6.5, a duplicates edge is evidence of a state in transition, never steady-state). (DERIVED: this
scoping is necessary because a topology that counted every transient consumes edge as structural
would never stabilize long enough to be measured, drift-checked, or budgeted — the object would be
different on every read.) The topology is the primary object IAS-G1 governs; every engine in
Parts III-XIII operates on it or on a property computed over it.

2.3 COUPLING, the cost of a structural edge. COUPLING is DEFINED as a property of a DEPENDS-ON or
FEEDS edge measuring how much a change on one side of the edge forces, or risks forcing, a
corresponding change on the other side. A DEPENDS-ON edge (F1 §6.3, criticality-flagged) carries
HIGH coupling by construction — the receiving system's correctness is bound to the providing
system's behavior. A FEEDS edge carries coupling proportional to how narrowly the receiving system
interprets the fed data: a system that consumes another's output through a stable, versioned,
narrow interface is loosely coupled even across a standing FEEDS edge, while a system that reaches
into another's internal representation is tightly coupled regardless of the edge's nominal type.
(NORMATIVE: coupling is measured at the interface, not at the edge type alone, because the edge
type names the RELATION while coupling names the FRAGILITY of that relation to change — two
different questions F1's typed graph does not itself distinguish.) Coupling is the single most
load-bearing metric in this dataset's quality science (Part III), because nearly every architecture
pathology named in Parts XVII-XIX is, at bottom, coupling that grew unnoticed.

2.4 COHESION, the internal counterpart to coupling. COHESION is DEFINED as a property of a single
SYSTEM (not an edge) measuring how tightly the capabilities that system provides (F1 §2.4) belong
together under one distinct object. A system whose capability signature (F1 §9.2) is a coherent
cluster — every capability serving the same object — is high-cohesion; a system whose capability
signature has drifted into an unrelated grab-bag, each capability serving a different object that
happened to accrete onto the same codebase, is low-cohesion regardless of how loosely it couples to
its neighbors. (DERIVED: cohesion and coupling are classically inverse-correlated but not
identical — a system can be simultaneously low-cohesion and loosely coupled, which Part XVII names
as a specific edge case, the SPLIT CANDIDATE, distinct from ordinary architectural decay.) Cohesion
is what the mutation sandbox (Part X) tests when a proposed evolution is a SPLIT rather than a
MERGE or a topology re-route.

2.5 SEAM, the estate's natural division line. A SEAM is DEFINED as a boundary in the topology
across which coupling is naturally low — a set of systems on one side that depend heavily on each
other and lightly, or not at all, on the systems on the other side. Seams are not designed; they
are DISCOVERED by reading the topology's coupling distribution, and the topology optimizer (Part
VII) treats an existing, healthy seam as a resource to preserve rather than a gap to close — a
seam is precisely where a future mutation (split, retirement, replacement) can act with minimal
ripple, because the low cross-seam coupling means change on one side rarely forces change on the
other. (OBSERVED, generically: the estate's own tier structure — Foundations, Cross-System
Circulation, Intelligence Amplification, Capability Economics, System Ecology, Observability,
Foresight — is itself evidence that natural seams already exist in the corpus's own organization,
though IAS-G1 measures seams in the RUNTIME topology of systems, not in the DOCUMENT topology of
this dataset family, and the two need not coincide.)

2.6 LAYER, the ordering seams imply. A LAYER is DEFINED as a set of systems, bounded by seams, that
share a similar DEPENDS-ON depth from the estate's foundational systems (those with zero outbound
depends-on edges, analogous to F1 itself, which no other system's correctness structurally
requires). Layering is not hierarchy of importance — a Tier-3 architecture system and a Tier-0
foundation system can be equally consequential — it is hierarchy of FAILURE PROPAGATION DEPTH: a
foundational system's failure ripples through every dependent layer above it, while a leaf-layer
system's failure is contained to its own immediate consumers. (DERIVED from F1 §6.3's depends-on
propagation rule, generalized from a pair of systems to the whole graph.) IAS-G1 uses layer depth
directly in the complexity budget (Part VIII, where deeper layers carry a higher per-edge budget
cost because their failure blast radius is larger) and in the drift detector (Part VI, where an
edge that crosses layers unexpectedly — a leaf-layer system suddenly depended upon by a
foundational one — is a specific, high-severity drift signature).

2.7 The five objects as a closed set, and what is deliberately excluded. Topology, coupling,
cohesion, seam, and layer are DEFINED as the minimum sufficient vocabulary for the engines that
follow, chosen by the same discipline F1 §2.5 applies to its own primitive layer: no term is added
here that a later Part does not require, because an architecture vocabulary inflated beyond
necessity duplicates the governance-theater risk F1 already names. Deliberately excluded: any
notion of "architectural style" (microservices, monolith, event-driven) as a first-class object,
because style names an implementation pattern at the resource level, not a property of the
ensemble topology — a style judgment belongs to arch-decision at the point of one system's design,
never to IAS-G1, which reasons only about the graph shape, never about the internal implementation
choice that produced any one node.

2.8 How the five objects compose into the engines that follow: a preview map. Topology (§2.2) is the
substrate every engine in Parts III-XIII reads or writes. Coupling (§2.3) is the primary input to
quality science's density dimension (Part III) and the unit the complexity budget prices in (Part
VIII). Cohesion (§2.4) drives the SPLIT move's generation logic (Part VII) and the debt market's
cohesion-spread-weighted issuance (Part IX). Seams (§2.5) bound the topology optimizer's hardest
constraint (Part VII §7.4) and anchor the quality science's seam-integrity dimension. Layers (§2.6) set
the complexity budget's per-tier ceilings (Part VIII §8.2) and the dependency governor's scan priority
(Part XIII §13.4). No engine in this dataset introduces a sixth object; every mechanism in Parts III
through XIII is expressible as a computation OVER these five, which is the concrete sense in which this
Part is a genuine foundation for what follows rather than a loosely-related preamble.

2.9 A terminological note: coupling and cohesion are not opposites measured on one shared scale. A
common informal shorthand treats "loose coupling, high cohesion" as a single design maxim, implying the
two properties trade off against each other on one axis. This dataset is precise about why that
shorthand, useful as a rule of thumb, is not literally true under its own definitions: coupling (§2.3)
is a property of an EDGE between two systems, while cohesion (§2.4) is a property of the INTERNAL
capability set of one system, and the two are measured over entirely different objects — a system can
score high on both simultaneously (Part II §2.5's T-01-through-T-05 cluster, previewed here and
detailed in Part III §3.5, is exactly this case), or low on both simultaneously (Part XVII §17.1's
SPLIT-CANDIDATE-that-is-also-a-MERGE-CANDIDATE edge case). Treating them as one inverse-scaled axis
would be a category error this dataset avoids by keeping the two definitions strictly separate
throughout, even where they are frequently correlated in practice.

FINAL LAW — PART II. Topology is the structural subgraph, coupling prices an edge's fragility to
change, cohesion prices a system's internal coherence, seams are where coupling is naturally low,
and layers are ordered by failure-propagation depth — five objects, no more, because an
architecture vocabulary that outgrows the engines built on it has stopped describing the estate
and started decorating it.

===============================================================================================
PART III — ARCHITECTURE QUALITY SCIENCE FOR THE ENSEMBLE
===============================================================================================

3.1 Why "quality" needs a science, not an adjective. Every existing system in the estate is,
informally, sometimes called "well-architected" or "getting messy," and those judgments are real
but unmeasured — a vibe, not a metric, OBSERVED to vary by which engineer or agent last touched the
system and how recently. arch-decision judges quality LOCALLY, at the moment of one decision, using
whatever criteria that decision's context makes salient. IAS-G1's quality science is the discipline
that makes "well-architected" a computed property of the topology rather than an impression, built
from the five objects of Part II, because every downstream engine in this dataset — fitness (Part
IV), trade-offs (Part V), drift (Part VI), the budget (Part VIII), the debt market (Part IX) — needs
a shared, non-impressionistic measure to operate on, exactly as F1 Part IV's property model exists
so that C1, E1, and G1 do not each invent a different "cost."

3.2 The four quality dimensions, each grounded in Part II's objects. COUPLING DENSITY is DEFINED
as the estate-wide distribution of coupling scores (Part II §2.3) across all DEPENDS-ON and FEEDS
edges, read as a distribution rather than a single average, because an estate with mostly-loose
coupling and a handful of dangerously-tight edges is architecturally different from one with
uniformly-medium coupling everywhere, and an average collapses that difference — a specific
instance of the ratio-gating failure the estate has already learned to avoid (a lesson this dataset
inherits as institutional memory rather than re-derives). COHESION SPREAD is DEFINED as the
distribution of cohesion scores (Part II §2.4) across all systems, where a wide spread — many
high-cohesion systems and a long tail of low-cohesion ones — signals localized decay rather than
estate-wide decay, informing whether the topology optimizer (Part VII) should intervene broadly or
target specific systems. SEAM INTEGRITY is DEFINED as the ratio of cross-seam coupling to
within-seam coupling (Part II §2.5): a healthy seam keeps this ratio low, and a rising ratio over
time is the earliest quantitative signal that a once-natural division is eroding, before any single
arch-decision would have reason to notice. LAYER CLEANLINESS is DEFINED as the proportion of edges
that respect strict layer ordering (Part II §2.6) versus edges that skip or invert layers — an
edge from a foundational system depending on a Tier-3 system, for instance, inverts the expected
propagation direction and is, by this metric, always flagged regardless of whether the specific
edge is otherwise harmless, because layer inversions are the single strongest predictor of
cascading-failure blast radius the estate's own reliability discipline (feeding IAS-E2) has reason
to care about.

3.3 Composing the four dimensions without a false single score. The quality science deliberately
DOES NOT compose these four dimensions into one "architecture health score," for the same reason
the memory ledger warns against mapping a composite provider score onto a hard verdict: a single
number invites exactly the length-biased, vocabulary-biased collapse the estate has already paid to
learn about in an unrelated context (T-DRK-PRECEDENT-LENGTH-BIAS-001, carried here as institutional
caution rather than re-litigated). Instead, the quality science reports the four dimensions as a
VECTOR, and each downstream consumer reads the dimension it needs: the fitness evaluator (Part IV)
weights coupling density and layer cleanliness most heavily because both predict mission-time
fragility; the debt market (Part IX) weights cohesion spread because low-cohesion systems are where
technical debt concentrates; the topology optimizer (Part VII) weights seam integrity because its
entire object is preserving and creating good seams. (NORMATIVE: no single scalar substitutes for
the vector anywhere in this dataset; any future implementer tempted to collapse the four dimensions
into one score for dashboard convenience is reintroducing the exact failure Part III exists to
prevent, and Part XIX's anti-Goodhart countermetrics name this collapse explicitly as a metric to
watch for.)

3.4 Baseline and trend, not snapshot alone. A single reading of the four-dimension vector describes
the topology's state at one moment; the quality science's actual institutional value is in the
TREND — whether coupling density is rising or falling release over release, whether seam integrity
is eroding faster than the topology optimizer can repair it. (DERIVED from F1 §4.3's staleness
discipline, generalized from a single property's freshness to a whole vector's trajectory: a
quality reading without a trend is as incomplete as a property without a last-reconciled marker.)
IAS-G1 maintains a rolling baseline, reconciled at a cadence this dataset does not fix (per F1
§4.3's precedent, the cadence is an operational decision for whichever mechanism reads the record,
not a constant this foundation should hardcode), and every later engine that claims a change is
"drift" or "improvement" does so against this baseline, never against an absolute threshold
invented ad hoc.

3.5 Worked example: reading the vector for a synthetic ten-system slice. Consider a synthetic
ten-system slice of the estate, illustrative only. Systems T-01 through T-05 form a tightly
interlinked cluster with coupling density HIGH (average coupling score 0.78 on the dataset's 0-1
scale, DEFINED so that 1.0 is maximal fragility-to-change) and layer cleanliness HIGH (100% of
edges respect layer order) — a cluster that is tightly coupled but by design, because it forms one
coherent capability delivered across five cooperating systems, which the cohesion score confirms:
each of T-01 through T-05 individually scores cohesion HIGH, meaning their internal capabilities
belong together even as their inter-system coupling runs high. This combination — high coupling,
high cohesion, clean layering — is read by the quality science as a candidate for a MERGE
disposition (feeding evolution_engine, Part XI), not as a defect: five systems this tightly
interdependent and this internally coherent may be one system wearing five addresses. Systems T-06
through T-10, by contrast, show LOW coupling density (0.12 average) but also LOW seam integrity
(cross-seam coupling ratio 0.61, meaning most of their few edges cross what should be a clean
division) and MIXED layer cleanliness (three of eleven edges invert layer order) — a pattern the
quality science flags not for merging but for the topology optimizer's re-routing attention (Part
VII), because the problem here is not excess coupling but MISPLACED coupling: the few edges that
exist connect the wrong things, and the fix is redirection, not consolidation.

3.6 What the quality science explicitly does not judge. The four dimensions say nothing about
whether the ESTATE SHOULD HAVE a given system at all — that is a fitness (Part IV) and portfolio
(IAS-C1) question, informed by mission outcomes, not by topology shape alone. A system can score
excellently on all four dimensions — loosely coupled, highly cohesive, well-seamed, cleanly
layered — and still be architecturally elegant while serving a capability nobody uses; elegance and
value are orthogonal, and the quality science, deliberately, measures only the former, leaving the
latter to IAS-D1's fitness and IAS-E1's observability, which this dataset consumes (Part IV) rather
than duplicates.

3.7 Measurement source: how the four dimensions are actually computed, not only defined. Each
dimension's computation is grounded in the topology object of Part II rather than in any judgment call:
coupling density is computed from the interface-narrowness reading at each DEPENDS-ON and FEEDS edge
(§2.3, itself grounded in observed usage breadth via the dependency governor's own minimality scan,
Part XIII §13.2, reused rather than duplicated here); cohesion spread is computed from each system's
declared capability signature (F1 §9.2) clustered by observed co-invocation patterns from the control-
bus log, so that cohesion is not a subjective judgment about whether capabilities "feel" related but a
measured clustering result; seam integrity is the cross-seam-to-within-seam coupling ratio (§3.2),
itself downstream of the same coupling computation; and layer cleanliness is a simple count over the
layer assignment (Part II §2.6) already fixed by depends-on depth. (DERIVED: every one of the four
dimensions traces to a measurement, not an opinion, which is the specific property that makes the
quality science a SCIENCE in the sense this Part's title claims rather than a rubric.)

3.8 What "healthy" means under this vector, stated without inventing a universal threshold. This
dataset deliberately does not assert one universal numeric threshold below which a dimension counts as
"healthy" across every estate that might ever run this family's mechanisms — per F1 §4.3's precedent
that operational thresholds belong to the consuming mechanism, "healthy" here means, precisely, WITHIN
the estate's own rolling baseline's normal variance (§3.4), never an absolute constant imported from
outside this specific estate's own history. A coupling density of 0.6 might be entirely healthy for one
estate's typical topology and a severe outlier for another's, and this dataset's quality science is
explicitly baseline-relative rather than absolute-threshold-based for exactly this reason — the same
discipline that lets Part XII §12.5's lock-in threshold be locally set rather than universally fixed.

FINAL LAW — PART III. Quality is a four-dimension vector — coupling density, cohesion spread, seam
integrity, layer cleanliness — read as a trend against a rolling baseline, never collapsed into one
score, because a federation that reduces its own architecture to a single number has traded
measurement for theater the moment the number becomes the thing optimized instead of the thing
observed.

===============================================================================================
PART IV — THE ARCHITECTURE FITNESS EVALUATOR
===============================================================================================

4.1 From shape to value: why quality is not the same question as fitness. Part III measures
whether the topology is well-formed; it does not measure whether the topology is FIT for the
missions the estate actually runs. A perfectly clean, low-coupling, high-cohesion topology can
still be a poor fit if its seams fall in the wrong places for the estate's real workload — for
instance, dividing two systems the estate's missions almost always invoke together, forcing every
such mission to cross a seam that quality science alone would score as healthy. The FITNESS
EVALUATOR is DEFINED as the engine that scores a topology, or a candidate topology change, against
observed mission patterns rather than against structural elegance alone, and it is the specific
point at which IAS-G1 consumes IAS-D1's fitness signal (per the Strengthening Matrix and the
primary compounding loop) rather than re-deriving fitness from nothing.

4.2 The fitness function's three inputs, each sourced rather than invented. Mission-pattern
frequency is READ from IAS-A2's control-bus event log (F1 §6.7's consumes edges, aggregated over
time), giving IAS-G1 an OBSERVED distribution of which systems missions actually invoke together —
not a claimed audience (F1 §4.6's double-booked-consumer caution applies here directly: fitness
must be computed on observed CONSUMES edges, never on self-reported importance). System-level
fitness, per system, is READ from IAS-D1's ecology map rather than recomputed, per the compounding
graph's explicit edge ("IAS-D1 fitness/parasitism → IAS-G1 topology, retire-vs-invest"); IAS-G1
does not build a second fitness meter, exactly as F1's cost field is read from the Cognitive OS
rather than re-measured (F1 §4.2). Failure history is READ from IAS-D2's fingerprint federation,
because a topology that is elegant and mission-aligned but concentrates failure risk on one
overloaded seam is not fit in the sense IAS-E2's reliability discipline requires. (DERIVED: fitness
is a composite IAS-G1 assembles from three parents' existing signals, never a fourth independent
measurement — this is the specific mechanism by which the fitness evaluator strengthens D1, A2,
and D2 rather than duplicating any of them: it is a new READING of their outputs, not a new
INSTRUMENT.)

4.3 Topology-fitness versus system-fitness: the distinction that makes this engine ensemble-level.
IAS-D1 scores individual systems' fitness for their environment; the fitness evaluator in this
dataset scores the TOPOLOGY's fitness for the estate's actual mission mix — a genuinely different
object, because a topology composed entirely of individually high-fitness systems can still be a
poor topology if those systems are wired together in a shape that forces excess mission-time
coordination cost. (DERIVED, directly parallel to Part I §1.2's local-versus-global argument: just
as locally-sound arch-decisions can sum to a globally poor shape, individually-fit systems can be
wired into a globally poor topology.) The evaluator's output is therefore always a TOPOLOGY-level
score, or a topology-CHANGE-level delta, never a re-statement of a system-level fitness number
IAS-D1 already owns.

4.4 The mission-cost proxy and why it is the evaluator's central instrument. For a given mission
pattern (a recurring sequence of system invocations observed in the control-bus log), the fitness
evaluator computes a MISSION-COST PROXY: the number of seam crossings, layer inversions, and
high-coupling edges the mission's actual path through the topology traverses. A mission whose
frequent path crosses three seams and inverts one layer is, all else equal, less fit for than an
equally-valuable mission whose path stays within one seam and respects layer order — not because
the first mission is less important, but because its topology-imposed coordination tax is higher,
and that tax is exactly the quantity a topology change (Part VII) can reduce without touching any
individual system's internal logic. (EXPERIMENTAL: the mission-cost proxy is proposed here as the
evaluator's primary instrument; its predictive validity against real coordination overhead is a
claim this dataset marks EXPERIMENTAL pending the evaluation protocol of Part XX, which specifies
exactly how the proxy would be tested against retrodicted mission latency.)

4.5 Worked example: two candidate topologies scored against the same mission mix. Consider a
synthetic mission mix, illustrative only, dominated by one recurring pattern: a mission that
invokes a routing capability, then a knowledge-lookup capability, then a synthesis capability, in
that order, observed 340 times in a synthetic thirty-day control-bus window. Under CANDIDATE
TOPOLOGY ALPHA, these three capabilities are provided by systems T-11, T-19, and T-23 respectively,
wired with T-11 FEEDS T-19 FEEDS T-23 — a path that stays within one seam and crosses zero layer
boundaries; the mission-cost proxy for this pattern under Alpha is LOW. Under CANDIDATE TOPOLOGY
BETA — a hypothetical prior shape, retained here only for comparison — the same three capabilities
are provided by T-11, T-19, and a different system T-31 that sits in a separate seam and one layer
deeper, requiring the mission to cross a seam boundary and traverse an extra depends-on hop to
reach T-31's dependencies; the mission-cost proxy for the identical mission pattern under Beta is
HIGH. Because this pattern accounts for the large majority of observed mission volume in this
synthetic window, the fitness evaluator scores Alpha substantially fitter than Beta for the
estate's actual workload, even though a quality-science-only reading (Part III) might score Beta's
individual systems as equally well-formed — Beta's poor fitness is a topology-shape fact, not a
system-shape fact, and only the fitness evaluator, reading the mission mix, would surface it.

4.6 What the evaluator does not decide. The evaluator scores; it does not act. A low fitness score
for a given topology-mission pairing is evidence fed to the trade-off compiler (Part V) and,
ultimately, to arch-decision and evolution_engine as advisory context — never a standing order to
re-wire the topology. (NORMATIVE, per Part I §1.3's authority refusal: the evaluator's output is
advisory input into decisions those parents retain final authority over.) A further limit,
UNRESOLVED as a matter for future evaluation rather than asserted as settled: the evaluator scores
against OBSERVED mission patterns, which means it is structurally backward-looking and can, in
principle, undervalue a topology shape that is currently a poor fit for today's mix but well-suited
to a mix the estate has not yet developed — a tension this dataset names rather than resolves,
because resolving it would require the demand-forecasting signal IAS-C2 owns, and Part XXII
specifies that integration rather than building a second forecasting mechanism here.

4.7 Aggregating mission-cost proxies into a topology-level fitness reading. §4.1-4.6 describe the
proxy computed per mission PATTERN; the evaluator's actual deliverable to the trade-off compiler (Part
V) is a topology-LEVEL reading, DEFINED as the proxy values across every observed mission pattern
weighted by that pattern's OBSERVED frequency in the control-bus log (§4.2), never by an assumed or
declared importance. (NORMATIVE: frequency-weighting rather than importance-weighting is a deliberate
choice — a rarely-invoked but organizationally critical mission pattern is not thereby discounted
incorrectly, because criticality is a property IAS-D2's failure-budget discipline separately tracks
(§4.2's third input) and folding a subjective importance weight into the fitness aggregate would
reintroduce exactly the self-reported-audience problem F1 §4.6 already warns against for the CONSUMERS
property, applied here to mission patterns instead of systems.) The aggregate is reported alongside its
own per-pattern breakdown, never as a single collapsed figure alone, for the identical composition
discipline Part III §3.3 already applies to the quality vector — a topology can score well in aggregate
while concentrating its remaining poor fit in one high-frequency pattern, and the breakdown is what
lets the optimizer's marginal-impact filter (Part VII §7.3) target that specific pattern's location
rather than the topology broadly.

4.8 A boundary case: fitness for a mission pattern the topology has never been asked to serve. A
proposed NEW mission pattern — one arch-decision or a mission designer is considering routing through
the estate for the first time — has no OBSERVED frequency in the control-bus log by definition, and
the fitness evaluator cannot score it under §4.4's ordinary mechanism. This dataset does not attempt
to score such a pattern itself; per Part I §1.3's discipline against building a second forecasting
mechanism, a genuinely novel mission pattern's projected fit is IAS-F3's digital-twin territory (Part
XXII §22.2), which simulates outcomes for patterns that have not yet occurred, and this boundary is
named explicitly here, at the evaluator's own definitional edge, rather than left for a reader to
discover only when Part XXII draws the fuller sibling boundary.

FINAL LAW — PART IV. Fitness is not quality restated; it is quality read against the estate's real
mission mix, assembled from three parents' existing signals rather than a fourth meter, and its
verdict is always advisory context for arch-decision and evolution_engine, never a standing order
to reshape what those two systems alone have final authority to change.

===============================================================================================
PART V — THE TRADE-OFF COMPILER
===============================================================================================

5.1 Why a topology change is never a single-criterion decision. Every candidate topology change —
re-route an edge, split a system, merge two systems, retire a seam — improves some of Part III's
four quality dimensions and some fraction of Part IV's fitness score while, almost always,
degrading at least one other criterion: a re-route that improves seam integrity may add a layer
crossing; a merge that raises cohesion may raise coupling density elsewhere by concentrating more
capability behind fewer, larger interfaces. arch-decision, scoped to one system, resolves this kind
of trade-off locally and informally, weighing the criteria salient to that one decision. The TRADE-
OFF COMPILER is DEFINED as the engine that makes this weighing EXPLICIT and COMPARABLE across many
candidate changes at once, using F2's advantage algebra (imported by reference, not rebuilt here)
to express gain, cost, and risk in units comparable across the four quality dimensions and the
fitness score, so that two structurally different proposals — a re-route and a merge — can be
ranked on the same scale rather than argued past each other in incommensurable terms.

5.2 The compiler's inputs and the shape of its output. For each candidate change, the compiler
reads: the Part III quality-vector delta (which of the four dimensions improve, which degrade, and
by how much), the Part IV fitness delta (against the observed mission mix), an estimated
implementation cost (READ from arch-decision's own cost estimation practice where available,
marked UNRESOLVED where it is not, per F1 §4.6's discipline against guessing an unmeasured field),
and a risk estimate drawn from the mutation sandbox (Part X) once a candidate has been trialed
there. The output is not a single ranked list but a TRADE-OFF FRONTIER: the set of candidate
changes for which no other candidate is strictly better on every dimension simultaneously — the
standard Pareto-frontier construction, DEFINED here for architecture criteria specifically, chosen
over a single weighted score for the same reason Part III §3.3 refuses to compose its four
dimensions into one number. (NORMATIVE: presenting a frontier rather than a winner keeps the final
choice with arch-decision, which retains the authority to apply criteria — organizational priority,
Owner preference, timing — the compiler has no visibility into and no standing to weight.)

5.3 Resolving apparent ties: the dominance and near-dominance rules. Two candidates on the frontier
are formally incomparable when each is better than the other on at least one dimension; the
compiler does not force a resolution, but it does apply two DERIVED refinements that narrow the
frontier without collapsing it into a single answer. The DOMINANCE RULE removes a candidate from
the frontier if a second candidate improves on it by at least a defined margin on every dimension
where the two differ and is worse on none — a strict Pareto-dominance test, applied literally. The
NEAR-DOMINANCE ADVISORY flags, without removing, a candidate that a second candidate dominates on
all but one dimension, where the one exception is small relative to the estate's rolling baseline
variance (Part III §3.4) — this is advisory language ("Candidate C is near-dominated by Candidate D
except for a marginal seam-integrity difference within baseline noise") rather than a removal,
because a difference within measurement noise should not silently prune a genuinely distinct
option, only flag it for human attention.

5.4 Worked example: three candidates on one frontier. Consider a synthetic frontier for a single
proposed topology problem — reducing the excess coupling discovered in Part III §3.5's T-06
through T-10 slice — illustrative only. CANDIDATE R1 re-routes two misplaced edges to respect seam
boundaries: quality delta shows seam integrity improving from 0.61 to 0.24 (lower is healthier
under this dataset's ratio convention) and layer cleanliness improving from 73% to 100%, with
coupling density essentially unchanged; fitness delta is small positive; implementation cost is
LOW (two edge changes, no system-level mutation). CANDIDATE R2 merges T-08 and T-09, the two most
tightly-coupled systems in the slice, into one system: quality delta shows cohesion spread
improving substantially (their combined internal capability signature reads as coherent) and
coupling density improving because the merge eliminates the depends-on edge between them entirely,
but seam integrity is UNCHANGED because the merge does not touch the misplaced cross-seam edges;
fitness delta is near zero for the observed mission mix, which rarely invokes T-08 or T-09
directly; implementation cost is HIGH (a genuine system-level mutation, requiring evolution_
engine's full mutation protocol). CANDIDATE R3 does both R1 and R2 together: the combined quality
delta dominates each individual candidate on every dimension, but the combined implementation cost
is HIGHER than either alone and the combined risk (Part X) is not simply additive, because a
re-route and a merge touching overlapping systems in the same change window carries interaction
risk neither alone carries. The frontier the compiler returns is {R1, R3}: R2 is near-dominated by
R3 (R3 achieves everything R2 achieves plus R1's seam-integrity gain, at a cost premium the near-
dominance advisory flags but does not treat as decisive) and R1 remains on the frontier as the
strictly cheaper, lower-risk option for an Owner who prefers to defer the higher-cost merge.

5.5 What the compiler explicitly refuses to do. The compiler does not select. Selection among
frontier candidates is arch-decision's final authority, per Invariant Three; the compiler's
deliverable is the frontier itself, annotated with the dominance and near-dominance findings, handed
to arch-decision as structured evidence rather than as a recommendation with a name on it. This
refusal is not a limitation apologized for; it is the specific mechanism, per Part I §1.3, by which
the trade-off compiler strengthens arch-decision — giving it a comparable, evidence-backed frontier
instead of an intuition — without ever holding the decision right arch-decision alone retains.

5.6 Units and the import of F2's advantage algebra. Each of the compiler's four scored dimensions —
quality delta, fitness delta, implementation cost, risk — arrives from a different upstream engine in
a different native unit: Part III's quality delta is a four-element vector on a bounded 0-1 scale per
dimension, Part IV's fitness delta is a mission-cost-proxy reduction expressed in seam-crossings and
layer-inversions avoided, implementation cost is typically an effort estimate in whatever unit
arch-decision's own practice already uses, and risk (once sandboxed, Part X) is an observed-delta
confidence band rather than a point estimate. The compiler does not invent a fifth, bespoke
conversion; it imports F2's advantage algebra (Constitution-referenced foundation, not rebuilt here)
specifically for the one operation the frontier construction requires — placing four differently-
scaled deltas on the SAME relative-comparison axis long enough to test Pareto dominance — without
collapsing them into F2's own composite score, which would silently reintroduce the single-number
failure Part III §3.3 already refuses. (DERIVED: importing a comparison operation is a narrower,
safer act than importing a scoring operation, and the compiler is careful to use F2 for the former
only.)

5.7 Worked example: a wide frontier and what width itself communicates. Not every candidate set
resolves to a tight two- or three-entry frontier like Part V §5.4's worked case. Consider a second,
synthetic location, illustrative only, where the topology optimizer generates five structurally
distinct candidates for the same flagged coupling problem — two different RE-ROUTE targets, a SPLIT,
a MERGE with a different neighboring system than either re-route touches, and a combined RE-ROUTE-
then-SPLIT sequence. After dominance filtering (§5.3), four of the five survive as non-dominated: the
MERGE is removed because one of the RE-ROUTE candidates dominates it on every dimension the two share
and is strictly cheaper. A four-entry surviving frontier, compared to Part V §5.4's two-entry result
for a different location, is itself a signal worth surfacing rather than discarding: a wide,
non-collapsing frontier at one location and a narrow one at another suggests the first location has
genuinely multiple defensible architectural directions while the second has essentially one adequate
answer with a cheaper variant — information the trade-off compiler reports as a FRONTIER-WIDTH
annotation on the package (Part XIV §14.2), because a reviewer facing four live options benefits from
knowing that up front rather than discovering it only by reading all four entries in full.

FINAL LAW — PART V. A topology change is never judged on one criterion; the compiler expresses
every candidate as a point on a Pareto frontier across quality, fitness, cost, and risk, narrows it
only by strict dominance and flagged near-dominance, and hands the frontier — never a verdict — to
the one system with the authority to choose from it.

===============================================================================================
PART VI — THE ARCHITECTURE DRIFT DETECTOR: DECLARED VERSUS REAL TOPOLOGY
===============================================================================================

6.1 The gap between the map and the territory. Every system in the estate has a DECLARED topology
position — the relations recorded in its registry entry, its documentation, or the last arch-
decision that touched it — and a REAL topology position, the relations it actually exercises during
live missions, observable through IAS-A2's control-bus event log. These two are not guaranteed to
match, and the DRIFT DETECTOR is DEFINED as the engine that measures the divergence between them
and reports it as a first-class finding, because a federation that trusts its declared topology
without checking it against the real one is reasoning about a map that may no longer describe the
territory — precisely the failure F1 §3.3 warns against when it states that ensemble state is "a
hypothesis until reconciled against the disk."

6.2 Three drift signatures, each with a distinct cause and a distinct severity. EDGE GHOSTING is
DEFINED as a declared edge with no corresponding real-traffic evidence over a sustained observation
window — a FEEDS or DEPENDS-ON relation the registry still records but that no mission has actually
exercised, typically because the consuming system was refactored to use a different provider
without the declaration being updated. Edge ghosting is LOW-severity on its own (a stale record,
not a live risk) but feeds directly into IAS-E1's dead-capability sweep, per the compounding graph,
because a ghosted edge is often the first symptom of a capability quietly going unused. EDGE
SHADOWING is DEFINED as the inverse: a real, observed relation with no corresponding declaration at
all — a dependency that has grown in practice (often through an ad hoc integration added under
deadline pressure) without ever being recorded. Shadowing is HIGH-severity, because an undeclared
dependency is invisible to every mechanism that reasons over the declared graph, including the
complexity budget (Part VIII, which cannot price what it does not know exists) and the dependency
governor (Part XIII, which cannot flag it as excessive because it was never counted). COUPLING
MISCALIBRATION is DEFINED as a declared-and-real edge that matches in existence but not in coupling
score — a relation everyone agrees exists but that the registry records as loosely coupled (a
simple data feed) while the real interface has, over time, grown tightly coupled (the consuming
system now reaches into implementation details), or the reverse. Miscalibration is MEDIUM-severity
and is the hardest of the three to detect automatically, because it requires comparing an interface
CONTRACT against actual USAGE PATTERNS rather than merely comparing declared-edge lists against
observed-edge lists.

6.3 The detection mechanism, and what it reads rather than re-measures. The drift detector reads
two inputs it does not itself instrument: the DECLARED graph, sourced from the estate's own
registries (the same registries F1's property model and this family's own SYSTEM_REGISTRY document)
and the REAL graph, sourced from IAS-A2's control-bus event log aggregated over a rolling window
(the same source the fitness evaluator reads in Part IV §4.2, reused here rather than re-collected —
a direct instance of the strengthening mechanism F1 §4.4 names, where one shared fact serves
multiple consumers instead of each building its own measurement). The detector's core operation is
a graph diff between these two sources, typed by F1's six relation types so that a FEEDS ghost is
distinguished from a DEPENDS-ON ghost (the latter is more severe, because an unexercised critical
dependency is a stronger signal of stale declaration than an unexercised soft feed).

6.4 Worked example: a shadowing incident traced to its root. Consider a synthetic incident,
illustrative only. System T-44, declared to depend only on system T-12, is observed over a
sixty-day window to also issue calls consistent with a DEPENDS-ON relation to system T-58, a
relation absent from every registry entry. The drift detector flags this as EDGE SHADOWING,
HIGH-severity, and traces the observation window backward to identify when the real-traffic
pattern to T-58 began: the pattern is continuous from day one of the observation window with no
visible onset, suggesting the shadow relation predates the window itself rather than having been
introduced recently — a distinction that matters because a NEWLY shadowed edge (onset visible
within the window) points to a recent uncoordinated change worth investigating for cause, while an
OLD shadowed edge (no onset visible, present for the whole window) points to a registry that was
simply never updated at the relation's original creation, a documentation debt rather than an
uncoordinated recent change. In this synthetic case, the detector reports the old-shadow pattern,
routes the finding to arch-decision as a registry-correction item (a low-cost fix: declare the
relation that already exists) but ALSO routes it to the complexity budget (Part VIII) as a
retroactive correction, because T-58's true dependency count has been undercounted in every budget
calculation performed before the shadow was found, and Part VIII's budget ledger must be
recalculated once a shadow of this kind surfaces, not merely have the registry updated going forward.

6.5 Drift as a leading indicator, not only a hygiene finding. The detector's institutional value is
not merely correcting stale records; a rising rate of shadowing incidents across the estate, tracked
over time, is a leading indicator that the estate's change process itself has a gap — that
declarations are not being updated as a routine part of making a change, rather than as an
occasional oversight. (DERIVED: a single shadow is a data point; a rising shadow RATE is a process
signal, and the drift detector reports both the individual finding and the estate-wide rate as
separate outputs, because conflating them would bury the process signal inside a list of individual
corrections.) This rate becomes one of Part XX's core metrics.

6.6 Reconciliation cadence and its interaction with layer depth. The detector does not run
continuously against live traffic; it runs at a reconciliation cadence, and Part IV §4.3's staleness
discipline applies here directly — a drift finding is only as fresh as its last reconciliation. This
dataset does not fix one global cadence; it recommends, NORMATIVE, that cadence itself scale inversely
with layer depth (Part II §2.6): foundational-layer systems, whose shadow or ghost edges carry the
largest blast radius per Part VIII's per-layer budget logic, warrant more frequent reconciliation than
leaf-layer systems, where a stale drift reading is comparatively low-consequence. A single estate-wide
cadence, chosen for operational simplicity, would under-serve the foundational layer precisely where
the cost of missing a shadow longest is highest — the same layer-depth-weighted-attention principle
Part VIII §8.2 and Part XIII §13.4 both already apply, extended here to detection frequency rather
than to budget or scan-priority.

6.7 A second worked example: coupling miscalibration caught by contract-versus-usage comparison.
Consider a synthetic case, illustrative only, distinct from §6.4's shadowing example. System T-62
declares a FEEDS relation to T-70, documented at creation as "T-70 reads a summary field from T-62's
output" — a loosely-coupled, narrow contract. Eighteen months of incremental changes to T-70,
individually small and each separately approved by ordinary arch-decision review, have grown T-70's
actual usage to reach three additional fields from T-62's output structure, none of which were part
of the original documented contract, though the relation's TYPE (FEEDS) never changed and no single
change was large enough to trigger a re-declaration review on its own. The drift detector's coupling-
miscalibration check, comparing the declared contract's narrow field-scope against the control-bus-
observed real usage pattern, flags this relation as MEDIUM-severity miscalibration: the edge exists
and is correctly typed, but its true coupling has drifted well past what its declaration represents.
Unlike the T-44/T-58 shadow (§6.4), there is no missing declaration to add — the fix is a contract
RE-SCOPE, which the drift detector routes to the dependency governor (Part XIII §13.3) as a candidate
for exactly the SCOPE-NARROW-or-WIDEN correction that engine already performs, demonstrating that the
three drift signatures of §6.2 do not always route to the same downstream engine: ghosting and
shadowing feed the topology optimizer's PRUNE and declaration-only moves, while miscalibration
specifically feeds the governor's contract-scoping machinery, because only the latter concerns the
SCOPE of an edge that already correctly exists.

FINAL LAW — PART VI. The declared topology is a claim and the real topology, read from actual
mission traffic, is the check on that claim; ghosting, shadowing, and miscalibration are three
distinct failure signatures with three distinct severities, and a federation that never diffs its
map against its territory is trusting a document it has not looked at in longer than it thinks.

===============================================================================================
PART VII — THE INSTITUTIONAL TOPOLOGY OPTIMIZER: CONNECTIONS BETWEEN SYSTEMS
===============================================================================================

7.1 The engine that acts on what Parts III-VI observe. Quality science measures, the fitness
evaluator scores against mission mix, the trade-off compiler frontier-ranks candidates, and the
drift detector finds where declaration and reality diverge — each of these is an OBSERVING or
RANKING engine. The TOPOLOGY OPTIMIZER is DEFINED as the engine that GENERATES candidate topology
changes for the compiler to rank, closing the loop between measurement and proposal. Without it,
Parts III-VI would produce an accurate diagnosis with no treatment plan; the optimizer is what
converts "seam integrity is degrading in this slice" into "here are three concrete re-routes that
would repair it," each of which the trade-off compiler then frontier-ranks per Part V.

7.2 The optimizer's generative moves, a closed and named set. The optimizer proposes exactly four
move types, each a specific, mechanical transformation of the topology graph, chosen because each
directly targets one of Part III's four quality dimensions or one of Part VI's drift signatures.
RE-ROUTE moves an edge's endpoint from one system to a functionally-equivalent system that better
respects seam boundaries — the move Part III §3.5's T-06-through-T-10 example and Part VI §6.4's
shadow-correction both call for. SPLIT proposes dividing one low-cohesion system (Part II §2.4)
into two or more higher-cohesion systems along a cohesion fault line the optimizer detects by
clustering the system's capability signature into sub-groups with low internal coupling to each
other. MERGE proposes combining two high-coupling, high-cohesion-when-combined systems into one,
the move Part V §5.4's Candidate R2 exemplifies. PRUNE proposes removing a ghosted edge (Part VI
§6.2) once the drift detector confirms sustained non-use, converting a stale declaration into an
accurate one without any live-traffic impact. (NORMATIVE: these four moves are deliberately
exhaustive and deliberately minimal — a fifth move type would need to justify itself against
Article VI's padding prohibition the same way Part II's five objects justified themselves against
vocabulary inflation.)

7.3 How the optimizer selects WHERE to propose a move, not only WHAT move to propose. A naive
optimizer would propose moves everywhere a quality dimension could theoretically improve, flooding
arch-decision with more candidates than any human or agent reviewer could triage. The optimizer
instead ranks CANDIDATE LOCATIONS by a MARGINAL-IMPACT SCORE before generating moves at all: the
product of (a) how far the location's local quality-vector reading sits from the estate's rolling
baseline (Part III §3.4 — a location already near-baseline yields little marginal gain from
intervention) and (b) the location's mission-cost proxy under the fitness evaluator (Part IV §4.4 —
a location rarely traversed by real missions yields little practical value even if its structural
quality is poor). Only locations scoring above a threshold on BOTH factors generate move proposals;
a structurally poor but mission-irrelevant corner of the topology is deliberately left alone, not
because it is healthy but because intervening there would not pay for itself — this is the
optimizer's own internal application of the opportunity-cost discipline the wider CPP-IAS family
assigns to IAS-C2, imported here as a filtering heuristic rather than rebuilt as a second economics
engine.

7.4 The seam-preservation constraint: what the optimizer is forbidden from proposing. Because Part
II §2.5 defines a healthy seam as a resource to preserve, the optimizer carries a hard constraint,
NORMATIVE and non-negotiable within this engine: no generated move may REDUCE seam integrity at a
location where it is already healthy, even if doing so would improve a different dimension at that
location. A merge that would improve cohesion by combining two systems that straddle a healthy seam
is not proposed by this engine at all — it fails the constraint before reaching the trade-off
compiler, rather than reaching the compiler and being correctly out-competed on the frontier, because
seam-crossing merges are disproportionately expensive to reverse once evolution_engine executes
them (Part XI), and the optimizer is designed to avoid generating candidates whose downside, if
wrongly selected, is structurally hard to undo.

7.5 Worked example: the optimizer generating from a drift finding. Returning to Part VI §6.4's
T-44/T-58 shadow-edge finding, the optimizer receives the drift detector's output and evaluates it
against the four move types. A RE-ROUTE is not applicable (the shadow edge is real and load-bearing,
not misplaced). A SPLIT is not applicable (the finding concerns an edge, not a system's internal
cohesion). A MERGE is evaluated and rejected: T-44 and T-58 sit in different seams under the
current topology reading, and merging across a seam the quality science still reads as healthy
would violate the seam-preservation constraint of §7.4. A PRUNE is not applicable either — the
shadow edge is real-traffic-confirmed, not a ghost to be removed. The optimizer's actual output for
this finding is therefore not a topology MOVE at all but a DECLARATION-ONLY recommendation: register
the T-44 → T-58 DEPENDS-ON edge as it actually exists, leaving the topology's shape untouched and
correcting only the estate's record of it — the optimizer correctly recognizes that not every
drift finding calls for a structural intervention, and generating a forced move where none of the
four types genuinely applies would be manufacturing work rather than closing a real gap.

7.6 Interaction with evolution_engine and the sequencing question. The optimizer's output — a set
of candidate moves at ranked locations — feeds the trade-off compiler (Part V) for frontier
ranking and, once arch-decision selects from that frontier, feeds evolution_engine for execution.
The optimizer does not itself sequence multiple approved moves against each other when several are
approved in the same window; that sequencing is the evolution planner's distinct responsibility
(Part XI), because sequencing is a temporal-ordering problem (which move first, given that some
moves change the topology the next move would be evaluated against) distinct from the optimizer's
generative problem (which moves exist at all). (DERIVED: separating generation from sequencing
mirrors F1 §2.3's system/subsystem boundary discipline — two genuinely distinct responsibilities
kept as two engines rather than one over-broad one.)

7.7 Why the optimizer generates candidates rather than a single best guess. A tempting simplification
would have the optimizer return, per flagged location, its single highest-scoring move rather than a
set for the trade-off compiler to rank — collapsing Part V's frontier construction into a one-shot
recommendation. This dataset rejects that simplification structurally: the optimizer's own scoring at
generation time (§7.3's marginal-impact filter) evaluates only two factors — baseline distance and
mission-cost proxy — and deliberately does NOT weigh implementation cost, sandbox risk, or debt-ledger
context, because those three factors are the trade-off compiler's own inputs (Part V §5.2), assembled
from engines the optimizer itself does not consult. An optimizer that pre-selected a single winner
before those factors were weighed would be making the compiler's decision for it under a different
name — the same authority-boundary discipline Part I §1.3 applies to arch-decision applies here, one
level earlier in the pipeline, between two of this dataset's OWN engines rather than between this
dataset and a parent.

7.8 Generation is stateless per cycle; it does not remember its own prior rejections as a bias. Each
reconciliation cycle, the optimizer re-evaluates every FLAGGED location fresh against the current
marginal-impact filter, rather than carrying forward a discount or penalty against a location whose
prior candidate was rejected or withdrawn. (NORMATIVE: this statelessness is deliberate — Part XV
§15.6's retained provenance history exists precisely so that PATTERN information about repeated
findings is available to whichever engine needs it, Part XVIII §18.5's resubmission-pattern detection
being the concrete example, without requiring the optimizer's own generative logic to carry an implicit
grudge against a location. A generator that quietly deprioritized a location because its last proposal
was rejected would conflate "this specific candidate was not chosen" with "this location does not merit
future attention," two claims Part XVII §17.6's genuine-tie discussion already shows can diverge for
reasons having nothing to do with the location's own merit.)

FINAL LAW — PART VII. The optimizer generates from a closed set of four moves, filtered by marginal
impact against the mission-weighted baseline and bounded by a hard seam-preservation constraint;
it proposes structural change only where the diagnosis genuinely calls for one, and it hands every
proposal to the trade-off compiler rather than to evolution_engine directly, because generating a
candidate and selecting among candidates remain two different acts held by two different systems.

===============================================================================================
PART VIII — THE COMPLEXITY BUDGET FOR THE ESTATE
===============================================================================================

8.1 Why complexity needs a budget and not only a metric. Part III's quality science measures
coupling density as a distribution; it does not, on its own, say how much coupling the estate can
AFFORD before the topology's complexity begins costing more than the capability it delivers is
worth. The COMPLEXITY BUDGET is DEFINED as an explicit ceiling, expressed in the same coupling and
layer-depth units Part II defines, against which every proposed edge, system, or layer addition is
priced BEFORE it is approved — converting complexity from a quantity merely observed after the fact
(Part III) into a quantity constrained in advance, the same shift F2's advantage algebra makes for
cost generally, applied here specifically to structural complexity.

8.2 The budget's unit of account and why it is per-layer, not estate-flat. A single estate-wide
complexity ceiling would under-price complexity added at deep layers (Part II §2.6) relative to
complexity added at shallow, leaf-layer positions, because a new depends-on edge at a foundational
layer carries a larger failure-propagation blast radius than the identical edge type at a leaf
layer. The budget is therefore DEFINED per-layer: each layer carries its own coupling-density
ceiling, with foundational layers carrying a STRICTER (lower) ceiling than leaf layers, DERIVED
directly from Part II §2.6's propagation-depth definition — the deeper a layer's foundational role,
the smaller the complexity margin the estate can safely spend there before a single additional edge
materially raises the blast radius of every system above it.

8.3 Spending and reconciling the budget: the ledger mechanic. Every approved topology change that
adds an edge or a system DEBITS the relevant layer's budget by that change's coupling-density
contribution, computed by the trade-off compiler at approval time (Part V); every PRUNE or
successful complexity-reducing move CREDITS the budget. The budget is therefore a running LEDGER,
not a static allocation, and — per F1 §4.3's staleness discipline, applied here to a ledger rather
than a single property — the ledger's accuracy depends on every approved change actually being
recorded, which is precisely why Part VI's shadow-edge correction (§6.4) triggers a RETROACTIVE
budget recalculation rather than only a going-forward registry fix: an undercounted historical edge
means the ledger has been silently over-optimistic about remaining budget for as long as the shadow
existed undetected.

8.4 Overspend as a gate, not a silent overrun. When a proposed change's debit would exceed a
layer's remaining budget, the change does not proceed to evolution_engine automatically blocked —
IAS-G1 holds no final authority to block arch-decision's decisions (Part I §1.3) — but the trade-
off compiler's frontier presentation (Part V §5.2) carries a mandatory OVERSPEND FLAG on any
candidate that would exhaust or exceed the relevant layer's budget, forcing the overspend to be a
visible, named factor in arch-decision's final choice rather than a consequence discovered only
after the fact when the layer's fragility has already increased. (NORMATIVE: the flag is
mandatory-to-surface, never mandatory-to-obey — Article IX's governed self-evolution principle
applies here exactly: IAS-G1 proposes and flags, the Owner-authorized decision process promotes or
overrides.)

8.5 Worked example: a budget-constrained trade-off. Consider a synthetic case, illustrative only,
in which the Foundations layer (analogous to F1's own tier) carries a budget ceiling of 0.35
average coupling density and currently sits at 0.31 — a thin remaining margin of 0.04. A proposed
new DEPENDS-ON edge from a Tier-2 system directly onto a Foundations-layer system, needed to solve
a real integration problem, is priced by the trade-off compiler at a coupling-density contribution
of 0.06 — an overspend of 0.02 past the layer's remaining margin. The compiler does not reject the
candidate; it presents it on the frontier with the overspend flag attached, alongside an
ALTERNATIVE candidate the topology optimizer separately generated (per Part VII's four move types):
a RE-ROUTE through an existing Tier-1 intermediary system that already depends on the Foundations
layer, adding the new relation one layer up instead of directly at the foundation, at a coupling-
density cost of only 0.01 against Tier-1's much larger remaining margin. Both candidates solve the
stated integration problem; only the re-route respects the budget. arch-decision, presented with
both and the explicit overspend flag on the first, retains full authority to choose either — the
budget informs, it does not decide — but it can no longer choose the direct edge without having
seen, in the same frontier, that it is spending scarce foundational margin when a cheaper
alternative exists one layer up.

8.6 Budget reset and the risk of a ratchet that never loosens. A complexity budget that only ever
tightens, never widens, would eventually forbid the estate from adding any new structural relation
at all regardless of genuine need — the architecture-budget equivalent of a governance ratchet.
This dataset marks explicitly, HYPOTHESIZED pending real operating history, that the per-layer
ceiling should be periodically RECALIBRATED against the estate's demonstrated capacity to absorb
complexity without a corresponding rise in IAS-D2's failure fingerprints or IAS-E2's failure-budget
consumption — if a layer has run near its ceiling for a sustained period with no measurable
reliability degradation, the ceiling itself may be a conservative estimate rather than a true
capacity limit, and Part XIX names the failure mode of a budget that is never revisited as a
specific pathology this dataset must guard against in its own mechanism, not only in the topology
it governs.

8.7 Edges that span more than one layer. Part II §2.6 defines layer by depends-on depth, and the
ordinary case debits a change against the layer of the EDGE'S DEEPER endpoint — a DEPENDS-ON edge
from a Tier-2 system onto a Foundations-layer system debits the Foundations budget, per §8.2's logic
that the deeper endpoint determines blast radius. A less ordinary but real case is a single proposed
relation that itself SPANS multiple layers because it is, in substance, a shortcut — a new edge from
a leaf-layer system directly to a foundational one, skipping every intermediate layer a well-formed
topology would ordinarily route the relation through. Such a LAYER-SKIPPING edge is debited against
the deepest layer it touches, per the general rule, but it ALSO trips Part III §3.2's layer-cleanliness
dimension independently, because a layer-skip is, by that metric's own definition, an edge that fails
to respect layer ordering regardless of whether its raw coupling-density cost fits within budget. A
change can therefore be budget-compliant (within its layer's remaining margin) and simultaneously
quality-degrading (worsening layer cleanliness) — a case the trade-off compiler's frontier (Part V)
must surface as two SEPARATE findings rather than netting them into one composite verdict, consistent
with Part III §3.3's refusal to compose the quality vector.

8.8 The budget's relationship to the mutation sandbox: testing without spending. Because the budget
ledger only debits on APPROVAL (§8.3), a candidate that reaches SANDBOXED (Part X) without yet being
approved has not yet cost the layer anything, which is a deliberate design choice: the sandbox lets a
candidate's real, observed effect on mission-cost and quality be measured BEFORE the estate commits
budget to it, meaning a candidate that fails its sandbox trial and is subsequently abandoned (Part X
§10.6) never touches the ledger at all. This ordering — sandbox before spend, not spend before
sandbox — is what keeps the budget honest as a record of REAL commitments rather than a record
inflated by trials that were never actually adopted, and it is the specific reason this dataset places
the SANDBOXED lifecycle state (Part XV §15.2) strictly before APPROVED rather than after.

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. Complexity is priced per-layer, deeper layers pay more per edge because they
propagate failure further, every approved change debits the ledger and every prune credits it, and
an overspend is surfaced as a mandatory flag on the decision frontier — never a silent block and
never a silent overrun, because a budget nobody can see is not a budget, it is a hope.

===============================================================================================
PART IX — THE ARCHITECTURE DEBT MARKET
===============================================================================================

9.1 From budget to debt: the difference between spending ahead and spending without accounting. Part
VIII prices complexity BEFORE a change is approved; the ARCHITECTURE DEBT MARKET is DEFINED as the
mechanism that tracks complexity ALREADY SPENT beyond what a system's or a topology region's current
value justifies — the accumulated gap between the coupling and cohesion the estate would choose if
it were redesigning from scratch and the coupling and cohesion it actually carries, expressed as a
tradeable, priced instrument rather than a vague sense that "this area is messy." The debt market
does not prevent debt from being taken on — sometimes a locally expedient shape is the right call
under time pressure, exactly as financial debt can be a rational tool — it makes the debt VISIBLE,
PRICED, and, critically, RETIRABLE through a defined process rather than permanent by default.

9.2 Issuing debt: when a change knowingly trades future complexity for present speed. A DEBT
ENTRY is DEFINED as created whenever arch-decision approves a change the trade-off compiler flagged
as complexity-negative (a candidate off the Pareto frontier's healthy region, or a candidate that
triggered Part VIII's overspend flag and was chosen anyway) — the debt entry records the quality-
vector delta the choice cost, the layer and coupling-density amount by which the change exceeded
what the budget preferred, and the STATED REASON the choice was made anyway (a mandatory field,
NORMATIVE, because a debt entry with no stated reason is indistinguishable from an unnoticed
mistake, and this dataset needs to distinguish the two for the same honesty reasons F1's property
model demands a source for every claim). Debt is therefore never issued silently; it is issued only
at the moment a frontier candidate carrying an overspend or quality-degradation flag is knowingly
selected, which means the debt market's ledger is, by construction, a strict subset of decisions
the trade-off compiler already surfaced — no new instrumentation, only a new accounting layer over
decisions already made visible.

9.3 Pricing debt: interest as ongoing coupling cost, not a one-time penalty. A debt entry's PRICE is
not fixed at issuance; it accrues, DERIVED from Part III §3.4's trend discipline: as long as the
debt-carrying edge or system remains in the topology, it continues to consume a share of its
layer's complexity budget (Part VIII) every reconciliation cycle, and if the mission-cost proxy
(Part IV §4.4) for missions traversing that debt-carrying location rises over time — more missions
depending on the same fragile shape — the debt's effective price rises with it, because the blast
radius of eventually fixing it grows in proportion to how much has since come to depend on the
shape as-is. (NORMATIVE: pricing debt as accruing interest rather than a flat one-time charge is
what motivates timely retirement over indefinite deferral — a debt entry that never got more
expensive to carry would never compete successfully against other priorities on any portfolio
ranking IAS-C1 might apply.)

9.4 Retiring debt: the market's exit mechanism. A debt entry is RETIRED when a later topology
optimizer move (Part VII) specifically targets the debt-carrying location and the trade-off
compiler confirms the resulting quality-vector delta closes the original gap the entry recorded.
Retirement is recorded with the same rigor as issuance — the closing move, the resulting quality
delta, and the total accrued price paid before retirement — because an estate that tracks debt
issuance without tracking retirement can never answer the portfolio-level question IAS-C1 needs
answered: is the estate's total architectural debt load rising or falling release over release, and
which debt entries are the highest-priority retirement candidates given their accrued price. This
last question is exactly where the debt market feeds IAS-C1 as a distinct input alongside the
portfolio system's other signals (per the Strengthening Matrix, though C1 is not among G1's four
named-strengthened parents, the debt ledger is available to any consumer per the compounding
graph's general principle that a producer does not gate who may read a shared fact).

9.5 Worked example: one debt entry from issuance to retirement. Consider a synthetic entry,
illustrative only. At mission-window M14, arch-decision approves a direct point-to-point FEEDS edge
between systems T-27 and T-33 under deadline pressure, chosen from the trade-off compiler's frontier
over a cheaper-in-coupling-terms but slower-to-implement re-route through an intermediary; the debt
entry records a coupling-density overspend of 0.03 against T-27's layer budget, stated reason
"deadline M14, re-route deferred," and an initial price of 0.03 layer-budget-units. Over the
following four reconciliation cycles, the mission-cost proxy for missions traversing T-27 → T-33
rises as two new mission patterns begin routing through the same edge, and the debt's accrued price
rises to 0.11 layer-budget-units by cycle five — nearly four times its issuance price — at which
point the topology optimizer, scanning for high marginal-impact locations (Part VII §7.3), flags
T-27 → T-33 as a high-priority re-route candidate precisely because its accrued debt price now
dominates the marginal-impact score. The re-route is generated, frontier-ranked, approved, and
executed by evolution_engine; the debt entry is retired at cycle six, with the ledger recording a
total accrued cost of 0.11 units paid across five cycles before resolution — a concrete, auditable
number the estate would have had no way to compute without the market's continuous pricing, because
a debt entry priced only at issuance (0.03) would have understated, by more than three-fold, what
deferring the fix actually cost.

9.6 Portfolio-level aggregation: reading the debt market as one number when one number is actually
appropriate. Part III §3.3 refuses to compose the quality vector into a single score, and that refusal
does not extend automatically to the debt market, because debt is, by construction, already a single
priced unit (layer-budget-units) rather than a multi-dimensional vector — aggregating debt entries
into a TOTAL OUTSTANDING DEBT figure, summed across the estate or filtered per layer, is not the same
category of collapse Part III warns against, since debt price is defined in one unit from the moment
of issuance rather than reduced FROM several incommensurable dimensions. IAS-C1's portfolio ranking
(Part XXII §22.3) is the intended consumer of this aggregate, using total outstanding debt per system
or per layer as one input alongside cost, ROI, and recall signals it already reads from other parents
— a case where a single number is the right interface specifically because the debt market's own
internal unit discipline (§9.3) already earned that number's validity, unlike a quality score that
would have to manufacture validity by force.

9.7 Write-off: when a debt entry is retired without ever being repaid. Not every debt entry closes
through Part IX §9.4's ordinary retirement path. A debt-carrying edge or system can become IRRELEVANT
before it is ever fixed — for instance, the mission pattern that made it costly stops occurring
entirely, observed through IAS-A2's control-bus log going quiet for that pattern over a sustained
window. When this happens, the debt market performs a WRITE-OFF, DEFINED as closing the entry with a
stated reason of "underlying mission pattern retired" rather than "structural fix applied," and
recording the entry's final accrued price as the total cost the estate paid for a debt that was
ultimately dissolved by circumstance rather than resolved by architecture. (NORMATIVE: a write-off is
recorded with the same rigor as a retirement, never silently deleted from the ledger, because a debt
market that quietly erases entries whose mission pattern happened to disappear would understate, in
its historical record, how much genuine risk the estate carried during the period the debt was live —
the risk was real even though it never had to be paid down.) Write-offs are tracked as a distinct
category from retirements in any portfolio-level reporting, because a high write-off rate relative to
retirements is itself a signal — possibly that debt is being issued against locations whose mission
relevance is more volatile than the issuing decision accounted for, a pattern Part XX's metric set
is positioned to track once sufficient write-off history accumulates.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. Debt is issued only at a knowing, stated-reason choice off the healthy
frontier, priced as accruing interest tied to rising mission-cost exposure rather than a flat
one-time charge, and retired only when a later move closes the gap it recorded — making
architectural shortcuts visible, comparable, and time-pressured toward resolution instead of
permanent by silent default.

===============================================================================================
PART X — THE MUTATION SANDBOX
===============================================================================================

10.1 Why a topology move needs to be tried before it is committed. Every engine so far — the
optimizer generating moves, the compiler ranking them, the budget and debt market pricing them —
reasons about a candidate move's PREDICTED effect on the quality vector and fitness score. A
prediction is not the same as an observation, and Article X's prohibition on this mission mutating
the real estate does not mean the wider CPP-IAS runtime, once built, should ever commit a
topology-level mutation on the strength of prediction alone — a merge or split, once executed by
evolution_engine, is expensive to reverse (Part VII §7.4 already names this as the reason the
optimizer avoids seam-crossing merges specifically). The MUTATION SANDBOX is DEFINED as the isolated
environment in which a candidate move is trialed against a REPLAY of observed mission traffic before
evolution_engine is asked to execute it against the live topology, converting the compiler's
predicted quality and fitness deltas (Parts III-V) into an OBSERVED delta computed against real,
historical mission patterns run through the candidate shape.

10.2 What the sandbox actually does: replay, not simulation-from-scratch. The sandbox does not
generate synthetic missions to test a candidate against — that generative task belongs to IAS-F3's
digital twin, a distinct sibling this dataset explicitly does not duplicate (Part XXI specifies the
boundary in full). The sandbox instead REPLAYS the same observed mission-pattern log the fitness
evaluator already reads (Part IV §4.2, IAS-A2's control-bus event log) through the CANDIDATE
topology shape instead of the live one, recomputing the mission-cost proxy for every replayed
mission pattern under the candidate shape and comparing it directly against the same proxy computed
under the current, live shape. (DERIVED: this is a narrower, cheaper operation than full scenario
simulation — it answers "how would our actual recent history have gone under this candidate shape,"
not "how would a hypothetical future scenario go," which is exactly the boundary that keeps the
sandbox a component of G1's mutation-trial pipeline rather than a rebuild of F3's forecasting
engine.)

10.3 What the sandbox cannot observe, named honestly. Replay against historical mission traffic
validates a candidate's effect on already-observed patterns; it structurally cannot validate the
candidate's effect on a mission pattern that has never yet occurred, nor can it observe second-order
effects that only manifest after other systems have had time to adapt their own behavior to the new
shape (a system that, over weeks, begins relying more heavily on a newly-cheaper path once a
re-route makes it cheaper — an adaptation the sandbox's one-shot replay cannot capture because it
requires observing behavior change over time, not a single replayed window). This limitation is
marked UNRESOLVED rather than papered over: the sandbox's observed delta is a STRONGER evidence
class than the compiler's predicted delta (OBSERVED beats DERIVED under Article V's vocabulary) but
it is not a guarantee of post-execution reality, and Part XVII names the specific adversarial and
edge cases this gap creates.

10.4 The sandbox as a gate on the highest-risk move types, not a universal requirement. Requiring a
full sandbox trial for every candidate, including trivial ones (a single PRUNE of a confirmed ghost
edge, Part VII §7.2), would impose sandbox cost disproportionate to the risk being managed. The
sandbox is therefore REQUIRED, NORMATIVE, for MERGE and SPLIT moves — the two move types Part VII
§7.4 already flags as structurally hard to reverse — and OPTIONAL, at arch-decision's discretion,
for RE-ROUTE and PRUNE moves, whose reversal cost is low enough that a mispredicted delta can simply
be corrected by a follow-up move rather than requiring pre-commitment validation. (DERIVED: risk-
proportionate gating is the same discipline the estate already applies via its spec-gate mechanism
to task sizing, generalized here from task size to move-type reversal cost.)

10.5 Worked example: a merge candidate that fails its sandbox trial. Recall Part V §5.4's Candidate
R2, the proposed merge of T-08 and T-09. Predicted by the trade-off compiler: cohesion spread
improves substantially, coupling density improves, fitness delta near zero because the observed
mission mix rarely invokes either system directly. The merge, being a MERGE move, is required to
pass sandbox trial before proceeding. Replayed against the sixty-day mission log, the sandbox
surfaces a pattern the compiler's static analysis had not weighted heavily: in twelve of 340
replayed mission instances, T-08 and T-09 are invoked SEPARATELY, by different mission patterns,
each expecting a response latency profile suited to its own current, narrower interface; under the
merged candidate shape, both mission patterns route through the same combined interface, and the
replay shows a measurable mission-cost proxy INCREASE for these twelve instances even though the
aggregate quality vector still improves — a genuine trade-off the static frontier ranking, computed
from aggregate deltas, had smoothed over. The sandbox's observed delta is reported back to the
trade-off compiler as a REVISED entry on the frontier (not a rejection — twelve of 340 is a minority
pattern and may still be an acceptable cost), but it is now a frontier entry backed by OBSERVED
rather than merely DERIVED evidence, and arch-decision's eventual choice, whichever way it goes, is
made with the minority-pattern cost explicitly visible rather than averaged away.

10.6 Sandbox failure as institutional learning, not wasted work. A candidate that fails its sandbox
trial — shows a worse observed delta than predicted, or surfaces a cost the static analysis missed —
is not discarded silently; the GAP between the compiler's predicted delta and the sandbox's observed
delta is itself recorded and fed back to Part III's quality science and Part V's compiler as a
CALIBRATION signal, because a systematic gap direction (predictions consistently optimistic about
merges, for instance) is evidence the compiler's weighting of the four quality dimensions needs
adjustment for that move type specifically. (EXPERIMENTAL: this calibration loop is proposed but its
convergence behavior — whether repeated calibration narrows the predicted-versus-observed gap over
time or merely tracks noise — is a claim this dataset marks EXPERIMENTAL pending the evaluation
protocol of Part XXI.)

10.7 The sandbox's own cost, and why it is not free to run indiscriminately. Replaying a full
observed-history window (Part XVIII §18.3 fixes this window's minimum length specifically to defeat
cherry-picking) against a candidate topology shape is a real computational and evidentiary-assembly
cost, not a zero-cost formality — every replayed mission pattern requires recomputing its mission-cost
proxy under the candidate shape, and a long history window against a high-traffic location can be
substantial. This is the concrete reason §10.4 scopes the mandatory requirement to MERGE and SPLIT
only rather than all four move types: the sandbox's own cost must itself be proportionate to the risk
it manages, the same proportionality discipline Part VIII §8.5 applies to the budget ledger's overspend
flag and Part XIX §19.5 names as a general design commitment across this dataset's mechanisms — a
sandbox required for every trivial PRUNE would violate that commitment as surely as a budget check
triggered on every trivial change would.

10.8 Sandbox trials as a corpus of their own: what accumulates across many trials over time. Every
sandbox trial, whether the candidate is ultimately approved, revised, or abandoned, is retained (per
Part XV §15.6's general retention discipline) as part of a growing TRIAL CORPUS specific to move type
and location class. This corpus is what Part X §10.6's calibration loop and Part XXI §21.3's sandbox-
fidelity benchmark both depend on to detect a systematic predicted-versus-observed gap across MANY
trials rather than judging the sandbox's reliability from any single trial in isolation — a single
trial's divergence from prediction could be genuine noise; a systematic direction across dozens of
MERGE trials specifically is a calibration signal, and the distinction between the two readings is
only available once enough trials have accumulated to distinguish a trend from an outlier, which is
precisely why this dataset treats the sandbox's own historical trial record as an asset in its own
right rather than a disposable byproduct of each individual decision.

FINAL LAW — PART X. A merge or split predicted as an improvement is a hypothesis until replayed
against real mission history inside the sandbox; the sandbox cannot see a future that has not
happened or an adaptation that has not yet occurred, but it converts a predicted delta into an
observed one for exactly the two move types expensive enough to regret, and a failed trial is
calibration for the next prediction, never wasted work.

===============================================================================================
PART XI — THE EVOLUTION PLANNER: SEQUENCING INCREMENTAL TRANSITIONS
===============================================================================================

11.1 The problem sequencing solves that generation and ranking do not. Part VII generates candidate
moves; Part V ranks them; Part X validates the risky ones. None of these three answer a question
that only arises once MULTIPLE moves are approved for the same change window: in what ORDER should
they be executed, given that executing one move changes the topology the next move's own predicted
and even sandboxed deltas were computed against. The EVOLUTION PLANNER is DEFINED as the engine that
sequences a set of approved moves into an ordered, incremental transition path, handing evolution_
engine one executable step at a time rather than a batch whose combined effect was never itself
validated as a unit.

11.2 Why sequencing is not merely execution order but a search problem. Given N approved moves,
there are up to N-factorial possible execution orders, and different orders can produce genuinely
different intermediate topologies — a re-route executed before a merge can leave the merge with a
cleaner starting shape than the same merge executed first, or the reverse, depending on which edges
each move touches. The planner's search is bounded, DERIVED from Part VII §7.6's generation-versus-
sequencing separation, by two constraints rather than an exhaustive search of every ordering:
DEPENDENCY ORDERING, which requires that if move B's candidate shape assumes move A's edge already
exists (a re-route that targets a system a prior merge created), A must execute before B by
construction, not by choice; and RISK-DESCENDING ORDERING among moves with no dependency
relationship, which places lower-risk moves (per Part X's sandbox-validated risk estimates) earlier
in the sequence, so that if a later, higher-risk move needs to be paused or rolled back, the
already-executed lower-risk moves remain independently sound rather than half-committed alongside
a failed high-risk step.

11.3 The intermediate-state validity requirement. Every step in the planner's sequence must leave
the topology in a state that satisfies F1 Part V's five invariants — no orphan system, no
unresolved duplicate, singular authority per decision class, boundary respected, property honesty —
even though the FULL set of moves has not yet completed. (NORMATIVE, DERIVED from F1's own
invariant discipline: an invariant that only needs to hold at the beginning and end of a transition,
and may be violated mid-sequence, is not really an invariant, and this dataset extends F1's
legality requirement to every intermediate step of a planned evolution rather than only to the
final target state.) A sequence in which step three of five would temporarily orphan a system — for
instance, a split executed before its planned re-route, leaving one of the two split-off systems
with zero edges for the duration between steps — is not a valid plan under this requirement, and the
planner must either reorder the steps or insert a bridging step (a temporary edge, later pruned)
that preserves legality throughout.

11.4 Rollback points and the planner's relationship to the debt market. Each step in the sequence is
defined with an explicit ROLLBACK POINT — the prior topology state the estate returns to if the step,
once executed by evolution_engine, produces a real-traffic outcome diverging materially from its
sandboxed prediction (Part X §10.6's calibration signal, applied here as a live trigger rather than
only a post-hoc learning input). A step that is rolled back is recorded, per Part IX's debt
discipline, as neither a clean success nor simple noise: if the rollback was necessary because the
step's real effect diverged from even its sandboxed prediction, that divergence itself becomes a
debt-market entry — an unplanned cost the estate paid in execution and rollback effort — distinct
from the ordinary debt entries Part IX describes for knowingly-accepted trade-offs, and marked in
the ledger as EXECUTION DEBT rather than DECISION DEBT, so the two causes (a knowing trade-off
versus a genuine prediction failure) are never conflated in later portfolio-level reporting.

11.5 Worked example: sequencing three moves into a four-step plan. Consider a synthetic change
window, illustrative only, in which three moves are approved together: a SPLIT of a low-cohesion
system T-51 into T-51a and T-51b; a RE-ROUTE of an existing edge from T-51 (pre-split) onto T-51b
specifically, once it exists; and an unrelated PRUNE of a confirmed ghost edge elsewhere in the
topology with no dependency on either of the first two moves. Dependency ordering requires the split
to precede the re-route, because the re-route's target, T-51b, does not exist until the split
executes. The unrelated prune has no dependency relationship with either and is risk-negligible
(Part X §10.4 — prunes do not require sandbox validation), so risk-descending ordering places it
FIRST, executing the safest, most independently reversible step before either of the two
dependency-linked steps begins. The planner's four-step sequence is therefore: (1) prune the
confirmed ghost edge; (2) execute the split of T-51, with a bridging edge temporarily preserving
T-51a's connectivity to what was T-51's sole prior consumer, satisfying the no-orphan invariant
through the transition; (3) execute the re-route onto T-51b; (4) prune the bridging edge from step
two, now redundant once step three's re-route is confirmed live and stable across one reconciliation
cycle. Each step is independently invariant-legal; each carries its own rollback point; and the
four-step ordering was derived mechanically from the dependency and risk constraints, not chosen by
inspection.

11.6 What the planner does not decide, one more time, for emphasis. As with every engine in this
dataset, the planner sequences moves already approved by arch-decision from the trade-off compiler's
frontier; it does not itself decide which moves to approve, and evolution_engine, not the planner,
performs each step's actual execution. The planner's distinct contribution is purely temporal: it is
the difference between handing evolution_engine a batch and a bridge — a set of steps that, executed
in the sequence the planner derives, never leaves the estate's topology in an illegal or unnecessarily
fragile intermediate state.

11.7 When the planner cannot find a legal sequence at all. §11.3's intermediate-state validity
requirement is not always satisfiable by reordering or bridging alone: a set of approved moves can, in
principle, contain a genuine circular dependency — move A's candidate shape assumes move B's edge
already exists, while move B's candidate shape simultaneously assumes move A's — a configuration no
ordering can satisfy without a bridge for BOTH sides at once. When this occurs, the planner does not
silently pick an arbitrary order and accept a transient invariant violation; it returns an UNSEQUENCEABLE
finding to arch-decision, naming the specific circular pair, because F1's invariants (Part XI §11.3)
are non-negotiable at every intermediate step, and a genuinely circular approval set means at least one
of the two moves was approved on the basis of a candidate shape that implicitly assumed a topology state
that cannot legally be reached — an approval-time error the planner surfaces rather than papers over,
returning the finding to the SAME two lifecycle records (Part XV §15.3b's parallel-lifecycle mechanism)
for re-evaluation rather than forcing one through.

11.8 The planner's own complexity growth and why it does not scale unboundedly. §11.2 bounds the search
by dependency and risk ordering rather than exhaustive N-factorial enumeration, and this bound is what
keeps the planner's own computational cost proportionate even as the number of simultaneously-approved
moves in a change window grows — a practical concern this dataset names explicitly because an evolution
planner whose own cost scaled combinatorially with approval volume would become, ironically, exactly
the kind of governance bottleneck Part XIX §19.5 warns the complexity budget itself could become if its
proportionality erodes. The two-constraint bound (dependency ordering, a partial order computed in time
proportional to the number of edges between moves; risk-descending ordering, a simple sort) keeps the
planner's own cost linear-to-modest in the number of approved moves per window, a design property this
dataset considers as load-bearing as the sequencing logic itself, because a mechanism that cannot
scale to the estate's own eventual approval volume would not remain useful as that volume grows.

FINAL LAW — PART XI. N approved moves are not one batch but an ordered path; dependency constraints
fix what must come before what, risk-descending ordering protects the sequence's early steps from a
later step's failure, every intermediate state must itself be invariant-legal, and a step's
divergence from its own sandboxed prediction is recorded as execution debt — the specific cost of
having predicted, correctly or not, before having observed.

PART XII — LOCK-IN DETECTION (FOLDED FROM FAMILY M, CANDIDATE 124)
===============================================================================================

12.1 Why lock-in is an architecture-shape question, not only a technology question. The Non-
Duplication Ledger routes Family M (Optionality, candidates 121-130) to REFERENCE with a thin fold:
most of that family's territory — anti-model-dependence, representation portability, toolchain
succession — belongs to FD and the continuity kernel and is not re-narrated here (Constitution
Article VI). Candidate 124, lock-in detection specifically, folds into IAS-G1 rather than staying a
pure REFERENCE because, on inspection, model- and toolchain-lock-in is DERIVABLE from the same
topology object this dataset already governs: a system is LOCKED IN, DEFINED here, when its
DEPENDS-ON edges concentrate disproportionately on a single external provider, model family, or
toolchain generation such that replacing that one dependency would force correlated, simultaneous
change across an unusually large fraction of the systems that depend on it — a structural,
graph-shape property, not merely a labeling property of any one system in isolation.

12.2 The lock-in metric: fan-in concentration on a single dependency class. LOCK-IN CONCENTRATION is
DEFINED as the proportion of the estate's total DEPENDS-ON fan-in (F1 §6.3's inbound-dependency
count, aggregated across the whole topology) that traces, directly or through one intermediate
layer, to a single external dependency class — one model provider, one toolchain, one external
service. A topology where this concentration is HIGH is architecturally fragile to a single external
change even if every individual system's internal quality score (Part III) is excellent, because
the fragility here is a property of the AGGREGATE dependency shape, not of any one system's design —
precisely the emergent-property discipline F1 Part III already establishes for the ensemble
generally, applied here to one specific and consequential kind of emergent fragility.

12.3 Distinguishing healthy specialization from lock-in. Not every high concentration on a single
dependency is lock-in in the pejorative sense; a genuinely superior, well-supported dependency that
the estate has deliberately and knowingly standardized on is a rational choice, not a defect. The
detector therefore does not flag concentration alone; it flags concentration COMBINED with
UNEXAMINED SWITCHING COST — measured, HYPOTHESIZED pending real measurement, as the absence of any
recorded arch-decision or D2A record in the estate's history that evaluated an alternative to the
concentrated dependency within a defined lookback window. A high concentration the estate has
recently and explicitly re-examined and re-affirmed is READ differently — LOW-severity, informed
choice — than an identical concentration nobody has examined in the lookback window, which is READ
as HIGH-severity, because the second case is not a decision at all, only an accumulation nobody has
yet stopped to price.

12.4 What lock-in detection produces, and where it routes. A HIGH-severity lock-in finding routes to
two places, neither of which is a new authority this engine holds. It routes to the topology
optimizer (Part VII) as a candidate location for a RE-ROUTE move — specifically, a move that
introduces a second, structurally independent path for the concentrated capability, reducing
fan-in concentration without necessarily removing the primary dependency, per the general principle
that redundancy is not itself a defect (F1 §6.4's competes-with commentary: competition, including
between two providers of similar capability, is information for the portfolio and ecology systems
to judge as healthy diversity or wasteful redundancy, not something this engine adjudicates itself).
It also routes to IAS-C2's demand-forecasting territory as a flagged risk for that system's own
scenario modeling, since a locked-in dependency's future unavailability is exactly the kind of
forward-looking risk IAS-F3's digital twin (Part XXI) is built to simulate, not this engine.

12.5 Worked example: a synthetic concentration finding and its disposition. Consider a synthetic
reading, illustrative only, in which 41% of the estate's total depends-on fan-in traces, across
various intermediate systems, to a single toolchain generation — a concentration the detector flags
as HIGH by a fixed comparative threshold against the estate's own historical concentration baseline
(this dataset does not assert an absolute universal threshold, marking the specific cutoff NORMATIVE
and locally set, per F1 §4.3's precedent that operational cadences and thresholds belong to the
consuming mechanism, not to a foundation). Checking the switching-cost condition, the detector finds
no arch-decision or D2A record addressing this concentration within the defined lookback window,
so the finding is read as HIGH-severity unexamined concentration rather than an informed standing
choice, and is routed to the topology optimizer, which — per its own §7.3 marginal-impact filter —
evaluates whether any of the concentrated systems sit at a location where mission traffic is heavy
enough to justify the cost of introducing a second path, generating a re-route candidate only where
that filter clears, and otherwise leaving the finding recorded but unacted-upon as a known,
priced risk rather than a forced intervention nobody's mission volume currently justifies.

12.6 Lock-in and the complexity budget: why concentrated dependency is not itself debited. A high
lock-in concentration reading (§12.2) is not, by itself, converted into an architecture-debt entry
(Part IX) the way an overspend-flagged, knowingly-accepted trade-off is — the distinction matters
because a debt entry requires a STATED REASON at the moment of a knowing choice (Part IX §9.2), while
a lock-in concentration typically accumulates across many separate, individually-reasonable decisions
made by many different actors over a long period, none of whom was choosing to concentrate dependency
so much as independently choosing the same, individually-best-available option each time. (DERIVED,
directly parallel to Part I §1.2's local-versus-global argument once more: concentration is an
emergent property of many locally-sound choices, not a single choice with a debt-worthy stated reason
of its own.) Only once the switching-cost condition of §12.3 resolves to HIGH-severity — an
UNEXAMINED concentration, not a recently re-affirmed one — and a re-route candidate generated in
response is subsequently REJECTED by arch-decision with a recorded rationale, does the concentration
convert into a debt entry, at that point correctly classified as a knowing choice with a stated reason
attached, exactly as any other debt issuance requires.

12.7 A second worked example: a low-severity, examined concentration correctly left alone. Consider a
synthetic case, illustrative only, contrasting with §12.5's high-severity finding. A 34% concentration
of estate-wide fan-in on a single external knowledge-retrieval service is detected, comparably high
against the estate's own historical baseline. Checking the switching-cost condition, the detector
finds a D2A record, eleven reconciliation cycles prior, explicitly evaluating two alternative
retrieval services against the incumbent and recording a rationale for retaining the incumbent
(cost and integration-maturity advantages that outweighed the concentration risk at the time of
review). Because this record falls within the defined lookback window, the finding is read as
LOW-severity — informed, examined concentration — and is recorded but NOT routed to the topology
optimizer for re-route generation at all, distinguishing it sharply from §12.5's unexamined case: the
same raw concentration NUMBER, in isolation, would have looked identical between the two examples, and
only the switching-cost check, reading the estate's own decision history rather than the topology
alone, correctly separates a defensible standing choice from an accumulation nobody has stopped to
price — precisely the distinction §12.3 exists to draw, demonstrated here with the finding it produces
in the favorable case rather than only the unfavorable one.

12.8 Why lock-in detection could not have been built as a standalone Family-M dataset. The Non-
Duplication Ledger's own disposition for candidate 124 (MERGE → IAS-G1 rather than a standalone
build) is confirmed by this Part's own content in a way worth stating explicitly: every mechanism
§12.1-12.7 actually uses — the depends-on fan-in count (F1 §6.3), the topology optimizer's re-route
generation (Part VII), the switching-cost check against arch-decision and D2A records — is either
already defined in this dataset or reused from a named parent this dataset already integrates with. A
standalone lock-in dataset would have had to rebuild the fan-in computation over F1's relation graph
from nothing, duplicating Part II's ontology entirely, or would have had to define its OWN notion of
"concentration" disconnected from the topology object every other Family-G engine already reasons
about — either path recreates work this dataset's fold avoids, which is the concrete, checkable sense
in which DL-4's decision log entry is not merely a scoping preference but a duplication genuinely
avoided.

FINAL LAW — PART XII. Lock-in is fan-in concentration on one external dependency combined with the
absence of any recent, examined choice to accept that concentration; it is folded into this dataset
because it is a shape the topology already carries, and the detector's product is never a mandate
to diversify but a priced, routed finding for the optimizer and the forecasting systems to act on
only where the mission evidence justifies the cost.

PART XIII — THE CROSS-SYSTEM DEPENDENCY GOVERNOR (FOLDED FROM FAMILY A, CANDIDATE 9)
===============================================================================================

13.1 Why the governor folds here rather than staying in Family A. The Non-Duplication Ledger's own
row for candidate 9 states the disposition plainly: explicit minimal dependencies BETWEEN SYSTEMS,
as distinct from the code-level depends_on tracking `audit_cache` and GK-04 already perform inside
one repository, is a topology-shape concern once generalized across the whole estate, and the
ledger routes it to MERGE → IAS-G1 rather than to the Cross-System Circulation family (IAS-A2)
precisely because its object — the DEPENDS-ON subgraph's SHAPE, not the event-carrying mechanism
that moves data across it — is the same object Part II §2.6 and Part VIII already govern. The
governor is DEFINED, folded here, as the engine that enforces a specific discipline over that
subgraph: every depends-on edge must be MINIMAL (no system depends on more of another system's
capability than the depending mission actually requires) and EXPLICIT (every depends-on edge must
be declared, closing the loop directly against Part VI's shadowing signature).

13.2 Minimality, defined operationally. A depends-on edge is MINIMAL, DEFINED, when the depending
system's actual usage, observed through the same control-bus log the fitness evaluator and drift
detector already read (reused, not re-instrumented, per this dataset's repeated strengthening
mechanism), touches only a bounded subset of the providing system's full capability signature (F1
§9.2). An edge is NON-MINIMAL, and flagged, when a depending system's declared or real dependency
grants it access to the providing system's full surface while actual usage over a sustained window
exercises only a narrow subset — a specific, measurable form of unnecessary coupling distinct from,
but related to, Part II §2.3's general coupling score: minimality names WHY an edge is tightly
coupled (broad access, narrow use) rather than merely THAT it is.

13.3 The governor's corrective move: capability-scoped edges, not edge removal. Where the drift
detector's PRUNE move (Part VII §7.2) removes an edge no longer used at all, the governor's
corrective move is different and additive to the optimizer's four-move vocabulary: a SCOPE-NARROW
recommendation, which does not remove the edge but recommends it be re-declared against only the
subset of the providing system's capability signature actually exercised. This is deliberately not
one of Part VII's four generative moves, because it does not change the topology's graph shape at
all (the edge still exists, still connects the same two systems) — it changes the edge's DECLARED
CONTRACT, tightening what the depending system is entitled to assume it can reach, which is why the
governor is a distinct, folded engine rather than a fifth topology-optimizer move type: its object
is the edge's SCOPE, not the topology's SHAPE.

13.4 Why minimality matters disproportionately at foundational layers. A non-minimal edge into a
foundational-layer system (Part II §2.6) is more consequential than the identical pattern at a leaf
layer, for the same layer-depth reasoning Part VIII's per-layer budget already applies: broad,
unscoped access into a foundational system means any future change to that system's fuller surface —
even a change to a part of the surface the depending system never actually used — carries a false
risk of breaking the dependent, because the DECLARED contract claims a broader dependency than the
REAL one. The governor therefore weights its scan by layer depth, prioritizing scope-narrowing
recommendations at foundational and near-foundational layers first, mirroring Part VIII's own
per-layer prioritization rather than inventing a separate weighting scheme.

13.5 Worked example: a non-minimal edge narrowed without any topology change. Consider a synthetic
case, illustrative only, in which system T-19 declares a DEPENDS-ON edge granting it access to the
full capability signature of a foundational system, T-03, which provides four distinct capabilities.
Sixty days of control-bus observation show T-19 invoking exactly one of T-03's four capabilities,
consistently, and never the other three. The governor flags this as a non-minimal edge and issues a
SCOPE-NARROW recommendation: re-declare T-19's dependency as scoped to the single capability
actually used. This recommendation, routed to arch-decision as advisory context exactly as every
other engine's output is (Part I §1.3), is approved and the declaration updated — no evolution_
engine execution is required at all, because the topology's edges are unchanged; only the CONTRACT
attached to one existing edge is tightened. The practical effect surfaces later, at a genuinely
separate moment: when T-03 is subsequently modified to change one of the three capabilities T-19
never used, the now-accurate scope declaration correctly shows T-19 as unaffected, where the prior,
non-minimal declaration would have flagged T-19 as a system requiring re-verification for a change
that, in reality, could never have touched it.

13.6 The governor's relationship to the complexity budget and the debt market. A non-minimal edge is
not itself charged as architecture debt (Part IX) — over-broad access is a scoping problem, not a
knowingly-accepted trade-off — but an edge that REMAINS non-minimal after being flagged, past a
defined grace period with no corrective action taken, converts into a debt entry under Part IX's
ordinary issuance rule, with the stated reason recorded as "scope-narrow recommendation outstanding"
rather than a deliberate trade-off, distinguishing governance neglect from an informed choice in the
same ledger, using the same EXECUTION-DEBT-versus-DECISION-DEBT distinction Part XI §11.4 already
establishes, extended here to a third cause: NEGLECTED-RECOMMENDATION DEBT.

13.7 Interaction with lock-in detection: minimality as a partial defense against concentration risk.
Parts XII and XIII are both folded engines reading the same depends-on subgraph, and their findings
compound rather than merely coexist: a system with HIGH lock-in concentration (Part XII §12.2) whose
individual depends-on edges are also NON-MINIMAL (§13.2) presents a compounded risk the two engines,
read together, surface more precisely than either alone — the concentrated dependency is not only
structurally hard to replace but the estate does not even know, with precision, which SLICE of that
dependency's surface each depending system genuinely needs, meaning a future migration away from the
concentrated dependency would have to assume every consumer needs the FULL surface, inflating the
migration's true scope beyond what real usage would require. Conversely, a HIGH-concentration
dependency whose edges are already MINIMAL presents a narrower, better-understood migration surface
even though the concentration itself is unchanged — minimality does not resolve lock-in, but it
substantially reduces the SWITCHING COST §12.3 measures, meaning the governor's ordinary scope-
narrowing work has a second-order benefit for lock-in risk it was not designed to target directly, an
instance of the general compounding principle the wider CPP-IAS family names (COMPOUNDING_GRAPH.md)
applied here between two engines folded into the same dataset rather than between two sibling
datasets.

13.8 Minimality applied beyond DEPENDS-ON: a note on STRENGTHENS edges. The governor's ordinary scope
is DEPENDS-ON edges (§13.1), because those are the edges whose over-broad access creates false
re-verification risk (§13.5). F1's STRENGTHENS relation (F1 §6.6) is a different kind of edge — one
system improving another's own internal function without a normal-operation data dependency — and
minimality, strictly, does not apply to it the same way, since a strengthens edge has no "capability
surface" in the sense a depends-on edge's provider does. This dataset marks explicitly, UNRESOLVED,
whether an analogous discipline — perhaps a SCOPE-OF-CLAIM minimality, bounding how broadly a
strengthens relationship may be asserted without evidence (echoing F1 §4.4's provenance discipline for
properties generally) — belongs in a future extension of this governor or is better left to F1's own
evidentiary requirements for the strengthens relation type; this dataset does not resolve the question
here because doing so would require defining new object-level machinery outside Part II's closed
five-object vocabulary, a boundary this dataset has held deliberately throughout.

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. Every depends-on edge earns its keep by being both minimal — no system holds
more access than its real, observed usage requires — and explicit — no dependency exists undeclared;
the governor narrows scope without touching topology shape wherever it can, and a flagged edge left
unnarrowed past its grace period becomes exactly the kind of debt this dataset already knows how to
price and track.

===============================================================================================
PART XIV — DECISION MODELS: HOW IAS-G1 ARBITRATES COMPETING ARCHITECTURE PROPOSALS
===============================================================================================

14.1 The authority question, answered once and applied throughout. Every prior Part has stated, in
passing, that IAS-G1's outputs are advisory; this Part specifies the decision model precisely enough
to be checked rather than merely asserted. IAS-G1 holds NO final authority over any decision class
in F1's sense (F1 §4.2's authority field, Invariant Three): it does not decide whether a proposed
change ships, it does not decide which duplication disposition applies, and it does not decide
whether a mutation executes. Its authority, DEFINED here precisely, is EVIDENTIARY: every engine in
Parts III-XIII produces a structured artifact — a quality vector, a fitness score, a frontier, a
drift finding, a budget flag, a debt entry, a sandboxed delta, a sequenced plan, a lock-in finding,
a scope-narrow recommendation — and the decision models below specify how these artifacts combine
into the single evidentiary package arch-decision, D2A, and evolution_engine each consume.

14.2 Three consumer-specific packages from one shared evidence base. arch-decision receives the
TOPOLOGY IMPACT PACKAGE: the trade-off frontier (Part V) for the specific decision under review,
annotated with the complexity-budget flag (Part VIII §8.4), any relevant debt entries (Part IX), and
the sandbox's observed delta where one exists (Part X). D2A receives the DUPLICATION FREQUENCY
PACKAGE when evaluating a proposed system: whether the topology already shows a recurring pattern of
similar proposals in the same region (feeding D2A's judgment on whether this is a first occurrence
or the Nth instance of a class the estate keeps re-proposing, information no single-event view of
D2A's own object could generate). evolution_engine receives the SEQUENCED EXECUTION PACKAGE: the
evolution planner's ordered steps (Part XI) with each step's rollback point, never a bare instruction
to "execute this merge." (DERIVED: three different consumer-specific views over the SAME underlying
evidence base is the specific mechanism, generalized across all three parents at once, by which this
dataset satisfies the Strengthening Matrix's requirement that G1 strengthen at least four named
systems — each parent receives exactly the slice of evidence its own decision class requires, never
a one-size package that under-serves two of the three.)

14.3 What happens when two IAS-G1 findings conflict with each other. Because Parts III-XIII are
separate engines reading overlapping inputs, two findings can, in principle, point in different
directions for the same location: the topology optimizer's marginal-impact filter (Part VII §7.3)
might rank a location as low-priority for structural change while the lock-in detector (Part XII)
flags the same location as a high-severity unexamined concentration warranting a re-route. This is
not a bug to suppress; it is DERIVED that the two engines are answering different questions (mission-
weighted structural quality versus dependency-concentration risk) and a genuine disagreement between
them is itself informative. The decision model's rule, NORMATIVE: conflicting findings are NEVER
silently resolved by IAS-G1 into one merged verdict before reaching a consumer; both findings are
surfaced together, explicitly labeled by which engine produced each, so that arch-decision sees the
actual disagreement rather than a smoothed average that hides which concern is driving which
recommendation.

14.4 Escalation when the evidentiary package itself is contested. F1's epistemology (Article V)
provides for a CONTESTED marking when evidence points both ways; IAS-G1 applies this at the package
level when, for instance, the sandbox's observed delta (Part X) diverges materially from the
compiler's predicted delta (Part V) for the same candidate and the divergence has not yet been
explained by the calibration loop (Part X §10.6). A CONTESTED package is not withheld from arch-
decision — withholding evidence because it disagrees with itself would be a worse failure than
surfacing the disagreement — but it is flagged CONTESTED explicitly in the package header, and Part
XX's evaluation protocol tracks how often packages resolve to CONTESTED as a health signal on the
engines' own internal consistency, distinct from any signal about the topology itself.

14.5 The decision model's relationship to Article IX's governed self-evolution boundary. Article IX
states that IAS proposes and the Owner promotes; this dataset's decision model is the concrete
mechanism by which that boundary is honored specifically for architecture. No engine in Parts III-
XIII, individually or in combination, is permitted to produce an artifact framed as an instruction —
every artifact this dataset produces is framed, by construction, as EVIDENCE FOR a decision another
system or the Owner retains. (NORMATIVE: this framing discipline is checkable at the artifact-schema
level — Part XV specifies the lifecycle states an artifact passes through, and no state in that
lifecycle is "auto-executed"; every transition past PROPOSED requires an explicit external decision.)

14.6 Worked example: a package reaching arch-decision with an internal conflict intact. Consider a
synthetic case, illustrative only, combining two prior worked examples. The location examined in
Part III §3.5 (systems T-06 through T-10) generates, from the topology optimizer, a re-route
candidate ranked LOW marginal-impact under the current mission mix (Part VII §7.3's filter — this
region is not heavily mission-traversed right now). Independently, the same region's systems are
found, under the dependency governor's scan (Part XIII), to include a non-minimal edge into T-08 that
has sat unflagged past its grace period and has just converted to a NEGLECTED-RECOMMENDATION DEBT
entry (Part XIII §13.6). The evidentiary package presented to arch-decision for this region therefore
carries two genuinely different signals: "low priority for structural re-routing given current
mission traffic" from the optimizer, and "an outstanding governance debt now accruing interest" from
the governor. The decision model does not merge these into one verdict; it presents both, correctly
labeled, and arch-decision — which alone can weigh the debt's accrual cost against the low mission
urgency — makes the call the evidence base, deliberately, declined to make for it.

14.7 Audit trail requirements: every package traceable to the readings that produced it. A decision
model whose evidentiary packages could not be reconstructed after the fact would fail the estate's
own reality-contract discipline the moment a later reviewer asked "why did this frontier look the way
it did." This dataset therefore requires, NORMATIVE, that every package carry a full PROVENANCE CHAIN:
which quality-baseline reading (Part III §3.4, with its own timestamp), which fitness computation
(Part IV §4.2, with the mission-log window it read), which sandbox trial (Part X, with its replay
window per Part XVIII §18.3's fixed-window anti-cherry-picking rule) fed the package, such that a
package generated six months earlier can be re-derived, in principle, from the same inputs and
compared against the package the estate actually acted on at the time — the specific mechanism that
makes Part XXI's retrodiction and sandbox-fidelity benchmarks possible at all, since neither benchmark
can run without knowing exactly which historical inputs a given past package was built from.

14.8 Why the audit trail is evidentiary infrastructure, not bureaucratic overhead. Part XIX §19.5
names budget-ledger friction as a possible bureaucracy failure mode; the audit-trail requirement here
is deliberately NOT of that kind, because it imposes no review burden on any human or agent
decision-maker — it is a property of how each engine's output is STORED, not an additional step
anyone must perform before acting. (DERIVED: the distinction between friction that slows a decision
and structure that merely accompanies one is the same distinction Part VIII §8.5 draws for the
mechanical, automatic budget debit versus the occasional, threshold-triggered overspend flag — this
dataset applies the identical proportionality discipline to its own record-keeping.)

14.9 Why arbitration authority over which ENGINE's finding takes precedence is deliberately never
granted to this dataset either. §14.3 already forbids IAS-G1 from silently merging two conflicting
findings into one verdict; this closing subsection names the stronger, related claim explicitly: this
dataset holds no internal tie-breaking authority even between its OWN engines when their findings
conflict, because granting itself that authority would be equivalent, in effect, to granting itself a
finding-level final-authority decision class Invariant Three (F1 Part V §5.4) reserves for a single
named holder — and no engine within Parts III-XIII is named as that holder over any other. Where two
engines disagree, as in §14.6's worked example, the disagreement is a fact about the topology's current
readings, not a dispute this dataset is positioned, or entitled, to settle on its own authority; only
arch-decision, weighing both signals against context this dataset does not itself hold, is positioned
to resolve it, and this dataset's decision model is built, throughout, to preserve that positioning
rather than to quietly assume it.

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. IAS-G1's authority is evidentiary, never final; each of its three named
parents receives a consumer-specific package built from a shared evidence base, conflicting findings
are surfaced together rather than silently merged, and a contested package is flagged as contested
rather than suppressed — because a governor that hides its own engines' disagreements from the
decision-makers it serves has stopped strengthening them and started managing them.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — PROTOCOL AND LIFECYCLE: FROM PROPOSAL TO PROMOTION TO ROLLBACK
===============================================================================================

15.1 Why a lifecycle, not just a set of engines. Parts III-XIV describe WHAT each engine computes and
HOW authority is bounded; this Part specifies the STATE MACHINE a single architecture recommendation
moves through from the moment a location is first flagged to the moment its outcome is reconciled
back into the quality baseline — because without an explicit lifecycle, the many artifacts these
engines produce (findings, candidates, frontiers, sandboxed deltas, sequenced plans) have no shared
notion of "where in the process" any one of them currently sits, and two consumers reasoning about
the same recommendation at different lifecycle stages would talk past each other exactly as F1 §4.3
warns a property without a staleness marker invites.

15.2 The seven lifecycle states. FLAGGED: a location has been identified by quality science (Part
III), the fitness evaluator (Part IV), the drift detector (Part VI), the lock-in detector (Part XII),
or the dependency governor (Part XIII) as warranting attention; no move has yet been generated.
CANDIDATE-GENERATED: the topology optimizer (Part VII) has produced one or more move proposals for
the flagged location. FRONTIER-RANKED: the trade-off compiler (Part V) has placed the candidate(s) on
a Pareto frontier with quality, fitness, cost, and risk deltas attached. SANDBOXED: for MERGE and
SPLIT candidates (Part X §10.4's requirement), the mutation sandbox has produced an observed delta,
upgrading the frontier entry's evidence class from DERIVED to OBSERVED. APPROVED: arch-decision has
selected a candidate from the frontier, transferring the decision right that was never IAS-G1's to
begin with. SEQUENCED: the evolution planner (Part XI) has placed the approved candidate into an
ordered execution plan alongside any other approved candidates in the same window. EXECUTED-AND-
RECONCILED: evolution_engine has executed the sequenced step, and the quality science baseline (Part
III §3.4) has been updated to reflect the real, post-execution topology reading — closing the loop
back to the state that made the original flag possible.

15.3 Transitions are one-directional except for two named exceptions. The seven states form a
forward-moving sequence, NORMATIVE, with exactly two permitted backward transitions. ROLLBACK, from
EXECUTED-AND-RECONCILED (or from mid-sequence within SEQUENCED, per Part XI §11.4's rollback points)
back to FLAGGED, occurs when a step's real-traffic outcome diverges materially from its sandboxed or
predicted delta, restoring the topology to its pre-step state and re-opening the location as a fresh
FLAGGED finding, now carrying the divergence as additional context for whatever candidate is
generated next. WITHDRAWAL, from any state prior to APPROVED, occurs when a subsequent reconciliation
of the quality baseline (a new reading superseding the one that triggered the original flag) shows
the flagged condition no longer holds — for instance, a separate, unrelated change already resolved
the coupling issue a still-pending candidate was generated to fix. Withdrawal is not a failure; it is
the lifecycle correctly recognizing that the world moved before the recommendation did, and it
prevents an obsolete candidate from consuming arch-decision's review attention.

15.3b Parallel lifecycles at the same location. Nothing in this state machine forbids two independent
findings from opening two separate lifecycle records against overlapping systems — the quality
science (Part III), the drift detector (Part VI), and the dependency governor (Part XIII) can each
FLAG the same general region for entirely different reasons in the same reconciliation cycle, per
Part XIV §14.6's worked example of exactly this. Each such finding opens its OWN lifecycle record with
its own state, never sharing one record across engines, because collapsing two independently-caused
findings into one record would obscure which engine's evidence justified which eventual action — a
specific instance of Part XIV §14.3's rule against silently merging conflicting or simply distinct
findings before they reach a consumer. Where two parallel lifecycles' eventual CANDIDATE-GENERATED
moves would touch the same edge or system in a way Part XI's evolution planner must sequence together,
the planner's dependency-ordering logic (Part XI §11.2) is the mechanism that reconciles them at
execution time — the lifecycle layer tracks each finding's own progress independently; only the
planner, once both are APPROVED, treats them as entries in one combined sequencing problem.

15.4 The lifecycle's relationship to the debt market's grace periods. Part IX §9.2 and Part XIII
§13.6 both reference a grace period after which an unaddressed finding converts to a debt entry; the
lifecycle formalizes this as a TIME-IN-STATE rule: a finding that remains in FLAGGED or CANDIDATE-
GENERATED past its class-specific grace period (a NORMATIVE, locally-set threshold per the same
discipline Part XII §12.5 already applies to lock-in thresholds) is not silently dropped — it is
automatically annotated with the appropriate debt classification (DECISION DEBT if a frontier
candidate was available and not selected within the window, NEGLECTED-RECOMMENDATION DEBT if a
scope-narrow or similar low-cost recommendation was never acted on at all) and the annotation is
attached to the SAME lifecycle record, not a separate one, so that a reviewer examining any finding's
history sees both its architectural content and its governance timeline in one place.

15.5 Worked example: one recommendation's full lifecycle trace. Recall Part VI §6.4's T-44/T-58
shadow-edge finding. FLAGGED at the moment the drift detector's diff surfaces the undeclared edge.
CANDIDATE-GENERATED when the topology optimizer, per Part VII §7.5, determines none of its four
structural moves apply and produces the declaration-only recommendation instead — a degenerate but
valid candidate under this lifecycle, since a recommendation to correct a declaration without
changing topology shape is still a candidate artifact moving through the same state machine.
FRONTIER-RANKED trivially, as a single-candidate frontier with near-zero cost and near-zero risk.
SANDBOXED is SKIPPED, correctly, because a declaration-only change is neither a MERGE nor a SPLIT
(Part X §10.4's optional/required distinction applies literally here — this candidate does not even
reach the optional case, since it changes no topology shape for the sandbox to replay against).
APPROVED when arch-decision accepts the registry correction. SEQUENCED trivially, as a single-step
plan with no dependency ordering to resolve. EXECUTED-AND-RECONCILED when the registry is updated and
Part VIII's retroactive budget recalculation (§8.3, triggered by exactly this kind of correction) is
performed, closing the loop with the complexity ledger now reflecting T-58's true, previously-
undercounted dependency load.

15.6 Retention: what happens to a lifecycle record after EXECUTED-AND-RECONCILED. A completed
lifecycle record is not deleted once its terminal state is reached; it is retained, DEFINED here, as
part of the location's PROVENANCE HISTORY — the ordered set of past findings, candidates, and
outcomes at that location, which two later engines specifically depend on. The quality baseline's
trend computation (Part III §3.4) requires a history of past readings, not only the current one, and
a topology location whose prior interventions have been deleted would present as if it had never been
touched, undermining the trend the baseline exists to compute. The evolution planner's risk-descending
ordering (Part XI §11.2) also benefits from provenance: a location with a recent history of rollbacks
(Part XV §15.3's ROLLBACK transition) is treated, NORMATIVE, as carrying elevated risk for any NEW
candidate proposed there within a cooling-off window, directly implementing Part XIX's churn defense
(§19.2) as a lifecycle-retention rule rather than a separate mechanism — the retained record IS the
mechanism, not merely a log of one.

15.7 What retention does not imply: no automatic re-litigation. Retaining a completed record does not
mean a WITHDRAWN or ROLLED-BACK finding automatically re-opens; each remains a closed, historical
entry unless a NEW flag independently arises from the ordinary engines of Parts III, VI, XII, or XIII
re-detecting the same or a related condition. (NORMATIVE: this distinction matters because an
automatic re-opening rule would effectively resurrect Part XIX §19.1's premature-optimization risk
under a different name — a location that keeps getting re-flagged by its own history rather than by
fresh evidence.) A retained record is read, never re-triggered, by the engines that consume it.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. A recommendation is not a single event but a seven-state lifecycle from flagged
to reconciled, moving forward except for two named, principled exceptions — rollback when reality
diverges from prediction, withdrawal when the world resolves the flag before the recommendation
does — and every state, including a state a candidate correctly skips, is recorded, because a
lifecycle that only tracks the states a recommendation passed through and not the ones it correctly
bypassed cannot be audited for whether the bypass itself was correct.

===============================================================================================
PART XVI — WORKED EXAMPLE: ONE MISSION THROUGH THE FULL ENGINE STACK ON SYNTHETIC DATA
===============================================================================================

16.1 Purpose of a single end-to-end trace. Parts III-XV each carry their own worked example, isolated
to the one engine being defined; this Part exists to trace ONE synthetic scenario through every
engine in sequence, demonstrating that the pieces compose into a coherent pipeline rather than
merely each working in isolation — the same integration-over-units discipline the estate's own
Mistake #16 doctrine already applies to code ("compiles != works"; here, "each engine passes its own
example != the pipeline produces a coherent recommendation").

16.2 The scenario, stated in full. A synthetic thirty-system slice of the estate, illustrative only,
shows the following at a single quality-science reconciliation cycle: system T-71, providing a
knowledge-synthesis capability, has grown from two declared capabilities at its creation to five
observed-in-use capabilities over eighteen months, with cohesion score dropping from HIGH to LOW
across that period (Part III's cohesion-spread dimension). Two of the five capabilities are invoked
almost exclusively by mission patterns from one consumer cluster (systems T-80 through T-84); the
other three are invoked by an entirely separate consumer cluster (T-90 through T-93) with no
observed overlap in the sixty-day control-bus window.

16.3 Trace through Parts III-VII. Quality science (Part III) flags T-71's cohesion decline as the
primary signal — LOW cohesion, no coupling or seam anomaly independently. The fitness evaluator (Part
IV) reads the mission-cost proxy for both consumer clusters and finds each cluster's missions
traverse T-71 efficiently on their own terms (no excess layer crossing), meaning the LOW cohesion is
not currently manifesting as a mission-cost problem — a case, explicitly, where quality and fitness
diverge (fitness fine, quality poor), which Part III §3.6 already anticipates as a legitimate
possibility since the two dimensions measure different things. The drift detector (Part VI) finds no
declared-versus-real divergence — T-71's five capabilities are all correctly declared, just
increasingly incoherent as a set. The topology optimizer (Part VII), applying its marginal-impact
filter (§7.3), weighs the location: quality reading is far from baseline (strong signal for
intervention) but current fitness impact is low (weak signal). Because the filter requires BOTH
factors to clear threshold, the optimizer does NOT generate a SPLIT candidate at this cycle — the
location is FLAGGED but remains in that lifecycle state, not advanced to CANDIDATE-GENERATED,
because generating a costly split for a system whose current mission-cost impact is negligible would
be premature (a direct preview of Part XIX's premature-optimization failure mode, correctly avoided
here by the filter design itself).

16.4 What changes three reconciliation cycles later, and the trace resumes. At a subsequent cycle,
the control-bus log shows a THIRD mission pattern beginning to invoke T-71, requiring BOTH the T-80-
84 cluster's two capabilities and the T-90-93 cluster's three capabilities within a single mission,
forcing that mission to traverse all five of T-71's increasingly unrelated capabilities in one call.
The fitness evaluator's mission-cost proxy for this new pattern is HIGH (the mission pays a real
coordination cost navigating an incoherent interface), and the marginal-impact filter now clears on
both factors. The topology optimizer generates a SPLIT candidate: T-71a (serving the T-80-84
cluster's two capabilities) and T-71b (serving the T-90-93 cluster's three), with the new
cross-cluster mission re-routed to invoke both post-split systems explicitly rather than one
conflated one. The trade-off compiler (Part V) frontier-ranks this against a do-nothing baseline and
against a lighter-weight alternative (re-route only, keeping T-71 whole but adding an internal
routing layer) — the split dominates on cohesion and mission-cost-proxy improvement but carries
higher implementation cost, placing both on the frontier rather than collapsing to one answer.

16.5 Sandbox, sequencing, and reconciliation. Because SPLIT requires sandboxing (Part X §10.4), the
candidate is replayed against the full sixty-day log; the observed delta confirms the predicted
mission-cost improvement for the new cross-cluster pattern and finds no material degradation for
either original cluster's missions, upgrading the frontier entry to OBSERVED evidence. arch-decision
approves the split; the evolution planner (Part XI) sequences it as a single step with a bridging
edge preserving the new cross-cluster mission's connectivity through the transition; evolution_engine
executes; the quality baseline reconciles, showing T-71a and T-71b both at HIGH cohesion,
retroactively closing the FLAGGED state that persisted, correctly unactioned, for three full cycles
before the mission evidence justified the cost.

16.6 The counterfactual branch: what the trace would have shown had the sandbox trial failed. This
Part's value is strengthened by naming, briefly, the branch that did NOT occur in §16.5's trace.
Had the sandbox replay (Part X) surfaced a material degradation for one of the two original clusters
under the split candidate shape — for instance, if T-80-84's missions had, in reality, occasionally
relied on an undocumented interaction between the two capability groups the split would sever — the
sandbox's observed delta would have reported that degradation explicitly (Part X §10.5's T-08/T-09
worked example demonstrates the identical reporting pattern), the frontier entry would have carried a
REVISED, worse risk score, and arch-decision would have faced a genuinely different choice: accept the
split with the newly-surfaced cost named, request the topology optimizer generate an alternative
(perhaps a lighter-weight internal routing layer rather than a full split, echoing Part XVI's own
§16.4 discussion of the rejected lighter-weight alternative), or defer the whole candidate back to
FLAGGED pending further mission evidence. The pipeline's design guarantees that whichever branch
occurs, the decision is made with the sandbox's real finding visible — the counterfactual is named
here specifically because a reader who only sees the successful trace could mistakenly conclude the
pipeline always resolves cleanly, when its actual guarantee is narrower and more honest: it resolves
with the best available evidence, which is not the same claim as resolving without risk.

16.7 What this trace demonstrates about engine composition that no single-engine example can. Reading
Parts III through XV's individual worked examples in isolation, a reader could reasonably wonder
whether the nine-plus-two engine stack is more machinery than the problem requires — whether a
simpler, single-pass heuristic could reach the same T-71 split recommendation directly. This trace
answers that question empirically rather than rhetorically: the CORRECT recommendation (split T-71)
was NOT available at the first reconciliation cycle where the location was flagged (§16.3) — a
single-pass heuristic acting immediately on the cohesion signal alone would have generated the split
three cycles prematurely, before the cross-cluster mission existed to justify its cost, precisely the
premature-optimization failure Part XIX §19.1 names. The multi-engine composition's specific value,
demonstrated here rather than asserted, is that the fitness evaluator's mission-weighted gate (Part
IV) sat between the quality signal and the optimizer's generative step for exactly the number of
cycles the real evidence required, and no fewer.

16.8 What the trace deliberately left out, and why. This trace did not exercise the debt market (Part
IX), the lock-in detector (Part XII), or the dependency governor (Part XIII), because the synthetic
scenario's flagged condition never generated a knowingly-accepted trade-off, a concentration finding,
or a non-minimal edge — an honest reflection of the fact that not every recommendation traverses every
engine this dataset defines, and a worked example that artificially forced all thirteen engines into
one trace would misrepresent how the pipeline actually operates in the ordinary case, where most
flagged locations resolve through a SUBSET of the full engine stack rather than the entirety of it. A
second, shorter trace already exists earlier in this dataset for exactly this reason: Part VI §6.4's
T-44/T-58 shadow-edge example traverses the drift detector, the topology optimizer's declaration-only
path, and Part VIII's retroactive budget recalculation — a different subset, demonstrating a different
combination — and together the two traces span nine of this dataset's thirteen engines without either
one needing to force all thirteen into a single artificial scenario.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. A single flagged condition can sit correctly unactioned for cycles while its
mission-cost impact stays low, and the same condition, unchanged in its quality reading, becomes
actionable the moment real mission traffic makes it so — the pipeline's discipline is not to act on
every flag but to wait for the fitness evaluator's mission-weighted evidence to justify the cost the
optimizer would otherwise spend prematurely.

===============================================================================================
PART XVII — EDGE CASES
===============================================================================================

17.1 The SPLIT CANDIDATE that is also a MERGE CANDIDATE. Part II §2.4 previews this case: a system
that is simultaneously LOW-cohesion (its capabilities do not belong together) and LOOSELY coupled to
its neighbors (nothing tightly depends on it as a whole). Such a system is a candidate for splitting
on cohesion grounds alone, but because it is loosely coupled, a MERGE of its individual capabilities
into whichever neighboring systems most heavily consume each one is also structurally viable, and the
two candidates are not mutually exclusive alternatives on the same frontier but genuinely different
END STATES. The topology optimizer resolves this by generating BOTH: a SPLIT-only candidate
(preserving the system's identity, two children) and a SPLIT-then-distribute candidate (dissolving
the system entirely into its highest-affinity neighbors), letting the trade-off compiler's frontier
carry both rather than the optimizer pre-judging which end state is preferable — DERIVED from Part V
§5.2's refusal to select on the compiler's behalf, extended here to the optimizer's own generative
step.

17.2 The zero-history system. A newly admitted system, per F1 §4.6's UNMEASURED PROPERTY discussion,
carries no control-bus history for the fitness evaluator (Part IV) to read and no reconciliation
history for the quality baseline (Part III §3.4) to trend against. IAS-G1 does not force a default
quality or fitness reading onto such a system — consistent with F1's UNRESOLVED-over-guessed
discipline — and instead marks every engine's output for a zero-history system explicitly UNRESOLVED
rather than LOW or HIGH, deferring any structural recommendation until at least one reconciliation
cycle of real observation exists, a minimum-evidence floor this dataset applies uniformly rather than
special-casing per engine.

17.3 The seam that heals itself. Occasionally a flagged seam-integrity erosion (Part III §3.2)
resolves without any IAS-G1-generated move at all, because an unrelated arch-decision, made for
reasons entirely outside this dataset's visibility, happens to correct the underlying cross-seam
coupling as a side effect. The lifecycle's WITHDRAWAL transition (Part XV §15.3) handles this
cleanly, but the edge case worth naming explicitly is the CREDIT ATTRIBUTION question: the quality
baseline correctly reconciles to show the improvement, but the improvement must NOT be attributed to
any IAS-G1 engine in the debt market's or portfolio's later accounting, because attributing a
self-healed seam to this dataset's intervention would overstate G1's demonstrated value — a
discipline this dataset applies to its own reporting with the same rigor Part III §3.6 applies to
distinguishing elegance from value.

17.4 The high-value low-quality system that should not be touched. A system can score poorly on
every one of Part III's four dimensions while scoring exceptionally on IAS-D1's fitness signal
(Part IV §4.2) because it is, despite its structural messiness, precisely calibrated to a demanding
and idiosyncratic mission pattern that a cleaner re-architecture would very likely disrupt during
transition, even if the end state would eventually be better. The fitness evaluator and quality
science do not automatically defer to each other in this case; the trade-off compiler's frontier
simply reflects a HIGH-cost, HIGH-risk candidate for such a system, and Part X's sandbox — required
for any SPLIT candidate this location would generate — is the mechanism that would most likely
surface the transition risk before arch-decision commits to it, but this dataset names the case
explicitly rather than relying on the pipeline to catch it silently, because a location this
sensitive deserves an explicit UNRESOLVED flag on the frontier package (Part XIV) advising
particular caution, not merely an ordinary high-cost entry indistinguishable from any other.

17.5 The PHANTOM SEAM: a declared boundary the real topology never respected. Part II §2.5 defines a
seam as DISCOVERED from the coupling distribution, never designed — but the estate's own document-
level organization (its tier structure, its family groupings) can create the APPEARANCE of a seam that
the runtime topology never actually honored, because two systems documented in different families may
nonetheless have accumulated heavy real coupling over time, or two systems documented together may
have drifted apart in practice. A phantom seam is DEFINED as a location where the declared/documented
grouping and the coupling-derived seam (Part II §2.5, computed from real coupling scores) disagree.
This is not the same case as Part VI's drift signatures, which compare declared EDGES against real
ones; a phantom seam compares declared GROUPING against derived STRUCTURE, a level up from any single
edge. IAS-G1 resolves this by treating seam boundaries as ALWAYS DERIVED from the coupling graph (Part
II §2.5's own definition already says this), never from document structure, meaning a phantom seam is
not an error state this dataset must specially detect — it is simply the expected, ordinary case that
the family's own document organization may not track the runtime topology's actual shape, and no
engine in this dataset ever reads document structure as a seam input in the first place, which is why
this edge case resolves by definition rather than by mechanism once named explicitly.

17.6 THE FITNESS-QUALITY TIE: two candidates identical on both frontiers. Occasionally the trade-off
compiler (Part V) produces two candidates whose quality delta and fitness delta are, within
measurement precision, indistinguishable — for instance, two structurally different re-route targets
that happen to reduce the same coupling by the same amount and serve the identical mission pattern
with the same proxy improvement. Neither the dominance rule nor the near-dominance advisory (Part V
§5.3) can separate them, because true dominance requires a difference and there is none to find. This
dataset does not manufacture a tie-breaker from an arbitrary rule (alphabetical system ID, generation
order); it surfaces the tie explicitly in the frontier package as a GENUINE TIE annotation, and
resolution is left to arch-decision's own criteria — implementation familiarity, timing convenience,
or Owner preference — exactly as Part V §5.2's NORMATIVE reasoning for presenting frontiers rather than
winners already anticipates, extended here to the specific case where even the frontier itself cannot
distinguish two live options.

17.7 THE CROSS-SEAM MERGE THAT THE SEAM ITSELF HAS ALREADY DISSOLVED. Part VII §7.4's seam-preservation
constraint forbids the optimizer from proposing a merge that would reduce seam integrity where a seam
is currently healthy — but Part III §3.4's baseline is a TREND, and a seam that was healthy at the last
reconciliation cycle can, by the time a candidate reaches FRONTIER-RANKED (Part XV), have already
eroded through unrelated changes approved elsewhere in the topology, meaning the seam-preservation
constraint that correctly blocked the merge at generation time no longer reflects the CURRENT state by
the time arch-decision reviews it. This dataset resolves the case by re-checking the constraint at
EVERY lifecycle transition that gates on it, not only at CANDIDATE-GENERATED: a candidate whose
generation-time constraint check would now read differently against the current baseline is flagged
STALE-CONSTRAINT-CHECK and re-evaluated before advancing to APPROVED, rather than trusting a
constraint result computed against a baseline reading that may no longer be current — a direct
application of Part IV §4.3's general staleness discipline to a CONSTRAINT rather than to a property.

17.8 A SYSTEM THAT IS ITS OWN ONLY CONSUMER. A degenerate but real topology shape: a system whose sole
FEEDS or CONSUMES relationship is to itself — a capability that only ever invokes its own other
capabilities internally, with zero external inbound or outbound edges to any OTHER system. Under F1's
Invariant One (no orphan system, Part V §5.2 of F1), a system with genuinely zero edges to any other
system is an orphan and a defect; a system whose only edges are self-referential sits at the boundary
of that invariant, since it has edges in the graph-theoretic sense but none that connect it to the rest
of the ensemble. This dataset reads such a case as FUNCTIONALLY ORPHANED for the purposes of Part IV's
fitness evaluator (its mission-cost proxy is trivially zero, since no cross-system mission traverses
it) even though it is not, strictly, edge-count-zero under F1's literal invariant text — a distinction
this dataset flags UNRESOLVED as a question for F1's own invariant-enforcement mechanism (a future
implementation phase, not this authoring pass) rather than one this dataset resolves unilaterally by
reinterpreting a foundation-owned invariant.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. A system can be simultaneously splittable and mergeable, unmeasurable and
therefore un-actionable, self-healing without this dataset's help, or too valuable in its mess to
touch safely — four shapes the engines individually would each handle correctly in isolation but
that this Part names explicitly, because an edge case handled correctly by accident is not the same
guarantee as an edge case the dataset was built to expect.

===============================================================================================
PART XVIII — ADVERSARIAL CASES
===============================================================================================

18.1 GAMING THE MARGINAL-IMPACT FILTER. An actor — human or agent — wanting a specific topology
change approved without earning it through the ordinary evidence path could attempt to inflate a
location's apparent mission-cost proxy (Part IV §4.4) by generating synthetic mission traffic through
that location, pushing it past the marginal-impact filter's threshold (Part VII §7.3) artificially.
The defense, DERIVED from this dataset's own sourcing discipline: the fitness evaluator reads
mission-pattern frequency from IAS-A2's control-bus log, the SAME log IAS-D2's failure-fingerprint
federation and IAS-E1's observability fabric independently consume; synthetic traffic dense enough to
move IAS-G1's filter would also be visible, and flaggable as anomalous, to those independent
consumers reading the identical log for different purposes — a cross-consumer consistency check this
dataset does not itself implement but that its shared-log sourcing (rather than a private,
G1-only traffic meter) structurally enables.

18.2 CONCEALING SHADOW EDGES TO AVOID THE COMPLEXITY BUDGET. A change introduced without updating the
declaration (Part VI's EDGE SHADOWING) understates the true coupling load a layer carries, letting
subsequent changes spend against a budget that looks healthier than it is (Part VIII). This is
adversarial when done knowingly rather than through the ordinary documentation-debt path Part VI §6.4
describes for an old, onset-invisible shadow. The defense is the same drift-detection mechanism
already specified (Part VI), strengthened by the observation that a KNOWINGLY concealed shadow, once
discovered, produces a NEW-ONSET shadow signature (visible onset within the observation window,
per §6.4's distinction) rather than an old, onset-invisible one — and a new-onset shadow at a
foundational layer, where the complexity budget is tightest (Part VIII §8.2), is exactly the drift
signature this dataset's severity weighting (Part VI §6.2, HIGH-severity for shadowing) already
prioritizes for review, closing the loop without requiring a separate anti-gaming mechanism.

18.3 SANDBOX CHERRY-PICKING. A proposer could select a replay window for the mutation sandbox (Part
X) that happens to exclude the specific mission patterns most likely to reveal a candidate's real
cost — for instance, choosing a thirty-day window that misses a recurring but infrequent
cross-cluster mission pattern like Part XVI §16.4's example, which might only occur every six weeks.
The defense, NORMATIVE: the sandbox's replay window is fixed by this dataset's own protocol at a
minimum duration long enough to include at least two occurrences of every mission pattern observed
at any frequency in the estate's full historical log, not chosen ad hoc per candidate by whichever
party benefits from a favorable result — a fixed, non-negotiable window length removes the discretion
that would otherwise make cherry-picking possible, and any sandbox trial run against a shorter,
custom window is marked NON-CANONICAL in the lifecycle record and cannot advance a candidate past the
SANDBOXED state.

18.4 DEBT LAUNDERING THROUGH REPEATED SMALL CHANGES. A series of individually-small changes, each
staying just under the complexity budget's per-change overspend threshold (Part VIII §8.4), can
accumulate into the same aggregate coupling increase a single large, correctly-flagged change would
have produced — evading the overspend flag not by avoiding the cost but by fragmenting it below the
flag's per-change granularity. The defense: the budget ledger (Part VIII §8.3) reconciles CUMULATIVE
spend per layer per reconciliation cycle, not only per-change deltas in isolation, so that a sequence
of small changes that together exceed the layer's remaining margin trips the SAME overspend flag on
whichever change crosses the cumulative threshold, regardless of how small that specific change was
in isolation — the flag attaches to the LEDGER STATE the change would produce, never only to the
change's own individual size, closing the fragmentation loophole by construction rather than by
detecting the pattern after the fact.

18.5 AUTHORITY LAUNDERING THROUGH REPEATED NEAR-IDENTICAL PROPOSALS. An actor whose candidate was
rejected on the frontier, or whose finding was correctly withdrawn (Part XV §15.3's WITHDRAWAL
transition), could re-submit a near-identical proposal repeatedly, relying on ordinary variance in the
quality baseline's trend (Part III §3.4) or the mission-cost proxy's rolling window (Part IV) to
eventually produce a frontier reading favorable enough to clear whatever threshold blocked the
original attempt — effectively laundering a rejected recommendation through resubmission rather than
through genuine changed evidence. The defense is the retention discipline of Part XV §15.6-15.7: a
retained provenance history lets any NEW candidate at a previously-contested location be compared
against its own lineage, and a pattern of near-identical resubmissions with no materially new evidence
between attempts is itself a detectable signature in that history — not blocked automatically (this
dataset holds no authority to refuse a resubmission outright, per Part I §1.3's refusals), but
surfaced explicitly in the evidentiary package as a RESUBMISSION-PATTERN annotation, so arch-decision
sees the repetition rather than evaluating each attempt as if it were the first.

18.6 GRACE-PERIOD GAMING VIA COMPONENT RENAMING. Because the debt market's grace-period conversion
(Part IX §9.2, Part XIII §13.6) operates against a specific flagged FINDING, an actor could attempt to
reset an accruing grace period by trivially renaming or superficially restructuring the flagged system
or edge — a cosmetic change that alters the system's CURRENT LOCATOR (F1 §9.2) without touching its
STABLE ID, hoping the grace-period tracking keys off the locator rather than the identity and
therefore resets. The defense is structural rather than detective: every grace-period and debt-ledger
record in this dataset keys exclusively off F1's STABLE ID component of a system's address (F1 §9.2),
never off the current locator, precisely because F1 already defines stable ID as the component that
"outlives" every legitimate change including a rename — a cosmetic restructuring that changes only the
locator leaves the stable ID, and therefore the grace-period clock and the debt ledger entry, entirely
unaffected, closing this gaming vector by inheriting F1's own identity discipline rather than building
a separate anti-gaming check.

18.7 FRONTIER STUFFING: burying an unfavorable candidate among many favorable-looking ones. An actor
wanting a specific, otherwise weak candidate approved could attempt to have the topology optimizer
generate several additional, deliberately mediocre alternative candidates alongside it, so that the
desired candidate appears comparatively strong within a crowded field without ever being tested against
a genuinely competitive alternative — gaming the RELATIVE presentation of the frontier (Part V §5.7's
frontier-width discussion) rather than any single candidate's own scores. The defense: the trade-off
compiler's dominance rule (Part V §5.3) operates on ABSOLUTE deltas against the estate's rolling
baseline (Part III §3.4), never on relative rank among the SUBMITTED set alone — a candidate is only
removed from or retained on the frontier by comparison against every OTHER generated candidate's
absolute scores, and a deliberately weak filler candidate does not inflate the desired candidate's own
absolute quality, fitness, cost, or risk figures in the slightest; it only affects how many entries
surround it, which the frontier-width annotation (§5.7) reports honestly rather than as a signal of
comparative strength. Padding the frontier changes its WIDTH, not its DOMINANCE STRUCTURE, and only
the latter drives any downstream consequence.

18.8 The general principle underlying every defense in this Part, stated once more for emphasis. Eight
adversarial cases across this Part and its predecessor's §18.1-18.4 share one structural answer: this
dataset never lets an actor's own submission be the sole evidence for that submission's own evaluation.
Every score, flag, or ranking this dataset computes is grounded in a source EXTERNAL to the proposer —
a shared control-bus log, a cumulative ledger, a stable identity, an absolute baseline — precisely
because a self-referential evaluation (where the thing being judged supplies the yardstick it is judged
against) is the common root of every gaming vector named across both Parts, and closing that root once,
structurally, is a stronger defense than patching each specific gaming technique as it is separately
discovered.

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. Every adversarial case in this dataset is defeated the same structural way:
by refusing to let any engine build a private, ungrounded measurement an actor could selectively
feed, and instead sourcing every engine from shared, cross-consumed logs and cumulative ledgers that
a narrow manipulation cannot move without also becoming visible somewhere else in the estate that is
watching the same evidence for a different reason.

===============================================================================================
PART XIX — FAILURE MODES: PREMATURE OPTIMIZATION, TOPOLOGY CHURN, AND THEIR KIN
===============================================================================================

19.1 PREMATURE OPTIMIZATION, the failure this dataset is most structurally exposed to. Because Parts
III and VI generate FLAGGED findings purely from structural readings (a coupling score, a drift
signature) independent of mission-cost impact, the single greatest risk this dataset carries is
converting every structural imperfection into an actioned move regardless of whether the imperfection
is currently costing the estate anything — spending real implementation and review effort on
topology elegance the mission mix does not yet need. Part VII §7.3's marginal-impact filter and Part
XVI's worked example are this dataset's PRIMARY defense, already built into the pipeline rather than
bolted on afterward, but the failure mode is named here explicitly because a filter that exists is
not the same guarantee as a filter that is never bypassed: an Owner or agent under pressure to "clean
up the architecture" broadly, rather than by evidenced location, could request the optimizer generate
candidates for every FLAGGED location regardless of the filter's verdict, and Part XIV's decision
model does not itself forbid such a bulk override — it can only ensure that doing so is visible,
because every candidate generated against a low-marginal-impact flag still carries that flag's
reading in its frontier package (Part V §5.2), making a premature-optimization override a checkable,
attributable choice rather than an invisible default.

19.2 TOPOLOGY CHURN, the failure of correcting too fast rather than too slow. The inverse pathology:
a topology that is re-optimized so frequently that the estate spends more effort adapting to its own
architecture's constant motion than it spends on the missions that architecture exists to serve.
Churn is DERIVED as a specific reading of the debt market's EXECUTION DEBT category (Part XI §11.4)
trending upward over successive cycles — a rising rate of rollbacks and mid-flight reversals is the
leading indicator that changes are being approved and sequenced faster than the sandbox's calibration
loop (Part X §10.6) can validate them, and this dataset's countermeasure is not a hard rate limit
(which would re-introduce the ratchet problem Part VIII §8.6 already names) but a REQUIRED
COOLING-OFF WINDOW, NORMATIVE: a system that has undergone an EXECUTED-AND-RECONCILED structural
move (MERGE, SPLIT, or major RE-ROUTE) is not eligible for a second structural move within a defined
minimum number of reconciliation cycles, forcing the quality baseline to stabilize and be re-measured
against real post-change traffic before a second change compounds on top of the first — this is the
architecture-specific instance of the same discipline that prevents a financial position from being
re-hedged faster than its own risk can be re-measured.

19.3 METRIC FIXATION, the failure of optimizing the reading instead of the topology. Because Part
III's four-dimension vector and Part IV's mission-cost proxy are the instruments every other engine
reads, a subtle failure mode is optimizing FOR the instrument's reading rather than for the
underlying property the instrument is meant to track — for instance, an optimizer move that improves
the coupling-density NUMBER by re-routing an edge through an intermediary system whose sole function
becomes absorbing that one relationship, technically lowering the direct coupling score between the
original two systems while leaving the REAL, end-to-end fragility-to-change unchanged or worse (the
intermediary is now a single point every future change must also traverse). This is the specific
mechanism Part XX's anti-Goodhart countermetrics exist to catch, and it is named here as a failure
mode rather than only as a metric-design problem because its INSTITUTIONAL consequence — a topology
that reads as improving while its real fragility does not — is exactly the kind of quiet capital
misallocation the debt market (Part IX) and portfolio (IAS-C1) are built to prevent, and a metric-
fixated optimizer defeats both by construction if left unchecked.

19.4 OVER-DEFERENCE TO PARENTS, the failure of an evidentiary system that never actually gets read.
The mirror-image risk to Part XIV's careful authority-boundary discipline: a system built entirely
around producing advisory evidence can fail not by overreaching but by being IGNORED — packages
generated, frontiers ranked, sandboxes run, and no consuming decision ever actually incorporates them
because the review burden of reading a structured package is itself nontrivial and busy decision-
makers default to their prior informal judgment anyway. (HYPOTHESIZED: this dataset cannot, from its
own text, guarantee its evidence is used; Part XX's evaluation protocol specifies the concrete
measurement — a package-consumption rate — that would surface this failure empirically rather than
leave it as an unmeasured hope.) The structural mitigation available to this dataset, short of
claiming authority it should not hold, is package CONCISENESS: Part XIV §14.2's consumer-specific
packages exist precisely so a decision-maker receives only the slice relevant to their own decision
rather than the full engine stack's raw output, lowering the review cost that would otherwise drive
this failure.

19.5 THE COMPLEXITY BUDGET AS A NEW BUREAUCRACY. A budget mechanism intended to make complexity
visible and priced can itself become a source of friction if every trivial change requires a full
budget-ledger consultation disproportionate to its actual risk — the governance-overhead failure
Part VIII's own text gestures at without fully resolving. The mitigation, consistent with Part X
§10.4's risk-proportionate gating: the budget ledger is consulted and debited automatically for every
approved change (a mechanical bookkeeping step, not a review gate), and the OVERSPEND FLAG — the
only point at which the budget imposes review friction — fires only when a threshold is actually
crossed, meaning the large majority of ordinary, budget-healthy changes never surface any budget-
related friction to their approver at all; the bureaucracy risk is real only if this proportionality
is later eroded by lowering thresholds until most changes trip the flag, a drift this dataset names
as a failure mode of its OWN mechanism rather than only of the topology it governs.

19.6 CASCADING FAILURE MODES: when churn and premature optimization compound. The four failure modes
above are presented individually, but two of them — topology churn (§19.2) and premature optimization
(§19.1) — can compound into a worse combined pathology than either alone: a location subjected to a
premature, low-mission-impact structural change (§19.1's failure) is, by construction, more likely to
require a subsequent correction once real mission evidence eventually diverges from the premature
change's assumptions, and that correction, arriving before the cooling-off window (§19.2) would
ordinarily require, is itself exposed to the churn pathology. (DERIVED: this is not a new, third
failure mode requiring its own defense; it is the SAME two mechanisms interacting, and the correct
institutional response is not a third countermeasure but stricter enforcement of the FIRST — the
marginal-impact filter of Part VII §7.3 — because every premature-optimization instance the filter
correctly defers is also a churn instance that never gets the chance to occur.) This dataset names the
compound case explicitly because a reviewer evaluating churn in isolation might conclude the cooling-
off window alone is sufficient defense, when the more leveraged fix is upstream, at the point a
premature change is generated at all.

19.7 THE SILENT DEGRADATION FAILURE: a slow trend nobody's threshold catches. A final failure mode
distinct from the four above: the quality baseline's trend (Part III §3.4) is read against a ROLLING
window, and a sufficiently slow, sufficiently steady degradation — a coupling density creeping upward
by an amount below any single reconciliation cycle's noise floor, cycle after cycle, for a very long
period — can, in principle, never trip a threshold-based flag at any individual reconciliation point
even though the CUMULATIVE drift over many cycles is substantial. (UNRESOLVED: this dataset does not
currently specify a long-horizon trend detector distinct from the per-cycle baseline comparison Part
III already performs, and marks the gap honestly rather than claiming the existing mechanism already
covers it — a dedicated slow-trend detector, comparing the CURRENT reading against a reading from many
cycles prior rather than only the immediately preceding one, is named here as a plausible future
extension rather than specified as an existing engine.)

FINAL LAW — PART XIX. This dataset's own worst failures are not exotic; they are moving too fast
(churn), moving too slow (premature optimization spent on the wrong locations, or its inverse,
real signals ignored through over-deference), and optimizing the number instead of the thing the
number was built to track — four failure modes this dataset defends against structurally where it
can and names honestly where a structural defense does not yet exist.

===============================================================================================
PART XX — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

20.1 The primary metrics, each already defined in an earlier Part and gathered here as a set. This
Part does not introduce new instruments; it collects the metrics Parts III-XIX already define and
pairs each with the countermetric that catches the specific way it could be gamed or misread, per
Part XIX §19.3's warning that a tracked number can become the optimized target instead of the tracked
property. COUPLING DENSITY (Part III §3.2) is paired with END-TO-END FRAGILITY, a countermetric that
traces the FULL chain of edges a mission traverses rather than any single edge's score, catching
exactly the intermediary-absorption gaming Part XIX §19.3 describes: a topology where every
individual edge scores well but the end-to-end chain a real mission traverses has grown longer is
flagged by the countermetric even when every component metric looks healthy.

20.2 SEAM INTEGRITY (Part III §3.2) is paired with SEAM COUNT DRIFT, a countermetric tracking the
total NUMBER of seams over time, not only the health of existing ones — because a topology optimizer
under pressure to improve seam-integrity readings could, in principle, satisfy the metric by
declaring MORE, SMALLER seams (each individually easier to keep clean) rather than by genuinely
reducing cross-boundary coupling, and a rising seam count with flat or improving per-seam integrity
is the specific signature this countermetric exists to catch — proliferating boundaries is not the
same achievement as tightening them.

20.3 THE MISSION-COST PROXY (Part IV §4.4) is paired with PROXY-REALITY CORRELATION, the calibration
signal already introduced in Part X §10.6 as a sandbox mechanism and elevated here to a standing
tracked metric: the persistent gap, if any, between the proxy's predicted mission cost and the
sandbox's or post-execution real-traffic observed cost, tracked over time and by move type. A proxy
whose correlation with real outcomes is degrading — predictions increasingly wrong in a consistent
direction — is a signal that the proxy itself needs recalibration, and this dataset treats a
degrading correlation as a first-class finding routed back into Part IV's own definition rather than
as noise to be smoothed over.

20.4 COMPLEXITY BUDGET UTILIZATION (Part VIII) is paired with DEBT-MASKED UTILIZATION, a
countermetric that recomputes budget utilization AFTER adding back every outstanding debt entry's
accrued price (Part IX §9.3), because raw utilization figures can look healthy purely because
expensive trade-offs have been deferred into the debt ledger rather than genuinely avoided — a layer
sitting comfortably under its raw budget ceiling while carrying a large outstanding debt balance is
not actually as healthy as the raw utilization number alone would suggest, and this countermetric is
the one this dataset considers most institutionally important, because it is the specific number the
debt market (Part IX) exists to prevent from being hidden.

20.5 SHADOW EDGE RATE (Part VI §6.5) is paired with SHADOW SEVERITY-WEIGHTED RATE, a countermetric
that weights the raw count by the severity distribution (HIGH for depends-on shadows, lower for feeds
shadows) rather than treating every shadow as equivalent, because a raw count could improve (fewer
total shadows) while the severity mix worsens (fewer but more dangerous ones) — the same distribution-
over-average discipline Part III §3.2 already applies to coupling density, generalized here to a
different metric family.

20.6 THE PACKAGE-CONSUMPTION RATE, the countermetric for the dataset's own institutional relevance.
Named in response to Part XIX §19.4's over-deference failure mode: the proportion of evidentiary
packages (Part XIV §14.2) that a consuming decision cites, references, or demonstrably incorporates
into its recorded rationale, versus the proportion generated and never referenced. (EXPERIMENTAL:
this metric requires arch-decision's own decision records to reference which evidence they consulted,
a recording discipline this dataset cannot itself impose on a parent system — it can only propose the
metric and mark its measurability EXPERIMENTAL pending that parent's own instrumentation.) A
persistently low consumption rate would not indict the evidence's quality automatically — Part XIX
§19.4 already names review-burden as the likelier cause — but it would be the first empirical signal
that this dataset's evidentiary-authority model (Part XIV) is not, in practice, functioning as
strengthening at all, and this dataset commits, NORMATIVE, to treating a sustained low rate as
grounds for revisiting package design rather than as a fact to explain away.

20.7 DEBT RETIREMENT RATE (Part IX §9.4) is paired with RETIREMENT QUALITY, a countermetric
distinguishing a retirement that fully closes the original quality-vector gap the debt entry recorded
from a PARTIAL retirement that reduces the debt's accrued price without genuinely closing the
underlying structural condition — for instance, a re-route that reduces coupling density enough to
stop further interest accrual (§9.3) without ever addressing the seam-integrity component of the
original finding. A rising retirement RATE paired with a falling retirement QUALITY is the specific
signature this countermetric exists to catch: an estate that appears to be paying down architectural
debt quickly while, in substance, converting fully-closed debt into perpetually-reopened partial fixes
— gaming the rate metric exactly as Part XIX §19.3 warns a tracked number can be gamed, applied here
to the debt market specifically rather than to the quality vector generally.

20.8 LOCK-IN CONCENTRATION (Part XII §12.2) is paired with EXAMINED-CONCENTRATION SHARE, a
countermetric tracking what proportion of the estate's total HIGH-severity concentration readings have
actually cleared the switching-cost examination (Part XII §12.3) versus remaining unexamined —
distinguishing an estate that is genuinely reducing lock-in risk from one that is merely reducing the
RAW concentration number by redistributing dependencies without ever examining whether the
redistribution was warranted, potentially trading one unexamined concentration for a different,
equally unexamined one elsewhere in the topology. A rising examined-concentration share, even without
a corresponding drop in raw concentration, is read as genuine institutional progress under this
countermetric — because it means more of the estate's dependency structure now rests on records
decisions rather than accumulation — while a falling raw concentration with a falling examined share
is read as no progress at all, only redistribution.

20.9 Why every pair in this Part shares one underlying discipline. Each of the six countermetric pairs
gathered across §20.1 through §20.8 defends against the identical structural risk named once in Part
XIX §19.3: a tracked number, once known to be tracked, becomes a target an actor can satisfy without
improving the property the number was built to represent. (NORMATIVE: this dataset does not treat
Goodhart's general observation as a reason to avoid metrics — every engine in Parts III-XIII depends on
measurement — but as a reason no metric in this family ships without its own paired check for the
specific way IT, individually, could be gamed, rather than a generic warning appended once and trusted
to cover every instrument implicitly.)

20.10 Storage and consumption of the metric set: who reads what. The nine primary-countermetric pairs
gathered in this Part are not consumed uniformly by every downstream party. IAS-C1's portfolio reads
the debt-market pair (§20.4, §20.7) and lock-in pair (§20.8) as capital-allocation inputs; IAS-D1's
ecology reads the coupling and cohesion pairs (§20.1-20.2, folded from Part III's dimensions) as
fitness-adjacent context; IAS-E2's reliability discipline reads the shadow-severity pair (§20.5) as a
failure-budget input, per the Compounding Graph's defense loop naming failure fingerprints as a shared
signal across siblings. This dataset does not gate any of these reads — per the recurring shared-fact
principle (Part XXII §22.3) — but names, explicitly, that the metric set's VALUE is realized only once
a consuming sibling actually reads a given pair, echoing §20.6's package-consumption discipline applied
here to metrics rather than to evidentiary packages: a countermetric nobody reads defends against a
gaming vector nobody is watching for, which is not a defect in the countermetric's own design but a
reminder that this Part's metrics are, like every other artifact in this dataset, evidentiary
infrastructure rather than self-executing enforcement.

FINAL LAW — PART XX. Every primary metric in this dataset ships with a paired countermetric built
specifically to catch the way that metric could be satisfied without the underlying property actually
improving — coupling by end-to-end fragility, seam integrity by seam-count drift, the mission-cost
proxy by its own correlation with reality, budget utilization by debt-masked utilization, shadow rate
by severity weighting, and this dataset's own relevance by whether its evidence is ever actually used.

===============================================================================================
PART XXI — EVALUATION PROTOCOL, BENCHMARKS, AND HOW TO PROVE THIS DATASET WORKS
===============================================================================================

21.1 Why evaluation is a distinct Part from the metrics that feed it. Part XX defines WHAT is
measured; this Part defines HOW those measurements would be assembled into a defensible claim that
IAS-G1's mechanisms function as specified — the difference between having a thermometer and having a
protocol for what temperature range counts as healthy and how the thermometer itself is checked for
accuracy, per this dataset's own EVALUATION/EXPERIMENTAL discipline (Article V): no mechanism in
Parts III-XIX is claimed to WORK until it has been run through the protocol below, and several are
explicitly marked EXPERIMENTAL or HYPOTHESIZED precisely because that protocol has not yet been
executed against real operating history at the time of this writing.

21.2 The retrodiction benchmark, borrowed by structure from F1's own precedent. F1 §8.7 validates its
level-test formalization by running it against estate-derived shapes without knowing the answer in
advance; this dataset's primary evaluation instrument applies the identical structure to the fitness
evaluator and mission-cost proxy specifically (Part IV, flagged EXPERIMENTAL at §4.4): select a
window of the estate's ALREADY-OBSERVED history, compute the mission-cost proxy's predictions for
that window using only data available BEFORE the window began, then compare those predictions against
what the control-bus log shows actually happened during the window itself. A proxy whose retrodicted
predictions correlate strongly with the window's real observed mission costs earns OBSERVED status
for its predictive validity; a proxy that does not is either recalibrated (per Part XX §20.3's
correlation countermetric) or its EXPERIMENTAL marking is retained honestly rather than silently
upgraded.

21.3 The sandbox-fidelity benchmark. Analogous in structure: for every EXECUTED-AND-RECONCILED
structural move (Part XV's lifecycle terminal state), compare the sandbox's pre-execution observed
delta (Part X) against the quality baseline's actual post-execution reading. A systematic gap in one
direction — sandbox predictions consistently more optimistic than reality across many moves — is the
concrete signal Part X §10.6's calibration loop is designed to consume, and this benchmark is the
mechanism by which that loop's own EXPERIMENTAL convergence claim would be tested: tracking whether
the predicted-versus-observed gap narrows across successive moves of the same type (MERGE gaps
narrowing over the tenth MERGE compared to the first) or fails to narrow, in which case the
calibration loop's convergence claim is falsified rather than merely unconfirmed.

21.4 The zero-intervention control. Because every engine in this dataset is evaluated against
outcomes that also depend on decisions arch-decision, D2A, and evolution_engine make independently,
attributing an observed quality-baseline improvement to IAS-G1's evidence specifically (rather than
to those parents' own independent judgment, which might have reached the same conclusion unaided)
requires a control: a comparison, wherever ethically and practically available, between locations
where a full evidentiary package was generated and consumed and locations of comparable initial
quality reading where no package was generated (Part XIX §19.1's premature-optimization filter
naturally creates this control by deferring intervention at many FLAGGED locations) — tracking
whether the deferred, package-free locations drift further from baseline than the packaged,
consumed ones over the same number of cycles. (UNRESOLVED: this dataset does not have, at authoring
time, a sufficient volume of real dual-track history to run this control with statistical confidence;
it specifies the protocol and marks the actual comparison as a future evaluation task, consistent
with Article VI's ban on claiming evidence this dataset does not yet possess.)

21.5 Domain-appropriate benchmark scope, and what this dataset explicitly refuses to benchmark
against. Per Constitution Article VII, no benchmark in this protocol imports any other product
ecosystem's architecture metrics as a comparison baseline — the external depth-calibration reference
the constitution names is a depth benchmark for THIS DOCUMENT's prose density only, never a source of
comparative architecture data, and this dataset's benchmarks are scoped exclusively to the estate's
own historical topology, its own control-bus log, and its own quality-baseline trend, with no
cross-ecosystem comparison of any kind.

21.6 The seal condition for this dataset's own claims. Per the state machine (Constitution, Dataset
state machine section), this dataset reaches HARDENED only once each EXPERIMENTAL claim in Parts III-
XX has either been run through the relevant benchmark above and upgraded to OBSERVED, or has been
explicitly retained as EXPERIMENTAL with the retrodiction, sandbox-fidelity, or control protocol named
as its pending test — this dataset does not claim HARDENED status for itself in this text, because
the benchmarks above require operating history this authoring pass does not possess; it claims only
CONTENT_COMPLETE, per Article XI's explicit distinction between the two, with the evaluation path to
HARDENED fully specified rather than merely gestured at.

21.7 Benchmark cadence: how often the protocols above should run once operating history exists. The
retrodiction benchmark (§21.2) and sandbox-fidelity benchmark (§21.3) are not one-time validations
performed once and trusted indefinitely; per Part III §3.4's staleness discipline applied here to the
evaluation layer itself, a proxy or a calibration loop validated against one window of history is only
evidenced as OBSERVED for the conditions that window represented, and the estate's mission mix is
itself expected to shift over time (the exact premise Part IV §4.6's UNRESOLVED backward-looking
limitation names). This dataset recommends, NORMATIVE, that both benchmarks re-run on the same cadence
Part VI §6.6 already establishes for drift reconciliation — scaled by layer depth — so that a proxy's
predictive validity is re-confirmed at roughly the same rate the topology itself is checked for drift,
rather than validated once at authoring time and never revisited as the estate's mission mix evolves
underneath it.

21.8 Auditability as a benchmark property, not only a compliance nicety. Because Part XIV §14.7's audit
trail requirement guarantees every historical package's inputs are reconstructable, this dataset's
evaluation protocol is itself independently AUDITABLE by a party other than the one that ran it — a
different reviewer, given the same retained provenance chain, should be able to re-derive the same
retrodiction and sandbox-fidelity scores from the same historical inputs and reach the same OBSERVED-
or-EXPERIMENTAL verdict. (DERIVED: this reproducibility requirement is what separates an evaluation
protocol from a self-report — a benchmark whose results only the running party can verify is not
evidence in the sense Article V's epistemology intends, and this dataset holds its own evaluation
claims to the identical evidentiary bar it holds every engine's output to throughout Parts III-XIX.)

21.9 The minimum operating history this protocol requires before it can run meaningfully at all. None
of the three benchmarks above (retrodiction, sandbox-fidelity, zero-intervention control) is
computable from zero or near-zero operating history — a retrodiction test needs a window of PRIOR
history to train on and a SEPARATE window to validate against, meaning at minimum two full
reconciliation-cycle windows of real control-bus data must exist before the benchmark can run even
once; the sandbox-fidelity benchmark needs at least one completed EXECUTED-AND-RECONCILED lifecycle
per move type to have any predicted-versus-observed pair to compare; and the zero-intervention control
needs enough parallel FLAGGED-but-deferred locations (per Part XIX §19.1's marginal-impact filter
correctly deferring many of them) to build a comparison group with any statistical meaning. This
dataset states the minimum honestly, UNRESOLVED as to the exact cycle count required, rather than
implying the protocol is runnable from this authoring pass's zero operating history — a distinction
consistent with Article VIII's Reality Contract, which forbids claiming a mechanism works before the
evidence for that claim exists.

FINAL LAW — PART XXI. A mechanism is not proven by being specified; the retrodiction benchmark tests
the mission-cost proxy against history it did not see in advance, the sandbox-fidelity benchmark
tests whether predicted deltas converge toward observed ones across repeated moves, and the
zero-intervention control isolates this dataset's own evidentiary contribution from its parents'
independent judgment — three protocols this dataset specifies honestly as pending rather than claims
it has not yet earned the right to make.

PART XXII — INTEGRATION WITH D1, F3, C1, AND THE THREE NAMED PARENTS; EVOLUTION OF G1 ITSELF
===============================================================================================

22.1 Integration with IAS-D1 (System Ecology), the fitness source. Per the Compounding Graph's
primary loop ("IAS-D1 fitness/parasitism → IAS-G1 topology, retire-vs-invest"), IAS-D1 is the single
most load-bearing upstream input this dataset consumes: Part IV's fitness evaluator reads D1's
system-level fitness scores directly rather than re-deriving them, and Part XII's lock-in detection
and Part XIII's dependency governor both route their findings back toward D1's own retire-vs-invest
judgment where a topology finding implicates a system's continued existence rather than merely its
wiring. The relationship is bidirectional: this dataset's topology-level readings — which systems sit
at high-fan-in concentration points, which systems show declining cohesion — are themselves a signal
D1's ecology map has reason to read back in, since a system's fitness for its environment is partly a
function of how well-placed it is in the topology, an input D1 does not itself instrument and that
this dataset is positioned to supply, closing the primary loop exactly as the Compounding Graph
specifies rather than merely receiving from D1 one-directionally.

22.2 Integration with IAS-F3 (Digital Twin), the scenario source. Part X §10.2 draws the boundary
explicitly: the mutation sandbox replays OBSERVED history through a candidate shape; it does not
generate hypothetical future scenarios. IAS-F3's digital twin is the sibling that simulates
architecture scenarios the estate has not yet lived through — a NEW mission type, a demand spike, a
provider outage — and this dataset's contribution to that simulation is supplying the CURRENT
topology object (Part II) and its typed relation graph as F3's simulation substrate, exactly as the
Compounding Graph's cross-dataset feedback ledger specifies ("IAS-F3 simulated outcomes → DRK,
one_shot, IAS-G1 — decision, mission plan, architecture"): F3 returns simulated outcomes for
CANDIDATE topology changes this dataset's trade-off compiler (Part V) could not otherwise price,
because a candidate's cost against a mission pattern that has never occurred is not something Part
X's replay-only sandbox can compute, and F3's forward-looking simulation is precisely the
complementary instrument this dataset needs and does not itself build.

22.3 Integration with IAS-C1 (Capability Portfolio), the allocation consumer. Though C1 is not among
this dataset's four named-strengthened parents in the Strengthening Matrix, the debt market (Part IX
§9.4) and the complexity budget (Part VIII) both produce ledgers C1's own portfolio ranking is
positioned to consume as an additional input alongside its existing signals (cost, ROI, recall) —
this dataset does not build a portfolio ranking itself, per Part I §1.3's authority refusals
generalized to a fourth sibling, but its debt and budget ledgers are exactly the kind of shared fact
F1 §4.4 describes as converting what would be redundant measurement into one shared record multiple
consumers read, here demonstrated for a sibling outside G1's own named-strengthened set, confirming
this dataset's outputs compound beyond its four formally declared parents.

22.4 Integration with arch-decision, D2A, and evolution_engine, synthesized. Part XIV already
specifies the consumer-specific packages each of these three receives; this Part adds the temporal
frame: arch-decision consumes this dataset's evidence AT THE MOMENT of a proposed change (the
topology impact package), D2A consumes it WHEN A DUPLICATION IS FIRST DETECTED (the duplication
frequency package, informing whether an overlap is a first occurrence or a recurring pattern this
dataset's own history already shows), and evolution_engine consumes it AT EXECUTION TIME (the
sequenced execution package). The three integrations are not redundant paths to the same information;
each activates at a different point in a decision's own lifecycle, and this dataset's evidence base
is built once and read three times, at three different moments, by three different parents — the
concrete mechanism by which Article III's "strengthen, never replace" is satisfied for all three
named parents simultaneously rather than sequentially.

22.5 The evolution of IAS-G1 itself, and the recursion this implies. Because IAS-G1 governs the
estate's topology and IAS-G1 is itself part of that topology (a system with its own edges — DEPENDS-
ON toward IAS-A2's control-bus log, F1's ontology, F2's advantage algebra; FEEDS toward arch-decision,
D2A, evolution_engine; STRENGTHENS toward D1, F3, C1), this dataset is, in principle, a valid input to
its own quality science, fitness evaluator, and drift detector. This dataset does not resolve the
resulting recursion by exempting itself — Part VII §7.6's near-miss discipline forbids special
pleading — but it names the practical constraint explicitly: applying Part X's mutation sandbox to a
candidate change in IAS-G1's OWN wiring would require replaying mission traffic that includes IAS-G1's
own evidentiary packages as part of what is replayed, a self-referential trial this dataset marks
UNRESOLVED as a matter for whichever future implementation phase actually operationalizes this
mechanism, rather than a gap this foundation-adjacent authoring pass can close from text alone.

22.6 Open questions, named rather than answered. Three questions this dataset leaves explicitly
UNRESOLVED, consistent with Article V's discipline that an open question named is more honest than a

22.8 Closing synthesis: what changes about the estate once this dataset is read and used. Before
IAS-G1, the estate's topology existed only as the residue of many separate, locally-sound decisions,
visible in full to no single mechanism and legible, at the ensemble scale, to nobody — not because any
individual decision was wrong, but because the object those decisions collectively shaped was never
anyone's to govern. After this dataset, that object has a name, a measured quality vector, a mission-

— each retains exactly the decision right it held before this dataset existed — and that is precisely
the measure of this dataset's success under Article III: the estate's shape is now visible, priced,
and sequenced, and the three systems that act on that shape act better for it, without any one of them
having been replaced.

===============================================================================================
DECISION LOG
===============================================================================================

DL-1. Decision: scope the topology object to structural edges only (FEEDS, DEPENDS-ON, COMPETES-
WITH, STRENGTHENS), excluding CONSUMES and treating DUPLICATES as transitional (Part II §2.2).
Alternatives considered: (a) include CONSUMES edges as structural, giving the optimizer visibility
into every transient mission-time invocation; (b) treat DUPLICATES as a stable sixth structural
type. Why this: a topology that recomputed on every transient consumes edge would never stabilize
long enough to baseline-trend (Part III §3.4), and a duplicates edge is, by F1's own definition,
evidence of a state in transition rather than a steady-state relation. Trade-offs: the optimizer is
blind to mission-time routing patterns except as read secondhand through the fitness evaluator's
separate consumption of the control-bus log — an acceptable cost because that secondhand read
already exists and duplicating it into the topology object itself would be redundant instrumentation.
Reversal conditions: if a future operating history shows CONSUMES edges recur with enough regularity
to be effectively structural (the same mission pattern invoking the same pair of systems every cycle
for a year), the topology definition should be revisited to promote such recurring consumes edges
into the structural set.

DL-2. Decision: refuse to compose Part III's four quality dimensions into a single architecture
health score. Alternatives considered: (a) a weighted composite score for dashboard simplicity; (b)
letting each downstream consumer define its own composite. Why this: a single scalar invites the
exact length/vocabulary-biased collapse the estate's own memory ledger already documents for an
unrelated composite provider score, and a per-consumer composite would silently fragment into
mutually incomparable dialects, the DFP-02 failure F1 §3.3 names. Trade-offs: every downstream
engine must read a four-element vector and apply its own weighting logic rather than one number,
raising each consumer's integration cost modestly. Reversal conditions: none currently anticipated;
this is treated as a durable design commitment rather than a provisional one, because the failure
mode it prevents is structural, not merely inconvenient.

DL-3. Decision: require sandbox validation (Part X) for MERGE and SPLIT moves only, leaving RE-ROUTE
and PRUNE optional. Alternatives considered: (a) mandatory sandboxing for all four move types; (b)
no mandatory sandboxing, leaving it entirely to arch-decision's discretion. Why this: mandatory-for-
all imposes sandbox cost disproportionate to the low reversal cost of re-routes and prunes, while
no-mandatory-anywhere leaves the two highest-reversal-cost move types unvalidated by construction.
Trade-offs: a genuinely high-risk re-route (one that happens to touch a foundational-layer edge) is
not automatically sandboxed under this rule and depends on arch-decision electing the optional path.
Reversal conditions: if execution-debt data (Part XI §11.4) shows a material share of rollbacks
originate from unsandboxed re-routes specifically, the requirement should extend to any move type
whose target sits at or above a defined layer-depth threshold, regardless of move type.

DL-4. Decision: fold lock-in detection (candidate 124) and the dependency governor (candidate 9)
into this dataset rather than leaving them as thin references elsewhere. Alternatives considered: (a)
leave both as pure REFERENCE per the ledger's general disposition for their parent families; (b)
build the dependency governor as a subengine of IAS-A2 instead, since it touches the relation graph
IAS-A2's control bus carries. Why this: both candidates' distinct objects, on the level test of F1
§8.2, resolve to properties of the SAME topology object this dataset already governs (fan-in
concentration and edge-scope minimality are both graph-shape properties, not event-transport
properties), and building either as a genuinely separate engine would duplicate Part II's object
model rather than reuse it. Trade-offs: this dataset's scope is measurably wider than a strict
reading of "Family G candidates only" would suggest, raising its own internal complexity — a cost
this dataset accepts consciously and names in Part XIX §19.5 as a risk to watch in its own mechanism.
Reversal conditions: if either engine's evidence needs diverge enough from the topology object's
existing property model that they require a genuinely separate instrumentation path, they should be
split back out as independent subengines under their originally-suggested parents.

DL-5. Decision: hold IAS-G1's authority as strictly evidentiary, with zero final-authority decision
classes of its own (Part XIV §14.1). Alternatives considered: (a) grant IAS-G1 final authority over
low-stakes move types (PRUNE, declaration-only corrections) to reduce arch-decision's review burden
for trivial cases; (b) grant conditional authority, auto-approving any candidate scoring above a
fixed confidence threshold. Why this: Invariant Three (F1 Part V §5.4) requires singular authority
per decision class, and "architectural changes to this system" is arch-decision's decision class in
full, with no principled sub-carve-out this dataset could draw without effectively creating a second
final authority over a fraction of the same class. Trade-offs: even trivial, near-zero-risk
corrections (Part XV §15.5's declaration-only example) require an explicit approval step, adding
minor process overhead to the lowest-risk cases specifically. Reversal conditions: if this overhead
is empirically shown, via the package-consumption metric (Part XX §20.6), to suppress adoption of
even trivial corrections at a materially higher rate than higher-stakes ones, a narrowly-scoped
delegated-approval mechanism for PRUNE-only, declaration-only candidates could be proposed to
arch-decision as a governance change arch-decision itself would need to authorize.

DL-6. Decision: define lock-in severity by concentration COMBINED with unexamined switching cost,
never by concentration alone (Part XII §12.3). Alternatives considered: (a) flag on concentration
alone, treating any high fan-in on a single dependency as inherently a defect; (b) never flag
concentration, treating it as entirely arch-decision's business. Why this: concentration alone
cannot distinguish a rational, deliberate standardization from an unexamined accumulation, and
flagging every high concentration regardless of context would generate false-positive findings that
erode trust in the detector's other, genuinely actionable output. Trade-offs: the switching-cost
condition depends on arch-decision and D2A records being searchable within a lookback window, a
dependency this dataset cannot itself guarantee is well-maintained. Reversal conditions: if the
estate's decision-record archive proves too sparse or inconsistent to reliably support the
switching-cost check, the severity model should fall back to concentration-alone with a wider,
more conservative HIGH threshold until record-keeping improves.

Gap closed. Before this dataset, no system read the estate's topology as one governed object over
time: arch-decision judges one change at a time, D2A disposes one duplication event, evolution_engine
executes one mutation once chosen — three parents, each correct within its own scope, none able to
answer whether the ACCUMULATED sum of their locally-sound work coheres into a topology that is, as a
whole, legible, appropriately layered, and free of unpriced complexity. IAS-G1 closes exactly this
gap: it supplies the quality science, fitness evaluation, trade-off compilation, drift detection,
generative optimization, budget/debt economics, sandboxed validation, and sequenced planning that
together let the estate reason about its own shape rather than only about its most recent decision.

Systems strengthened, with the mechanism restated concretely. arch-decision is strengthened by
receiving a topology-impact package (Part XIV §14.2) — a Pareto frontier with quality, fitness, cost,
and risk deltas — in place of unstructured intuition for every reviewed change. D2A is strengthened
by receiving a duplication-frequency package showing whether a proposed overlap is a first occurrence
or the Nth instance of a recurring class, information no single-event view of D2A's own object could
generate. evolution_engine is strengthened by receiving sequenced, invariant-legal execution steps
with named rollback points instead of an unordered batch of approved moves. EXISTING_SYSTEM_
INTEGRATION is strengthened because the dependency governor's minimality discipline (Part XIII) and
the drift detector's shadow-edge detection (Part VI) both directly reduce the class of undeclared or
over-broad integration this system's own wiring checks exist to catch, giving it topology-level
context — which edges are minimal, which are shadowed — its own per-integration checks do not
themselves compute.

Duplications avoided, named specifically. This dataset avoided re-building a second cost meter (Part
IV §4.2 reads IAS-D1's fitness and the Cognitive OS's cost telemetry rather than re-measuring), a
second event-transport mechanism (Part VI §6.3 reads IAS-A2's control-bus log rather than
instrumenting a parallel one), a second forecasting engine (Part IV §4.6 and Part XXII §22.2
explicitly defer forward-looking scenario work to IAS-F3 and demand forecasting to IAS-C2), and a
second portfolio ranking (Part XXII §22.3 supplies ledgers to IAS-C1 rather than ranking capital
allocation itself).

New capabilities this dataset uniquely introduces. The four-dimension architecture quality vector
with its refusal to compose into one score (Part III); the mission-weighted marginal-impact filter
that prevents premature structural intervention (Part VII §7.3); the per-layer complexity budget
with mandatory-to-surface overspend flags (Part VIII); the debt market's issuance-price-retire
lifecycle distinguishing decision, execution, and neglected-recommendation debt (Part IX, Part XI
§11.4, Part XIII §13.6); the replay-based mutation sandbox as a cheaper alternative to full scenario
simulation for validating already-observed-history fit (Part X); and the fold of lock-in detection
and dependency-minimality governance into a single topology-shape object rather than two separate
mechanisms (Parts XII-XIII).

Open risks, named without softening. The package-consumption rate (Part XX §20.6) is, at authoring

engines — remains UNRESOLVED. And the fold of two Family-A/M candidates into this dataset (DL-4)
measurably widens its own internal complexity, a cost this dataset must itself remain honest about
under its own quality science once sufficient operating history exists to apply that science
reflexively.

===============================================================================================
ANTI-PATTERNS
===============================================================================================

AP-1. Treating a FLAGGED finding as an implicit mandate to act. The most likely implementation error:
a team or agent operationalizing this dataset reads Part III's quality science as producing a to-do
list, and routes every FLAGGED location straight to CANDIDATE-GENERATED without applying Part VII
§7.3's marginal-impact filter, converting the pipeline into exactly the premature-optimization
failure Part XIX §19.1 names. Prevention: implement the marginal-impact filter as a mandatory gate
BEFORE candidate generation, not as an optional check a time-pressured implementation can skip, and
require every generated candidate's frontier package to carry the filter's own reading (both factors,
not a pass/fail bit) so a bypass is visible rather than silent, exactly as Part XIX §19.1 already
specifies for the deliberate-override case.

AP-2. Collapsing the four-dimension quality vector into a single score for dashboard convenience. The
second most likely error, because a vector is genuinely harder to display and reason about at a
glance than one number, and the pressure to simplify for a status report is real and recurring.
Prevention: any dashboard or summary artifact built on this dataset's output must display the vector
by name (coupling density, cohesion spread, seam integrity, layer cleanliness) even in a compressed
form, never reduce it to a computed composite before the point of consumption — Decision Log DL-2
already names this as a durable commitment, and an implementation that quietly composes the vector
"just for the summary view" has violated that commitment regardless of whether the underlying engines
still compute the vector correctly internally.

AP-3. Letting IAS-G1's evidentiary packages drift toward de facto authority through repeated,
unquestioned acceptance. The subtlest error: even without any explicit authority grant, if arch-
decision, D2A, and evolution_engine consistently and mechanically accept every candidate this dataset
generates without independent scrutiny over a long enough period, the evidentiary relationship
Part XIV establishes erodes in PRACTICE into something closer to final authority, even though no
single decision violated Invariant Three explicitly. Prevention: the package-consumption metric (Part
XX §20.6) should track not only whether a package was consulted but whether consultation ever
produced a DEVIATION from the package's own frontier ranking — a 100% acceptance rate sustained over
many cycles is itself a signal worth investigating, not necessarily evidence of good evidence, because
it is indistinguishable from rubber-stamping until independently checked.
