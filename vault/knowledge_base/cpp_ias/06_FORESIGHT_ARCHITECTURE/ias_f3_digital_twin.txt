PART I — THE FEDERATION DIGITAL-TWIN OBJECT
===============================================================================================

1.1 The founding gap. The Claude Power Pack estate, as OBSERVED across the eighteen sealed dataset
domains and seventy-plus executable modules that IAS-F1 catalogues, makes every one of its
consequential choices by direct experiment on the real estate. A new hook is wired and its effect on
the Bash-bridge failure rate is observed after the fact. A new dataset family is authored and its
effect on token burn is measured after the fact. A capability is retired and its downstream breakage
is discovered after the fact, by the systems that depended on it failing. (OBSERVED: the Post-Ship
Value axis, T-OWNER-QUEUE-INVISIBLE-001, and the DRK precedent-length-bias finding in
project_drk_active_scs_c89 are three independent instances of the same pattern — the estate learns
its own consequences empirically, one real event at a time, because it has no way to rehearse them.)
This is not a failure of the estate's component systems; DRK computes blast radius correctly for the
one decision it is given, one_shot checks feasibility correctly for the one contract it is given, and
arch-decision reasons correctly about the one architectural choice in front of it. The gap is that
none of them, individually or in sum, holds a standing model of the ensemble that can absorb a
hypothetical change and report what happens next without the change actually happening. (DERIVED: a
federation that can only learn by doing pays the full cost of every mistake before it can price the
mistake.)

1.2 What a digital twin is, precisely, in this corpus. A DIGITAL TWIN, as DEFINED here, is a
maintained computational representation of a real system that (a) mirrors the real system's
structure and state closely enough that operations performed on the twin predict, with a stated
uncertainty, the outcome of the same operation performed on the real system, and (b) is kept
synchronized with the real system so that the prediction does not silently decay into fantasy. Two
properties separate a twin from a mere model: FIDELITY (how closely the twin's structure and state
track the real estate, specified in Part X) and CURRENCY (how quickly the twin's state is
re-synchronized after the real estate changes). A twin with high fidelity and low currency is a
photograph, accurate about a moment that has already passed. A twin with high currency and low
fidelity is a rumor updated in real time. IAS-F3 exists to hold both properties in tension
deliberately, naming the trade-off rather than pretending it away. (NORMATIVE.)

1.3 The federation twin's four constituent models. The twin is not one artifact but four coupled
sub-models, each mirroring one layer of the ensemble object IAS-F1 defines. The STRUCTURAL MODEL
mirrors the system/subsystem/engine/capability graph and the typed relations among them (imported by
reference from IAS-F1 Part II, III, VI — never re-derived). The STATE MODEL mirrors the ensemble
state IAS-F1 Part III defines: which systems are active, which capabilities are currently invested
in, current portfolio allocation from IAS-C1, current observability readings from IAS-E1. The
DYNAMICS MODEL encodes how the ensemble evolves under an action — a mission dispatch, a build, a
retirement, a governance rule change — expressed as transition functions over the state model,
sourced from the empirically observed transition patterns IAS-D1 (ecology) and IAS-E2 (reliability)
maintain. The UNCERTAINTY MODEL carries, for every prediction the twin emits, an explicit confidence
band derived from how much of the prediction rests on OBSERVED transition data versus HYPOTHESIZED
extrapolation. A twin that reports a single point prediction without an uncertainty band is not
reporting a simulation; it is reporting a guess dressed as a fact, and Part XV names this failure mode
explicitly. (DEFINED + NORMATIVE.)

1.4 Why the twin is federation-scoped, not decision-scoped — the explicit boundary against DRK. The
ledger's verdict is precise on this point and IAS-F3 must honor it exactly: DRK computes blast radius
for ONE decision, walking outward from that decision through the dependency graph to the systems it
touches, and returns a bounded, decision-local answer. IAS-F3 does not replace that computation; it
supplies the substrate DRK's blast-radius walk runs against, and it extends the question DRK cannot
ask: "if we ran ten different missions this quarter, in what order, under what capital constraint,
what does the FEDERATION look like at the end, not just what does any one decision touch?" A
decision-scoped tool answers "what does this touch." A federation-scoped tool answers "what does the
federation become." The two are complementary and irreducibly different in scope: DRK is exercised
per-decision, at decision time, by the system proposing the decision; IAS-F3 is exercised per-mission
or per-quarter, at planning time, by whichever system (one_shot, IAS-C1, the Owner) needs to see the
aggregate consequence of a sequence of decisions before committing to the sequence. (DERIVED from
SYSTEM_STRENGTHENING_MATRIX row IAS-F3: "simulates the federation before spending real resources";
NORMATIVE: any later extension of IAS-F3 that collapses back to single-decision blast radius is
duplicating DRK and must be rejected under Article I.)

1.5 The route-before-spend doctrine. The twin's institutional purpose, stated as the single sentence
that governs every mechanism in this dataset, is: never spend real cognitive capital on a mission,
build, or governance change until the twin has run it and the predicted outcome (with its uncertainty
band) has been seen. This is not a claim that simulation replaces execution — Article VIII of the
Constitution forbids production code this mission, and even in a later implementation mission a twin
is advisory, never authoritative, per the D2A precedent that IAS proposes and the Owner promotes. It
is a claim that the first-order default changes: today the estate's default is "build, then observe
the consequence"; the twin's normative claim is that the default becomes "simulate, then build only
if the simulated consequence clears the bar the real decision-owning system (DRK, one_shot, IAS-C1)
sets." (NORMATIVE.) The remainder of this dataset specifies the architecture, mechanisms, and gates
that make that reordering possible without inflating into a coordinator that duplicates the
decision-owning systems it serves.

1.6 The cost of remaining ungoverned, and why this is the layer built now rather than deferred.
This dataset does not claim the estate is in crisis without a twin — the eighteen sealed domains
function, missions complete, and the estate has survived on hindsight-driven learning for its entire
history to date. The claim is narrower and, this corpus holds, stronger: the COST of hindsight-only
learning is not visible in any single mistake, because any single mistake is absorbed and the estate
moves on; it is visible only in AGGREGATE, as the sum of every mission that was launched and later
rerouted, every architecture change that landed and later needed unwinding, every capability built
that later sat idle. (DERIVED: an aggregate cost that no single incident report captures is exactly
the kind of cost an ensemble-level system, not a resource-level one, exists to see — the same
argument IAS-F1 Part I.4 makes for the ensemble object generally, applied here to the specific
currency of wasted rehearsal-free spend.) The three precedents cited in 1.1 are not exhaustive; they
are illustrative samples of a pattern this dataset asserts, at HYPOTHESIZED confidence pending the
divergence-record evidence Part XVII specifies, recurs continuously and invisibly rather than
occasionally and visibly. This is why the twin belongs among the CPP-IAS foundation-adjacent builds
rather than as a late-stage refinement: every quarter without it is a quarter of hindsight-only spend
compounding, and the compounding graph's foresight loop (COMPOUNDING_GRAPH, "the foresight loop")
cannot begin tightening until this dataset's mechanisms exist for IAS-C2 and IAS-C1 to close the loop
against.

1.7 What the twin explicitly does not claim to solve, stated for calibration. Consistent with this
dataset's epistemic discipline, it is worth naming three things a federation digital twin, however
well built, does NOT solve, so that its eventual implementation is not held to a standard it never
proposed to meet. First, it does not eliminate genuine novelty — Part 22.5 names this directly: a
mission type with zero calibration history produces a wide, honest band, not a narrow wrong one, and
the twin cannot rehearse its way out of the estate's first encounter with something genuinely new.
Second, it does not replace Owner judgment on values questions — whether a given trade-off (more
capital spent for a lower failure rate, say) is worth making is a preference the twin cannot compute,
only the trade-off's shape, per Part 14.3's advisory-only boundary. Third, it does not, by existing,
guarantee its own fidelity — Part X and Part XI exist precisely because a twin is exactly as
trustworthy as its maintenance discipline, never more, and this dataset treats that as a permanent
operating condition rather than a bug to be fixed once. (NORMATIVE: naming what a system does not
solve, at the point of specifying what it does, is itself a discipline this dataset applies to itself
that it expects of no other system in this corpus by name — a modesty this dataset's own central risk,
simulation-trusted-as-reality, makes worth practicing from its very first Part.)

FINAL LAW — PART I. A federation that can only learn its own consequences by living them has no
foresight, only hindsight with a delay; the digital twin exists to let the estate rehearse a
consequence at the ensemble scale before paying for it, and it does so by modeling the federation DRK,
one_shot, and arch-decision each see only a slice of, never by replacing any one of their
decision-local judgments.

===============================================================================================
PART II — MISSION SIMULATION: ROUTE BEFORE SPEND
===============================================================================================

2.1 The mission as the twin's primary unit of rehearsal. A MISSION, imported by reference from the
capability router's vocabulary (IAS-A1) and the ensemble object (IAS-F1), is a bounded unit of work
that a capability router would otherwise dispatch directly to one or more systems. Mission simulation
is DEFINED as running the router's dispatch decision against the twin's state model instead of against
the live estate, observing which systems the twin predicts would be engaged, what capital the twin
predicts would be consumed, and what state changes the twin predicts would result, all before the
router issues the real dispatch. (DERIVED: the router (IAS-A1) already decides which systems a
mission reaches; mission simulation does not replace that decision, it previews it.) The output of a
mission simulation is a MISSION FORECAST: a structured prediction naming the systems engaged, the
estimated capital cost banded by uncertainty, the estimated duration, the systems whose state changes
as a side effect, and a confidence level for the forecast as a whole, derived from how much of the
dispatch path runs through OBSERVED transition data versus HYPOTHESIZED extrapolation.

2.2 The route-before-spend gate mechanics. When a mission's estimated cost (from the twin's capital
sub-model, itself fed by IAS-C1's portfolio state) exceeds a threshold the Owner or a governing
contract sets — analogous to the L/XL size classes HR-ONESHOT-001 and HR-SPEC-001 already gate on —
the mission must pass through mission simulation before real dispatch. This is DEFINED as the
route-before-spend gate, and it is deliberately narrow: below the threshold, simulation overhead
exceeds its value and the mission dispatches directly, matching the estate's existing NANO/MICRO cost
discipline (HR-COST-003) that reserves heavier machinery for heavier missions. (NORMATIVE.) The gate
does not duplicate one_shot's feasibility check on a single contract; it operates one level up, on
the mission before it is decomposed into the contract one_shot would compile, catching mismatches
between what the mission implies for the ensemble and what the estate can currently sustain, before
the cost of compiling a detailed contract for a mission that was never going to be feasible.

2.3 Divergence tracking as the twin's core discipline. Every simulated mission that subsequently
executes for real produces a DIVERGENCE RECORD: the delta between the mission forecast and the
observed outcome, across every dimension the forecast predicted (systems engaged, capital consumed,
duration, side-effect state changes). A divergence record is not an error report on the mission; it is
an accuracy report on the TWIN. (DERIVED: the twin's only defense against becoming an internally
consistent fantasy is a running ledger of how often, and by how much, its predictions miss.) A
divergence record with zero variance across many missions is itself a warning sign worth
investigating — Part XI treats a suspiciously perfect twin as a fidelity failure mode, not a success,
because real federations have irreducible stochasticity the model should reproduce, not smooth away.
Divergence records accumulate into the fidelity metric of Part X and feed directly back into
recalibrating the dynamics sub-model, closing a loop the estate has never had: simulate, act,
compare, recalibrate, simulate better.

2.4 Worked example on synthetic data — a cross-system mission. Consider a synthetic mission,
constructed for illustration and not drawn from any real project: "author twelve new dataset Parts
across three domains this week, using two parallel agent panes, while the Owner is offline for four
of the seven days." Mission simulation decomposes this against the twin's structural model: which
systems the mission would engage (the authoring pipeline, the two-pane parallel-subagent cap the
Windows Bash-bridge doctrine imposes, the compound-learnings sentinel that fires on session end, the
context-pressure watchdog). The twin's dynamics sub-model, calibrated on OBSERVED session-length and
context-consumption patterns from prior authoring sessions of comparable density, forecasts: capital
cost in the MACRO band with 70% confidence and a HYPOTHESIZED tail risk of crossing into ULTRA if
either pane hits three consecutive tool-shape failures and must escalate per HR-ONESHOT-003; expected
completion in five to six of the seven days at the twin's stated confidence; and a side-effect state
change flagging that the compound-learnings sentinel will very likely fire (more than five new
learning files) given the volume, which the mission forecast surfaces as an expected, not accidental,
downstream event. This forecast is what a route-before-spend gate would show the Owner or the
dispatching system before the mission launches — not a guarantee, a rehearsal with named uncertainty.

2.5 Non-simulation as an admissible outcome, not a silent default. A mission may legitimately bypass
simulation — genuine urgency, a mission type the twin has no calibrated dynamics for yet, an Owner
override — but the bypass must be RECORDED, not silent, because an unsimulated MACRO+ mission is
exactly the kind of event the divergence ledger of Part II.3 needs visibility into even when it never
passed through the gate at all. (NORMATIVE: bypass is a first-class, logged path, never an invisible
default; an invisible bypass would let the route-before-spend doctrine erode one exception at a time,
the same erosion pattern the Reality Contract names for code that quietly ships incomplete.) This
closes the loop between the aspirational doctrine of Part I.5 and the operational mechanism:
route-before-spend is enforced not by making unsimulated dispatch impossible, but by making it
visible, logged, and therefore subject to the same Owner review any other governance exception
receives.

2.6 Mission simulation's relationship to the mission's own internal decomposition. A mission, once
dispatched, typically decomposes into sub-tasks that a router (IAS-A1) assigns across systems in
sequence or in parallel; mission simulation, as specified in 2.1, forecasts the mission as a whole
rather than simulating every sub-task's internal dispatch decision separately, a deliberate
resolution choice. (DERIVED: simulating at the sub-task level would require the twin's dynamics model
to mirror IAS-A1's own routing logic in full fidelity, which risks exactly the kind of shadow-system
duplication Part 14.4 forbids for observability — the twin forecasts what IAS-A1's router is EXPECTED
to produce in aggregate, sourced from the router's own historical dispatch patterns, rather than
re-implementing the router's decision function internally.) This resolution choice has a named cost:
a mission forecast can be wrong about AGGREGATE capital and duration while being silent on which
specific sub-task drove the miss, and a divergence record (2.3) attributed to the whole mission cannot,
by itself, localize the error to one sub-task. Where localization matters — for instance when a
recalibration decision (Part 15.5) needs to know whether a persistent miss traces to one sub-task
class or is genuinely aggregate — the twin's dynamics sub-model MAY be extended, as a documented future
refinement rather than a present commitment, to carry sub-task-level attribution; this dataset flags
that refinement as a candidate rather than specifying it, because Article VI's floor on depth does not
require specifying every possible future extension, only the mechanism this dataset commits to now.

2.7 Worked edge case — a mission whose real dispatch diverges from its simulated dispatch path
entirely. Consider a synthetic mission simulated as engaging systems {A, B, C} with a MICRO-band
capital forecast, but whose real dispatch, once launched, is redirected by IAS-A1's router to systems
{A, D, E} instead, because a capability that did not exist at simulation time (a system sealed between
simulation and dispatch, Part 10.3's reconciliation-lag scenario) became the router's preferred path.
This is DEFINED as a PATH DIVERGENCE, distinct from a MAGNITUDE DIVERGENCE (Part 2.3's ordinary case
where the same systems are engaged but the cost or duration forecast misses): a path divergence means
the forecast's systems-engaged field itself was wrong, which is a stronger signal of structural drift
(Part X) than of ordinary dynamics-model miscalibration, because the dynamics model cannot correct a
forecast whose STRUCTURAL premise — which systems even exist to be routed to — was already stale at
simulation time. (DERIVED: a path divergence should route, at the divergence-record layer, into the
structural-fidelity axis of the dashboard (Part 10.1) rather than the dynamics-fidelity axis, because
recalibrating dynamics parameters cannot fix a forecast that was reasoning over an outdated structural
graph; conflating the two divergence types would cause exactly the kind of misattributed recalibration
Part 11.2 warns dynamics drift detection against.) This distinction — path divergence versus magnitude
divergence — is therefore specified as a required field on the DivergenceRecord object (13.4), not an
optional annotation, so that the twin's own self-correction loop routes each miss to the sub-model
actually responsible for it.

FINAL LAW — PART II. A mission forecast is worth nothing if it is never checked against what actually
happened; mission simulation is not a one-time prediction service but a standing loop of forecast,
execute, diverge, recalibrate — and the loop's integrity depends as much on logging every bypass as on
running every simulation.

PART III — ARCHITECTURE SCENARIO LABORATORY
===============================================================================================

3.1 The scenario laboratory as a distinct twin mode from mission simulation. Where Part II simulates
a single mission's dispatch, the ARCHITECTURE SCENARIO LABORATORY is DEFINED as the twin mode that
simulates a proposed structural change to the ensemble itself — a new system, a merge of two systems,
a topology change, a new relation type — and reports the ensemble-wide consequence, feeding
arch-decision (IAS-F3's named parent per the strengthening matrix) with a rehearsal it cannot get from
reasoning about the one decision in isolation. (DERIVED: arch-decision, and its estate-wide successor
IAS-G1, already reason about topology, drift, and complexity budget; the laboratory supplies them the
twin's forward-simulation capability rather than duplicating their judgment about what a good topology
is.) A scenario is a hypothetical structural edit to the twin's structural sub-model — "what if system
X and system Y merged," "what if a new relation type were introduced between the observability fabric
and the immune system," "what if a system's authority were narrowed" — and the laboratory runs the
twin's dynamics model forward under that edited structure to observe emergent-property change.

3.2 The scenario protocol: propose, fork, simulate, compare, discard-or-promote. Every scenario
follows a fixed five-step protocol. PROPOSE: the scenario is stated as a precise structural edit,
never a vague aspiration ("what if we had better coordination" is not a scenario; "what if the
Control Bus (IAS-A2) absorbed the pane-level cross_signal_bus as a subsystem" is). FORK: the twin's
current state is copied into an isolated scenario branch — this is a DEFINED operation on the
twin's data model, not a claim about parallel real-estate execution, and it is the twin-native
analogue of a git branch, existing only inside the simulation substrate. SIMULATE: the dynamics model
runs the branch forward across a representative window of synthetic missions (drawn from the mission
mix IAS-C2's demand forecasting projects, imported by reference rather than re-derived) and the
emergent properties of Part I.3's state model are recomputed at each step. COMPARE: the branch's
trajectory of emergent properties (portfolio balance, complexity budget consumption, biodiversity per
IAS-D1's vocabulary, observability coverage per IAS-E1) is compared against the unmodified twin's
trajectory over the same synthetic mission window. DISCARD-OR-PROMOTE: the scenario branch is either
discarded (the overwhelming majority of scenarios, by design — a laboratory that promotes most of what
it tests is not testing rigorously) or promoted to a recommendation surfaced to arch-decision /
IAS-G1, never auto-applied to the live structural model, per Article IX's governed self-evolution
requirement.

3.3 Comparative scenario evaluation and the counterfactual pairing. Because a single scenario's
absolute numbers are hard to interpret in isolation — is a complexity-budget increase of eleven
units good or bad? — the laboratory always evaluates a scenario PAIRED against a counterfactual: the
same synthetic mission window run against the unchanged twin. The comparison is differential, not
absolute: "under this scenario, portfolio balance improves by a stated amount relative to the
no-change baseline, at a stated cost in complexity budget, over a stated confidence band." (DERIVED:
differential comparison is the only honest way to evaluate a structural change, because the baseline
itself is drifting under normal estate evolution, and an absolute score conflates the scenario's
effect with background drift.) This differential-against-counterfactual pattern is the same mechanism
Part IX (counterfactual institution) generalizes to a standing capability; the scenario laboratory is
its first and narrowest application, scoped specifically to architecture.

3.4 Worked example — merging two observability-adjacent capabilities. Consider a synthetic scenario:
"merge the dead-capability detector into the dark-matter detector as one subsystem of IAS-E1, on the
premise that both read the same usage telemetry and a shared subsystem would reduce redundant
scanning." The laboratory forks the twin, applies the structural edit (two subsystem nodes become
one, their consumer edges redirected to the merged node), and simulates a representative quarter of
missions. The forecast, HYPOTHESIZED pending real calibration data because no such merge has occurred
in the actual estate, might show: complexity budget decreases modestly (fewer nodes to reason about),
but observability latency increases in the branch where dark-matter detection was previously
interruptible independently of dead-capability scanning and the merge coupled their scan cycles. The
comparison against the counterfactual surfaces a trade-off that was invisible from the proposal text
alone — "merge redundant scanners" sounds unambiguously good until the laboratory's simulated
trajectory shows the coupling cost. This is the laboratory's central value: it converts a plausible-
sounding structural argument into a forecast with a cost the human proposing the merge had not
priced. (HYPOTHESIZED throughout this specific numeric example; the mechanism generating such
forecasts is DERIVED and NORMATIVE.)

3.5 Guarding against laboratory capture — never a second arch-decision authority. The laboratory must
not become a second architecture authority alongside arch-decision / IAS-G1; its output is always a
forecast attached to a scenario, never a verdict. (NORMATIVE, reinforcing CONSTITUTION Article II: a
scenario laboratory that starts approving or rejecting architecture changes has drifted from
"simulates the federation" into "governs architecture," which is IAS-G1's distinct object, and the
drift must be caught by the same D2A discipline that governs every other boundary in this family.)
The laboratory's discipline is enforced structurally by never granting it a promote-to-production
mechanism — only a promote-to-recommendation mechanism whose recipient (arch-decision, IAS-G1) is
the sole holder of the actual decision.

3.6 Scenario proposers and the question of who may open the laboratory. The scenario laboratory's
protocol (3.2) specifies HOW a scenario runs; this Part specifies WHO may propose one, because an
unrestricted proposer set risks turning the laboratory into a venue for relitigating every disagreement
as a simulation request, consuming the twin's capital (Part V, Part VIII.3) on low-value hypotheticals.
Proposers are DEFINED across two tiers: NAMED SYSTEM PROPOSERS — arch-decision/IAS-G1 (Part XIX),
IAS-C2 via the demand-forecast channel (Part XVIII), and IAS-D1 for extinction-adjacent structural
questions (Part VII) — whose proposals are pre-qualified by virtue of arriving through an existing,
already-governed channel; and OWNER-INITIATED PROPOSERS, where the Owner directly requests a scenario
outside any named channel, always admissible without qualification per the Owner's standing authority
(Part 14.3). A proposal from neither source — for instance, a scenario an agent generates spontaneously
mid-mission without being asked — is NOT specified as admissible by default; it requires either
promotion to Owner-initiated status (the Owner is asked and agrees to sponsor it) or rejection at the
laboratory's intake step. (NORMATIVE: this is not a judgment that agent-originated scenario ideas lack
value — it is a capital-discipline boundary, directly analogous to HR-COST-003's default-to-cheap
posture, ensuring the laboratory's finite run-count budget is spent on proposals with an accountable
sponsor rather than on every speculative "what if" an agent generates while reasoning about something
else.)

3.7 Worked example — a scenario proposal rejected at intake for lacking a precise structural edit.
Consider a synthetic proposal submitted through the Owner-initiated channel: "what if the estate had
better cross-system communication?" Per 3.2's PROPOSE step, this fails intake immediately, before any
fork is created, because it does not name a precise structural edit — it names an aspiration, not an
edit to a system, subsystem, capability, or relation in the twin's structural sub-model. The laboratory's
intake function, specified here as a mandatory pre-fork validation step, returns the proposal to its
sponsor with a request for the precise edit it implies — in this synthetic case, perhaps "add a new
relation type between IAS-A2's Control Bus and IAS-D2's Immune System for federated-fingerprint
broadcast" once the sponsor clarifies intent. (DERIVED: intake validation is what keeps 3.2's PROPOSE
step from becoming a formality — a laboratory that forks on vague aspirations either burns capital on
unsimulable requests or silently invents the precise edit itself, the latter being a quiet expansion of
the laboratory's own authority that Part 3.5 forbids.) This worked example also illustrates why intake
validation is cheap relative to a wasted fork-simulate-compare cycle: rejecting an imprecise proposal
before the Monte-Carlo subengine (Part VIII) is invoked costs a fraction of what running the cycle on an
unsimulable premise would cost, and never reaches the point where a meaningless result could be
mistaken, per Part XI's central risk, for a meaningful one.

FINAL LAW — PART III. A scenario is worth running only when it is paired against its own
counterfactual and evaluated on the differential, not the absolute; and a laboratory that starts
handing down verdicts instead of forecasts has stopped being a laboratory and started being a rival
government.

===============================================================================================
PART IV — RISK SIMULATION AND PROPAGATION
===============================================================================================

4.1 Risk as a first-class simulated quantity, not a narrative add-on. RISK, as DEFINED for the twin,
is the probability-weighted magnitude of an adverse state change to one or more systems, computed by
running the dynamics model forward under a stressed input (a failure injected into one system, a
resource shortfall, a governance rule misfire) rather than under the normal mission mix. Risk
simulation differs from mission simulation (Part II) in direction: mission simulation asks "what does
this intended action produce," while risk simulation asks "what does this unintended disturbance
produce, and how far does it travel." (DERIVED: the two share the same dynamics sub-model; they differ
only in whether the injected input is a planned mission or an adverse perturbation.)

4.2 Propagation as the object risk simulation exists to capture. The distinguishing institutional
value of risk simulation over a static risk register (which the estate could, in principle, maintain
without any twin at all) is PROPAGATION: a risk register lists "system X might fail," but only a
running model of the ensemble's relation graph (imported from IAS-F1 Part VI, never re-derived) can
show that a failure in system X propagates through three consumer edges to degrade systems Y and Z, at
what delay, and whether the immune system's (IAS-D2) containment mechanisms catch it before it
reaches a fourth system W. Risk simulation is therefore DEFINED as a directed walk of the dynamics
model outward from a perturbed system, at each hop asking whether the receiving system's own
resilience properties (SLOs and failure budgets from IAS-E2, imported by reference) absorb the shock
or forward it, terminating when the shock is absorbed everywhere or exits the ensemble boundary
IAS-F1 Part III.4 defines.

4.3 The propagation graph as risk simulation's core artifact. Every risk simulation run produces a
PROPAGATION GRAPH: a directed, weighted trace of which systems were touched, in what order, with what
estimated severity at each hop, and where (if anywhere) the propagation was contained. This is the
mechanism that makes the compounding graph's "defense loop" concrete (COMPOUNDING_GRAPH: "IAS-D2
Immune System fingerprints failure events; IAS-F3 Digital Twin simulates the failure before it
recurs"): a failure fingerprint the immune system has already federated across projects becomes, in
IAS-F3, a CALIBRATED perturbation input whose propagation graph the twin can now compute for the
CURRENT ensemble structure, even for a project the original fingerprint never touched. (DERIVED: this
is the twin's preventive value over the immune system's reactive value — IAS-D2 recognizes a failure
pattern once it has occurred somewhere; IAS-F3 asks whether that same pattern, run against the twin
of a DIFFERENT project's current structure, would propagate the same way, before it occurs there.)

4.4 Worked adversarial case — a cascading secret-firewall bypass. Consider a synthetic risk scenario
built for illustration: an adversarial input crafted to slip past the Secret Firewall's pattern
matcher (HR-SECRET-001 class) by encoding a credential in a format the CRITICAL pattern set does not
yet recognize. Risk simulation perturbs the twin's state model at the Secret Firewall node with "match
fails silently," and walks the propagation graph outward: the credential reaches whatever emission
path was underway (a sub-agent prompt, a hook's additionalContext), which HR-SECRET-006's redaction
bus should intercept as a second line of defense — the twin's simulation therefore tests not just
"does the primary control fail" but "does the SECOND control catch what the first missed," surfacing
containment depth rather than a single point of failure. If the twin's dynamics model (calibrated on
the Universal Redaction Bus's documented behavior) shows the second control also passing the encoded
credential through because it shares the same pattern-matching approach as the first, the propagation
graph reveals a CORRELATED failure — both controls share a blind spot — which is a materially
different and worse risk than two independent controls each with an isolated gap. (HYPOTHESIZED as a
specific numeric propagation; the correlated-failure detection mechanism itself is DERIVED from
standard defense-in-depth reasoning applied to the twin's structural model.)

4.5 Risk simulation's honest limit — it cannot invent the adversary. The twin's risk simulation is
bounded by the perturbations it is given to inject; it does not autonomously discover novel attack
surfaces the way an adversarial red-team exercise might. (CONTESTED: one view holds the twin should
eventually generate its own perturbation candidates by mutating past propagation graphs; the opposing
view, adopted here as NORMATIVE for this dataset, holds that autonomous perturbation generation risks
manufacturing risk narratives untethered from anything OBSERVED, and Article V's epistemic honesty
requires that HYPOTHESIZED perturbations be clearly distinguished from ones the immune system's
federated fingerprints or a real incident actually evidenced.) The twin's risk simulation is therefore
scoped, honestly, to propagating given perturbations faithfully, not to inventing them; perturbation
sourcing is itself a named UNRESOLVED question in Part XXI.

4.6 Severity calibration and the danger of a single scalar. Each hop in a PropagationGraph (13.3)
carries an "estimated severity," and this Part specifies that severity must never be reduced, at the
point of consumption, to a single undifferentiated scalar, because two hops with the same scalar
severity can represent qualitatively different risks: a hop that degrades a system's PERFORMANCE
(slower, more expensive, still correct) is a different institutional event than a hop that degrades a
system's CORRECTNESS (faster or unchanged cost, but wrong output), and collapsing both into one number
loses exactly the distinction a consuming system (Part XIV) needs to decide how to respond. Severity is
therefore specified as a DEFINED tuple — {performance_degradation, correctness_degradation,
availability_degradation} — each independently banded by the twin's uncertainty model (Part VIII), with
the "single scalar" a consuming report may compute as a derived summary but never the sole carried
value. (DERIVED: this three-way decomposition is directly informed by IAS-E2's own SLO vocabulary,
imported by reference rather than invented fresh, so that a propagation graph's severity tuple resolves
cleanly against the failure-budget categories IAS-E2 already tracks, rather than introducing a fourth,
incompatible severity taxonomy into the estate.)

4.7 Worked example — distinguishing contained-but-costly from contained-and-cheap propagation. Consider
two synthetic risk simulations sharing an identical origin perturbation (a hypothetical timeout in a
shared telemetry ingestion path) but differing in the receiving system's absorption behavior. In the
first, the receiving system's graceful-degradation path (IAS-E2) absorbs the perturbation by falling
back to a cached telemetry snapshot — contained, with a correctness_degradation near zero but a
performance_degradation reflecting the staleness of the cached data. In the second, a DIFFERENT
receiving system absorbs the same perturbation by retrying aggressively, which contains the immediate
failure but consumes disproportionate capital doing so — contained, with performance and availability
both nominal but a hidden capital cost the severity tuple's three fields, as specified, do not directly
capture unless a fourth field or a cross-reference to Part V's capital sub-model is added. This gap is
named honestly rather than patched over: the severity tuple of 4.6 captures degradation along
IAS-E2's SLO categories, and a propagation graph whose containment strategy is capital-expensive rather
than SLO-degrading requires the reporting layer to CROSS-REFERENCE the capital sub-model's forecast for
the same simulated window, a cross-reference this dataset specifies as a reporting-layer join (Part
12.5) rather than a fifth severity field, keeping the PropagationGraph object itself scoped to what
IAS-E2's vocabulary already defines. (HYPOTHESIZED specific scenario; DERIVED cross-reference
mechanism.)

FINAL LAW — PART IV. A risk register that cannot show how a shock travels through the relation graph
is a list of fears, not a model of danger; risk simulation earns its keep only by tracing propagation
to the systems the register never named, and by refusing to pretend it invented the danger it merely
propagated.

PART V — CAPITAL FORECASTING SIMULATION
===============================================================================================

5.1 Capital forecasting as the twin's economic layer. CAPITAL, in the vocabulary this dataset inherits
from IAS-C1's portfolio object and IAS-F2's advantage algebra (imported by reference, never
re-derived), is the estate's finite cognitive spend — token budget, Owner attention, session count,
context headroom — allocated across systems and missions. CAPITAL FORECASTING SIMULATION is DEFINED
as running the twin's dynamics model forward under a proposed allocation (a portfolio rebalance from
IAS-C1, a demand forecast from IAS-C2) to predict the capital trajectory: how consumption, headroom,
and allocation balance evolve over the simulated window, before the real allocation is committed.
(DERIVED: this is the mechanism that makes the foresight loop's third stage concrete — "IAS-C1
Portfolio pre-allocates to the winners" per COMPOUNDING_GRAPH — because pre-allocation without a
forward capital trajectory is a bet, and with one, it is a rehearsed bet.)

5.2 The capital trajectory and its bands. A capital forecast is not a single number; it is a TRAJECTORY
across the simulated window, banded by the same uncertainty discipline Part I.3 requires of every twin
output. A trajectory names, at each simulated checkpoint: cumulative capital consumed, remaining
headroom against the estate's stated ceiling (the OD3 budget classes and the CO-01/02/03 session
discipline this dataset strengthens by extending them across a forecast window rather than a single
session), and the probability, at the confidence band's lower and upper bound, that the trajectory
crosses into an over-budget state before the window closes. (NORMATIVE: a capital forecast that
reports only the expected case and hides the tail is a worse artifact than no forecast, because it
manufactures false confidence — this is the same discipline HR-COST-002's 2x-budget stop-gate already
enforces at the single-task level, extended here to a forecast window.)

5.3 Worked example — a quarter's authoring capacity against three concurrent dataset families. Consider
a synthetic capital forecast for a quarter in which three dataset families (analogous in scale to the
IAS corpus itself) are proposed to author concurrently, each requiring roughly the density this very
dataset targets. The twin's capital sub-model, seeded from OBSERVED per-Part authoring cost in
comparable past sessions, forecasts: at the twin's median case, all three families complete within the
quarter at MACRO-class aggregate spend; at the twin's 90th-percentile tail, the third family's
authoring collides with a context-pressure watchdog threshold (per the Context Pressure Response
protocol) more than twice, each collision costing a compact cycle whose overhead the capital sub-model
prices from OBSERVED compact-cycle token cost, pushing the aggregate into ULTRA-class spend territory.
The forecast's actionable output is not "don't do this" but "the tail risk is concentrated in the
third family; sequence it after the first two clear, or budget its ULTRA-class contingency explicitly
before starting" — a rehearsed trade-off IAS-C1's portfolio allocator can act on, which neither IAS-C1
alone (which sees allocation, not forward dynamics) nor CO-01 alone (which sees one session, not a
quarter) could produce unaided. (HYPOTHESIZED as a specific numeric forecast; the mechanism generating
it — seeding from observed per-unit cost, projecting a trajectory, banding the tail — is DERIVED.)

5.4 Governance-linked capital constraints. Capital forecasting must respect the same governance
ceilings the estate already enforces at run time — the HR-CASCADE-005 context thresholds, the OD3
size-class budgets, the Session Cost Discipline's 70%-compact trigger — not as external checks bolted
onto the forecast after the fact, but as CONSTRAINTS baked into the dynamics model itself, so that a
forecasted trajectory which would breach a governance ceiling is flagged as INFEASIBLE within the
simulation, not merely undesirable. (DERIVED: a capital forecast that predicts a state the estate's
own governance would refuse to permit is not describing a possible future; it is describing a future
the estate has already legislated against, and the twin must know the difference between "expensive"
and "governance-forbidden.")

5.5 The forecast-to-allocation handoff and its honest boundary. Capital forecasting simulation
produces the trajectory; it does not itself decide the allocation. The handoff to IAS-C1 is explicit
and one-directional: the twin supplies "here is what this allocation would cost, with what
probability of breach," and IAS-C1's portfolio logic — which alone holds the authority to weigh cost
against expected value across the whole portfolio, per the strengthening matrix's description of
IAS-C1 as the consumer of F2's fusion scores — decides whether to commit. (NORMATIVE, reinforcing
Article II's boundary discipline: IAS-F3 forecasts; it does not allocate, the same non-duplication
line drawn against arch-decision in Part III.5 and against DRK in Part I.4.)

5.6 Capital forecasting across heterogeneous currencies. The definition of CAPITAL in 5.1 names token
budget, Owner attention, session count, and context headroom together, and this Part specifies how the
twin handles the fact that these are not fungible: a mission can be cheap in tokens but expensive in
Owner attention (a mission requiring frequent approval checkpoints), or cheap in session count but
expensive in context headroom (a single long session that repeatedly approaches the compact threshold).
A CapitalTrajectory (13.5) is therefore specified as carrying a checkpoint value PER CURRENCY, not one
blended figure, and a governance-constraint-violated flag is raised independently per currency, because
a mission that is comfortably within token budget but repeatedly triggers Owner-attention checkpoints
above a stated frequency threshold is a distinct governance concern from one that is comfortably within
attention budget but threatens to exceed context headroom. (DERIVED: blending heterogeneous currencies
into one figure would hide exactly the kind of currency-specific breach the Session Cost Discipline's
own multi-pronged rules — the 70%-compact trigger, the 2-parallel-session cap, the per-task cost
routing — already treat as independently gated, and the twin's capital sub-model should mirror that
multi-currency structure rather than flatten it for reporting convenience.)

5.7 Worked example — a mission cheap in tokens but expensive in Owner attention. Consider a synthetic
mission requiring the twin to forecast: "run a twelve-step governance-impact review requiring Owner
sign-off at each step before proceeding to the next." The twin's capital sub-model, calibrated on
OBSERVED per-checkpoint attention cost from comparable past reviews, forecasts a LOW token-currency
trajectory (the underlying computation at each step is cheap) but a HIGH attention-currency trajectory
(twelve separate points at which the mission cannot proceed without a real human decision, each
consuming Owner time and context-switching cost the twin's attention sub-model bands by a wider
confidence interval than its token estimates, reflecting the greater aleatoric variance in human
response timing versus computational cost). The forecast's actionable output, specific to this
multi-currency framing, is not "this mission is cheap" (true only in the token currency) nor "this
mission is expensive" (true only in the attention currency) but the disaggregated pair, letting the
dispatching system decide whether twelve checkpoints should be batched into fewer, coarser approval
gates before the mission launches — a restructuring decision the single-currency version of this
forecast could never have surfaced, because it would have reported one misleadingly moderate blended
number. (HYPOTHESIZED specific numeric framing; DERIVED multi-currency mechanism.)

5.8 Currency-specific rollback of a capital forecast when one currency alone breaches. Because the
CapitalTrajectory (13.5) carries governance-constraint-violated flags per currency rather than one
blended flag (5.6), a mission whose TOKEN trajectory is comfortably feasible but whose ATTENTION or
CONTEXT-HEADROOM trajectory breaches a ceiling should not be treated identically to a mission breaching
on every currency at once. This Part specifies that a single-currency breach routes to a narrower
remediation than a full INFEASIBLE flag: the dispatching system is shown the specific currency at risk
and offered the restructuring path most relevant to THAT currency (batching approval checkpoints for an
attention breach, splitting a long session for a context-headroom breach) rather than a generic
"reduce scope" recommendation that might target the wrong currency entirely. (DERIVED: a
currency-specific remediation is strictly more useful than a generic one, and costs nothing additional
to compute once the multi-currency trajectory of 5.6 already exists — the specificity is a direct,
low-cost dividend of that earlier design choice, not a new mechanism requiring separate justification.)

FINAL LAW — PART V. A capital forecast that reports only its expected case is a forecast that has
already decided to be wrong in the direction that feels safest to report; the trajectory's tail, and
its collision with the estate's own governance ceilings, are not footnotes to the forecast but its
most load-bearing content.

PART VI — GOVERNANCE-IMPACT SIMULATION
===============================================================================================

6.1 A governance rule as a structural edit to the ensemble's behavior, not just its text. A GOVERNANCE
RULE — a Hard Rule, a done-gate threshold, a spec-gate size class, a new CLAUDE.md directive — is, from
the twin's perspective, DEFINED as an edit to the dynamics model's transition CONSTRAINTS: it does not
change which systems exist (that is a structural edit, Part III's scenario laboratory) but changes
which transitions those systems are permitted or required to take. GOVERNANCE-IMPACT SIMULATION is the
twin mode that forks the current state (identical mechanism to Part III.2's fork step), applies a
proposed rule as a constraint edit rather than a structural edit, and simulates the ensemble's
behavior under the new constraint across a representative mission window, comparing the branch against
the unconstrained counterfactual exactly as the scenario laboratory does for structural changes.
(DERIVED: governance-impact simulation and the architecture scenario laboratory of Part III share the
fork-simulate-compare mechanism; they differ only in whether the edit under test is structural or
constraint-level, and this shared mechanism is why both are twin modes rather than separate systems.)

6.2 Why governance needs simulation at all — the estate's own history of rule-collision. The estate's
own hard-rule archive, as OBSERVED in the CLAUDE.md HARD RULES block and the broader vault archive
this dataset must not re-narrate, already records instances where a new rule interacted badly with an
existing one in ways no one predicted until the collision occurred in production — the router
digest-vs-archive mismatch that left the kill switch unable to fire on any of its four triggers for an
unknown period (per project_enforcement_systems_scs_c92) is exactly the kind of rule-collision
governance-impact simulation exists to catch before it recurs elsewhere: a proposed new rule can be
simulated against the twin's current constraint set to check whether it conflicts with, duplicates, or
is silently shadowed by an existing rule, before it is written into a live CLAUDE.md or hooks
directory. (OBSERVED: the enforcement-systems finding that a rule can be inert for an unmeasured
period without detection; DERIVED: a simulated pre-check of a new rule against the live constraint set
is cheaper than discovering the collision after the rule has been dead for months.)

6.3 The impact report's three required sections. Every governance-impact simulation produces an
IMPACT REPORT with three mandatory sections, none of which may be omitted: COVERAGE — which systems
and mission types the rule's trigger condition actually reaches, simulated against the mission mix,
distinguishing "reaches every mission of this class" from "reaches missions matching this narrow
literal pattern," the exact distinction the router-vs-archive mismatch turned on; COLLISION — whether
the rule's constraint conflicts with, subsumes, or is subsumed by any existing constraint in the
twin's current governance layer, flagged even when the conflict is partial; and COST — the capital and
friction the rule imposes on the simulated mission mix, banded by uncertainty per Part V's discipline,
because a rule that is both correct and unenforceably expensive is a rule that will be quietly
bypassed under real pressure. (NORMATIVE: all three sections mandatory; a governance-impact report
missing the COLLISION section is not a partial simulation, it is the specific gap that let the
router-archive mismatch persist undetected.)

6.4 Worked example — simulating a proposed new done-gate threshold. Consider a synthetic proposal:
"raise the OQS done-gate threshold from 70 to 85 for all code contracts." Governance-impact simulation
forks the twin, edits the constraint (contracts below 85 are now blocked rather than 70), and runs the
representative mission mix forward. The forecast, HYPOTHESIZED pending real calibration, might show:
COVERAGE — the new threshold reaches every code contract as intended, no gap; COLLISION — the new
threshold interacts with HR-ONESHOT-003's three-consecutive-fail escalation ladder, because a stricter
gate increases the rate at which a contract needs a second or third attempt to clear 85, which was
calibrated against the 70 baseline and may now escalate to Owner review more often than the ladder's
designers intended; COST — simulated aggregate authoring time for code contracts increases by a
banded estimate because more contracts require a rewrite pass to clear the new bar. The impact
report does not recommend for or against the change; it hands arch-decision-adjacent governance
authority (a role the estate has not yet named a single owner for, flagged honestly as UNRESOLVED in
Part XXI) a rehearsed trade-off instead of a guess. (HYPOTHESIZED numeric specifics; DERIVED
mechanism.)

6.5 Governance simulation must not become governance. The same capture risk named in Part III.5
applies here with higher stakes: a twin mode that simulates rule impact must never be granted
authority to apply, veto, or auto-tune governance rules, because governance authority over Hard Rules
is explicitly Owner-held per the confirmation-before-irreversible-action pattern and Article IX's
governed self-evolution requirement. (NORMATIVE, CRITICAL boundary: governance-impact simulation is a
rehearsal tool for whoever holds governance authority, never a substitute for that authority.)

6.6 Retroactive governance-impact simulation on the estate's own already-live rules. Sections 6.1
through 6.5 specify governance-impact simulation for a PROPOSED new rule; this Part extends the mode,
as a distinct and valuable application, to an ALREADY-LIVE rule whose real-world effect has never been
formally checked against the twin's constraint model. A RETROACTIVE IMPACT REVIEW is DEFINED as running
the same fork-simulate-compare protocol (6.1) with the target rule's constraint REMOVED from a forked
branch rather than added, comparing the branch (rule absent) against the live twin (rule present) to
estimate what the rule is currently costing or preventing. (DERIVED: this is the twin's specific
contribution to catching a rule that has quietly become inert or quietly become miscalibrated without
anyone proposing a change — the estate's own kill-switch precedent (6.2) was discovered only by a
manual audit; a standing capability to retroactively simulate "what is this rule actually doing" is
the twin's preventive answer to needing another manual audit to catch the next one.) Retroactive review
is NORMATIVE to run periodically over the estate's oldest, least-recently-reviewed rules — a
maintenance cadence analogous to Part 15.4's reconciliation triggers, though scoped to governance rather
than structure — rather than only on-demand when a rule is suspected of malfunctioning, because the
kill-switch precedent's defining feature was that no one suspected anything until the audit ran.

6.7 The limits of retroactive review — it cannot recover data the live estate never logged. A
retroactive impact review's COVERAGE component (6.3) depends on the twin's dynamics sub-model having
been calibrated with divergence records from the period the rule has been live; for a rule sealed
before the twin itself existed, or before sufficient divergence-record history accumulated, the review
can only report on the WINDOW for which calibration data exists, honestly flagged as a partial-coverage
finding rather than presented as a full-lifetime audit. (DERIVED, directly following Part 17.5's
honest-evidence-ceiling discipline: a retroactive review of a rule's entire history is only as complete
as the twin's own calibration history, and this dataset refuses to let a retroactive review's
partial-window finding be reported as if it covered the rule's full lifetime — the same false-precision
failure mode Part XI.3 names for forecasts applies equally to retrospective analysis, and naming the
coverage window explicitly in every retroactive review's output is the specified mitigation.)

FINAL LAW — PART VI. A governance rule is a constraint on the ensemble's behavior and deserves the
same forward rehearsal a mission or an architecture change gets; the estate's own recorded history of
a kill switch that could not fire is the standing proof that an unsimulated rule can be silently dead
for an unmeasured period, and a coverage-collision-cost report run before the rule ships is cheaper
than discovering the gap after.

PART VII — SYSTEM-EXTINCTION SIMULATION
===============================================================================================

7.1 Extinction as a structural edit the ecology dataset needs rehearsed, not narrated. EXTINCTION, in
the vocabulary IAS-D1 (System Ecology) owns as its resource-level object, is the retirement of a
system from the ensemble. SYSTEM-EXTINCTION SIMULATION is DEFINED as the twin mode that removes a
named system's node from the structural sub-model (a specific, bounded structural edit distinct from
the general scenario laboratory of Part III, warranting its own Part because its failure modes are
distinct enough to require dedicated treatment) and simulates the ensemble's behavior across the
representative mission window with that node absent, surfacing every downstream consumer whose
mission forecast changes as a result. (DERIVED: IAS-D1 decides WHETHER a system should be retired,
using its ecology model of fitness, parasitism, and symbiosis; IAS-F3's extinction simulation supplies
IAS-D1 the forward-looking consequence of a candidate retirement before the decision is finalized,
extending the "SUBENGINE → IAS-D1" relationship the ledger's candidate 38 records, now specified as a
twin mechanism rather than left as a bare verdict.)

7.2 The orphaned-consumer report as extinction simulation's core artifact. Removing a system's node
severs every edge where other systems consumed its capability. Extinction simulation's primary output
is the ORPHANED-CONSUMER REPORT: the list of every system whose simulated mission forecast, under the
extincted structure, either fails outright (a hard dependency with no substitute) or degrades (a soft
dependency the ensemble absorbs at a cost). (DERIVED, drawing directly on the ensemble's relation
graph IAS-F1 Part VI defines: a system's true retirement cost is not measured by the system's own
size but by the number and criticality of the edges pointing INTO it, which only a relation-graph walk
— not an inspection of the system in isolation — can surface.) This is the mechanism that prevents the
naive failure mode of retiring a system because it "looks small" while it silently underpins three
larger systems' hard dependencies — precisely the blind spot a resource-level inspection of the
candidate-for-retirement system cannot see, because the dependency lives in the CONSUMERS, not in the
system itself.

7.3 Graceful-versus-hard extinction and the twin's role in choosing between them. Not every extincted
system's consumers must break; IAS-E2's graceful-degradation vocabulary (imported by reference, per
the strengthening matrix's "graceful degradation instead of hard failure" evidence requirement)
distinguishes systems that can be extincted GRACEFULLY — consumers fall back to a degraded but
functioning path — from ones that can only be extincted HARD — consumers fail outright unless a
replacement is provisioned first. Extinction simulation classifies each orphaned consumer into one of
these two categories, and a proposed extinction whose orphaned-consumer report contains even one HARD
classification without a named replacement is flagged INFEASIBLE for immediate execution, requiring
either a phased extinction (a scenario-laboratory sequence: introduce the replacement, migrate
consumers, then extinct, each step separately simulated) or an explicit Owner acceptance of the hard
breakage. (NORMATIVE.)

7.4 Worked example — simulating the retirement of a duplicated cross-signal mechanism. Consider a
synthetic extinction proposal: "retire the legacy cross_signal_bus now that IAS-A2's Control Bus
absorbs its function, per the ledger's MERGE verdict for candidate 3." Extinction simulation removes
the cross_signal_bus node and walks consumers. The orphaned-consumer report, HYPOTHESIZED pending real
calibration, finds two consumers: one already migrated to IAS-A2's interface (a GRACEFUL classification
— no simulated forecast change) and one legacy hook still directly wired to the old bus's event format
(a HARD classification, because the hook's dynamics-model entry has no fallback path encoded). The
report's actionable recommendation is not "don't retire" but "migrate the legacy hook first, then
re-run the extinction simulation to confirm zero HARD classifications remain" — the exact phased
pattern Part 7.3 specifies, now demonstrated end-to-end. (HYPOTHESIZED specifics; DERIVED mechanism.)

7.5 Extinction simulation's honest limit — it cannot detect a consumer the twin's structural model
omits. If the twin's structural sub-model has fallen out of currency with the real estate (Part I.2's
currency property), an orphaned-consumer report can silently miss a real consumer that was added to
the live estate after the twin's last synchronization. (DERIVED: this is not a defect specific to
extinction simulation; it is the general currency-drift failure mode of Part X and Part XI, but it is
named here specifically because extinction is the twin mode where a missed consumer causes the most
irreversible harm — a system, once retired for real, is not free to un-retire without redoing the work
that built it.) NORMATIVE mitigation: extinction simulation always re-synchronizes the structural
sub-model immediately before running, rather than trusting a cached structural state, even at the cost
of the resynchronization latency Part XVII's evaluation protocol must measure.

7.6 Partial extinction and the granularity gap between system and capability. Part 7.1 scopes
extinction to removing a system's NODE, but a real retirement candidate is often narrower — one
CAPABILITY a system provides among several (Part II.4's capability-as-currency definition), not the
whole system. This Part specifies PARTIAL EXTINCTION as a distinct, finer-grained simulation: the
structural edit removes one capability edge from a system node rather than the node itself, and the
orphaned-consumer walk (7.2) proceeds identically but scoped to consumers of that ONE capability rather
than all of the system's capabilities. (DERIVED: without this distinction, a proposal to retire "the
legacy export format a system supports, while keeping the system's other three capabilities" would
have no honest simulation path except the full-node extinction mechanism, which would falsely report
every consumer of the system's OTHER capabilities as orphaned too — a false positive this dataset must
guard against with the same rigor it guards against false negatives.) Partial extinction reuses the
scenario engine's structural-edit configuration (Part 12.2) at finer granularity rather than requiring
a seventh subengine, consistent with this dataset's reflexive non-duplication discipline.

7.7 Worked example — partial extinction of one capability among several. Consider a synthetic proposal:
"IAS-A2's Control Bus retires its legacy polling-based subscription capability now that the
event-driven capability (Part 10.3) has fully replaced it, while the Control Bus itself, and its other
capabilities, continue unchanged." Partial extinction simulation removes only the polling-capability
edge and walks consumers of THAT capability specifically. The orphaned-consumer report, HYPOTHESIZED
pending real calibration, finds zero consumers still depending on polling exclusively (all have
migrated per Part 10.3's own strengthening narrative), yielding a clean GRACEFUL-across-the-board
result — a materially more precise and more reassuring finding than a full-node extinction simulation
would have produced, because a full-node run would have needlessly reported every consumer of the
Control Bus's OTHER, unaffected capabilities as part of the analysis, diluting the signal with
irrelevant noise. This worked example demonstrates why granularity, not just correctness, is a
first-class design concern for extinction simulation: a technically correct but overly coarse
simulation can still produce a report too noisy for its consumer (IAS-D1) to act on efficiently.

7.8 Extinction simulation's relationship to the twin's own lifecycle when the extincted system is a
twin dependency. A structurally reflexive edge case worth naming: if the system proposed for extinction
is itself one the twin's mirror layer depends on for calibration data (IAS-D1 for dynamics, IAS-E2 for
resilience patterns, per Part 1.3), the orphaned-consumer walk (7.2) must include the TWIN ITSELF as a
consumer, not only estate-facing systems. (DERIVED: omitting the twin from its own consumer walk would
be a blind spot exactly analogous to Part 2.6's caution against the router shadow-duplicating itself —
the twin's own dependency graph is part of the ensemble's relation graph IAS-F1 defines, and nothing in
this dataset's scope exempts the twin's own mirror-layer dependencies from the same consumer-protection
discipline applied to every other system.) A HARD classification on the twin's own dependency would
therefore block the extinction under 7.3's ordinary rule unless a replacement calibration source is
named first — the twin protecting its own calibration dependencies with the identical rigor it applies
to protecting the estate's.

FINAL LAW — PART VII. A system's retirement cost lives in the edges pointing into it, not in the
system itself; extinction simulation exists to walk those edges before the retirement is irreversible,
and its single most dangerous failure mode is trusting a structural model that has quietly drifted
out of currency with the estate it claims to mirror.

===============================================================================================
PART VIII — INSTITUTIONAL MONTE-CARLO UNDER UNCERTAINTY
===============================================================================================

8.1 Why a single forecast is insufficient — the case for repeated stochastic simulation. Every twin
mode specified so far (mission, scenario, risk, capital, governance, extinction) has been described as
producing one forecast, banded by an uncertainty range derived from the input data's confidence. That
description understates the mechanism: the uncertainty band is not asserted, it is COMPUTED, and it is
computed by INSTITUTIONAL MONTE-CARLO SIMULATION, DEFINED as running the dynamics model many times over
the same proposed action or perturbation, each run sampling its stochastic inputs (mission arrival
timing, individual mission cost variance, the probability a given propagation hop is contained versus
forwarded) from the distributions the twin's calibration data supports, and aggregating the resulting
spread of outcomes into the confidence bands every other Part has referenced. (DERIVED: every
uncertainty band cited in Parts II through VII is an output of this Part's mechanism, not an
independently invented number — Monte-Carlo simulation is the twin's uncertainty ENGINE, and the other
seven modes are its CONSUMERS.)

8.2 The three sources of stochasticity the estate's Monte-Carlo model must represent honestly. First,
ALEATORIC variance: genuine randomness in how long a mission takes or how a propagation hop resolves,
which no amount of additional calibration data eliminates, because it reflects real variability in how
agents, Owners, and the model provider behave session to session. Second, EPISTEMIC uncertainty: the
twin's own ignorance of the true dynamics, which SHRINKS as more divergence records (Part II.3)
accumulate and the dynamics model recalibrates — this is the uncertainty a better-calibrated twin
reduces over time, distinct from aleatoric variance which it never eliminates. Third, MODEL-STRUCTURE
uncertainty: the risk that the dynamics model's functional FORM is wrong, not just its parameters —
that an interaction the model assumes is linear is actually threshold-triggered, for instance — which
neither more data nor more Monte-Carlo runs alone can fully resolve, and which is why Part XI treats
suspiciously narrow confidence bands as a warning sign of overconfidence in a possibly wrong model
shape, not evidence of a good twin. (DERIVED, standard decomposition applied honestly to this
estate's specific twin architecture; CONTESTED whether the three sources can be cleanly separated in
practice — the corpus takes the NORMATIVE position that even an imperfect separation is more honest
than reporting one undifferentiated uncertainty number.)

8.3 The Monte-Carlo run count and its cost-accuracy trade-off. Running the dynamics model N times per
forecast trades simulation cost (itself consuming the estate's cognitive capital the twin exists to
protect, an ironic but real constraint) against the tightness of the resulting confidence band. The
twin's run-count policy is NORMATIVE and threshold-based, mirroring the estate's own size-class
discipline: NANO-class forecasts (a small mission below the route-before-spend threshold) receive a
low run count or are exempted from Monte-Carlo entirely, relying on the dynamics model's point
estimate with a wide default band; MACRO and ULTRA-class forecasts (large missions, architecture
scenarios, extinction proposals) receive a substantially higher run count, because the cost of an
imprecise forecast at that scale (a mis-sized capital trajectory, an undetected HARD extinction
consumer) exceeds the cost of the additional simulation runs. (DERIVED, applying HR-COST-003's
default-to-cheap discipline to the twin's own internal operation, closing a potential infinite
regress: the twin must not become so expensive to consult that consulting it defeats its own
route-before-spend purpose.)

8.4 Worked example — Monte-Carlo on a contested mission-timing assumption. Consider a synthetic
mission forecast (Part II) whose point estimate assumes missions arrive sequentially, one pane at a
time. Institutional Monte-Carlo, run at the mission's MACRO-class run count, samples across a
distribution of arrival patterns including the two-pane parallel cap the Bash-bridge doctrine permits,
and the resulting spread reveals that the point estimate's capital cost is accurate under sequential
arrival but understates cost by a HYPOTHESIZED margin under the parallel-cap case, because parallel
panes compete for the same context-pressure watchdog threshold and each pane's compact cycle is
slightly more likely to trigger when the other pane is also consuming context. The Monte-Carlo spread
surfaces this correlation-driven tail that a single point-estimate run, run once under one arrival
assumption, would have entirely missed. (HYPOTHESIZED specifics; DERIVED mechanism: repeated stochastic
sampling surfaces correlated tail risk that a single deterministic run structurally cannot.)

8.5 Monte-Carlo output as a distribution, never collapsed prematurely to a single number. The
Monte-Carlo engine's output — the full spread of simulated outcomes — must be preserved through the
reporting layer, not collapsed to a mean or median before the decision-owning system (DRK, IAS-C1,
arch-decision) sees it, because the decision-owning system, not the twin, is the correct authority to
decide how much tail risk it is willing to accept for a given decision class. (NORMATIVE, reinforcing
the non-duplication boundary of Parts I, III, V, and VI: the twin forecasts the distribution; it does
not decide the risk tolerance, which remains the decision-owning system's authority in every case.)

8.6 Variance reduction techniques and their honest cost. A naive Monte-Carlo implementation samples
independently and identically for every run, which converges to a stable confidence band only slowly —
the classic square-root-of-N convergence rate. This Part names, as a specification-level option rather
than a commitment, VARIANCE REDUCTION techniques (importance sampling weighted toward historically
high-divergence mission classes; stratified sampling across the three uncertainty sources of 8.2 rather
than pooling them) that a later implementation MAY use to tighten confidence bands at a fixed run-count
budget. (HYPOTHESIZED: whether variance reduction meaningfully improves band tightness for THIS twin's
specific dynamics model is untested, and this dataset does not claim the improvement, only names the
technique family as a legitimate implementation-time lever within the run-count policy Part 8.3
specifies — a lever that trades implementation complexity for either a tighter band at the same cost or
the same band at lower cost, never a free improvement.) CRITICAL caveat, stated NORMATIVELY: variance
reduction must never be used to manufacture an artificially narrow band by under-sampling the tail —
the suspiciously-perfect-twin warning of Part 11.4 applies with equal force to a band narrowed by
sampling technique as to one narrowed by any other means, and any variance-reduction implementation
must be validated, via the backtesting protocol (Part 17.2), to confirm it narrows bands by improving
genuine precision rather than by quietly under-representing tail scenarios.

8.7 Worked example — run-count exhaustion under a capital-constrained quarter. Consider a synthetic
edge condition: the estate enters a quarter where CO-08's parallel-capacity envelope (Part V, VIII.4)
is itself under strain, and the Monte-Carlo subengine's own run-count policy (8.3) — which consumes
capital to compute uncertainty about capital — begins competing with the missions it is meant to
forecast for the same scarce budget. The twin's own governance-linked constraint check (Part 5.4),
applied reflexively to the twin's OWN operation, would flag a MACRO-class forecast's requested run
count as itself INFEASIBLE within the current capital trajectory, triggering a documented fallback: run
at the next-lower size class's run count, releasing a wider-than-normal band with an explicit
CAPACITY-CONSTRAINED annotation distinct from the ordinary confidence band, so the consuming system
knows the width reflects a resource constraint on the twin's OWN operation rather than a genuine
epistemic gap about the mission itself. (HYPOTHESIZED scenario; DERIVED mechanism: the twin's
uncertainty engine must be capital-aware about its own consumption, closing the same potential infinite
regress Part 8.3 already names, now specified with an explicit fallback rather than left implicit.)

FINAL LAW — PART VIII. An uncertainty band that is asserted rather than computed from repeated
stochastic simulation is decoration, not epistemics; the twin's Monte-Carlo engine exists to let
correlated, threshold-triggered, and aleatoric risk surface on their own terms, and its output must
reach the decision-owning system as a distribution, never pre-collapsed into a false single number.

===============================================================================================
PART IX — THE COUNTERFACTUAL INSTITUTION
===============================================================================================

9.1 Generalizing the paired-counterfactual pattern into a standing capability. Part III introduced
counterfactual pairing narrowly, scoped to architecture scenarios: every scenario compared against the
unmodified twin run over the same mission window. THE COUNTERFACTUAL INSTITUTION generalizes that
pattern into a standing twin mode, DEFINED as the capability to construct and maintain an entire
ALTERNATE structural model of the ensemble — not a single-edit fork evaluated once, but a
persistently maintained "what if the federation had been built differently" twin, run in parallel with
the primary twin across the same real mission history, so that at any point the estate can ask "how
would things have gone under architecture B instead of the architecture we actually have." (DERIVED:
this is the deepest and most speculative mode this dataset specifies, because unlike the other modes
it does not evaluate one proposed change against the live twin — it maintains a second, permanently
divergent twin as a running comparison.)

9.2 What a counterfactual institution is for — and what it is emphatically not for. The counterfactual
institution exists to answer questions the divergence-record mechanism (Part II.3) cannot: divergence
records compare the twin's forecast against what DID happen; the counterfactual institution compares
what DID happen against what WOULD HAVE happened under a structurally different federation, holding
the real mission history fixed. This is valuable for exactly one purpose, stated NORMATIVELY and
narrowly to prevent scope creep: informing FUTURE structural decisions with evidence about how a past
period would have unfolded differently, never for relitigating past decisions as mistakes to assign
blame for. (NORMATIVE, CRITICAL boundary: the counterfactual institution is a learning tool for IAS-G1
and arch-decision, explicitly not an audit tool for judging past Owner or agent choices — a corpus that
used counterfactual replay to grade historical decisions would corrode the trust the Owner places in
the estate's autonomy, which several Hard Rules elsewhere in the estate already protect by keeping
even legitimate concerns advisory-only.)

9.3 The counterfactual's calibration problem and why it is harder than scenario simulation. A
scenario (Part III) is evaluated over a SYNTHETIC mission window drawn from a demand forecast, which
sidesteps the hardest calibration problem: real missions have real, path-dependent consequences that
depend on decisions made in response to EARLIER missions in the same window, and a counterfactual
architecture run over REAL mission history must decide, at each point where the real federation made a
choice conditioned on its actual structure, what the counterfactual structure would have chosen
instead. (DERIVED: this is a strictly harder simulation problem than scenario evaluation, because it
requires simulating not just the structural difference but the DECISION PROCESS under that structure,
which is itself a model of a model.) The corpus takes the CONTESTED position honestly: some
counterfactual questions are answerable with reasonable confidence (a structural difference that
clearly would not have changed a given mission's routing), and some are not (a structural difference
whose effect compounds across many path-dependent decisions quickly exceeds the twin's calibrated
confidence), and the counterfactual institution must REPORT which category a given comparison falls
into rather than manufacturing a confident answer to an unanswerable question.

9.4 Worked example — counterfactual on a foundational sequencing choice. Consider a synthetic
counterfactual question: "if the CPP-IAS family had built IAS-C1 (Capability Portfolio) before IAS-F1
(Federation Ontology) instead of after, per the actual build order this corpus follows, how would the
first quarter's dataset authoring have unfolded?" The counterfactual institution constructs an
alternate twin whose ontology sub-model is absent for the counterfactual's early period, forcing every
downstream dataset's object-resolution step (which F1's design explicitly exists to make unambiguous,
per F1 Part I.5) to proceed without a settled vocabulary. The simulated counterfactual trajectory,
HYPOTHESIZED pending any real basis for comparison since the actual estate did build F1 first, would
likely show increased translation friction between early datasets — each coining locally, exactly the
failure mode F1 Part I.5 names as "the corpus will spend its life translating." This worked example
illustrates the counterfactual institution's proper use: informing FUTURE build-sequencing decisions
(should a later dataset family sequence its foundation dataset first) with evidence from a rehearsed
alternate past, not grading the Owner's actual sequencing choice.

9.5 Resource discipline — the counterfactual institution is the most expensive twin mode and must be
gated accordingly. Maintaining a full alternate structural model in parallel with the primary twin,
across real mission history, is the most capital-intensive mode this dataset specifies. (NORMATIVE:
the counterfactual institution is invoked rarely, by explicit Owner or IAS-G1 request tied to a
specific pending structural decision, never run as a standing background process — this is the twin's
own application of the Session Cost Discipline and HR-COST-002's 2x-budget stop-gate to its own
internal operation, the same self-restraint Part VIII.3 imposes on the Monte-Carlo run count.)

9.6 The counterfactual's relationship to the estate's actual history as a single, non-repeatable
sample. A subtle statistical honesty this Part names explicitly: the estate's real mission history,
against which a counterfactual institution replays an alternate structure (9.1), is ONE realized path
through what was, at each decision point, a distribution of possible outcomes — the real history is not
"the" outcome architecture B would be compared against in some platonic sense, it is the ONE sample the
real federation happened to draw. (DERIVED, extending Part 8.2's aleatoric-variance discussion from
forward forecasting to backward counterfactual replay: even a perfectly calibrated counterfactual
institution is comparing architecture B's simulated trajectory against a single noisy draw from
architecture A's own distribution, not against architecture A's TRUE expected trajectory, and a
counterfactual finding that architecture B "would have done better" must be read as "better than this
one realized sample," never as "better than architecture A's central tendency," unless the comparison
is itself run through the Monte-Carlo subengine (Part VIII) against MULTIPLE resampled draws of the
real history's own noise, not just the single real trajectory.) This is named as a required
methodological caveat on every counterfactual finding, not an optional footnote, because omitting it is
precisely the kind of false-precision failure Part 11.3 warns against, transplanted from forward
forecasting into backward replay.

9.7 Why the counterfactual institution is deliberately NOT specified as a routine planning tool. Given
the depth of mechanism this Part specifies, a reader might reasonably ask why the counterfactual
institution is not simply folded into the scenario laboratory (Part III) as a seventh routine
configuration. The answer, restated for emphasis because it governs Decision 5's architectural ceiling
(Part XXIII): the counterfactual institution's evidence ceiling (Part 17.4) is permanently lower than
every other mode's, its resource cost (9.5) is permanently higher, and its single-sample caveat (9.6)
means its findings require more interpretive care than a forward scenario's differential comparison. A
system that made this mode as routinely available as mission simulation would, by sheer volume of
low-confidence outputs, degrade the estate's trust in the twin's outputs generally — the exact
erosion-by-volume risk the honesty gate (Part 11.5) exists to prevent at the individual-forecast level,
here named as a risk at the MODE level: a rarely-invoked, carefully-sponsored counterfactual is a
credible learning tool; a routinely-invoked one becomes noise indistinguishable, to a time-pressured
consumer, from a forward forecast, undermining exactly the confidence-band discipline this entire
dataset is built to protect.

9.8 A minimal worked illustration of the single-sample caveat's practical effect. To make 9.6's
caveat concrete: suppose a counterfactual finding reports architecture B "would have reduced aggregate
authoring cost by an estimated margin" relative to the real, realized architecture A trajectory. Under
the single-sample discipline, this finding must be qualified as "better than the one realized path
architecture A happened to take," and the counterfactual institution's own recommended presentation
format (a specification requirement, not merely a stylistic preference) is to accompany any such finding
with a resampled comparison — architecture B's trajectory against several Monte-Carlo-resampled draws of
architecture A's own noise, per 9.6 — so that a reader sees whether the reported advantage survives
resampling or was itself an artifact of the one historical path's particular luck.

FINAL LAW — PART IX. A counterfactual institution earns its cost only when it informs a future
structural choice with a rehearsed alternate past; the instant it is used to relitigate a past
decision's blame, it has stopped being a learning tool and started being a tribunal the estate never
asked for.

===============================================================================================
PART X — TWIN FIDELITY AND DRIFT FROM REALITY
===============================================================================================

10.1 Fidelity as a measured property, never an assumed one. FIDELITY, as DEFINED in Part I.2, is how
closely the twin's structure and state track the real estate. This Part specifies fidelity as a
MEASURED quantity rather than a design aspiration, because a twin whose fidelity is merely assumed is
indistinguishable, from the outside, from a twin that has quietly become disconnected from the
estate it claims to mirror — the exact failure this dataset exists to prevent the estate from
repeating in a new form. Fidelity is measured along three independent axes, none of which substitutes
for the others: STRUCTURAL FIDELITY (does the twin's system/relation graph match the real estate's
current system/relation graph, checked by periodic reconciliation against the same source-of-truth
registries IAS-E1's observability fabric reads); STATE FIDELITY (does the twin's state model match
the real ensemble state at synchronization time, checked by comparing twin-reported values against
IAS-E1's live readings for the same metrics); and DYNAMICS FIDELITY (does the twin's forecast
accuracy, measured by the divergence-record mechanism of Part II.3, meet a stated threshold across a
rolling window of completed missions).

10.2 Drift as the natural, expected, and dangerous default. DRIFT is DEFINED as the accumulation of
unreconciled difference between the twin and the real estate across any of the three fidelity axes,
and it is the twin's DEFAULT trajectory absent active maintenance, not an occasional failure — every
real structural change to the estate (a new system built, a system merged, a relation added) that is
not immediately reflected in the twin's structural sub-model is drift accruing from the moment the
real change lands. (DERIVED: this is a direct consequence of the currency property Part I.2 defines —
a twin's currency is never perfect, and every gap between real-world change and twin re-synchronization
is drift by construction.) The dangerous failure mode is not drift itself, which is unavoidable, but
UNDETECTED drift: a twin whose fidelity has degraded below a usable threshold while its outputs
continue to be consumed by DRK, IAS-C1, or arch-decision as if fidelity were still high.

10.3 The reconciliation protocol and its cadence. Structural fidelity is reconciled on a cadence tied
to the estate's own rate of structural change, not a fixed calendar interval: every time a new system
is sealed (a dataset SEALED per Article XI's state machine, a module wired and verified), a
reconciliation pass updates the twin's structural sub-model before the next mission simulation
consumes it. (NORMATIVE.) State fidelity is reconciled at a tighter cadence, ideally continuous or
near-continuous, by subscribing the twin's state sub-model to the same signals IAS-E1's observability
fabric and IAS-A2's control bus already carry — this is the specific mechanism by which IAS-F3
strengthens IAS-A2, per the strengthening matrix: the control bus's cross-system events become the
twin's state-synchronization feed, rather than the twin polling for state independently and duplicating
IAS-A2's transport. Dynamics fidelity is reconciled the slowest, by design, because it requires
accumulated divergence records across enough completed missions to distinguish a real calibration
drift from ordinary aleatoric noise (Part VIII.2's distinction).

10.4 The fidelity dashboard and its consumers. Fidelity measurements across all three axes are
surfaced as a standing FIDELITY DASHBOARD, consumed primarily by the twin's own stewards (whoever
maintains IAS-F3's calibration, an explicit human-or-agent role this dataset names but does not staff,
flagged UNRESOLVED in Part XXI) and secondarily by every decision-owning system that consumes twin
output, so that DRK, IAS-C1, and arch-decision can weight a given forecast's confidence not only by
the forecast's own stated uncertainty band (Part VIII) but by the CURRENT fidelity of the sub-model
that produced it — a forecast from a twin whose dynamics fidelity has silently degraded should carry
LOWER trust than its own stated confidence band claims, and the dashboard is the mechanism that lets a
consuming system discover that discrepancy rather than trusting the forecast's self-reported band
blindly. (DERIVED: a forecast's stated uncertainty and the twin's actual current fidelity are two
different things, and conflating them is the single most dangerous simplification this dataset must
guard against.)

10.5 Worked example — detecting a structural fidelity gap after a fast-moving build week. Consider a
synthetic scenario: three new capability subsystems are wired into IAS-A1's router over one intensive
build week, but the twin's structural reconciliation, scheduled at the SEALED-dataset cadence of Part
10.3, has not yet run because none of the three subsystems triggered a full dataset sealing event. A
mission simulation run during that window would silently route around the three new subsystems as if
they did not exist, understating the router's real capacity and potentially recommending an
extincted-consumer-style HARD classification (Part VII.3) for a system that, in reality, now has a
graceful fallback the twin does not yet know about. The fidelity dashboard's structural-fidelity axis,
if actively monitored, would surface the reconciliation lag as a dashboard flag before the mission
simulation's output was trusted, converting a silent gap into a visible, actionable maintenance item.
(HYPOTHESIZED specific scenario; DERIVED general mechanism: any change that does not trip the
reconciliation trigger accrues invisible drift until the dashboard's next check.)

10.6 Fidelity's relationship to twin AGE and the danger of treating a young twin as a mature one.
A newly INITIALIZED twin instance (Part 15.1) has, by definition, no divergence-record history and
therefore no measurable dynamics fidelity — the CALIBRATING lifecycle state exists precisely to name
this honestly. This Part specifies that fidelity measurement must be AGE-AWARE: a twin instance's
dynamics-fidelity score is not merely a number but a number PAIRED with the sample size (the count of
divergence records) it rests on, and a fidelity score computed from a small sample must be reported
with a correspondingly wider confidence interval on the fidelity score ITSELF — a second-order
uncertainty, uncertainty about the twin's own uncertainty-reporting accuracy, which this dataset names
rather than smooths over. (DERIVED: a young twin reporting a fidelity score with the same apparent
precision as a mature twin's score, absent this pairing, would mislead a consumer into extending equal
trust to structurally less-evidenced numbers — exactly the false-precision failure this dataset
repeatedly guards against, here applied reflexively to the fidelity metric that itself exists to guard
against false precision elsewhere.) The sample-size pairing requirement therefore applies recursively:
the fidelity dashboard (13.5) carries not just three fidelity scores but three (score, sample-size)
pairs, and the CALIBRATING-to-OPERATIONAL lifecycle transition (Part 15.1) is gated on sample size
crossing a stated minimum, not merely on the score crossing a stated threshold — a twin that happens to
score well on three lucky early missions is not yet OPERATIONAL under this discipline.

10.7 Worked example — a young twin's misleadingly good early accuracy. Consider a synthetic scenario:
a newly initialized twin's first five simulated missions all happen to be routine, well-precedented
mission types close to the historical patterns its structural and state sub-models were seeded from,
producing five divergence records that all fall comfortably within band — an apparent 100% accuracy
rate. Under the age-aware discipline of 10.6, this result is reported as "100% accuracy, n=5,
CALIBRATING state, wide fidelity-confidence interval" rather than as "100% accuracy" unqualified,
because five routine missions provide almost no evidence about how the twin performs on the genuinely
novel or edge-case missions (Part 22.5) that determine whether it is trustworthy under real operating
conditions. A consuming system that read the unqualified "100% accuracy" figure and treated the twin as
mature would be exhibiting exactly the simulation-trusted-as-reality failure mode (Part XI), not
because the number was FALSE, but because its evidentiary weight was misrepresented by omission — the
single most common way this dataset expects the twin's own honesty gate to be tested in early
operation, before enough divergence-record volume accumulates to make the danger moot.

FINAL LAW — PART X. A twin's fidelity is either measured on all three axes or it is assumed, and an
assumed fidelity is disconnection wearing the twin's name; the dashboard exists precisely so that a
consuming system can discover a degraded twin before it inherits the twin's overconfidence as its own.

===============================================================================================
PART XI — FAILURE MODES: TWIN DRIFT AND SIMULATION TRUSTED AS REALITY
===============================================================================================

11.1 The two failure modes this Part names and why they are treated together. This dataset's single
most important governance concern, foreshadowed throughout Parts I through X, is specified here as two
DISTINCT but mutually reinforcing failure modes. TWIN DRIFT (the structural, state, or dynamics
fidelity degradation Part X measures) is the failure of the MODEL. SIMULATION TRUSTED AS REALITY is
the failure of the MODEL'S CONSUMERS — a decision-owning system, or the Owner, treating a twin
forecast as a fact about the real estate rather than a rehearsal with a stated confidence band. The
two compound: an undetected drift combined with a consumer that has stopped checking the fidelity
dashboard produces exactly the outcome this entire dataset was built to prevent — a decision made on
the strength of a rehearsal that no longer describes the thing it claims to model. (DERIVED, the
central risk of the whole dataset stated plainly.)

11.2 Twin drift's sub-modes and their detection signatures. Structural drift manifests as the
reconciliation-lag pattern of Part 10.5 — new or changed systems invisible to the twin's structural
model — and is detected by comparing the twin's system count and relation-edge count against IAS-E1's
live observability readings on the reconciliation cadence. State drift manifests as the twin's reported
portfolio balance, capital headroom, or active-capability set diverging from IAS-E1's live readings
between synchronization events, and is detected by the same comparison at a tighter cadence. Dynamics
drift is the subtlest and most dangerous: it manifests not as a visible gap but as a SYSTEMATIC bias in
divergence records — forecasts that are consistently optimistic, or consistently miss a specific
propagation pattern, in a way that a single divergence record cannot reveal but a rolling window of
many divergence records can. (DERIVED: dynamics drift detection requires exactly the accumulated
divergence-record discipline Part II.3 and Part X.1's dynamics-fidelity axis both depend on, which is
why this dataset treats the divergence ledger as load-bearing infrastructure, not an optional audit
trail.)

11.3 Simulation-trusted-as-reality's characteristic signature — confidence bands quietly dropped in
transmission. The second failure mode has a specific, observable signature this dataset names
explicitly so it can be watched for: a forecast that left the twin with a stated confidence band
(per Part VIII.5's mandate that the distribution reach the consumer intact) but arrives at the
decision-owning system, or at the Owner, summarized as a single point figure with the band silently
dropped — "the mission will cost approximately X" rather than "the mission will cost between X-low and
X-high at this confidence, with this tail risk." (DERIVED, drawing a direct parallel to the general
estate discipline against false precision that the Constitution's Article V epistemic-marking
requirement already enforces for every OTHER kind of claim in this corpus — a twin forecast is no
exception, and reporting layers that collapse a distribution into a point estimate are committing the
exact violation Article V forbids, just laundered through a simulation rather than stated directly as
fact.) NORMATIVE mitigation: every reporting surface between the twin and a decision-owning system is
required to preserve the band; a reporting layer that cannot display a band is not fit to consume twin
output.

11.4 The suspiciously perfect twin as its own warning sign. Part II.3 named this briefly; it is
elevated to a full failure mode here because it is counter-intuitive enough to be missed by a steward
looking only for LOW-fidelity signals. A twin whose divergence records show near-zero variance across
many missions, or whose confidence bands are consistently narrow, is not necessarily a well-calibrated
twin — real federations, per Part VIII.2's aleatoric-variance discussion, have irreducible
stochasticity, and a twin that fails to reproduce that stochasticity is more likely UNDER-modeling
uncertainty (a model-structure failure) than achieving genuinely superior prediction. (DERIVED,
CONTESTED at the edges: it is possible for a mission class to be genuinely low-variance, and the
dataset does not claim every narrow band is false; the NORMATIVE guidance is that a narrow band should
trigger a model-structure review, not an assumption of excellence, especially for the more speculative
twin modes — scenario laboratory, counterfactual institution — whose calibration data is thinnest.)

11.5 Institutional hardening against both failure modes — the twin's own honesty gate. This dataset
proposes, as a specification for a later implementation mission per Article VIII, a HONESTY GATE that
sits between every twin output and its consumer: the gate checks (a) that the fidelity dashboard's
relevant axis is within a stated freshness window before the forecast is released, (b) that the
forecast's confidence band is structurally present in the output payload, not merely computed and then
discarded, and (c) that a suspiciously narrow band (per 11.4's threshold) is flagged for review rather
than passed through silently. A forecast failing any of the three checks is not blocked outright — the
twin's outputs are always advisory, per the D2A precedent — but is labeled DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE at the
point of consumption, so the decision-owning system inherits the honest state of the twin's own
self-knowledge rather than a laundered point estimate. (NORMATIVE + EXPERIMENTAL: proposed here as a
specification; its effectiveness is untested pending implementation, carrying its own test per Article
V's EXPERIMENTAL marking — the test being whether DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE labels correlate, in the
divergence-record history, with forecasts that actually missed by more than the un-degraded population.)

11.6 A third failure mode named for completeness — honesty-gate fatigue. Beyond twin drift (the
model's failure) and simulation-trusted-as-reality (the consumer's failure), this Part names a third,
subtler risk: HONESTY-GATE FATIGUE, DEFINED as the pattern where DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE labels (Part 11.5)
appear so frequently — because the twin is chronically under-maintained rather than occasionally
drifted — that consuming systems begin to treat the label as background noise rather than as an
actionable signal, functionally reproducing simulation-trusted-as-reality's outcome through habituation
rather than through a dropped band. (DERIVED: a warning label that fires on every output stops
functioning as a warning, a general property of alert systems this dataset imports by analogy rather
than invents; the estate's own hook-fanout cost problem — many PreToolUse hooks firing on every Write —
is a structural cousin of this same habituation risk in a different subsystem.) The specified
mitigation is not to soften the honesty gate's threshold to fire less often — Part 11.5 marks the gate
CRITICAL and non-negotiable — but to treat a SUSTAINED high rate of DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE labels as
itself a signal routed to the fidelity dashboard's steward (Part 21.1's named gap) as an escalating
maintenance priority, distinct from and prior to any individual forecast's label: chronic fatigue is a
twin-maintenance failure to fix at the source, not a labeling-frequency problem to fix by labeling less.

11.7 Interaction between the two named failure modes and the twin's lifecycle state. Twin drift (11.2)
and simulation-trusted-as-reality (11.3) do not occur uniformly across the lifecycle Part 15.1
specifies: a CALIBRATING twin's honest wide bands (Part 10.7) make simulation-trusted-as-reality LESS
likely (a wide band is harder to mistake for a precise fact) but twin drift is, definitionally,
inapplicable to a twin with no baseline fidelity yet established. An OPERATIONAL twin inverts this: its
narrower, more confident bands make simulation-trusted-as-reality MORE likely precisely because the
twin has earned trust, while twin drift becomes the dominant risk because an operational twin has
enough history to drift silently away from a baseline that was, at some point, genuinely earned. (DERIVED:
this dataset's risk posture should therefore not be uniform across the lifecycle — Part 21.1's
calibration steward, once staffed, would reasonably apply LOOSER drift-monitoring cadence to a
CALIBRATING twin, whose fidelity is expected to be unstable by design, and TIGHTER cadence to an
OPERATIONAL twin, whose stability is precisely what makes an undetected departure from it most
dangerous — an explicit design implication for the reconciliation cadence of Part 15.4, stated here as
the rationale that cadence should itself be lifecycle-state-aware rather than uniform.)

FINAL LAW — PART XI. Twin drift is a failure of the model and simulation-trusted-as-reality is a
failure of the model's audience, and the two compound silently unless the confidence band survives
every hop from the twin to the decision it informs — a rehearsal without its uncertainty is not a
humbler forecast, it is a lie of omission wearing a simulation's credibility.

===============================================================================================
PART XII — TWIN ARCHITECTURE: LAYERS, ENGINES, AND SUBENGINES
===============================================================================================

12.1 The four-layer architecture, stated as a specification, never as code. Consistent with Article
VIII's prohibition on production code this mission, this Part specifies the twin's architecture as
named layers and their responsibilities, for a later implementation mission to build against, not as
any executable artifact. The MIRROR LAYER holds the four sub-models of Part I.3 (structural, state,
dynamics, uncertainty) and is the only layer permitted to read the live estate's source-of-truth
registries directly, via the same channels IAS-E1's observability fabric already reads — a deliberate
non-duplication choice: the mirror layer does not independently re-scan the estate, it subscribes to
E1's existing telemetry, which is precisely the mechanism by which the strengthening matrix's IAS-E1
row ("observability fabric" strengthened) is realized for this dataset specifically.

12.2 The scenario engine and its five twin-mode subengines. The SCENARIO ENGINE implements the
fork-simulate-compare protocol shared by Part II (mission), Part III (architecture scenario), Part IV
(risk), Part V (capital), Part VI (governance), and Part VII (extinction) — six modes, one shared
mechanism, specified here as ONE engine with SIX subengine configurations rather than six separate
engines, precisely to avoid the trap of building a redundant coordinator that Part 1.2 names: a
federation twin that implements six near-identical fork-simulate-compare pipelines as six independent
systems has duplicated itself six times over; one engine parameterized by which sub-model layer the
fork edits (structural for architecture/extinction, constraint for governance, perturbation-injected
for risk, allocation-input for capital, dispatch-input for mission) is the non-duplicative design.
(DERIVED, applying the Constitution's own supreme law reflexively to this dataset's internal
architecture, not just to its relationship with external parents.)

12.3 The Monte-Carlo engine as a shared uncertainty service, not a per-mode feature. Part VIII
specified Monte-Carlo simulation as the uncertainty ENGINE consumed by every other mode; architecturally
this is realized as a single MONTE-CARLO SUBENGINE that the scenario engine calls with a run-count
parameter (per Part 8.3's cost-accuracy policy) rather than each of the six scenario-engine
configurations implementing its own repeated-sampling logic. This is the second reflexive application
of the non-duplication discipline: uncertainty computation is a capability (per IAS-F1 Part II.4's
capability-as-currency definition), provided once, consumed by six configurations, exactly the pattern
IAS-F1 names as the hinge that lets the estate be reasoned about as an economy.

12.4 The counterfactual subengine's distinct resource profile. Part IX's counterfactual institution is
architecturally distinct enough from the shared scenario engine to warrant its own subengine rather
than a seventh scenario-engine configuration, because it does not fork-simulate-compare against the
LIVE twin's current state; it maintains a persistently divergent alternate structural model replayed
against REAL mission history, a fundamentally different data-flow shape (Part 9.1's own framing:
"unlike the other modes it does not evaluate one proposed change against the live twin"). The
COUNTERFACTUAL SUBENGINE is therefore specified as consuming the scenario engine's fork mechanism as a
primitive (it forks once, at the counterfactual's inception point) but owning its own replay-against-
history logic independently, and it is gated by Part 9.5's rare-invocation discipline at the
architectural level — the subengine specification includes an explicit invocation-count ceiling per
quarter, not merely a norm stated in prose.

12.5 The reporting layer and the honesty gate as its mandatory final stage. Every twin mode's output
passes through a single REPORTING LAYER before reaching a consumer, and Part XI.5's honesty gate is
specified as that layer's mandatory final stage — not an optional post-processing step a consumer
could bypass by calling the scenario engine directly, but the only sanctioned egress path for any twin
output. (NORMATIVE, closing the architectural loop against the exact failure Part XI.3 names: a
consumer that reaches around the reporting layer to read the scenario engine's raw output directly has
created a second, ungated egress path, and the confidence-band-dropping failure mode becomes possible
again. The architecture specification therefore requires the scenario engine's outputs to be
unaddressable except through the reporting layer — a specification-level access-control decision, not
an implementation detail, because Article VIII scopes this dataset to specification and the access
boundary is exactly the kind of architectural decision a later implementation must not silently
relax.)

12.6 Failure isolation across layers — what happens when one sub-model is unavailable. Because the
mirror layer (12.1) holds four sub-models sourced from different upstream systems (IAS-E1 for state,
IAS-D1/IAS-E2 for dynamics calibration, IAS-F1 for structure), this Part specifies the twin's behavior
when one sub-model's upstream source is itself unavailable or degraded — a FAILURE ISOLATION
requirement distinct from ordinary fidelity drift (Part X), because unavailability is a harder failure
than staleness. If IAS-E1's telemetry feed is unavailable, the state sub-model freezes at its
last-known values and every subsequent forecast carries a STATE-STALE annotation (a sibling of the
DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE label, Part 11.5, but attributable specifically to state rather than dynamics or
structure); the scenario engine (12.2) continues to operate on the frozen state rather than refusing to
run entirely, because a stale-but-present state is more useful, honestly labeled, than no simulation at
all — consistent with this dataset's general preference (Part 2.5) for a visible, logged degradation
over a silent unavailability. (NORMATIVE: fail-open with honest labeling, not fail-closed, mirrors the
estate's own broader fail-open discipline for advisory gates, applied here to the twin's own
sub-model architecture.)

12.7 The architecture's testability as a design property, stated explicitly. Because the scenario
engine (12.2) is specified as ONE parameterized mechanism rather than six independent ones, this Part
notes a direct consequence for evaluation (Part XVII): a backtest or unit-level validation of the fork-
simulate-compare mechanism itself, run against ANY one configuration (say, mission simulation, the most
frequently exercised), provides evidence about the shared mechanism's correctness that TRANSFERS,
partially, to the other five configurations, because they share the same underlying fork and compare
logic and differ only in which sub-model layer the fork edits. (DERIVED: this transferability is a
direct architectural dividend of Decision 2's non-duplication choice — six independent engines would
require six independent validation efforts with zero transfer, while one shared engine's validation
effort compounds across modes, precisely the kind of reuse the strengthening matrix and compounding
graph reward elsewhere in this corpus, here realized internally to this single dataset's own
architecture rather than across sibling datasets.)

12.8 Versioning the architecture specification itself, distinct from versioning the dynamics model.
Part 15.5 specifies calibration-version rollback for the dynamics sub-model's parameters; this Part
notes, for architectural completeness, that the ARCHITECTURE this Part specifies — the layer
boundaries, the six-configuration scenario engine, the mandatory reporting layer — is a separate axis
of change entirely, governed not by the calibration-rollback mechanism but by the same governance-
impact simulation (Part VI) this dataset specifies for any other structural or constraint edit to the
ensemble, because a change to the twin's OWN architecture is, from the estate's perspective, a
structural edit to a system (the twin itself) like any other, and should be proposed, forked, simulated,
and promoted through the identical scenario-laboratory discipline (Part III) rather than through an
ad hoc architecture-revision process invented specially for the twin. (DERIVED, the final reflexive
application of this dataset's non-duplication discipline: the twin does not get a bespoke change-
management process exempt from the very mechanisms it specifies for the rest of the estate.)

FINAL LAW — PART XII. Six twin modes sharing one scenario engine, one Monte-Carlo subengine, and one
mandatory reporting layer is not architectural minimalism for its own sake; it is the twin obeying, in
its own internal structure, the same non-duplication law it was built to help the rest of the estate
obey.

PART XIII — OBJECTS AND DATA MODEL
===============================================================================================

13.1 The MissionForecast object. Specified as a record carrying: mission identifier, dispatch systems
predicted engaged (a set of system references resolved against IAS-F1's system object), capital cost
band (point estimate, low/high at a stated confidence level, sourced from the Monte-Carlo subengine),
duration band, side-effect state changes (a list of predicted state deltas on non-primary systems),
overall confidence level, and a provenance field naming which dynamics-model calibration version
produced the forecast — the provenance field exists specifically so that a later divergence record
(13.4) can be attributed to the correct calibration version rather than blended across versions that
may have since been recalibrated. (DEFINED.)

13.2 The ScenarioBranch object. Specified as a record carrying: parent twin-state snapshot reference,
the structural or constraint edit applied, the mission window used for simulation, the resulting
emergent-property trajectory (a time series over the ensemble state model's emergent properties named
in IAS-F1 Part IV, imported by reference), the counterfactual baseline trajectory it was differenced
against, and a disposition field (DISCARDED / PROMOTED-TO-RECOMMENDATION), enforcing at the data-model
level the Part III.2 and Part III.5 discipline that a branch never silently becomes a structural change
— disposition must be explicitly set, and PROMOTED-TO-RECOMMENDATION is the only value that triggers
a handoff, and even then only to arch-decision / IAS-G1, never to a live-apply path. (DEFINED,
NORMATIVE.)

13.3 The PropagationGraph object. Specified as a record carrying: the perturbation's origin system and
type, an ordered list of hops (each hop naming the receiving system, estimated severity, whether the
hop was absorbed or forwarded, and the SLO/failure-budget reference from IAS-E2 that determined
absorption), and a termination reason (contained / exited ensemble boundary / reached simulation
horizon uncontained). The termination reason field is what lets a risk simulation's output be
compared meaningfully against a real incident's actual containment path, closing the same
divergence-and-recalibration loop Part II.3 establishes for missions, applied here to risk. (DEFINED.)

13.4 The DivergenceRecord object and its role as the twin's central feedback artifact. Specified as a
record carrying: the originating forecast's identifier and provenance (linking back to 13.1's
provenance field), the dimension of comparison (capital / duration / systems-engaged / side-effects),
the forecasted value and band, the observed real value, and a computed divergence magnitude expressed
in units of the forecast's own confidence band (e.g., "the observed value fell 1.3 confidence-widths
outside the forecasted band") — this normalized unit is what lets divergence records from forecasts of
very different absolute scale be aggregated meaningfully into the rolling dynamics-fidelity window Part
X.1 specifies, rather than raw absolute errors that would make a large mission's small percentage miss
look identical in magnitude to a small mission's large percentage miss. (DEFINED, DERIVED: the
normalization choice is what makes dynamics-fidelity measurement possible across a heterogeneous
mission population.)

13.5 The FidelityDashboard object and the CapitalTrajectory object, specified briefly. The
FidelityDashboard is specified as three time series (structural, state, dynamics fidelity scores) each
against a stated threshold, plus a current-status field per axis (HEALTHY / DEGRADED / STALE), directly
realizing Part X.4's consumer-facing artifact. The CapitalTrajectory is specified as the object Part V.2
describes: a series of checkpoints, each carrying cumulative-consumed, remaining-headroom, and
breach-probability at the confidence band's bounds, plus a governance-constraint-violated boolean per
checkpoint realizing Part 5.4's INFEASIBLE-flagging requirement at the data-model level rather than
leaving it as prose policy alone. (DEFINED.)

13.6 The ScenarioProposal object, upstream of the ScenarioBranch. Section 3.6 specifies who may propose
a scenario and 3.7 specifies the intake validation a proposal must pass; this Part formalizes the
SCENARIOPROPOSAL object those sections presuppose but did not schema-specify. A ScenarioProposal is a
record carrying: the sponsor identity (a named system per 3.6's two tiers, or an Owner-initiated flag),
the precise structural or constraint edit requested (never a free-text aspiration, per 3.7's intake
gate), the requested mission window source (a demand-forecast reference per Part XVIII, or a fixed
versioned window per Part 22.2's anti-gaming mitigation), and an intake-status field (REJECTED-IMPRECISE
/ ADMITTED). Only an ADMITTED proposal is eligible to consume a fork (3.2) and produce a ScenarioBranch
(13.2); this two-object separation — proposal versus branch — is what makes the intake rejection of 3.7
enforceable at the data-model level rather than as an unenforced convention, mirroring the same design
principle Part XIII's closing law states for every other object in this Part.

13.7 The DemandSignal and PaysOffVerdict objects, formalizing Part XVIII's integration. A DemandSignal,
consumed from IAS-C2 per Part 18.1, is specified as a record carrying: the predicted capability gap,
the predicted mission mix that motivated the prediction, a predicted-demand confidence level (sourced
from IAS-C2's own forecasting, imported rather than re-derived), and a timestamp establishing how
current the signal is at the point IAS-F3 consumes it — this last field matters because a demand signal,
like any other twin input, is subject to its own currency decay (Part I.2), and a demand-forecast-sourced
scenario run against a stale DemandSignal inherits that staleness as an additional uncertainty
component the PaysOffVerdict (18.3) must reflect. The PaysOffVerdict object itself is specified as a
record carrying the three named components (CAPACITY MATCH, CAPITAL COST, OPPORTUNITY COST) each as an
independently banded value, plus a reference back to the originating DemandSignal and ScenarioBranch,
so that the feedback edge of Part 18.5 — a later real-usage observation retrodicting the ORIGINAL demand
forecast — has an unambiguous object chain to walk backward through: real usage, to PaysOffVerdict, to
ScenarioBranch, to DemandSignal, to IAS-C2's original forecast. (DEFINED, DERIVED: without this
explicit chain, the retrodiction test of Part 18.5 would have no reliable way to attribute a real
outcome back to the specific forecast that motivated the build decision, especially once multiple
demand signals and scenarios accumulate over time.)

13.8 Object lifecycle and retention — nothing this dataset specifies is deleted on disposition. A
cross-cutting rule for every object in this Part: a DISCARDED ScenarioBranch, a REJECTED-IMPRECISE
ScenarioProposal, and a bypassed mission's absent MissionForecast (Part 2.5's logged-bypass path) are
all RETAINED, never purged, because each is negative or absent evidence the twin's own evaluation
regime (Part XVII) and the estate's broader learning discipline (the Cognitive Multiplier, IAS-B1, which
this dataset does not re-derive but whose appetite for cross-system signal this retention rule directly
serves) can draw on later. (NORMATIVE: retention is not the same claim as unlimited retention cost —
older discarded objects may be compacted or summarized per the estate's own context-management
discipline, GK-06's context pack pattern, imported by reference — but summarization is not deletion, and
the twin's object model must never treat a DISCARDED or REJECTED disposition as grounds to forget the
proposal existed, because a pattern of repeatedly rejected proposals of a similar shape is itself a
signal — possibly of a genuine unmet need being proposed in the wrong form, possibly of a proposer
misunderstanding the laboratory's intake bar — that only survives if the rejected proposals remain
queryable.)

13.9 The severity tuple and multi-currency checkpoint as extensions of, not exceptions to, the object
model's discipline. Parts 4.6 and 5.6 introduce a severity tuple on PropagationGraph hops and a
per-currency checkpoint on CapitalTrajectory respectively; this Part confirms both are specified as
structured sub-fields of the objects already defined in 13.3 and 13.5, not as separate new objects,
keeping the total object inventory this dataset commits to at five top-level schemas (13.1 through
13.5) plus their two upstream companions (13.6, 13.7) — seven objects total governing every twin mode
this dataset specifies, a deliberately small surface consistent with the minimum-vocabulary discipline
IAS-F1 Part II.5 establishes for the federation ontology generally and which this dataset inherits
rather than re-argues.

FINAL LAW — PART XIII. An object model that cannot express its own provenance, its own confidence band,
and its own disposition is not a specification a later implementation can build honestly against; every
object in this Part carries the field that makes the corresponding Part's discipline enforceable rather
than aspirational.

PART XIV — RELATIONS AND DECISION MODELS
===============================================================================================

14.1 The twin's relation to its decision-owning consumers, specified as a fixed protocol. Three
decision-owning systems consume twin output — DRK, one_shot, and arch-decision/IAS-G1 — and each
relation is specified with a distinct triggering condition and a distinct consumed object, preventing
the twin from becoming a generic oracle any system queries ad hoc, which would erode the boundary
discipline Parts I, III, V, VI, and VIII each establish separately. DRK consumes RISK SIMULATION
output (Part IV) when computing blast radius for a decision whose propagation reaches beyond DRK's own
decision-local walk depth, extending rather than replacing DRK's existing computation. ONE_SHOT
consumes MISSION FORECAST output (Part II) at contract-compile time for L/XL-sized contracts, per the
route-before-spend gate's threshold (Part 2.2), feeding the contract's own feasibility check with a
federation-scoped forecast rather than a contract-scoped one. ARCH-DECISION / IAS-G1 consumes
SCENARIO BRANCH and GOVERNANCE-IMPACT REPORT output (Parts III, VI) exclusively via the
PROMOTED-TO-RECOMMENDATION disposition (13.2), never raw scenario-engine output.

14.2 The decision model each consumer applies to twin output — specified as a boundary, not a
mechanism the twin owns. This dataset deliberately does NOT specify how DRK, one_shot, or arch-decision
WEIGH a twin forecast against their own independent reasoning, because that weighting is each
consumer's own decision authority, per the non-duplication boundary restated in Parts 1.4, 3.5, 5.5,
6.5, and 8.5. What IS specified is the CONTRACT each consumer receives: a forecast or report carrying a
confidence band, a fidelity-derived trust discount (per Part X.4's dashboard-informed weighting), and a
DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE flag where applicable (Part XI.5). (NORMATIVE, DEFINED: the contract is specified;
the consumer's internal decision function over that contract is explicitly out of this dataset's
scope, by design, to keep the ensemble-object boundary clean.)

14.3 The Owner's relation to the twin — advisory input, never binding output. Per Article IX's governed
self-evolution requirement and the D2A precedent that IAS proposes and the Owner promotes, the Owner's
relation to any twin output is specified as strictly advisory: a mission forecast, a scenario
recommendation, or a governance-impact report is information the Owner may weigh, override, or ignore
entirely, and no twin output is specified to carry any binding or blocking authority over an Owner
decision. (NORMATIVE, CRITICAL: this is the same authority boundary reserved for secret rotation and
for destructive operations elsewhere in the estate — advisory surfacing, never autonomous authority —
applied here to the twin's entire output surface.)

14.4 Inter-system relation: the twin as a consumer of, never a competitor to, IAS-E1 and IAS-A2. This
Part makes explicit, as a relation rather than a mechanism, what Parts X.3 and XII.1 established
architecturally: the twin's mirror layer is a CONSUMER of IAS-E1's observability telemetry and IAS-A2's
control-bus events, never an independent source of estate-state truth. (NORMATIVE, DERIVED: this
relation is what prevents IAS-F3 from becoming a second, competing observability system — exactly the
duplication risk the ledger's family-F verdict for candidates 51-60 was built to fold into IAS-E1
alone, and IAS-F3 must not silently re-acquire that scope by building its own independent telemetry
collection.)

14.5 The decision-model summary table, stated in prose. For a route-before-spend mission gate: input
is a proposed mission above the capital threshold; the twin's output is a mission forecast; the
decision owner is whichever system dispatches the mission (typically IAS-A1's router or one_shot);
the twin's authority is zero — it informs, never blocks, though a governance rule COULD be written to
make DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE forecasts trigger a mandatory Owner review, which would be a governance
decision made and simulated through Part VI's own mechanism, not a hardcoded twin behavior. For an
extinction proposal: input is a candidate system for retirement; output is an orphaned-consumer report;
decision owner is IAS-D1 (ecology); authority is zero, with the same governance-simulable escalation
path available. This pattern — twin informs, named external system decides, escalation itself
governable — repeats across every relation this dataset specifies, and its consistency is itself a
design property worth stating as a law.

14.6 The relation between IAS-F3 and IAS-C1, specified even though C1 is not a Part-header integration.
Parts XVIII through XX give arch-decision/IAS-G1, IAS-C2, and DRK each a full integration Part; IAS-C1
(Capability Portfolio) is mentioned throughout as a consumer of capital trajectories (Part V) and
pays-off verdicts (Part XVIII) but has not yet had its relation stated as a self-contained boundary the
way the others have. This Part closes that gap: IAS-C1 consumes CapitalTrajectory objects (13.5) and
PaysOffVerdict objects (13.7) to inform allocation decisions across the ENTIRE portfolio, not one
mission or one scenario at a time — meaning IAS-C1's consumption pattern differs structurally from
DRK's or one_shot's single-object consumption (14.1): IAS-C1 aggregates MANY twin outputs across MANY
missions and scenarios into one portfolio-level view, a many-to-one relation this dataset has not
previously named explicitly. (DERIVED: this many-to-one shape is why IAS-C1's own dataset, not IAS-F3,
owns the aggregation logic — IAS-F3 supplies well-formed individual objects; IAS-C1 supplies the
portfolio-level synthesis across them, the same non-duplication boundary restated once more, this time
against the family's own most closely-adjacent sibling in the compounding graph's primary loop.)

14.7 A relation this dataset does NOT specify — direct twin-to-twin consumption across IAS datasets.
For completeness, this Part names an integration surface this dataset deliberately leaves unspecified:
whether OTHER IAS datasets (IAS-D1's ecology model, IAS-E1's observability fabric) should themselves
consume twin SIMULATION output, as opposed to merely supplying the twin with calibration data as
described throughout Parts I through XIII. The current specification treats those relations as
ONE-DIRECTIONAL — IAS-D1 and IAS-E1 feed the twin; the twin does not feed simulated (as opposed to
real, OBSERVED) data back into their own resource-level models. (NORMATIVE, CONTESTED: one could argue
IAS-D1's fitness model would benefit from simulated ecological outcomes the way IAS-C1 benefits from
simulated capital trajectories; this dataset takes the conservative position that simulated data
flowing into another system's OWN resource-level model, rather than into a clearly twin-scoped
consumer like a portfolio allocator or a decision-owning system, risks contaminating that system's
OBSERVED-data-calibrated model with HYPOTHESIZED twin output, an epistemic mixing this dataset prefers
to leave as a named, UNRESOLVED question — see Part 21 — rather than resolve by default expansion of
scope.)

14.8 A consolidated relation inventory, stated once for reference. This dataset has now specified
seven distinct relations across Parts XIV, XVIII, XIX, and XX: DRK (request-driven risk-simulation
handoff), one_shot (mission-forecast-fed feasibility), arch-decision/IAS-G1 (scenario and
governance-impact recommendations), the Owner (advisory-only across every output), IAS-E1/IAS-A2
(consumed infrastructure, never competed with), IAS-C1 (many-to-one aggregation consumer), and IAS-C2
(demand-signal producer and pays-off-verdict feedback recipient). Each relation carries its own
triggering condition, its own consumed object, and its own authority boundary, and no two relations
share an identical shape — a deliberate design property, because a twin whose every external relation
looked the same would be evidence that the twin had not actually differentiated its consumers'
distinct needs, merely offered one generic interface to all of them, which this dataset's Part 14.1
opening commitment explicitly rejects.

FINAL LAW — PART XIV. Every relation this dataset specifies follows the same shape — the twin informs,
a named external system decides, and even the escalation rules governing when a low-confidence forecast
demands extra scrutiny are themselves subject to governance simulation rather than hardcoded — and a
future extension that breaks this shape by granting the twin binding authority has broken the dataset's
central non-duplication contract, not merely added a feature.

===============================================================================================
PART XV — PROTOCOLS AND LIFECYCLE
===============================================================================================

15.1 The twin's own lifecycle, distinct from any single simulation's lifecycle. The TWIN INSTANCE
itself (as opposed to any one forecast or scenario branch it produces) has a lifecycle: INITIALIZED
(structural sub-model seeded from a full reconciliation pass against the live estate, zero calibration
history); CALIBRATING (accumulating divergence records, dynamics fidelity below the usable threshold
Part X.1 names, outputs carry a mandatory LOW-CALIBRATION flag distinct from but related to the
DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE flag of Part XI.5); OPERATIONAL (dynamics fidelity has crossed the usable
threshold across a sufficient rolling window, outputs consumed normally by DRK/one_shot/arch-decision
per Part XIV); DEGRADED (fidelity dashboard shows sustained decline on any axis, per Part 11.2's
detection signatures, outputs carry DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE across the board until reconciliation
resolves it); and RETIRED (a twin instance is itself extincted — an application of Part VII's own
mechanism to the twin's own lifecycle, coherent rather than paradoxical — if a structural change to
the estate is severe enough that recalibration from scratch is cheaper than incremental reconciliation,
in which case a new twin instance is INITIALIZED and the old one's divergence-record history is
archived, not discarded, because it remains valuable calibration data for the new instance's dynamics
sub-model).

15.2 The mission-simulation protocol, restated as a numbered sequence for implementation clarity. (1)
A mission is proposed to a dispatching system. (2) The dispatching system checks the mission's
estimated cost against the route-before-spend threshold (Part 2.2). (3) If above threshold, the
mission is submitted to the scenario engine's dispatch-input configuration (Part 12.2). (4) The
Monte-Carlo subengine runs the dynamics model at the mission's size-class run count (Part 8.3). (5)
The reporting layer's honesty gate checks freshness, band-presence, and narrow-band flagging (Part
11.5). (6) The MissionForecast object (13.1) is released to the dispatching system. (7) The
dispatching system decides, per its own authority, whether to proceed. (8) If the mission executes,
its real outcome is recorded. (9) A DivergenceRecord (13.4) is computed and fed back into the dynamics
sub-model's calibration. This nine-step sequence is the canonical protocol every other twin mode's
protocol is a variant of, differing only at steps 3 (which scenario-engine configuration) and 9 (what
kind of outcome is compared).

15.3 The scenario-laboratory protocol as a five-step variant, cross-referenced rather than restated.
Part 3.2 already specifies propose-fork-simulate-compare-discard/promote as the laboratory's protocol;
this Part notes, for lifecycle completeness, that step "compare" in the laboratory protocol corresponds
to step 5 (honesty gate) in the mission protocol's numbering, and "discard-or-promote" corresponds to
step 7 (dispatching system decides) generalized to "the consuming decision-owning system decides" —
making explicit that the nine-step canonical sequence and the five-step laboratory sequence are the
same underlying protocol at different granularity, not two independently invented processes.

15.4 The reconciliation protocol's trigger conditions, listed exhaustively. Structural reconciliation
triggers on: a dataset SEALED event (Article XI state machine), a module wired-and-verified event, or a
manual Owner-invoked refresh. State reconciliation triggers on: every IAS-A2 control-bus event tagged
as state-changing (a subscription, not a poll, per Part 10.3's non-duplication choice), and a periodic
freshness check that flags STALE (Part 13.5's status field) if no state-changing event has been
received within a threshold window — this freshness check exists specifically to distinguish "the
estate genuinely had no state changes" from "the subscription silently failed," a distinction Part XI's
failure-mode discipline requires the twin to be able to make about its OWN health, not just the
estate's. Dynamics reconciliation triggers on: a rolling window of N completed missions with recorded
divergence, recalibrating the dynamics sub-model's parameters (not its structural form, which requires
the rarer model-structure review Part 11.4 names) at that cadence.

15.5 Versioning and rollback of the dynamics model, per Article IX's governed self-evolution
requirement. Every dynamics-model recalibration produces a new CALIBRATION VERSION, never overwriting
the prior version in place, so that a MissionForecast's provenance field (13.1) always resolves to an
identifiable, reproducible calibration state, and so that a recalibration that turns out to degrade
forecast accuracy (detectable by comparing post-recalibration divergence records against the
pre-recalibration baseline over a subsequent window) can be ROLLED BACK to the prior calibration
version without losing the twin's structural or state sub-models. (NORMATIVE, DEFINED, directly
implementing Article IX's evidence-threshold-validation-versioning-rollback requirement for this
dataset's specific self-evolving component, which is the dynamics sub-model's calibration, and only
that component — the twin's structural and state sub-models are reconciled against ground truth, not
"evolved," and therefore do not require the same rollback discipline.)

15.6 The governance-impact and extinction protocols, stated as numbered sequences for symmetry with
15.2. For completeness alongside the mission and scenario-laboratory protocols already restated
numerically, the GOVERNANCE-IMPACT protocol runs: (1) a rule is proposed or selected for retroactive
review (Part 6.1, 6.6). (2) The twin forks its current state. (3) The proposed constraint edit is
applied to the fork. (4) The scenario engine's constraint-input configuration simulates the
representative mission mix forward. (5) COVERAGE, COLLISION, and COST are each computed against the
unconstrained counterfactual (Part 6.3). (6) The honesty gate applies (Part 11.5). (7) The Impact
Report is released to the rule's governance authority. (8) The governance authority decides, per its
own authority (Part 6.5), whether to promote, revise, or discard the rule. The EXTINCTION protocol
runs in parallel structure: (1) a system or capability is proposed for retirement (Part 7.1, 7.6). (2)
The twin forks. (3) The node or capability edge is removed from the fork. (4) The scenario engine's
structural-input configuration simulates the mission window forward. (5) The orphaned-consumer walk
produces GRACEFUL/HARD classifications (Part 7.3). (6) The honesty gate applies. (7) The
Orphaned-Consumer Report is released to IAS-D1. (8) IAS-D1 decides whether to proceed, phase, or
decline the extinction. Naming both protocols numerically, alongside the mission and laboratory
protocols already specified, closes the symmetry Part 15.3 begins and makes explicit that all six
scenario-engine configurations (Part 12.2) share the identical eight-or-nine-step shape, differing only
in step 3's edit type and step 7's report type.

15.7 What triggers a full re-initialization versus an incremental reconciliation. Part 15.1 names
RETIRED as a lifecycle state reached when "recalibration from scratch is cheaper than incremental
reconciliation," and this Part specifies the decision criterion rather than leaving it as an
unquantified judgment call. A full re-initialization is triggered when the STRUCTURAL reconciliation
(Part 15.4) would need to touch a proportion of the twin's system/relation graph above a stated
threshold in a single reconciliation pass — for instance, a major estate reorganization that merges,
splits, or retires a large fraction of systems simultaneously, as opposed to the steady one-or-two-
system-at-a-time drift Part 10.2 describes as the DEFAULT trajectory. (DERIVED: incremental
reconciliation assumes the twin's existing structural model is a good starting point requiring
targeted correction; when the required correction approaches the size of the model itself, starting
fresh is both cheaper and less error-prone than patching a structure so extensively that patch-order
dependencies and partial-update inconsistencies become their own source of drift, a threshold-based
judgment directly analogous to when a codebase's own maintainers choose a rewrite over an
incremental refactor.) This threshold is itself a governance-simulable parameter (Part VI), consistent
with this dataset's practice of routing its own internal tuning decisions through the same mechanisms
it specifies for the estate at large.

FINAL LAW — PART XV. A twin that recalibrates without versioning has traded a known-bad forecast for
an unknown one, and a twin that reconciles state by polling instead of subscribing has built a second,
shadow observability system it swore in Part XIV.4 not to become; the lifecycle and protocols exist to
make both mistakes structurally difficult to make by accident.

PART XVI — METRICS AND ANTI-GOODHART COUNTERMETRICS
===============================================================================================

16.1 The primary metric — forecast accuracy — and its immediate corruption risk. FORECAST ACCURACY,
DEFINED as the proportion of divergence records (13.4) whose observed value falls within the
forecasted confidence band, is the most obvious metric for twin quality, and it is also the metric
most easily gamed: a twin that simply widens its confidence bands achieves near-perfect accuracy by
predicting almost nothing precisely, exactly the inverse of Part 11.4's suspiciously-narrow-band
warning — a suspiciously wide band is the corrupted version of the same metric. (DERIVED, standard
Goodhart reasoning applied specifically to this dataset's central metric.) The countermetric is BAND
SHARPNESS: the average width of the confidence band relative to the forecasted point estimate, tracked
ALONGSIDE accuracy, so that a twin cannot improve its accuracy score by sacrificing sharpness — the two
metrics must be reported as a PAIR, never singly, and a governance rule (simulable via Part VI's own
mechanism, a deliberately self-referential closing of the loop) could require that band sharpness not
widen beyond a stated bound even as accuracy is optimized.

16.2 The coverage metric and its corruption risk. COVERAGE, DEFINED as the proportion of missions
above the route-before-spend threshold that were actually routed through mission simulation before
dispatch (as opposed to the logged-bypass path of Part 2.5), measures the doctrine's real adoption, not
just its existence as a gate. The corruption risk: a system under time pressure could reclassify
missions downward in size class specifically to duck under the simulation threshold, inflating
apparent coverage of the missions that DO get classified as large while silently reducing how many
missions the doctrine actually protects. The countermetric is THRESHOLD-ADJACENT DENSITY: the
proportion of missions classified just below the route-before-spend threshold, tracked over time — a
sudden increase in threshold-adjacent classifications, without a corresponding real-world shift in
typical mission size, is the observable signature of threshold gaming, the same pattern the estate's
own NANO/MICRO/MACRO/ULTRA cost-routing discipline must guard against independently and which this
dataset borrows the countermetric shape from rather than reinventing.

16.3 The fidelity metric and the calibration-volume corruption risk. DYNAMICS FIDELITY (Part 10.1) is
itself a metric subject to gaming: a twin's dynamics-fidelity score, if computed naively as accuracy
over ALL missions ever simulated, can be inflated by an early, easy calibration period that swamps a
recent degradation with historical volume. The countermetric is a RECENCY-WEIGHTED ROLLING WINDOW,
already implicit in Part 10.1's "rolling window" phrasing but made explicit here as a metric-design
requirement: fidelity must be computed over a bounded recent window (sized to balance statistical
power against responsiveness to real degradation), never over the twin's entire lifetime history,
specifically so that a twin cannot coast on old calibration success while its current dynamics have
silently drifted.

16.4 The strengthening-evidence metric, tied directly to the strengthening matrix's requirement. The
matrix requires, as evidence for IAS-F3's row, "a simulated mission whose predicted path matched/
diverged with reasons." This is operationalized as the SIMULATION VALUE METRIC: for each mission that
passed through route-before-spend simulation, whether the forecast's information changed the
dispatching system's actual decision (a mission rerouted, resequenced, or declined because of what the
forecast showed) versus the forecast simply confirming what would have happened anyway. A twin that is
consulted but never changes a decision is not yet demonstrating strengthening value, however accurate
its forecasts are — accuracy without decision influence is a metric that looks good and does nothing,
the specific corruption this countermetric exists to catch. (DERIVED, directly serving the matrix's own
evidence requirement rather than inventing an unrelated proxy.)

16.5 The composite reporting discipline — metrics always reported as pairs or triples, never alone.
Consistent with the anti-Goodhart pattern established across 16.1-16.4, this Part's NORMATIVE closing
requirement is that no single twin metric is ever reported, in a fidelity dashboard, an Owner briefing,
or a governance-impact report, without its paired countermetric appearing alongside it in the same
surface. (NORMATIVE: a reporting template that permits accuracy without sharpness, coverage without
threshold-adjacent density, or fidelity without window-recency disclosure is itself a specification
defect, per this dataset's own standard, and any later implementation's reporting-layer design (Part
XII.5) must enforce paired display, not merely recommend it.)

16.6 A fifth metric this dataset names for symmetry — propagation-graph precision and its countermetric.
Parts 16.1 through 16.4 pair metrics for forecast accuracy, coverage, fidelity, and strengthening
evidence; risk simulation (Part IV) has not yet had its own metric named. PROPAGATION PRECISION is
DEFINED as the proportion of a PropagationGraph's (13.3) predicted hops that match a subsequently
OBSERVED real incident's actual containment path, when a simulated risk scenario is later validated
against a real occurrence of the same perturbation class (per the retrodiction discipline, Part 17.3,
applied specifically to risk rather than mission forecasts). Its corruption risk mirrors 16.1's exactly:
a propagation graph that predicts EVERY system as a possible hop, at low estimated severity each,
trivially achieves high precision by never being confidently wrong about any single hop's absence. The
countermetric is PROPAGATION SPECIFICITY: the proportion of the ensemble's total systems that a given
PropagationGraph names as touched at all, tracked alongside precision, so that a twin cannot improve
apparent precision by simply predicting broad, low-confidence involvement everywhere — precision and
specificity, like accuracy and sharpness, must be reported as a pair.

16.7 Why anti-Goodhart discipline is a permanent operating cost, not a one-time design decision. This
Part closes the metrics discussion by naming, explicitly, that the pairing discipline established across
16.1 through 16.6 is not a property this dataset's specification GRANTS once and then forgets — it is a
PERMANENT operating cost every future metric this twin's implementation introduces must inherit. (NORMATIVE:
any metric proposed for this twin AFTER this dataset seals — for instance, a future metric on the
counterfactual institution's usage patterns, or on honesty-gate fatigue (Part 11.6) — must be
accompanied by its own named countermetric before it is added to any dashboard or report, following
the same Pre-Report-style discipline the estate's own code-review doctrine already applies to findings:
a metric without a countermetric is a claim without a check, and this dataset extends that discipline
from code review to institutional foresight reporting as a matter of consistent method, not
coincidence.) The five paired metrics this Part specifies (accuracy/sharpness, coverage/threshold-
density, fidelity/recency-window, simulation-value/decision-influence, precision/specificity) are
therefore a FLOOR, not a ceiling — a starting inventory a later implementation is expected to extend
under the same paired discipline as new twin modes mature and accumulate enough operating history to
be measured honestly.

16.8 A consolidated metrics inventory, stated once for reference alongside 14.8's relation inventory.
This dataset specifies five paired metric families: forecast accuracy/band sharpness (16.1), coverage/
threshold-adjacent density (16.2), dynamics fidelity/recency-weighted window (16.3), simulation value/
decision influence (16.4), and propagation precision/specificity (16.6) — ten individual metrics across
five pairs, each pair required to be reported together per 16.5's and 16.7's composite discipline. No
metric in this inventory is specified as sufficient alone; the inventory's completeness is judged by
whether every twin mode this dataset specifies (mission, scenario, risk, capital, governance, extinction,
Monte-Carlo, counterfactual) has at least one paired metric family covering it, which this ten-metric
inventory achieves by design rather than by accident — a coverage property a later implementation's
own reporting-module test suite (Part XXIV's paired-metric reporting module candidate) should verify
empirically rather than assume from this dataset's narrative claim alone.

FINAL LAW — PART XVI. Every metric this dataset defines has a documented way to be gamed and a named
countermetric that catches the gaming; a twin quality report that shows only the primary metric is not
a report, it is the first half of one, and the corpus refuses to treat a half-report as evidence.

PART XVII — EVALUATION AND HOW TO PROVE THE TWIN WORKS
===============================================================================================

17.1 The evaluation problem's peculiar difficulty for a foresight system. Evaluating whether a router
routes correctly, or whether a portfolio allocates well, can be checked against a stated objective
function relatively directly. Evaluating whether a TWIN's forecasts are trustworthy requires waiting
for real outcomes to arrive and comparing — the divergence-record mechanism is, in this precise sense,
the evaluation protocol, not merely a feedback signal, and Part XVII exists to specify how that
mechanism is assembled into a formal evaluation regime rather than left as an ambient practice. (DERIVED:
a foresight system's evaluation is inescapably RETROSPECTIVE and DELAYED — a forecast cannot be graded
until its predicted window has closed — which is a structural property this dataset must design around
rather than wish away.)

17.2 The backtesting protocol — evaluating the twin against history it has never simulated forward. A
BACKTEST is DEFINED as running the twin's dynamics model, calibrated only on data available BEFORE a
historical point in time, forward to predict what actually happened AFTER that point, using real
recorded outcomes as ground truth the twin was blinded to during calibration. This is the single
strongest evaluation method available, because unlike live divergence-record accumulation (which
necessarily evaluates the CURRENT calibration against the FUTURE, taking real time to accrue), a
backtest can evaluate a proposed dynamics-model change against ALREADY-KNOWN history immediately,
compressing the evaluation timeline from months to a single computation. (DERIVED, standard backtesting
methodology applied to this dataset's specific dynamics model; NORMATIVE: any proposed recalibration
per Part 15.5's versioning discipline should be backtested against held-out historical data BEFORE being
promoted to the live calibration version, not merely validated prospectively as live missions
accumulate.)

17.3 The retrodiction test as the twin's strongest available proof, cross-referenced against the
estate's own precedent. The estate's own Post-Ship Value axis finding (project_strategic_gaps_scs_c87,
"a forecast that matched a later real demand — retrodiction on history") already establishes
retrodiction as the estate's accepted evidence standard for a foresight claim — IAS-C2's demand
forecasting was proven, not merely asserted, by checking a past forecast against what later actually
happened. IAS-F3 adopts the identical standard for its own strongest evaluation claim: a mission
forecast, a risk propagation graph, or a governance-impact report is licensed to be cited as
OBSERVED-effective (rather than merely HYPOTHESIZED-effective) only after a retrodiction test — run
against real historical mission data the twin was blinded to at calibration time — confirms the match,
with the specific dimensions and magnitude of any divergence stated honestly rather than rounded away.
(DERIVED, directly reusing a precedent the estate has already validated, per this dataset's own
discipline against re-inventing mechanisms other systems already proved.)

17.4 The evaluation of non-forecast twin modes — scenario, extinction, counterfactual — and their
weaker evidence ceiling. Mission forecasts and risk propagation graphs can be backtested and retrodicted
because real missions and real incidents provide ground truth. Scenario branches (Part III) and
counterfactual institutions (Part IX) evaluate hypothetical structural changes that, by definition,
never actually happened — there is no ground truth to compare a "what if we merged system X and Y"
scenario against, because the merge was never executed. (CONTESTED, resolved NORMATIVELY: these modes'
evidence ceiling is honestly capped at DERIVED/HYPOTHESIZED, never OBSERVED, unless and until a
scenario is actually promoted and executed, at which point it retroactively becomes a mission-forecast-
style backtest opportunity for the NEXT similar scenario. This is not a gap to engineer away; it is
an honest epistemic boundary this dataset names rather than obscures, consistent with Article V's
requirement that a claim's evidence level be stated, not inflated.)

17.5 The evaluation harness's minimum sealing bar for this dataset's own claims. Per this mission's
Article VIII scope (specification, not implementation), IAS-F3 itself cannot be evaluated by running
real backtests — there is no live twin instance yet. This dataset's own claims are therefore honestly
marked at the ceiling Part 17.4 describes for a specification: every load-bearing mechanism claim
(the fork-simulate-compare protocol works, the divergence-record feedback loop improves calibration,
the honesty gate prevents confidence-band loss) is DERIVED from the estate's own analogous, already-
validated precedents (the retrodiction standard, the propose-never-build discipline, the OQS scoring
pattern) rather than claimed as OBSERVED, because nothing in this dataset has been run. A later
implementation mission inherits the specific obligation to run the backtesting and retrodiction
protocols this Part specifies and to upgrade this dataset's DERIVED claims to OBSERVED only where the
evidence actually accrues. (NORMATIVE, honest closing of the evaluation Part.)

17.6 A staged evidence-upgrade protocol, made explicit rather than left as a general principle. Part
17.4 states that a scenario's evidence ceiling rises from DERIVED/HYPOTHESIZED to OBSERVED-eligible
once promoted and built; this Part specifies the STAGES that upgrade must pass through, because
"promoted and built" is not itself sufficient — a built capability must also be USED before its
real-world performance says anything about the original scenario's forecast. The staged protocol: (1)
PROMOTED — the scenario branch's disposition is set (13.2), but no evidence upgrade yet occurs. (2)
BUILT — the recommended structural change is implemented by whatever later mission undertakes it,
still no automatic upgrade, because a built-but-unused capability has not yet generated the outcome
data the original scenario forecasted. (3) OBSERVED-IN-USE — the built capability accumulates enough
real mission history (a minimum sample size, mirroring 10.6's age-awareness discipline) for its
real-world emergent-property trajectory to be compared against the scenario's originally forecasted
trajectory. (4) RETRODICTED — the comparison at stage 3 is formally run per Part 17.3's protocol, and
only at this final stage does the ORIGINAL scenario's forecast earn the right to be cited as
OBSERVED-effective rather than DERIVED. (NORMATIVE: collapsing these four stages into "it got built, so
it worked" is precisely the premature-confidence failure this dataset's evaluation doctrine exists to
prevent, and the four-stage protocol makes the honest waiting period explicit rather than implicit.)

17.7 The evaluation protocol's own dependency on the divergence ledger's integrity. Every evaluation
mechanism this Part specifies — backtesting, retrodiction, the staged evidence-upgrade protocol —
ultimately depends on the DivergenceRecord ledger (13.4, 13.8) being complete and honestly maintained;
an evaluation regime built on top of a ledger that silently drops inconvenient divergence records (a
forecast that missed badly, quietly not logged) would produce evaluation results indistinguishable from
a genuinely well-calibrated twin's results, while being, in fact, evidence of nothing. (DERIVED: this is
the evaluation Part's own version of the honesty-gate discipline Part 11.5 specifies for individual
forecasts — the LEDGER itself needs an integrity guarantee, not just each individual output, and this
dataset specifies that guarantee as a structural one: DivergenceRecord creation (Part 15.2, step 9) is
NOT conditional on the outcome being favorable to the twin's reputation, it fires unconditionally on
every mission that was previously simulated, favorable or not, per Part 13.8's retention discipline,
making selective non-logging a violation of the object model's own specified behavior rather than a
permitted editorial choice.)

17.8 A consolidated evaluation-ceiling table, stated once for reference. Mission forecasts and risk
propagation graphs: backtestable (17.2) and retrodictable (17.3), evidence ceiling OBSERVED once
retrodicted. Governance-impact reports: backtestable against historical rule-collision data where such
data exists, otherwise capped at DERIVED. Extinction orphaned-consumer reports: retrodictable once an
extinction is actually executed and its real downstream effect observed, otherwise DERIVED. Scenario
branches and the counterfactual institution: capped at DERIVED/HYPOTHESIZED per 17.4 until promoted and
lived through the staged protocol of 17.6. This table is offered as the single place a later
implementation, or an Owner reviewing a twin output, can check what evidentiary weight a given output
type is entitled to claim, without re-deriving the ceiling from first principles each time.

FINAL LAW — PART XVII. A foresight system's evaluation is inescapably retrospective, and the honest
response to that constraint is not to claim proof it does not have but to name its exact evidence
ceiling per mode — backtestable and retrodictable for forecasts and risk, honestly capped at
hypothesis for scenarios and counterfactuals until one is actually promoted and lived.

PART XVIII — INTEGRATION WITH C2 (DEMAND FORECASTING)
===============================================================================================

18.1 The foresight loop's second stage, specified precisely. COMPOUNDING_GRAPH names the foresight
loop as "IAS-C2 Demand Forecasting predicts next-needed capabilities → IAS-F3 Digital Twin simulates
whether building them pays off." This Part specifies the interface, not merely restates the diagram: a
DEMAND SIGNAL from IAS-C2 (a predicted future need for a capability the estate does not yet have, or
does not yet have enough of) arrives at IAS-F3 as a proposed STRUCTURAL EDIT — a new system, subsystem,
or capability node to be added to the twin's structural sub-model — and is evaluated through EXACTLY
the architecture scenario laboratory protocol of Part III, with the demand signal supplying the
proposal and IAS-C2's own predicted mission mix (the demand that motivated the forecast in the first
place) supplying the mission window the scenario is simulated against. (DERIVED: this is not a new
twin mode; it is the scenario laboratory's canonical use, sourced from a specific, named upstream
producer rather than an arbitrary architecture proposal — the non-duplication discipline of Part XII.2
holds even at this integration boundary.)

18.2 Why simulating a demand forecast is different from simulating an arbitrary architecture proposal.
An arbitrary scenario (Part III) tests a structural change against a REPRESENTATIVE mission window
drawn generically from IAS-C2's overall demand model. A demand-forecast-sourced scenario tests the
SPECIFIC proposed capability against the SPECIFIC predicted mission mix that generated the demand
signal in the first place — a tighter, more directly relevant test, because the scenario is asking
precisely "does building the thing IAS-C2 says we'll need actually satisfy the missions IAS-C2 predicts
will need it," rather than the more general "does this structural change help across a generic
workload." (DERIVED, a direct specialization of the general mechanism to a narrower, better-evidenced
question.)

18.3 The pays-off verdict and its three components. The scenario's comparison against its counterfactual
(Part 3.3) yields, for a demand-forecast-sourced scenario specifically, a PAYS-OFF VERDICT with three
components: CAPACITY MATCH (does the simulated new capability actually absorb the predicted demand, or
does the predicted mission mix exceed what the new capability's simulated capacity can handle);
CAPITAL COST (per Part V's capital forecasting simulation, run over the same window, banded); and
OPPORTUNITY COST (what the same capital would have produced if allocated to the highest-ranked
alternative in IAS-C1's current portfolio instead — importing IAS-C1's ranking rather than inventing an
independent opportunity-cost calculation, per the non-duplication discipline). A pays-off verdict is
never a single yes/no; it is these three components reported together, letting IAS-C1 make the actual
allocation call with full information rather than a collapsed recommendation.

18.4 Worked example — simulating a forecasted need for a dedicated propagation-pattern library. Consider
a synthetic demand signal from IAS-C2: "predicted rising need, over the next two quarters, for a
reusable library of pre-modeled propagation patterns (Part 4.3's artifact) so that risk simulations
do not need to construct a propagation graph from scratch for every recurring failure class." IAS-F3
simulates the proposed capability (a new subsystem of the risk-simulation configuration) against the
predicted mission mix. The pays-off verdict, HYPOTHESIZED pending real calibration, might show:
CAPACITY MATCH strong (the predicted mission mix is dominated by a small number of recurring failure
classes the library would directly serve); CAPITAL COST modest (a one-time authoring cost against
ongoing marginal reuse); OPPORTUNITY COST low (IAS-C1's current portfolio ranking, per the imported
data, shows no higher-ranked pending alternative competing for the same capital in the relevant
window) — yielding a verdict IAS-C1 can act on with the specific reasoning, not just a green light, made
visible for the Owner or portfolio system to inspect.

18.5 The feedback edge back to IAS-C2 — forecast recalibration from simulated pays-off outcomes. When a
demand-forecast-sourced scenario is eventually promoted and built (per the scenario laboratory's
disposition field, Part 13.2), and the built capability's REAL usage is later observed by IAS-E1, that
real usage becomes a retrodiction test (Part 17.3) not just of IAS-F3's own dynamics model but of
IAS-C2's original demand forecast — closing the loop COMPOUNDING_GRAPH names: "outcome confirms/refutes
the forecast → IAS-C2 recalibrates → tighter forecasts." IAS-F3's specific contribution to that closing
loop is supplying the rehearsed pays-off verdict that made the build decision informed in the first
place, which is the artifact against which the eventual real outcome is most meaningfully compared —
without it, IAS-C2 would only be able to compare its forecast against a raw build-or-not decision, losing
the capacity-match/capital-cost/opportunity-cost decomposition that makes the eventual comparison
diagnostic rather than merely pass/fail.

18.6 What happens when IAS-C2's demand forecast and IAS-F3's pays-off verdict disagree. A worked
tension this Part names explicitly: IAS-C2 predicts a capability will be NEEDED; IAS-F3's pays-off
verdict may nonetheless find CAPACITY MATCH weak (the predicted mission mix, when actually simulated
against the proposed capability's structural shape, does not absorb the demand as cleanly as the
demand signal implied) or OPPORTUNITY COST high (a competing portfolio item would produce more value
for the same capital). This is not a contradiction to resolve in favor of one system over the other —
it is exactly the kind of cross-system tension IAS-A2's Control Bus and, where it escalates, the
Family-A candidate-7 conflict-resolution subengine (folded into IAS-A2 per the ledger) exist to carry
between systems, with IAS-C1 as the final arbiter of the allocation decision (Part 5.5, 14.6). (DERIVED:
IAS-F3's role in this tension is strictly to make the disagreement LEGIBLE — a demand forecast and a
pays-off verdict that quietly diverge without either system's reasoning being visible to the arbiter
would force IAS-C1 to choose blind; the pays-off verdict's three-component structure (18.3) exists
precisely so that WHEN it diverges from the demand signal, the specific component driving the
divergence — weak capacity match, or high opportunity cost, or both — is visible rather than buried in
a single collapsed recommendation.)

18.7 The integration's contribution to IAS-C2's own calibration, beyond the single retrodiction loop
of 18.5. Beyond the per-scenario feedback loop already specified, this Part names an aggregate
contribution: over many demand-forecast-sourced scenarios, a pattern in HOW OFTEN pays-off verdicts
diverge from their originating demand signals — and in which direction, systematically over- or
under-estimating capacity match — becomes its own calibration signal for IAS-C2's forecasting model,
distinct from any single retrodiction. (HYPOTHESIZED: whether such a systematic bias exists in IAS-C2's
demand forecasting is an empirical question this dataset cannot answer at the specification stage, but
the MECHANISM for detecting it — aggregating pays-off-verdict divergence direction across many demand
signals, the same aggregation-over-many-records discipline Part 16.3's fidelity metric already applies
to mission forecasts — is DERIVED directly from mechanisms this dataset has already specified
elsewhere, requiring no new machinery to realize, only a report that aggregates PaysOffVerdict objects
(13.7) by their originating IAS-C2 forecast source over time.)

18.8 Why demand-forecast-sourced scenarios should be prioritized within the laboratory's finite budget.
Given the scenario laboratory's finite run-count and fork-maintenance budget (Part 3.6's proposer
tiering, Part 21.7's isolation-cost concern), this Part specifies that demand-forecast-sourced
scenarios — arriving through the pre-qualified IAS-C2 channel with a named, motivated mission window
already attached — warrant priority scheduling over less-qualified proposals when the laboratory's
capacity is contended, because a demand-forecast-sourced scenario's PAYS-OFF VERDICT feeds directly into
an allocation decision IAS-C1 is actively waiting on, whereas an unscheduled, speculative architecture
proposal has no comparable time pressure. (NORMATIVE: this priority rule is a scheduling policy, not an
admissibility rule — Part 3.6's tiering already governs admissibility; this Part governs ORDER among
admissible proposals when capacity is the binding constraint, a distinction worth keeping separate so
that a low-priority but admissible proposal is delayed, never silently dropped.)

FINAL LAW — PART XVIII. A demand forecast tells the estate what it might need; the twin's specific
contribution is telling the estate, before spending a token to build it, whether the thing it might
need would actually pay for itself against the demand that motivated the forecast — and the pays-off
verdict's three separated components are what let that answer be audited rather than merely trusted.

PART XIX — INTEGRATION WITH G1 (ARCHITECTURE INTELLIGENCE)

19.1 The topology loop's fourth stage, specified precisely. COMPOUNDING_GRAPH's primary loop names
"IAS-D1 outcomes update fitness → IAS-G1 fitness informs topology + complexity-budget decisions →
IAS-C1 allocation improves." IAS-F3's relationship to IAS-G1 is not itself named as a primary-loop

strengthening: IAS-G1 reasons about estate-wide topology, drift, and complexity budget, exactly the
emergent properties Part 3.2's comparison step tracks per scenario branch, and IAS-G1 is the primary

19.2 Complexity budget as a twin-simulable quantity, not merely an IAS-G1-computed one. IAS-G1 owns the
authority to set and enforce a COMPLEXITY BUDGET — a ceiling on how much structural intricacy the
estate's topology may carry before further additions degrade more than they contribute (imported by
reference from IAS-G1's own dataset, not re-derived here). IAS-F3's contribution is that a proposed
structural change's complexity-budget IMPACT can be simulated (as one of the emergent-property
trajectories the scenario laboratory tracks, per Part 3.2) before the change consumes any of the real

19.3 Drift detection's twin-assisted mode. IAS-G1 detects architectural DRIFT — the estate's real
topology diverging from its intended or previously-approved shape — as one of its named capabilities
per the ledger's family-G verdict. IAS-F3's fidelity dashboard (Part X.4) and IAS-G1's drift detection
are DISTINCT mechanisms measuring DISTINCT things — twin fidelity measures how well the TWIN tracks the
REAL estate; architectural drift measures how far the REAL estate has diverged from its INTENDED shape
— but they share infrastructure: both consume IAS-E1's observability telemetry and both benefit from
the same reconciliation cadence discipline Part 15.4 specifies. This dataset names the distinction
explicitly to prevent a natural but incorrect conflation: a well-calibrated twin (high twin fidelity)
can faithfully mirror an estate that has itself drifted badly from its intended architecture (high
architectural drift) — the twin's job is to mirror reality accurately, not to judge whether that
reality is architecturally healthy, which remains IAS-G1's distinct authority.

19.4 Worked example — simulating a topology change proposed to reduce IAS-G1-measured complexity debt.
Consider a synthetic proposal, arising from IAS-G1's own complexity-debt-market mechanism (candidate
68 per the ledger, folded into IAS-G1): "collapse two overlapping observability subsystems, identified
by IAS-G1 as redundant complexity, into one." This is precisely the merge scenario worked example
already given in Part 3.4, now specified as arriving FROM IAS-G1 rather than from an arbitrary
proposer — the integration point is that IAS-G1 is both a valid scenario PROPOSER (per this Part) and
the primary CONSUMER of the resulting recommendation (per Part 14.1), a closed loop where the same
system that identified the complexity debt receives the rehearsed consequence of paying it down before
committing capital to the paydown.

19.5 The boundary this integration must not cross — IAS-F3 never sets or judges the complexity budget
itself. Consistent with every other integration boundary this dataset draws, IAS-F3 simulates the
CONSEQUENCE of a proposed topology change against whatever complexity-budget threshold IAS-G1 currently
enforces (imported as a constraint, per Part 6.1's constraint-edit mechanism applied here to an
architectural rather than a governance-rule context); IAS-F3 never proposes what the complexity budget
SHOULD be, and never judges, independently of IAS-G1's own standard, whether a given topology is
healthy. (NORMATIVE, the ninth restatement across this dataset of the same non-duplication discipline,
repeated deliberately because it is the single most important recurring risk this entire dataset
manages — a foresight system's natural gravitational pull is toward accumulating judgment authority it
was never granted, precisely because it is the system that "sees the whole picture," and every
integration Part in this dataset must name the boundary explicitly rather than let it be assumed.)

19.6 Biodiversity as a second IAS-D1-adjacent quantity the laboratory can simulate alongside complexity
budget. IAS-D1's ecology model names biodiversity — the estate avoiding both monoculture (too many
systems converging on one approach) and chaotic proliferation (too many redundant systems) — as a
tracked emergent property (imported by reference, Part 3.2's trajectory list already names it). This
Part specifies, for symmetry with the complexity-budget integration of 19.2, that a proposed structural
change's BIODIVERSITY impact is equally simulable through the same scenario-laboratory mechanism: a
merge scenario (the worked example of 3.4 and 19.4) predictably DECREASES biodiversity by construction
(two approaches become one), and the laboratory's comparison step (3.2) should report this decrease
alongside the complexity-budget decrease, letting IAS-D1 weigh a proposal's biodiversity cost against
its complexity-budget benefit rather than seeing only the latter. (DERIVED: reporting complexity budget
without biodiversity would bias every laboratory-mediated proposal toward consolidation, because
consolidation near-always improves complexity budget while its biodiversity cost is exactly the kind of
second-order effect a narrow report would omit — the same omission risk Part 4.6 names for severity and
Part 5.6 names for capital currency, here recurring a fourth time as this dataset's most persistent
structural concern: a single favorable number hiding an unfavorable one the laboratory's own trajectory
tracking already computes but a careless report might not surface.)

19.7 IAS-G1's authority over WHICH emergent properties matter for a given decision, restated as a
boundary. Sections 19.2 and 19.6 both specify that the twin CAN simulate multiple emergent-property
trajectories (complexity budget, biodiversity, and by extension any property IAS-F1's Part IV state
model names); this Part closes the integration by restating, once more, that WEIGHING those properties
against each other for a specific decision — is a complexity-budget improvement worth a biodiversity
cost, in this specific case — remains IAS-G1's authority, never IAS-F3's. (NORMATIVE, the tenth
restatement of this dataset's central non-duplication discipline counting the DRK-integration
restatement in Part XX, underscoring that even as this dataset's integration Parts grow richer with
each additional simulable property, the boundary discipline scales WITH the richness rather than eroding
under it — a twin that simulates more dimensions is not thereby licensed to weigh more dimensions; it
is licensed only to REPORT more dimensions, with the weighing remaining exactly as far outside its
authority as it was when only complexity budget was in scope.)

19.8 Why this integration Part is shorter on worked mechanism than Parts XVIII and XX, stated honestly.
Unlike IAS-C2 (Part XVIII, whose demand-signal interface this dataset specifies down to the object
level in 13.7) and DRK (Part XX, whose handoff is specified as a precise triggering condition in 20.2),
IAS-G1 itself is, per the ledger, still a maturing dataset within this same corpus (family-G verdict,
BUILD+MERGE) rather than a long-established parent this dataset can cite OBSERVED behavior from. This
Part's integration is therefore honestly marked at a slightly lower specification density than its
siblings — the boundary (19.5, 19.7) is firm and NORMATIVE, but the precise object-level interface (an
IAS-G1-side equivalent of DemandSignal, 13.7) is left for IAS-G1's own dataset to specify on its side of
the boundary, consistent with this dataset's practice of specifying only what IAS-F3 itself owns and
importing the rest by reference rather than presuming to draft another dataset's own object model on
its behalf.

FINAL LAW — PART XIX. A twin that simulates complexity-budget impact is strengthening IAS-G1's
judgment with rehearsed evidence; a twin that starts setting the complexity budget itself has stopped
strengthening IAS-G1 and started replacing it, and the difference between the two is enforced by
naming the boundary in every Part where the temptation to cross it is highest.

PART XX — INTEGRATION WITH DRK AND THE BLAST-RADIUS BOUNDARY RESTATED
===============================================================================================

20.1 Restating the boundary with the full architecture now specified. Part I.4 stated the DRK boundary
as the dataset's founding distinction; this Part restates it with the complete mechanism now
available, because the boundary is precise enough, and important enough, to deserve a full
architectural treatment rather than resting on Part I's earlier, necessarily abbreviated statement.
DRK computes blast radius for ONE decision by walking the dependency graph outward from that decision
to a bounded depth, returning which systems the decision touches and with what estimated severity — a
DECISION-LOCAL, GRAPH-WALK computation, fast, precise, and scoped to exactly the decision in front of
it. IAS-F3's risk simulation (Part IV) is FEDERATION-SCOPED: it runs the full dynamics model forward
under a perturbation, across a representative mission window, producing a propagation graph that
reflects not just direct dependency edges but SECOND-ORDER effects the dynamics model captures (a
system degraded by the perturbation subsequently degrading its OWN consumers in a way a single-hop
dependency walk would not reveal without recursing the walk itself).

20.2 The precise handoff — when DRK reaches beyond its own walk depth. DRK's dependency-graph walk is
bounded (a design choice DRK's own dataset specifies and IAS-F3 does not alter) to keep blast-radius
computation fast enough for per-decision use. When DRK's walk reaches its depth bound without
terminating — the touched-system set is still growing at the boundary, suggesting the decision's real
consequence extends further than DRK's bounded walk can see — DRK is specified (per Part 14.1) to hand
the decision off to IAS-F3's risk simulation for a deeper, dynamics-model-based propagation trace,
receiving back a PropagationGraph object (13.3) that extends the blast-radius picture beyond DRK's own
walk depth. This handoff is the precise, narrow mechanism by which IAS-F3 "extends DRK's blast-radius
reasoning" per this dataset's own header claim, specified concretely rather than left as a slogan.

20.3 Why DRK does not simply adopt IAS-F3's mechanism wholesale. A reasonable question this Part
answers directly: if IAS-F3's federation-scoped risk simulation is more thorough, why does DRK retain
its own bounded, decision-local walk at all, rather than always deferring to the twin? The answer is
COST and LATENCY: DRK's walk is fast enough to run on every decision, including NANO and MICRO-class
ones where a full Monte-Carlo risk simulation (Part VIII.3's run-count policy) would be prohibitively
expensive relative to the decision's own size. (DERIVED, directly applying HR-COST-003's default-to-
cheap discipline to this specific architectural choice.) DRK's bounded walk is the correct default for
the overwhelming majority of decisions; IAS-F3's deeper simulation is the correct ESCALATION for the
minority whose blast radius genuinely exceeds what a bounded walk can safely characterize — exactly
the same threshold-based escalation pattern this dataset applies to its own route-before-spend gate
(Part 2.2) and Monte-Carlo run count (Part 8.3), a consistent design philosophy restated at this
integration boundary rather than a special case invented for DRK alone.

20.4 Worked example — a decision whose DRK walk hits its depth bound. Consider a synthetic decision:
"retire the legacy session-resilience fallback path now that the newer recovery mechanism has been in
OBSERVED stable operation for two quarters." DRK's bounded walk finds three directly dependent
consumers, then a further five second-hop consumers, and at its configured depth bound the touched-
system count is still growing — a signature DRK's own dataset specifies as the escalation trigger.
IAS-F3's risk simulation, invoked per Part 20.2's handoff, runs the perturbation (the fallback path's
removal) through the full dynamics model and returns a PropagationGraph showing the consequence
terminates two hops further out, contained by IAS-E2's graceful-degradation absorption at a specific
named system, with an estimated aggregate severity the twin's Monte-Carlo subengine bands at a stated
confidence. DRK's final blast-radius report to the decision's owner now incorporates the extended
trace, correctly attributed as twin-simulated beyond DRK's own walk depth rather than presented as if
DRK itself had computed it. (HYPOTHESIZED specific numeric example; DERIVED handoff mechanism.)

20.5 The boundary's enforcement — IAS-F3 never initiates a blast-radius computation DRK has not
requested. Consistent with every other boundary this dataset enforces, the DRK integration is
ONE-DIRECTIONAL and REQUEST-DRIVEN: IAS-F3's risk simulation does not proactively second-guess or
re-run every DRK computation federation-wide (which would be both prohibitively expensive per Part
20.3's cost reasoning and a quiet arrogation of DRK's own decision-local authority); it activates only
on DRK's own explicit handoff when DRK's bounded walk signals it has reached its limit. (NORMATIVE, the
tenth and final explicit restatement of this dataset's central non-duplication discipline, closing the
integration Parts with the same law that opened Part I.)

20.6 A converse handoff this dataset names but does not specify in full — IAS-F3 escalating TO DRK.
Sections 20.1 through 20.5 specify the handoff in one direction: DRK escalates to IAS-F3 when its
bounded walk hits its depth limit. This Part names the converse case for completeness: a federation-
scoped risk simulation (Part IV), run for an unrelated purpose (say, a governance-impact review's
COLLISION check, Part 6.3), may INCIDENTALLY surface a propagation path implicating a specific pending
DECISION that DRK has not yet been asked to evaluate. In this converse case, IAS-F3 does not autonomously
act on the finding — consistent with the request-driven discipline of 20.5 — but surfaces it as an
ADVISORY signal to DRK's own intake, exactly as any other twin output reaches its decision-owning
consumer only as information (Part 14.3): DRK, on receiving the signal, may CHOOSE to run its own
bounded walk on the newly-implicated decision, but the choice to formally evaluate remains DRK's, not
IAS-F3's. (NORMATIVE: this converse path is intentionally thinner than the primary DRK-to-IAS-F3 handoff
— it is a signal, not a computation handoff, because IAS-F3 initiating a DRK-authority computation on
DRK's behalf would cross exactly the boundary Part 20.5 draws, merely from the opposite direction.)

20.7 A summary comparison table, stated in prose, of DRK versus IAS-F3 across the dimensions this
dataset has specified. Scope: DRK is decision-local; IAS-F3's risk simulation is federation-scoped.
Cost: DRK's walk is cheap enough to run on every decision; IAS-F3's simulation is reserved for the
minority whose blast radius exceeds a bounded walk's safe characterization. Trigger: DRK runs
proactively on every decision by its own design; IAS-F3's risk simulation, in this integration, runs
only on DRK's explicit request (20.2) or as a component of another twin mode (governance-impact,
extinction) that happens to surface a decision-relevant signal (20.6). Authority: both are strictly
advisory to their respective decision-owning consumers, never binding. Evidence ceiling: DRK's
bounded-walk output is treated, throughout the estate, as sufficiently well-understood to need no
separate backtesting regime of its own; IAS-F3's federation-scoped output is subject to the full
evaluation discipline of Part XVII precisely because its dynamics-model-based reasoning is less
directly traceable than a graph walk and therefore requires the heavier evidentiary apparatus this
dataset specifies. This comparison, stated together in one place, is offered as a consolidated
reference for any later implementation deciding, at the point of building both systems' interfaces,
exactly where the DRK-owned surface ends and the IAS-F3-owned surface begins.

20.8 A closing note on why the DRK integration receives the most detailed treatment of the three named
parents. Of arch-decision, one_shot, and DRK, this dataset gives DRK's boundary the deepest specification
(a full restatement Part, a converse-handoff subsection, a comparison table) because DRK's blast-radius
computation is the parent whose OBJECT — decision consequence — is closest in shape to IAS-F3's own
object, making the two easiest to conflate by accident and therefore the pair most in need of an
explicit, heavily-worked boundary. arch-decision's object (one architectural choice) and one_shot's
object (one contract's feasibility) are each further from IAS-F3's federation-scale object and
correspondingly less prone to accidental duplication, which is reflected honestly in the relative depth
this dataset invests in each integration Part rather than applying uniform treatment regardless of risk.

FINAL LAW — PART XX. DRK's bounded walk is the correct default and IAS-F3's federation-scoped
simulation is the correct escalation, never the correct replacement; the handoff fires exactly once,
exactly when DRK's own walk signals its limit, and never before.

PART XXI — OPEN QUESTIONS AND EVOLUTION
===============================================================================================

21.1 UNRESOLVED — who operates the twin's calibration. Part X.4 named this gap directly: the fidelity
dashboard has consumers (DRK, IAS-C1, arch-decision) but this dataset does not staff a single
accountable owner for maintaining calibration quality, triggering model-structure reviews (Part 11.4),
or deciding when a twin instance should be RETIRED (Part 15.1) rather than reconciled. Candidate
answers include: a rotating responsibility among the decision-owning consumers, a dedicated subsystem
of IAS-E1 (extending observability to include twin-health observability), or an explicit Owner-held
role analogous to the rotation-authority reservation the estate already applies to secret handling.
This dataset takes no NORMATIVE position and names the gap as UNRESOLVED rather than defaulting
silently to an unstated assumption.

21.2 UNRESOLVED — perturbation sourcing for risk simulation beyond federated fingerprints. Part 4.5
named the honest limit that risk simulation propagates given perturbations rather than discovering
novel ones, and took a NORMATIVE (not merely descriptive) position against autonomous perturbation
generation for this dataset's initial scope. Whether a LATER, more mature twin instance — with enough
accumulated divergence-record history to ground autonomous perturbation generation in OBSERVED pattern
rather than invented narrative — should relax that restriction is explicitly left UNRESOLVED, to be
revisited only with the evidence threshold Article IX requires for any self-evolution proposal, not
assumed as a natural next step.

21.3 CONTESTED — whether the counterfactual institution's calibration problem (Part 9.3) is tractable
at all for path-dependent structural questions. This dataset took the position that some counterfactual
questions are answerable and some are not, and that the mode must report which category applies rather
than force an answer. An unresolved tension remains: the boundary between "answerable" and "not" is
itself judged by the twin's own dynamics-fidelity confidence, creating a mild circularity (a
low-fidelity twin might under-report its own counterfactual reach, being falsely conservative, or a
falsely-confident twin might over-report it) that this dataset flags as CONTESTED rather than resolved,
because resolving it convincingly would require exactly the kind of empirical evidence Article V
reserves for OBSERVED claims, which does not yet exist for a mode this dataset specifies but a later
mission has not yet implemented.

21.4 HYPOTHESIZED evolution path — twin-of-twins for multi-project federations. This dataset scopes
the twin to the single-estate federation IAS-F1 bounds (Part 1.4's ensemble boundary). The estate's own
Immune System dataset (IAS-D2) already establishes that failure fingerprints federate ACROSS projects,
not just within one; a HYPOTHESIZED future evolution of IAS-F3, explicitly out of this dataset's own
scope and not specified in detail here, would extend the twin to model MULTIPLE related-but-distinct
project federations (the pattern the estate's own multi-repo, multi-pane operating reality already
exhibits) and simulate cross-project propagation the way IAS-D2 already tracks cross-project failure
fingerprints. This is named as a HYPOTHESIZED direction, explicitly not committed to, because Article
II's ensemble-object boundary would need to be re-examined for a multi-federation twin in a way this
dataset has not done the work to specify honestly.

21.5 The self-application question — should the twin simulate the CPP-IAS corpus's own authoring
process. A final, deliberately provocative open question this dataset names rather than resolves: the
IAS corpus itself is a mission, subject in principle to the same mission-simulation mechanism (Part
II) this dataset specifies for any other estate mission — a future authoring wave of a sibling IAS
dataset could, in principle, be route-before-spend gated through IAS-F3 before the authoring session
launches. This dataset does not claim that reflexive application, because IAS-F3 does not yet exist as
a live twin instance to apply (per Article VIII's specification-only scope), but names it as the
clearest illustration of what "simulating the federation before spending" would mean applied to the
very corpus that specified the capability, and leaves it, honestly, UNRESOLVED until a live
implementation exists to test it against.

21.6 UNRESOLVED — the many-to-one aggregation logic at the IAS-C1 boundary (Part 14.6) is named but not
specified. This dataset states that IAS-C1 aggregates many twin outputs into a portfolio-level view and
that the aggregation logic belongs to IAS-C1, not IAS-F3, but does not — and, per its own non-duplication
discipline, should not — specify HOW that aggregation should weigh capital trajectories against
pays-off verdicts against a portfolio's existing holdings. This is named as an open interface question
for IAS-C1's own dataset to resolve, not a gap in IAS-F3's specification, but flagged here so that a
later implementation reading both datasets together does not mistake the silence for an oversight
rather than a deliberate boundary.

21.7 CONTESTED — whether a twin instance should ever be shared across multiple concurrent scenario
proposals rather than forking per-proposal. Part 3.2's fork mechanism assumes each scenario gets its own
isolated branch, but at high proposal volume (a busy quarter with many concurrent architecture
proposals), maintaining many simultaneous forks has a capital cost of its own (Part V, applied
reflexively to the twin's own operation as in 8.7). One position holds that forks should be pooled or
batched — running several proposals' simulations against a shared, briefly-held fork before discarding
it — trading some proposal isolation for lower aggregate cost; the opposing position, adopted here as
the dataset's NORMATIVE default absent further evidence, holds that isolation is worth its cost because
a shared fork risks one proposal's simulated side effects contaminating another's baseline — an
epistemic contamination risk this dataset treats as more dangerous than the capital saved, consistent
with its general preference (Part 9.6, Part 17.7) for evidentiary cleanliness over efficiency wherever
the two are in tension. This is named CONTESTED rather than settled because the capital cost of
isolation at genuinely high proposal volume has not been estimated with any real data, and a future
mission with such data may reasonably revisit the default.

21.8 The relationship between this dataset's open questions and the estate's own epistemic-ladder
discipline. Several of the open questions this Part names — steward accountability (21.1), perturbation
sourcing (21.2), counterfactual tractability (21.3) — share a common shape: each names a mechanism this
dataset specifies at DERIVED or NORMATIVE confidence that could, in principle, be promoted to a more
settled status once real operating evidence accrues, mirroring the estate's own epistemic-ladder
discipline (ACIS's promotion chain from deposit through proof to law) applied here to a single dataset's
internal open questions rather than to a cross-dataset theorem. (NORMATIVE: this dataset does not itself
claim any of its open questions are close to resolution — naming them at this stage is exactly the
deposit-level act the ladder's early rungs describe, and this dataset explicitly does not claim the
later rungs, consistent with Article V's prohibition on inflating a claim's evidence level beyond what
this specification-stage mission can support.)

21.9 A consolidated open-questions inventory, stated once for reference. This dataset names eight open
items across this Part: steward accountability (21.1), perturbation sourcing (21.2), counterfactual
tractability (21.3), twin-of-twins evolution (21.4), self-application (21.5), the IAS-C1 aggregation
interface (21.6), fork-sharing versus fork-isolation at high proposal volume (21.7), and the ladder
framing connecting all of them (21.8). None is treated as blocking this dataset's sealing — Article VI's
depth requirement and Article XI's sealing gate concern completeness of SPECIFICATION, not resolution of
every open question a rigorous specification surfaces, and a dataset that manufactured false closure on
any of these eight items to appear more finished would violate Article V more seriously than honestly
leaving them open.

FINAL LAW — PART XXI. A dataset that resolves every question it raises has stopped being honest about
the limits of specification-stage reasoning; naming who operates the twin, how far its counterfactual
reach honestly extends, and whether it should ever simulate its own creators is more valuable than
inventing confident answers this stage of the mission has no evidence to support.

PART XXII — ADVERSARIAL CASES AND EDGE CONDITIONS
===============================================================================================

22.1 Adversarial case — a proposer gaming the route-before-spend threshold by mission-splitting.
Consider an adversarial (not necessarily malicious, possibly just incentive-driven) actor who wants to
avoid the friction of mission simulation and splits a single MACRO-class mission into several
NANO-class sub-missions, each individually below the route-before-spend threshold (Part 2.2), that
collectively achieve the same federation-wide effect the original MACRO mission would have. (DERIVED:
this is a structural vulnerability of any threshold-based gate, directly analogous to how a spending
policy with a per-transaction limit can be defeated by splitting one large transaction into several
small ones.) The mitigation this dataset specifies: the route-before-spend threshold check considers
not only a single mission's individual estimated cost but a ROLLING AGGREGATE of related missions from
the same proposer within a bounded time window, flagging aggregate crossings of the threshold even
when no individual mission crosses it alone — a mechanism directly analogous to the scope-deviation
check one_shot already applies to file-touch scope, applied here to cost rather than files. (NORMATIVE
mitigation, EXPERIMENTAL: the "related missions" grouping heuristic itself carries uncertainty and is
flagged as requiring its own calibration once implemented.)

22.2 Adversarial case — a scenario proposer engineering a favorable mission window to bias comparison.
Because scenario evaluation (Part 3.3) compares a branch against a counterfactual over a chosen mission
window, a proposer motivated to see their preferred scenario promoted could, in principle, select or
influence the mission window to favor their proposal's strengths and avoid its weaknesses. (DERIVED,
CONTESTED how likely this is in practice given the estate's current single-Owner governance structure,
but named regardless because Article V requires naming a failure mode even when its current likelihood
is low.) The mitigation: the mission window for scenario comparison is sourced from IAS-C2's demand
forecast (Part 18.1's canonical use) or from a FIXED, versioned representative-mission-window artifact
maintained independently of any single scenario proposal, never hand-picked per-scenario by the
proposer, closing the manipulation surface at the data-sourcing layer rather than relying on manual
review to catch a biased window after the fact.

22.3 Edge condition — simulating a mission that would itself modify the twin. A structurally unusual
edge case: what happens when the mission being simulated is itself a proposal to modify IAS-F3 (a
recalibration, a twin-instance retirement per Part 15.1, an extension of the twin's own scope)? This is
DEFINED as a SELF-REFERENTIAL SIMULATION, and it is handled by a strict rule: the twin simulating a
change to itself uses a FORKED COPY of its own current state (identical mechanism to Part 3.2's
scenario fork), never the live twin instance, because allowing a self-referential simulation to run
against and potentially mutate the live instance mid-simulation would create exactly the kind of
uncontrolled self-modification Article IX forbids. (NORMATIVE, CRITICAL: self-referential simulation is
permitted and specified, but strictly sandboxed to a fork, with promotion to the live instance requiring
the same Owner-mediated evidence-threshold-validation-versioning-rollback discipline Part 15.5 already
specifies for ordinary recalibration.)

22.4 Edge condition — a mission forecast whose confidence band is degraded before the mission even
begins. Part XI.5's honesty gate specifies DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE labeling for forecasts released under a
STALE or DEGRADED fidelity dashboard state. The edge condition this Part names: what should the
dispatching system (one_shot, IAS-A1) do when a MACRO-or-larger mission's simulation returns a
DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE forecast — proceed with a known-unreliable rehearsal, delay the mission until
fidelity recovers, or fall back to a coarser, non-twin estimate (a size-class heuristic without twin
involvement)? This dataset does NOT resolve this operational question with a single mandated answer,
because the correct choice depends on the mission's own urgency, which only the dispatching system can
weigh (per Part 14.2's boundary that the twin does not own consumer decision logic); it specifies only
that the DEGRADED-CONFIDENCE label must be visible and unambiguous at the point of that decision, so
the dispatching system is choosing with full information rather than unknowingly trusting a compromised
forecast.

22.5 Edge condition — irreducible novelty: a mission type the dynamics model has zero calibration
history for. When a proposed mission's type has no prior divergence records at all (a genuinely novel
mission class, distinct from anything the dynamics model has been calibrated against), Monte-Carlo
simulation (Part VIII) can still run, but its output is definitionally EPISTEMIC-uncertainty-dominated
(Part 8.2's second uncertainty source) rather than aleatoric, and the resulting confidence band should
be WIDE by construction, not narrow — a narrow band on a genuinely novel mission type would itself
trigger Part 11.4's suspiciously-perfect-twin warning, and this Part names the expected, healthy
behavior explicitly so it is not mistaken for a defect: a twin correctly reporting high uncertainty on
something it has never seen is the twin working as specified, not failing.

22.6 Adversarial case — sponsor identity spoofing at the scenario-laboratory intake. Section 3.6 admits
proposals from named-system sponsors or the Owner; this Part names the adversarial concern that a
proposal claiming to originate from a named-system channel (say, appearing to arrive via IAS-C2's
demand-forecast path) could, through a malfunctioning or compromised integration, misrepresent its true
sponsor, letting a proposal skip the Owner-initiated tier's explicit sponsorship requirement. (DERIVED,
CONTESTED how likely this is given the estate's current single-Owner, single-integration-surface
architecture, but named per Article V's requirement to name failure modes regardless of current
likelihood.) The mitigation: every ScenarioProposal object (13.6) carries its sponsor identity as a
field resolved against IAS-F1's system object model (Part II.1's addressability criterion), not as a
free-text claim, so that "this proposal came from IAS-C2" is a structurally verifiable assertion — the
proposal's provenance is checked against the actual channel it arrived through, the same provenance
discipline Part 13.1 already applies to MissionForecast calibration-version attribution, reused here for
sponsor attribution.

22.7 Adversarial case — divergence-ledger poisoning through repeated low-stakes missions. A subtler
threat than 22.1's threshold-splitting: an actor could deliberately simulate and execute many low-stakes,
easily-predictable missions specifically to inflate the twin's apparent dynamics fidelity (Part 10.1)
with a flood of easy successes, diluting the rolling window (Part 16.3's countermetric) with volume
rather than genuinely improving calibration on the harder mission classes that actually determine
trustworthiness. (DERIVED: this is a volume-based variant of the suspiciously-perfect-twin risk (Part
11.4), now framed as an adversarial rather than an incidental failure mode.) The mitigation: the
recency-weighted rolling window (16.3) already partially addresses volume dilution by bounding the
window size, but this Part adds a STRATIFICATION requirement — fidelity should be reported not only in
aggregate but broken down by mission-class difficulty (a proxy derivable from the mission's own
predicted confidence level, Part 2.1), so that a flood of easy missions cannot mask stagnant or
degrading performance on the hard tail the estate actually depends on the twin to handle well.

22.8 Edge condition — a scenario laboratory proposal that, once simulated, reveals itself to BE an
extinction proposal in disguise. Occasionally a structural-merge proposal (Part III's canonical use)
simulated through the laboratory will reveal, once its trajectory is compared against the counterfactual,
that the "merged" system's distinct capabilities were in practice never used independently — the merge
is, functionally, an extinction of the absorbed system's independent identity rather than a true
combination of two live capabilities. This Part specifies that such a finding should be RECLASSIFIED,
at the reporting layer, from a scenario recommendation into an extinction recommendation (routed to
IAS-D1 per Part VII rather than to arch-decision alone per Part III), because the orphaned-consumer
discipline (7.2) — checking whether anything depended on the absorbed system's INDEPENDENT existence —
is the more rigorous and more appropriate lens for a proposal that turns out, on simulation, to be a
retirement rather than a genuine combination. (DERIVED: this reclassification rule prevents a
structural change that is really an extinction from bypassing extinction simulation's stronger
consumer-protection discipline merely because it was PROPOSED using architecture-scenario language.)

FINAL LAW — PART XXII. Every gate this dataset specifies has an adversarial or degenerate case that
defeats a naive implementation — threshold-splitting, window-gaming, self-reference, degraded honesty,
irreducible novelty — and naming each one at specification time is what lets a later implementation
close them by design rather than discover them in production the way the estate's own router-digest
mismatch was discovered.

DECISION LOG

Decision 1 — Scope the twin to route-before-spend at the FEDERATION level, not add a seventh
decision-local blast-radius tool. Alternatives considered: (a) build a faster, deeper version of DRK's
own bounded walk; (b) build a twin scoped to one project rather than the whole estate. Why this: the
ledger's verdict for candidates 101-110 is explicit that the unowned object is "the whole federation,"
and a project-scoped or decision-local twin would duplicate DRK or a resource-level system. Trade-offs:
federation scope means the twin is more expensive to build and calibrate than a narrower tool would
have been. Reversal conditions: if, after implementation, federation-scope calibration proves
intractable (Part 21.3's CONTESTED tractability concern resolving negatively), the scope could be
narrowed to the architecture-and-capital domains only, dropping governance-impact and counterfactual
modes as too speculative to sustain.

Decision 2 — Specify six twin modes as configurations of ONE scenario engine rather than six separate
engines. Alternatives considered: (a) six independently specified engines, one per mode, matching the
ledger's ten absorbed candidates more literally; (b) two engines split by whether the edit is
structural or non-structural. Why this: Part 12.2's reflexive-non-duplication reasoning — a twin that
builds six near-identical pipelines has failed its own founding law. Trade-offs: a shared engine is
architecturally more elegant but requires the engine's parameterization to be genuinely general enough
to cover risk-perturbation, governance-constraint, and structural edits uniformly, a harder design
problem than six bespoke pipelines. Reversal conditions: if a later implementation finds the shared
engine cannot cleanly parameterize across all six modes without leaking mode-specific logic back into
the shared core, splitting the counterfactual and Monte-Carlo pieces out (already partially done per
Part 12.3-12.4) further into a genuinely mode-specific engine would be the correct reversal.

Decision 3 — Make the honesty gate (Part 11.5) a mandatory reporting-layer stage, not an optional
consumer-side check. Alternatives considered: (a) publish fidelity and confidence-band data
separately and trust consumers to check it; (b) make honesty-gate checking a recommended best practice
in documentation only. Why this: Part XI's central risk analysis — simulation-trusted-as-reality's
signature is precisely a dropped confidence band, and an optional check is exactly the kind of
optional discipline that erodes under real deadline pressure, an erosion pattern the estate's own
governance history already exhibits elsewhere. Trade-offs: a mandatory gate adds latency and a single
architectural chokepoint every twin output must pass through. Reversal conditions: none stated — this
decision is treated as a CRITICAL, non-negotiable boundary per Part 11.5's own marking, not subject to
ordinary cost-benefit reversal.

Decision 4 — Give DRK a request-driven handoff to IAS-F3 rather than having IAS-F3 proactively monitor
every DRK decision. Alternatives considered: (a) IAS-F3 shadows every DRK computation and flags
discrepancies; (b) merge DRK's walk into IAS-F3 entirely, retiring DRK's bounded-walk mechanism.
Why this: Part 20.3's cost-and-latency reasoning, directly applying the estate's default-to-cheap
discipline to this specific integration; shadowing every DRK call would be prohibitively expensive for
the low-value majority of decisions, and merging would violate the explicit ledger boundary between
decision-local and federation-scoped objects. Trade-offs: a request-driven handoff means DRK's own
depth-bound tuning directly determines how often the more expensive federation-scoped simulation is
invoked, coupling two systems' internal thresholds. Reversal conditions: if DRK's depth-bound proves
miscalibrated in a way that under- or over-triggers the handoff, the fix belongs in DRK's own threshold
tuning, not in loosening IAS-F3's request-driven boundary.

Decision 5 — Cap the counterfactual institution's invocation frequency explicitly at the architecture
level (Part 12.4), not merely as prose guidance. Alternatives considered: (a) leave invocation
frequency as an operational norm without a hard architectural ceiling; (b) omit the counterfactual
institution mode entirely, given its calibration difficulty (Part 9.3). Why this: the mode's resource
intensity (Part 9.5) combined with its speculative evidence ceiling (Part 17.4) makes it the dataset's
highest-cost, lowest-certainty capability, and a norm-only restraint has a documented estate-wide
failure pattern (governance eroding under deadline pressure) this dataset should not repeat for its
most expensive mode. Trade-offs: an architectural ceiling may occasionally block a genuinely valuable
counterfactual question during a quarter that has already exhausted its invocation budget. Reversal
conditions: if retrospective review shows the ceiling was set too conservatively (valuable
counterfactual questions routinely blocked), the per-quarter count can be raised via the same
governance-impact simulation mechanism (Part VI) this dataset specifies for any other constraint
change — a self-referential but architecturally sound path.

GAP META-ANALYSIS

Gap closed: prior to this dataset, no system in the estate held a maintained, forward-runnable model of
the ensemble as a whole; every foresight-adjacent capability (DRK, one_shot's feasibility check,
arch-decision's reasoning) operated at decision-local or contract-local scope, and the estate's only
mechanism for learning federation-wide consequence was living through it, as Part 1.1's three cited
precedents (Post-Ship Value, the owner-queue invisibility finding, the DRK precedent-length-bias
finding) each independently demonstrate. Systems strengthened: arch-decision and its estate-wide
successor IAS-G1 gain a rehearsal capability for topology and complexity-budget decisions (Parts III,
XIX); one_shot gains a federation-scoped feasibility signal to feed its contract-compile step (Parts
II, XIV); DRK gains an escalation path beyond its own bounded walk depth for the minority of decisions
whose blast radius exceeds it (Part XX); CO-08's parallel-capacity envelope becomes a variable the
twin's capital and mission sub-models can stress-test rather than a static, unexamined ceiling (Parts
II.4, V, VIII.4). Duplications avoided: the dataset explicitly refuses to become a second observability
system (Part 14.4, consuming IAS-E1 rather than re-scanning), a second architecture authority (Part
3.5, III.5), a second governance authority (Part 6.5), a second portfolio allocator (Part 5.5), or a
replacement for DRK's decision-local computation (Part 20.3) — five distinct non-duplication boundaries
actively enforced, not merely claimed. New capabilities: route-before-spend mission gating; the
architecture scenario laboratory; risk propagation graphs; capital trajectory forecasting with
governance-linked infeasibility flagging; governance-impact reports with mandatory coverage/collision/
cost sections; extinction orphaned-consumer reports with graceful/hard classification; institutional
Monte-Carlo as a shared uncertainty engine; the counterfactual institution as a rare, gated learning
tool. Open risks: the steward-accountability gap (21.1), the perturbation-sourcing limit (21.2), the
counterfactual tractability question (21.3), and — the risk this dataset treats as most consequential —
that a twin built to prevent overconfidence could itself become a new source of overconfidence if the
honesty gate (11.5) is implemented weakly or bypassed under the same deadline pressure that erodes
governance elsewhere in the estate.

ANTI-PATTERNS

Anti-pattern 1 — treating a twin forecast as ground truth because it came from "the simulation" rather
than from a human guess. Prevention: the honesty gate (11.5) and the mandatory paired-metric reporting
discipline (16.5) exist specifically to keep a forecast's uncertainty visible at every hop; any
implementation that allows a reporting surface to display a bare point estimate without its band has
reintroduced this anti-pattern regardless of how sophisticated the underlying simulation is.

Anti-pattern 2 — letting the twin's scenario or governance-impact modes accumulate implicit decision
authority because they are "the system that sees the whole picture." Prevention: every integration Part
(III.5, V.5, VI.5, XIX.5, XX.5) names the specific boundary and the specific consumer that retains
authority; a review of any later implementation should treat a change that grants the twin a live-apply
or auto-promote path as a CRITICAL finding regardless of how narrow the apply-path's initial scope
claims to be, because Part 22.3's self-referential-simulation rule shows how easily a sandboxed
capability can be proposed to graduate to live authority.

Anti-pattern 3 — building six bespoke simulation pipelines instead of one parameterized scenario engine,
then discovering the duplication only after all six have independently drifted in behavior. Prevention:
Part XII's architecture specification is written precisely to prevent this at design time; a later
implementation that finds itself writing mode-specific fork/simulate/compare logic six separate times
should treat that as a signal it has deviated from this dataset's specified architecture, not as a
reasonable engineering shortcut, and should return to Part 12.2's shared-engine parameterization before
proceeding.

FUSION SCORE

Systems strengthened: four named directly in the strengthening matrix (arch-decision, one_shot, DRK,
CO-08), with a fifth and sixth (IAS-E1 as telemetry source, IAS-A2 as state-synchronization transport)
strengthened as consumed infrastructure rather than as forecast recipients, and a seventh (IAS-C2) via
the pays-off verdict integration of Part XVIII, and an eighth (IAS-G1) via the complexity-budget
simulation of Part XIX — a broader footprint than the matrix's minimum four, consistent with this
dataset's position as a late-loop, cross-cutting foresight capability rather than a narrow point tool.
Integration quality: HIGH for the mission-forecast and risk-simulation paths, which map onto existing,
already-specified consumer mechanisms (DRK's bounded walk, one_shot's contract compile) with a precise
handoff condition; MODERATE for the scenario-laboratory and governance-impact paths, which depend on
IAS-G1 and a not-yet-named governance authority respectively, both still maturing in this corpus;
LOWER, honestly, for the counterfactual institution, whose calibration tractability is explicitly
CONTESTED (21.3) and whose integration value is real but speculative until implemented and tested.
Reuse: substantial — the scenario engine's single-mechanism design (Decision 2) is reused across six
modes, the Monte-Carlo subengine is reused as a shared uncertainty service, and the fork mechanism
underlying the scenario laboratory is reused by both the counterfactual institution's inception step
and the self-referential-simulation edge case (22.3). Complexity cost: MODERATE-TO-HIGH — four sub-
models, one shared engine with six configurations, two further subengines, a reporting layer with a
mandatory gate, and a full lifecycle-and-versioning discipline for the dynamics model is a substantial
specification, though the reflexive non-duplication choices (Part XII) keep it from being larger than
its ten absorbed candidates would otherwise imply. Redundancy risk: LOW against the estate's existing
systems, per the ten explicit non-duplication boundary restatements across this dataset (Parts I, III,
V, VI, VIII, XIV, XIX, XX each independently reinforce a boundary); the more material residual risk is
internal — the honesty gate becoming, in a rushed implementation, exactly the kind of governance that
erodes under pressure, which is why Decision 3 marks it non-negotiable rather than subject to ordinary
trade-off review.

FINAL LAW — PART XXIII. A federation that can rehearse its own future without ever mistaking the
rehearsal for the performance has built genuine foresight; a federation that cannot tell the difference
has built a more expensive way to be wrong with more confidence, and every decision, anti-pattern, and
score in this Part exists to keep this dataset on the correct side of that line.

D2A DECISION RECORD

Original D2A verdict: per NON_DUPLICATION_LEDGER FAMILY K, candidates 101-110 (digital twin, mission
simulation, architecture scenarios, demand simulation, risk simulation, capital forecasting simulation,
governance-impact simulation, extinction simulation, institutional monte-carlo, counterfactual) were
jointly rendered BUILD+MERGE → IAS-F3, against closest parents DRK (blast radius) and one_shot, with the
overlap named precisely as "one decision's radius" and the unique object named as "simulate the whole
federation before spending."

Irreducible core demonstrated: this dataset has specified, across twenty-four Parts, a mechanism — the
maintained four-layer twin (mirror, scenario engine, Monte-Carlo subengine, reporting layer) — that
performs an operation no single existing system can perform even in principle: running the ensemble's
dynamics model forward under a hypothetical change and observing FEDERATION-WIDE emergent-property
consequence, banded by computed (not asserted) uncertainty, before the change is committed. DRK's
bounded walk cannot do this because it is decision-local by design (Part 20.1). One_shot's feasibility
check cannot do this because it is contract-local and does not model dynamics forward at all. Arch-
decision cannot do this because it reasons about one architectural choice, not a simulated trajectory
compared against a counterfactual. The irreducible core — federation-scale forward simulation with
computed uncertainty and a mandatory honesty gate against overconfidence — survives independently of
every parent's own mechanism, and every one of the ten absorbed candidates (Part header, "Absorbs")
is realized as a configuration of this one core rather than as a separate system, per Decision 2's
architectural choice.

Sovereignty test result: PASS, with the following honest qualification stated per Article V. The
sovereignty test asks whether the proposed system's distinct object survives even after every named
overlap is folded into its closest parent — here, whether "simulate the whole federation before
spending" remains a coherent, non-duplicative, valuable object once DRK's blast-radius mechanism and
one_shot's feasibility mechanism are fully credited for what they already do. It does: DRK and one_shot
each answer a question IAS-F3 does not answer (what does THIS decision touch; is THIS contract
feasible), and IAS-F3 answers a question neither of them can phrase (what does the FEDERATION become).
The qualification: the sovereignty test is passed at the SPECIFICATION level, per this mission's
Article VIII scope; Part 17.5 already names honestly that no live twin instance exists to backtest
this dataset's own mechanism claims against, and the sovereignty verdict's full empirical confirmation
— an actually-built twin whose divergence records show real forecast accuracy — remains, correctly,
future work for an implementation mission this dataset does not undertake.

FINAL LAW — PART XXIV. The federation earns the right to call this dataset sovereign not because its
object is clever but because ten absorbed candidates collapse into one irreducible core that answers a
question none of its parents can phrase, that core is specified precisely enough for a later mission to
build honestly against, and its final honest qualification — that the sovereignty verdict itself awaits
the very empirical evidence this dataset's own evaluation doctrine (Part XVII) requires before any claim
graduates from DERIVED to OBSERVED — is the dataset practicing, on its own closing claim, the exact
epistemic discipline it demands of every twin forecast it specifies.
